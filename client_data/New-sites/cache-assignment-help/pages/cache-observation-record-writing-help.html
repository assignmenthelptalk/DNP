CACHE Observation Record Writing: How to Observe, Analyse, and Link to Child Development Theory

An observation record in a CACHE Level 3 Early Years portfolio is not a description of what a child did. It is a structured assessment document with two distinct sections — an objective description section and an analytical section — each assessed against separate criteria. The objective description records only what was directly seen and heard, in precise language free from interpretation. The analytical section then applies named child development theory with correct Harvard citations, identifies the relevant EYFS 2021 areas of learning and development, and proposes specific next steps for the child. Failing to maintain this structural separation is the most common cause of referral in observation records, even when the student's understanding of child development is accurate and detailed. This page explains each component of a CACHE observation record, all six observation methods, objective writing technique, theory-to-practice linking, EYFS area identification, and next steps structure.

What a CACHE Observation Record Is and Why It Is a Distinct Assessment Format

A CACHE observation record is a piece of portfolio evidence that documents a student's ability to observe children's behaviour and development in a real practice setting and to analyse what they observed against child development theory and the EYFS 2021 framework. It is distinct from other evidence formats — written assignments, reflective accounts, and professional discussion — because it requires the student to function as both an objective recorder and a theoretically informed analyst within the same document.

The distinction between the two sections of an observation record is a formal assessment requirement, not a stylistic preference. Assessors are trained to evaluate each section against specific criteria. The objective description section is assessed on accuracy, objectivity, and the use of appropriate recording format for the chosen method. The analytical section is assessed on the depth of theoretical application, the accuracy of EYFS area identification, and the specificity and developmental appropriateness of the next steps. Mixing interpretive language into the objective description — writing "the child seemed excited" rather than "the child smiled, jumped on the spot twice, and clapped her hands" — introduces subjective judgement that should appear only in the analytical section. The assessor cannot award the criterion for objective recording if interpretive language is embedded in the description.

Observation records must be completed in a registered Early Years setting with a child who has been identified with appropriate safeguarding consent for observation. The child's name must be replaced with a pseudonym or initials in the portfolio copy, and any identifying information must be removed. The record must include: the date of the observation, the child's age in years and months at the time of observation, the setting type, the observation method used, the length of the observation, and the student's name and assessor's signature. These administrative elements are not optional — missing administrative information may cause the record to be returned for completion before it can be assessed.

The Six CACHE Observation Methods: Narrative, Time Sampling, Event Sampling, Anecdotal, Sociogram, and Child Tracker

The six observation methods used in CACHE Level 3 Early Years portfolios each serve a specific observation purpose and require a different recording format. Selecting the wrong method for the stated purpose — or using one method while labelling another — will result in referral on the method identification criterion.

A narrative observation (also called a running record) is a continuous written record of everything a child does and says during a defined observation period, typically five to fifteen minutes. It is the most detailed observation method and is best used when the assessor wants a rich, sequential picture of a child's activity, language, or interaction pattern. The narrative is written in the present tense during the observation, switching to past tense in the analytical section. Example opening: "C. (aged 3 years 4 months) picks up the blue car and places it at the top of the ramp. He watches it slide down, then retrieves it and repeats the action."

A time sampling observation records a child's activity or behaviour at fixed intervals — typically every five or ten minutes — across a longer observation period of thirty to sixty minutes. The recording format is usually a table with time intervals in the left column and brief descriptions in the right column. Time sampling is best used to track activity patterns, attention span, or engagement across a session. It is not suitable for capturing detailed interaction sequences. Misidentifying a running record as a time sampling observation — or vice versa — is a frequent error in student portfolios.

An event sampling observation records each time a specific pre-defined behaviour or event occurs during an observation period, noting the time, trigger, child's response, and consequence. It is best used to track the frequency and context of a particular behaviour — for example, recording each instance of a child seeking adult reassurance, or each episode of conflict with peers. The recording format is a table with columns for time, antecedent (what happened before), behaviour, and consequence (ABC format). Event sampling is suited to behaviour analysis rather than to general developmental observation.

An anecdotal record is a brief written account of a significant event or interaction that was not pre-planned — recorded after the fact from memory, typically within the same day. It is the least structured of the six methods and is used to capture meaningful moments that occur spontaneously. Because it is written retrospectively, it is more susceptible to interpretive language entering the description, and students must be particularly careful to maintain objective language in the description section. Anecdotal records are best used alongside other methods to supplement planned observations with unplanned evidence of the same developmental area.

A sociogram maps the social relationships between children in a group setting, typically over a fixed observation period. The student observes whom each child interacts with, who initiates interactions, and the direction and nature of social connections. The result is a visual diagram showing nodes (children) and lines (interactions) — arrows indicate who initiated. A sociogram is suited to evidence relating to PSED (Personal, Social and Emotional Development), specifically the "building relationships" aspect of the EYFS, and to Bandura's (1977) social learning theory. It is not suited to individual developmental observation and should not be used as the sole observation format for a child's developmental profile.

A child tracker observation records a child's physical movements and locations within a setting over a defined period, typically using a floor plan diagram on which the student marks the child's path with a line. It is best used to analyse a child's use of space, engagement with different areas of provision, and physical development patterns. A child tracker is particularly suited to evidence relating to Physical Development in the EYFS Prime Areas. It provides quantitative location data rather than qualitative interaction data and is therefore always supplemented by a narrative or anecdotal component when used in a portfolio observation.

Writing the Objective Description Section: What to Record and What to Exclude

The objective description section of a CACHE observation record must contain only what was directly seen and heard during the observation period. This means the student records observable behaviours — actions, movements, verbalisations, facial expressions — without attributing emotion, intention, motive, or developmental meaning. The analytical interpretation of these behaviours appears only in the analytical section.

The key test for any sentence in the description section is: could two different practitioners, watching the same moment, both record this sentence identically? If one practitioner might write "C. seemed frustrated" and another might write "C. appeared calm," the sentence contains interpretive language and should be replaced with the specific observable behaviour: "C. pushed the block away, turned away from the table, and did not resume the activity." This records what was seen without attributing emotional state.

Language that must not appear in the objective description section includes: emotional attributions (happy, sad, frustrated, excited, scared, proud); intentional attributions (trying to, wanting to, hoping to, attempting to); developmental interpretations (showing understanding of, demonstrating, displaying cognitive development); and evaluative statements (impressively, confidently, well). These terms are all analytical — they interpret meaning from behaviour rather than recording the behaviour itself. Each of them can appear in the analytical section with appropriate theoretical grounding.

Appropriate objective language uses precise, behavioural verbs: picks up, places, says, looks at, moves to, reaches for, vocalises, makes eye contact, turns toward, pushes away, sits down. Facial expression and body posture can be recorded objectively by describing the observable feature rather than inferring the emotion: "C. raises the corners of her mouth and shows her upper teeth" rather than "C. smiles happily." The first sentence is objective; the second contains an emotional attribution ("happily"). At Level 3, assessors expect this level of precision in the description section, particularly for Distinction-level portfolios.

The description section should also record the environmental context: the area of the setting (creative area, outdoor area, book corner), the materials and resources present, the time of day, other children or adults present, and any adult interactions during the observation period. This context is essential for the analytical section — without knowing that an interaction occurred in the book corner at story time, the assessor cannot evaluate the claim that the behaviour relates to Literacy under the EYFS Specific Areas.

Linking Observations to Child Development Theory: Piaget, Vygotsky, Bowlby, Bruner, and Bandura with Citation Examples

The analytical section of an observation record must apply at least one child development theory to the observed behaviour, with a correct Harvard in-text citation and a specific explanation of how the theory applies to what was observed. Naming a theorist without connecting their specific framework to the specific observed behaviour is not sufficient — "this shows Piaget's theory" does not meet the analytical criterion. The connection must be explicit: the specific concept, the specific behaviour, and the specific link between them.

Jean Piaget's (1952) schema theory applies when a child repeats a physical action pattern across different materials or contexts — filling and emptying, rotating objects, transporting items. Example citation: "C.'s repeated action of filling the container with sand, emptying it, and refilling it is consistent with Piaget's (1952) concept of a filling and emptying schema, in which the child builds a cognitive structure through repeated schematic behaviour. This behaviour also reflects the process of assimilation (Piaget, 1952), as C. is incorporating the sand into an existing schema previously observed with water." Piaget's stage theory applies when the observation provides evidence of preoperational thought — egocentrism, animism, centration, or irreversibility — in children between two and seven years.

Lev Vygotsky's (1978) Zone of Proximal Development applies when an adult or peer interaction scaffolds a child's achievement beyond what they could accomplish independently. Example: "The interaction in which the key person offered verbal prompts rather than completing the puzzle for C. is consistent with Vygotsky's (1978) concept of scaffolding within the Zone of Proximal Development — the support adjusts to C.'s current level of competence, allowing C. to complete the task with guided assistance that would be beyond independent achievement." The citation should specify the concept and the year: (Vygotsky, 1978).

John Bowlby's (1969) Attachment Theory applies when the observation involves separation from a caregiver, the use of the key person as a secure base, or behaviour that suggests attachment security or insecurity. Example: "C.'s return to the key person after exploring the outdoor area, making eye contact and vocalising before returning to independent play, is consistent with Bowlby's (1969) concept of the secure base, in which a securely attached child uses the attachment figure as a safe haven from which to explore the environment." Where attachment type is identified, Ainsworth (1978) should be cited for the classification rather than Bowlby.

Albert Bandura's (1977) Social Learning Theory applies when the observation shows imitative behaviour or peer-influenced learning. Example: "C.'s adoption of the stirring action following observation of an older peer is consistent with Bandura's (1977) social learning theory, which proposes that children learn behaviours through observation and imitation of models." Where the sociogram method is used, Bandura's (1977) social learning framework is the most directly applicable theoretical basis for the analytical section. Jerome Bruner's (1966) enactive representation mode applies when the child is learning through physical action before progressing to iconic or symbolic representation — this is particularly relevant for observations of very young children or for new skill acquisition in older children.

Identifying EYFS Areas of Learning and Development in Observation Records

Every observation record must identify which EYFS 2021 areas of learning and development are evidenced by the observed behaviour. The identification must use the precise EYFS 2021 terminology — not approximations or previous framework terms. The seven areas are the three Prime Areas (Communication and Language; Physical Development; Personal, Social and Emotional Development) and the four Specific Areas (Literacy; Mathematics; Understanding the World; Expressive Arts and Design). Where relevant, the specific aspect within each area should also be named: for example, "Communication and Language — Listening, Attention and Understanding" rather than simply "Communication and Language."

Most observations will evidence more than one EYFS area. An observation of a child building with blocks typically evidences Physical Development (fine motor skills under "Moving and Handling" in older frameworks, now covered under Physical Development), Understanding the World (spatial reasoning, properties of objects), and Expressive Arts and Design (creative use of materials and construction). If the child talks during the activity, Communication and Language (speaking) is also evidenced. Identifying multiple areas and explaining how the observed behaviour demonstrates each one separately shows depth of EYFS knowledge. Identifying only one area when multiple are evidenced shows superficial application.

The EYFS Characteristics of Effective Learning — Playing and Exploring, Active Learning, and Creating and Thinking Critically — are sometimes confused with the areas of learning. They are a separate framework within the EYFS that describes how children engage with learning. A child who persists with a challenging puzzle demonstrates "Active Learning — Keeping on trying" from the Characteristics framework; the area of learning evidenced might be Understanding the World (spatial reasoning) or Literacy (if the puzzle involves matching pictures with letters). Both the relevant area and the relevant characteristic can be referenced in the analytical section, but they must be clearly distinguished from each other.

Writing Next Steps: The Four-Component Structure for Observation Conclusions

The next steps section of an observation record concludes the analytical section with specific, evidence-based recommendations for supporting the child's further development. Generic next steps — "continue to support the child's development" or "provide more opportunities for creative play" — do not meet the assessment criteria because they are not derived from the specific evidence in the observation. Next steps must be specific to the child, the observed developmental stage, and the EYFS area identified.

A well-structured next steps section has four components. The first component names the developmental area and the specific stage identified in the observation — for example, "the observation indicates that C. is in the early stages of developing symbolic representation, consistent with Piaget's (1952) pre-operational stage." The second component proposes a specific activity or intervention to support progression — "to support the development of symbolic play, the key person could introduce small world play materials with figurines that can be used to represent characters in a narrative, building on C.'s current interest in the car ramp." The third component identifies which adult, when, and in what context the next step will be implemented — "the key person to introduce this resource during the daily free play session in the home area over the next two weeks." The fourth component connects the next step to a specific EYFS area — "this activity will support progression within Expressive Arts and Design — Being Imaginative and Expressive, and Communication and Language — Speaking."

Next steps that jump from observation to activity without explaining the developmental rationale miss the analytical chain that connects evidence to planning. Assessors at Distinction level look for this explicit connection — the observation identifies what the child can do and what is at the edge of their development (Vygotsky's ZPD); the next step proposes the scaffolding that will advance development within that zone; the EYFS area identifies which aspect of the curriculum is being targeted. All three elements must be present for the next steps section to meet the Distinction criterion for planning.

How Observation Records Connect to the Wider Level 3 Portfolio — and Why Structure Determines Grade

Observation records in the Level 3 portfolio are not standalone pieces of evidence — they are cross-referenced to multiple assessment criteria across different units. An observation of a child's language development may meet criteria in the child development unit, the EYFS unit, the communication and language unit, and the planning and assessment unit simultaneously. Students who produce well-structured observations with accurate objective descriptions, theoretically grounded analysis, precise EYFS area identification, and specific next steps will find that a single observation record can satisfy many criteria across the portfolio. Students whose observations lack structural precision will find that even high-quality practice descriptions generate multiple referrals because the required analytical elements are missing or embedded in the wrong section.

The structural discipline required for observation records — maintaining the separation between description and analysis — is the same discipline required for reflective accounts, where the description of a practice episode must be separated from the analytical evaluation of that episode. Students who master this structural principle in observation records often find that their reflective accounts improve significantly as a result, because the underlying skill is the same: recording what happened objectively, then interpreting it theoretically. Both formats reward this discipline with fewer referrals and higher portfolio grades.

Reflective Accounts, Portfolio Mapping, and the Level 3 Assessment Cycle

Observation records and reflective accounts are the two most distinctive evidence formats in the CACHE Level 3 Early Years portfolio, and they complement each other within the assessment cycle. An observation identifies a specific developmental need or achievement in a child; the student then reflects on their own practice in observing and supporting that child; the reflective account analyses their professional response to the evidence gathered in the observation. This sequential relationship means that strong observation records create stronger reflective account opportunities — the more precisely the observation identifies the child's developmental position, the more analytically grounded the reflection on practice can be.

Portfolio mapping is the process that makes this connection visible to the assessor. When a student maps an observation record to the assessment criteria for the child development unit and also maps the related reflective account to criteria in the reflective practice unit, showing how they are connected in the mapping document, the assessor can follow the evidence chain from practice observation through to professional reflection. This cross-referencing demonstrates the integrated portfolio approach that characterises Distinction-level portfolios at CACHE Level 3.

Harvard referencing in observation records applies to the analytical section only — the objective description section does not require citations. But every theorist named in the analytical section must have an in-text citation in the format (Author, Year), and all sources cited in the analytical sections of all observation records must appear in the portfolio's reference list. Building the reference list incrementally as each observation is completed prevents the significant workload of constructing the entire reference list at the point of portfolio submission.

Can I include my opinion in the objective description section of a CACHE observation?

No — the objective description section must contain only what was directly observed: actions, movements, verbalisations, and facial expressions or body posture described in physical terms. Emotional attributions (the child seemed excited, appeared upset), intentional attributions (the child was trying to, hoping to), and developmental interpretations (the child was demonstrating mathematical understanding) must all appear in the analytical section only. Mixing interpretive language into the description section will result in referral on the objective recording criterion regardless of the accuracy of the interpretation.

Which observation method is best for recording social interaction between children?

A sociogram is specifically designed to map social relationships and interactions between children in a group setting, recording who interacts with whom, who initiates interactions, and the direction of social connections. For a more detailed sequential record of a specific social interaction episode, a narrative observation is more appropriate. Event sampling is suitable if the student wants to track a specific social behaviour — such as initiating play — each time it occurs over a session. The choice should be driven by the observation purpose stated in the record header.

How many theorists should I link to in one observation record?

The minimum is one theorist with a correct citation and specific explanation. Most Distinction-level observation records reference two theorists whose frameworks are both genuinely applicable to the specific behaviour observed. Referencing more theorists does not improve the grade if the connections are superficial — quality of application is more important than quantity. A single theorist applied with precision and specificity (naming the exact concept, the year, and the specific link to the observed behaviour) will meet Distinction criteria; three theorists named generically will not pass the analytical criterion.

What does "next steps" mean in a CACHE observation record?

Next steps in a CACHE observation record are specific, evidence-based recommendations for supporting the child's further development, derived directly from the observation evidence and the developmental analysis. They must name a specific activity or intervention, identify who will implement it and when, and connect it to a specific EYFS area of learning. Generic statements such as "provide more opportunities for play" or "continue to support the child's development" do not meet the criterion because they are not derived from the specific evidence gathered in the observation. Effective next steps form an explicit developmental chain: observed stage → ZPD or schema stage identified → targeted scaffolding activity → EYFS area advanced.
