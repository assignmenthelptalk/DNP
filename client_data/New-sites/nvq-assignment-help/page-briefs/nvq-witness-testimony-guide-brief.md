## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: NVQ witness testimony (third-party evidence type)
**Main Attributes**: definition and purpose, who qualifies as a witness, what the testimony must include, how to request one, how many are needed, VACS criteria applied to testimonies, cross-referencing across units, what makes a testimony invalid
**Derived Attributes**:
- **What a witness testimony is**: A written statement provided by someone who has directly observed the NVQ candidate carrying out specific work activities — confirming that the candidate performed those activities to the required standard. Also called "third-party evidence," "expert witness testimony," or "witness statement" depending on the awarding organisation. It is NOT a general character reference or a recommendation letter — it is evidence of specific observable competence.
- **Who qualifies as a witness**: (1) Direct line supervisor or senior colleague in the same workplace — must have directly observed the activity being testified to; (2) Registered manager or service manager — appropriate if they have supervised or observed the candidate's practice; (3) External professional — a visiting nurse, social worker, or other regulated professional who observed the candidate during a joint visit; (4) Occupational expert witness — a suitably qualified professional in the same occupational area who can confirm competence standards are met. Who does NOT qualify: family members of the candidate, friends with no professional relationship, or anyone who did not personally witness the activity.
- **What a valid witness testimony must include**: (1) The witness's full name; (2) The witness's job title and employer; (3) The witness's relationship to the candidate (e.g., "line manager," "visiting nurse"); (4) A clear description of the specific activities observed — naming the performance criteria or unit elements the testimony relates to; (5) Confirmation that the activity was carried out to the required standard; (6) The date(s) of observation; (7) The witness's signature; (8) Contact details for the witness (name, phone or email — so the assessor can verify the testimony if needed)
- **VACS criteria applied to witness testimonies**: Valid — the testimony describes activities directly relevant to the unit PCs being claimed; Authentic — the witness genuinely observed the activities (assessors may contact the witness to verify); Current — the observations are recent (within the last 2–3 years); Sufficient — the testimony addresses all PCs in the element it is filed against, or is supplemented by additional evidence for any PCs not covered by the testimony alone
- **How many testimonies are needed**: This varies by qualification, awarding organisation, and unit. Some units may require only one witness testimony alongside other evidence; others may need multiple testimonies across different observers. As a general guide: most NVQ portfolios include 5–15 witness testimonies across the full qualification. Candidates should not rely exclusively on testimonies — direct observation by the assessor remains the primary evidence type.
- **How to request a witness testimony**: The candidate approaches the witness, explains the NVQ and the specific units they are seeking evidence for, provides the witness testimony form (supplied by the awarding organisation or the training provider), and gives the witness enough information about what to include (specific activities observed, relevant PCs, dates). Candidates should not dictate or write the testimony for the witness — the testimony must be in the witness's own words. Some candidates write a brief bullet-point prompt card for witnesses to help them recall what they observed.
- **Cross-referencing testimonies across units**: A single witness testimony that describes activities relevant to multiple units can be cross-referenced in the portfolio index. The testimony is filed once and noted across all units it evidences. This is the same principle as cross-referencing personal statements.
- **What makes a testimony invalid**: (1) Written entirely by the candidate, with the witness only signing — the testimony must be the witness's own account; (2) No specific activities described — "Jane is an excellent care worker" is not evidence; (3) Undated — testimonies without dates cannot be verified as current; (4) From a non-workplace witness (family member, friend); (5) Testimonies describing activities the witness did not personally observe ("My colleague told me that Jane gave medication correctly")

#### H2: What a Valid NVQ Witness Testimony Must Include
> **EAV to cover**: All 8 required components with explanation and contrast (invalid vs valid extract)
> **Word count**: 280–330 words
> **Specific content**:
> - A valid witness testimony must include all of the following:
>   1. Witness full name
>   2. Witness job title and employer
>   3. Witness relationship to the candidate ("line manager," "registered nurse")
>   4. Description of the specific activities observed — naming what the candidate did (not just "she is competent")
>   5. Reference to the NVQ unit or performance criteria the testimony relates to
>   6. Date(s) of observation
>   7. Witness signature
>   8. Witness contact details (phone or email — for assessor verification)

Jane knocked before entering, explained each step of the care process to Mrs T, maintained her dignity throughout by keeping her covered, and recorded the activity in the care plan immediately after. This directly demonstrates competence in Unit 24.1a, 24.1b, and 24.1c." — Specific, dated, signed, with PC references. Accepted.

#### H2: What Makes a Witness Testimony Invalid — Common Reasons for Rejection
> **EAV to cover**: Four most common failure reasons
> **Word count**: 220–260 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **General impression statements**: "John is a competent support worker" — no specific activity, no observable evidence. Assessors cannot accept this as evidence of a specific PC.
> - **Candidate-written testimonies**: If the candidate writes the testimony and the witness only signs, it is not authentic — it reflects the candidate's view of their own competence, not independent observation. Testimonies must be in the witness's own words. Candidates can prompt witnesses with bullet points of what was observed — but the witness must write their own account.
> - **Undated testimonies**: Cannot be assessed as "current" without a date. Every testimony must state when the observation took place.
> - **Non-observing witnesses**: A witness who states "I have been told that Jane carries out personal care well" has not personally observed the activity. This is hearsay, not evidence.

#### H2: How to Request a Witness Testimony From a Workplace Observer
> **EAV to cover**: Process, what to give the witness, prompting vs writing for them
> **Word count**: 220–260 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Approach the witness professionally — explain you are completing an NVQ and that you need written confirmation of specific activities they have observed you carrying out
> - Provide the witness with: the awarding organisation's witness testimony form (or the format your training provider specifies); a brief explanation of the units and PCs you are seeking evidence for; a bullet-point prompt note listing the specific activities you want them to describe (so they remember exactly what they observed and know what to include)
> - The testimony must be in the witness's own words — do not write it for them and ask them to sign it. You can offer to review a draft for factual accuracy but the words must be the witness's
> - Allow the witness time — some workplace supervisors are busy. Give reasonable notice and follow up politely if needed.
> - Keep a copy of the witness's contact details for your records — the assessor may need to verify the testimony

#### H2: Cross-Referencing Witness Testimonies Across Units
> **Content**: A single witness testimony that describes activities relevant to performance criteria in more than one NVQ unit can be cross-referenced across units in the portfolio index. The testimony is filed in the unit where it is most directly relevant, and the portfolio index notes the other units it partially evidences. This maximises efficiency — one well-written testimony from a registered manager observing a complex care activity may provide evidence across three or four units simultaneously. Cross-referencing is accepted by all major awarding organisations (City & Guilds, Pearson, NCFE, Highfield) and reduces the total evidence burden on the candidate.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Can my employer write a witness testimony for my NVQ?**
An employer can provide a witness testimony if they have directly observed the specific activities being evidenced. A line manager, registered manager, or senior colleague who supervised or watched the candidate perform the activity is an appropriate witness. However, an employer who has not personally observed the specific activities described cannot provide a valid testimony — and a general employment reference ("this candidate is reliable and professional") does not function as NVQ evidence regardless of who writes it.

**Q: How many witness testimonies does an NVQ portfolio need?**
The number depends on the qualification, level, and awarding organisation guidance. Most NVQ portfolios include between 5 and 15 witness testimonies across the full qualification — typically spread across units where direct assessor observation was not arranged for all activities. Candidates should not aim for a specific number but should ensure every unit element has sufficient evidence, whether from direct observation, personal statements, professional discussion, or witness testimony, to meet VACS criteria.

**Q: Can a colleague provide a witness testimony if they are not my manager?**
A colleague at the same level (peer) can provide a witness testimony if they directly observed the activities described, hold an appropriate occupational role, and are accepted by the awarding organisation's witness category criteria. Most awarding organisations accept senior colleagues or specialist co-workers (e.g., a nurse colleague observed during a joint patient visit). A junior colleague or a peer with no direct supervisory relationship is less likely to be accepted. Confirm acceptable witness categories with your assessor before collecting testimonies from peer colleagues.

**Q: What happens if my assessor rejects a witness testimony?**
If a testimony is rejected, the assessor will explain why — typically because the testimony lacks specific activity descriptions, is undated, or appears to be non-authentic. The candidate must approach the witness again and request a revised statement addressing the assessor's specific concerns. If the witness is no longer available or willing to revise, the candidate must find alternative evidence for the PCs not covered — through a professional discussion, an additional personal statement, or requesting a new observation opportunity.
