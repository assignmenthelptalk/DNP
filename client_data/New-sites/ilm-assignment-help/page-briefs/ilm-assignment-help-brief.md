#### H2: How ILM Assignments Are Assessed: Work-Based Evidence and the Criterion-Addressing Rule
> **EAV to cover**: criterion-referenced assessment, work-based evidence requirement, evidence types (written reports, management plans, meeting minutes, 360-degree feedback, witness statements), the rule that every criterion must be addressed, referral consequence of missing one criterion, assessor role
> **Word count**: 300–380 words
> **Specific content**:
> - All ILM assignments are assessed criterion by criterion — the assessor checks each assessment criterion in the unit specification and marks whether the submission provides sufficient evidence for each one
> - THE critical rule: every criterion must be individually addressed in the assignment — missing one criterion results in a referral (fail), regardless of how well other criteria are covered; this makes criterion mapping (annotating the draft assignment against each criterion before submission) essential preparation
> - Work-based evidence requirement: all evidence must come from the candidate's own workplace — ILM does not accept hypothetical scenarios or simulated evidence; real management activities, real outcomes, and real workplace context are required
> - Evidence types that count: written analytical reports evidencing management practice, management plans (action plans, development plans, business cases), meeting agendas and minutes (as supporting evidence), 360-degree feedback results (with analysis), witness statements from line managers or senior colleagues (to corroborate management activity claims), coaching session records (for Coaching and Mentoring pathway)
> - The assessor's role: assessors are looking for specific evidence that each criterion has been met — they are not assessing the quality of the management described; a mediocre management decision that is thoroughly evidenced and analytically reflected on will pass; a brilliant management achievement described without connecting to each criterion will be referred
> - Common failure mode: candidates write extensive reports about their management role but do not specifically address each criterion — they describe what they did without connecting each section explicitly to the assessment criterion it evidences

#### H2: Reflective Writing for ILM Assignments: Gibbs, Kolb, and Schon in Practice
> **Content**: Covers the three primary reflective frameworks used in ILM assignments. Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988): six stages (Description, Feelings, Evaluation, Analysis, Conclusion, Action Plan) — the most commonly used in ILM Level 3 and Level 5; Distinction-level Analysis stage requires connecting workplace practice to leadership theory. Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (1984): four stages (Concrete Experience, Reflective Observation, Abstract Conceptualisation, Active Experimentation) — connects direct experience to theory (Abstract Conceptualisation stage) and back to practice (Active Experimentation); useful for Level 5 leadership development plans. Schon's reflection-on-action and reflection-in-action (1983, 1987): distinguishes between reflecting after an event and adjusting practice during an event — useful for Level 7 executive coaching assignments. The key principle across all models: moving beyond description (what happened) to analysis (what theory explains it) and forward planning (what will change). Links to hub page.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is ILM and how are its qualifications assessed?**
ILM (Institute of Leadership and Management) is an Ofqual-regulated leadership qualification body and a City & Guilds Group company. All ILM qualifications are assessed entirely through work-based written evidence — there are no exams. Candidates must provide evidence from their own workplace that addresses every assessment criterion in each unit. Evidence types include written analytical reports, management plans, reflective accounts, meeting minutes, 360-degree feedback analysis, and witness statements. Missing any one criterion in a unit results in a referral regardless of the overall quality of the submission.

**Q: Which ILM levels does this service cover?**
The service covers ILM qualifications at Level 3 (team leaders and supervisors — Understanding Leadership, Conflict Management, Motivating People), Level 5 (middle managers and operations managers — Leading Innovation and Change, Becoming an Effective Leader, Financial Management), and Level 7 (senior leaders — Developing Strategic Leadership, Strategic Change, Executive Coaching). Guidance is also available for the ILM Coaching and Mentoring pathway at Levels 3, 5, and 7. Unit-specific guidance addresses the exact assessment criteria for each unit at each level.

**Q: Do ILM assignments require Harvard referencing?**
Harvard referencing is required at ILM Level 5 and Level 7. At Level 5, management theory must be cited with in-text references (Author, Year) and a full reference list. At Level 7, academic source quality is assessed — peer-reviewed management journals (Harvard Business Review, Leadership Quarterly, Strategic Management Journal) are expected alongside academic textbooks. At Level 3, Harvard referencing is not formally required but using academic references to support management theory citations demonstrates academic rigour and strengthens the assignment.

**Q: What is the difference between an ILM Award, Certificate, and Diploma?**
ILM qualifications at each level are available in three types: Award (shortest — 1–3 units, targeted skill development, often employer-funded for a specific management capability), Certificate (medium — 4–6 units, more comprehensive qualification), and Diploma (full qualification — highest credit value, most comprehensive evidence of management competence at the given level). All three types are Ofqual-regulated. The Diploma provides the most credible qualification for career progression applications and professional development portfolios.
