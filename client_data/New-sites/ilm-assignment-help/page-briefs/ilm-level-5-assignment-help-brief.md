#### H2: ILM Level 5 Leading Innovation and Change Assignment: Kotter, Lewin, and ADKAR Evidence
> **EAV to cover**: Kotter's 8-step change model (Establish urgency, Build guiding coalition, Form strategic vision, Enlist volunteer army, Enable action by removing barriers, Generate short-term wins, Sustain acceleration, Institute change), Lewin's force field analysis (driving forces, restraining forces, unfreezing-moving-refreezing), Prosci ADKAR model (Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement), work-based evidence requirement (own change initiative), assessment criteria structure, Distinction-level critical evaluation
> **Word count**: 340–420 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Leading Innovation and Change is one of the most popular ILM Level 5 units and requires candidates to evidence managing a real change initiative in their organisation
> - Assessment criteria typically require: analysis of the factors driving and resisting change, planning a change initiative using an appropriate change model, implementation evidence, and evaluation of the change outcomes
> - Pass criteria: describe a change initiative managed in own organisation; apply one change management model (Kotter, Lewin, or ADKAR) to explain the approach; identify driving and resisting forces
> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate the effectiveness of the change management approach used; assess what worked, what did not, and why; compare the chosen change model to an alternative and justify the choice; plan what would be done differently if implementing similar change in future
> - Kotter's 8-step model: most widely used in ILM Level 5 assignments — Distinction requires evaluation of whether each step was adequately addressed in the actual change and why certain steps were problematic (often Step 1 — establishing genuine urgency — and Step 3 — forming a clear, communicable vision)
> - Lewin's force field analysis: candidates list driving forces (factors supporting change) and restraining forces (factors opposing change) for their specific initiative; Distinction requires evaluation of which forces were most significant and what the analysis reveals about change strategy; the unfreezing-moving-refreezing model must be applied to the actual change timeline
> - ADKAR: useful for evaluating individual-level change readiness — Distinction requires critical evaluation of where individual team members were in the ADKAR model during the change and what this reveals about the change management approach's effectiveness
> - Work-based evidence: candidates must describe a real change they managed (or contributed to managing); if the candidate's role was not the change leader, they can evidence their contribution to the change within their area of responsibility

> **EAV to cover**: 360-degree feedback process (self-assessment + line manager + peers + direct reports), leadership theory connection (Hersey and Blanchard situational leadership — four leadership styles S1/S2/S3/S4 matched to follower readiness R1/R2/R3/R4; transformational leadership — Bass four components; authentic leadership — George's five dimensions), personal development planning (SMART objectives, specific development activities, success measures), reflective account structure, Distinction-level critical evaluation
> **Word count**: 340–420 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Becoming an Effective Leader requires candidates to critically evaluate their own leadership effectiveness using evidence — 360-degree feedback data or a validated leadership questionnaire — and produce a personal development plan
> - Assessment criteria typically require: evidence of leadership style assessment, analysis of leadership strengths and development areas using leadership theory, personal development plan with SMART objectives
> - Pass criteria: describe the 360-degree feedback findings; identify leadership strengths and development areas; connect findings to a leadership theory; produce a development plan
> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate what the feedback data reveals about leadership effectiveness; synthesise multiple sources of feedback (quantitative rating data + qualitative comments) into a coherent analysis of leadership impact; evaluate which leadership theory most accurately describes the current leadership style and whether it is appropriate for the team and context; produce a highly specific development plan with SMART objectives, defined development activities, and success measures
> - Hersey and Blanchard situational leadership: the four styles (S1 — Directing; S2 — Coaching; S3 — Supporting; S4 — Delegating) matched to follower readiness levels (R1 — low competence, low commitment; R2 — low competence, high commitment; R3 — high competence, variable commitment; R4 — high competence, high commitment); Distinction requires evaluation of whether the candidate is matching their leadership style to the actual readiness levels of different team members
> - 360-degree feedback analysis: Distinction requires pattern analysis across rater groups — do direct reports rate the candidate differently from peers? Is there a gap between self-assessment and how others perceive leadership? What explains the discrepancy? — not just reporting the numerical scores
> - Personal development plan at Distinction: SMART development objectives, specific development activities (not generic "attend a course"), timeline, how progress will be measured, how the development addresses the specific gap identified in the feedback

> **Word count**: 300–380 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Understanding Financial Management at ILM Level 5 requires candidates to evidence their financial management responsibilities — typically budget management, P&L interpretation, or business case development — and demonstrate analytical commentary on financial data
> - Assessment criteria typically require: evidence of own financial management responsibilities, analysis of financial information relevant to the management role, application of financial concepts to a management decision
> - Pass criteria: interpret a P&L account or budget report relevant to own area; identify favourable and adverse variances; describe how financial information supports management decisions
> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate the financial performance of own area using trend analysis; analyse the causes of significant variances (not just identify them — explain what management actions caused or failed to prevent them); produce a justified financial recommendation or business case using financial evidence
> - Variance analysis: favourable variance (actual better than budget — lower costs or higher revenue than planned); adverse variance (actual worse than budget — higher costs or lower revenue than planned); Distinction requires causal analysis — a 15% adverse variance on labour costs must be explained (overtime, agency staff, error in original budget?) and the management implications assessed
> - EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation and Amortisation): commonly used as a measure of operational financial performance — relevant for managers who report to senior leaders using this metric; Distinction requires evaluation of what EBITDA reveals about operational efficiency vs what it masks (depreciation and capital investment are excluded)
> - Business case: if the unit requires a business case for a financial decision (e.g., investment in equipment, hiring a member of staff), the candidate must include cost-benefit analysis with quantified benefits and a break-even analysis

> - Mendelow's power/interest matrix: maps stakeholders on two dimensions (power — ability to affect the project; interest — motivation to engage with the project) into four quadrants: high power/high interest (manage closely), high power/low interest (keep satisfied), low power/high interest (keep informed), low power/low interest (monitor); Distinction requires critical evaluation of whether stakeholder management was aligned with the power/interest matrix analysis

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What are the most popular ILM Level 5 units and what does each require?**
The most popular ILM Level 5 units are: Leading Innovation and Change (evidence of managing a real organisational change, applying Kotter's 8-step, Lewin's force field, or Prosci ADKAR model); Becoming an Effective Leader (360-degree feedback analysis, leadership theory application, personal development plan); Understanding Financial Management (P&L interpretation, variance analysis, evidence of own budgetary responsibilities); Managing Workplace Projects (project plan, Mendelow's stakeholder analysis, risk register, project evaluation); and Developing a Marketing Strategy (PESTLE, Porter's Five Forces, 7Ps — relevant for commercial managers). Every unit requires evidence from the candidate's own management role.

**Q: What is the difference between ILM Level 5 Award, Certificate, and Diploma?**
The Award, Certificate, and Diploma are the three qualification types available at ILM Level 5. The Award comprises 1–3 units — the shortest commitment, designed for targeted skill development, commonly employer-funded for a specific management capability. The Certificate comprises 4–6 units — a more comprehensive qualification. The Diploma is the full ILM Level 5 qualification — covering the broadest range of management competencies and providing the most credible evidence portfolio for career progression. All three are Ofqual-regulated and include the same work-based, criterion-referenced assessment approach.

**Q: How do I structure work-based evidence for an ILM Level 5 assignment?**
ILM Level 5 assignments require evidence from the candidate's own management role — not hypothetical scenarios. The structure for each unit follows the assessment criteria: download the unit specification, identify every criterion listed, ensure each section of the assignment explicitly addresses at least one criterion, annotate the draft against each criterion before submission. Evidence types include written analytical reports describing and evaluating own management practice, management plans and action plans, meeting records, financial reports, and 360-degree feedback data. Criterion mapping — checking off every criterion against the draft — is the most effective way to avoid a referral.

**Q: Does ILM Level 5 require Harvard referencing and which management theories should I cite?**
Harvard referencing is required at ILM Level 5. Key management theories with standard academic citations: Kotter, J.P. (1996). Leading Change. Harvard Business School Press — for change management; Hersey, P. and Blanchard, K.H. (1969). Management of Organizational Behavior. Prentice Hall — for situational leadership; Bass, B.M. (1985). Leadership and Performance Beyond Expectations. Free Press — for transformational leadership; Gibbs, G. (1988). Learning by Doing. FEU — for reflective writing; Kolb, D.A. (1984). Experiential Learning. Prentice-Hall — for reflective learning models. Management journal articles (Harvard Business Review, Management Today) can supplement academic textbooks.
