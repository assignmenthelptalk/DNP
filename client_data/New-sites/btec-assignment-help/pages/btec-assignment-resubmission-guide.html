BTEC Assignment Resubmission Guide: Rules, Process, and What to Do After a Referral

A Referral is not the end of a BTEC qualification. For internally assessed units, Pearson regulations guarantee one resubmission opportunity following a Referral, and the resubmission process has specific rules that many students misunderstand in ways that produce second Referrals. Understanding exactly what triggered the Referral, what the targeted resubmission brief requires, and what grade is achievable through resubmission — before writing begins — is the difference between a successful resubmission and a repeated failure on the same criteria.

What Is a BTEC Referral and What Causes It

A Referral is the BTEC term for not meeting all Pass criteria on a unit assignment. It is not a partial pass, not a conditional pass, and not a low pass — it means the minimum threshold has not been reached and the unit has not been achieved at any grade level. The outcome is binary: either all Pass criteria are met (a minimum Pass grade is awarded) or any Pass criterion is unmet (a Referral is the result).

The binary rule is absolute. One unmet Pass criterion produces a Referral regardless of how many other criteria the submission satisfies. A submission that meets every Merit and Distinction criterion but fails to explicitly address P3 will receive a Referral — not a Merit with a note about P3, but a full Referral on the unit. This is the most common source of unexpected Referrals: students who produce high-quality responses for advanced criteria without first verifying that every Pass criterion has been clearly and explicitly addressed.

The most common causes of Referral, in frequency order: first, a Pass criterion not addressed in the submission at all — the student did not produce any content for that criterion's topic area, or the content was placed in the wrong task section; second, a Pass criterion addressed at the wrong cognitive level — content that analyses rather than describes, for example, where "describe" was the criterion verb; third, an incomplete submission — evidence expected in the brief (observation records, laboratory results, portfolio artefacts) was missing from what was submitted. In some centres, incomplete submissions are managed through an interim submission process before the final deadline; in others, the first submission is the submission regardless of completeness.

A distinction between Referral and "incomplete submission" applies at some centres: where an interim or draft submission process operates, students may be able to add missing evidence before the final deadline without triggering the formal Referral and resubmission process. Where that process does not exist, the first submission against the deadline is treated as the final submission for assessment purposes.

The BTEC Resubmission Process for Internally Assessed Units

For internally assessed BTEC units, Pearson's assessment regulations guarantee one resubmission opportunity following a Referral. This entitlement is a regulatory requirement, not a discretionary decision by the centre: a student who submitted their original work by the centre's deadline cannot be denied the resubmission opportunity. However, late submission of the original work may affect resubmission entitlement — centres are not required to offer resubmission for work submitted after the centre's own deadline. If a late submission is being considered, check the centre's late submission policy before submitting.

After the Referral is confirmed, the tutor's regulatory obligation is to issue a new or amended Assignment Brief that targets only the criteria not met in the original submission. This is not a full redo brief — issuing a full redo brief does not comply with Pearson's assessment regulations. The targeted brief must specify which criteria are being addressed in the resubmission. If the brief received appears to require a full redo of the entire assignment, ask the tutor or programme coordinator in writing to clarify which specific criteria the resubmission is expected to address. This question — and the response to it — should be documented.

The student submits only new evidence addressing the unmet criteria. Evidence that already met its criteria in the original submission remains in the portfolio and does not need to be resubmitted or improved. The resubmission should be focused exclusively on the criteria identified in the targeted brief — revisiting and rewriting already-awarded evidence risks inadvertently altering the quality of evidence that has already been credited.

Resubmission deadlines are set by the centre and must fall within the same academic year as the original submission. Missing the resubmission deadline typically means the resubmission opportunity is forfeited — there is no automatic extension, and no further attempt is available on that assignment without exceptional circumstances. If there is any risk of missing the deadline, contact the programme coordinator immediately: do not wait until after the deadline has passed.

Targeted Resubmission Brief: How to Use It Effectively

The targeted brief identifies the specific criteria not met in the original submission. Every preparation and writing effort for the resubmission must focus exclusively on those criteria. The brief is the definitive scope document for the resubmission: work that addresses criteria outside the targeted brief's scope will not improve the grade on those targeted criteria and may indicate to an assessor that the student has not understood the resubmission process.

Before writing the resubmission, obtain specific feedback on why each targeted criterion was not awarded — not just which criteria were not met, but the reason. There are four possible failure modes for each unmet criterion, and the appropriate response differs for each. Where the criterion was not addressed at all, write new content directly targeting that criterion. Where the criterion was addressed but at the wrong cognitive level — analysis written for a description criterion, or description written for an evaluation criterion — rewrite the content at the correct cognitive level. Where the criterion was partially addressed but the evidence was incomplete, complete the evidence. Where the criterion was addressed in the wrong task section, the content may already exist and simply need to be restructured and placed in the correct location.

Write directly to each criterion using the criterion's own command verb as the guide for the cognitive level required. Do not write generally about the topic area and hope that new content happens to satisfy the criterion — map the response explicitly to the criterion's requirements from the first sentence. Annotate the resubmission draft against each targeted criterion code before submitting: if a targeted criterion cannot be annotated against a specific sentence or paragraph, the evidence for it is not clearly present.

A resubmission may be considerably shorter than the original submission. This is appropriate — the scope is limited to the unmet criteria, not the full unit. The assessor is not evaluating the quantity of content; they are checking whether the specific criteria identified in the targeted brief have now been met. Brevity and precision are more effective than additional volume.

Grade Ceiling After Resubmission: What Can and Cannot Be Achieved

Pearson's regulations allow — but do not require — centres to award Merit and Distinction grades on resubmissions. The decision about whether to apply a Pass ceiling on resubmissions or allow the full grade range is a centre-level policy, not a Pearson-mandated rule. This means the grade available on resubmission varies between institutions, and students should never assume the policy without confirming it in writing.

In practice, the majority of BTEC centres apply a Pass ceiling on resubmissions: a Referred unit that achieves Pass on resubmission is graded as a Pass, regardless of whether the resubmission evidence meets Merit or Distinction criteria. This is the most common policy. Some centres — particularly at HNC and HND level — allow the full grade range on resubmission and will award Merit or Distinction if the resubmission evidence meets those criteria. A smaller number apply variable policies by unit type or programme.

Before writing the resubmission, ask the tutor or programme coordinator the following question in writing: "Is the maximum grade available on this resubmission a Pass, or can Merit and Distinction criteria also be awarded?" The answer determines the appropriate scope and depth of the resubmission response. Ask before submitting, not after receiving the resubmission grade.

The strategic implication of a Pass ceiling applies primarily to students whose intended qualification-level grade profile requires specific unit grades for university or professional progression. If a unit resubmission is capped at Pass and the qualification-level profile required for a specific university entry offer needs a Merit or Distinction on that unit, the student must identify whether other units' grades can compensate for the unit-level reduction, or whether the progression target is affected. Progression requirements should be reassessed as soon as the grade ceiling is confirmed — not assumed to be unaffected.

External Assessment Failures: The Resit Route and How to Prepare

External assessment units — those set and marked by Pearson rather than by the centre — have no resubmission route. This is a regulatory and non-negotiable distinction. Students who do not meet the required standard on an external assessment must resit in the next available Pearson assessment window.

Pearson runs two assessment windows per year for most externally assessed BTEC units: January/February and May/June. Exact dates for each window are published on Pearson's website and communicated through the centre's examinations office. Registration for the resit must be completed through the centre's examinations officer — students cannot self-register. Registration deadlines are typically several weeks before the assessment date, and missing the registration deadline means waiting for the following window.

The majority of external assessment failures result from insufficient preparation for the examination format rather than insufficient subject knowledge. Students who approach external assessment resits by reviewing subject content alone without addressing examination technique typically produce very similar results to their first attempt. Priority preparation for an external assessment resit should address: command word recognition — identifying exactly what each question verb (analyse, evaluate, discuss, assess) requires and practising matching response format to command word; timed response conditions — practising under examination conditions without notes, not just answering questions with unlimited time and access to materials; stimulus material engagement — practising analysis of provided data, case studies, or scenarios before answering questions about them; and review of available Pearson mark schemes and examiner feedback reports for past papers, which identify the specific gaps between common student responses and the standard required for each mark band.

Resitting an external assessment with a preparation strategy focused exclusively on content revision — without addressing examination technique, command word responses, and timed writing — is the single most common cause of repeated external assessment failure.

Authentication and Academic Integrity on BTEC Resubmissions

All resubmitted BTEC work requires an authentication declaration: the student signs to confirm that the work is their own, complies with the centre's academic integrity policy, and has not been submitted for a different assignment. This declaration is not a formality — it is a formal academic integrity statement with defined consequences if false.

Legitimate resubmission support includes: understanding what the unmet criteria require at the correct cognitive level; understanding what type of evidence — description, analysis, evaluation — each criterion verb demands; reviewing model answers or worked examples to understand what a creditable response looks like; receiving criterion-by-criterion guidance on where the original submission fell short and what would address those gaps. All of these forms of support develop the student's ability to produce their own work and are consistent with the authentication declaration.

Academic malpractice on resubmission — having work written externally and submitted as the student's own — constitutes the same violation as plagiarism and is arguably more serious on a resubmission, because the student has already received feedback specifying what needed to improve and has chosen to circumvent that process rather than address it. The authentication declaration on resubmission is not a routine administrative step; it is a formal attestation made with full awareness of what the work is expected to represent.

Pearson's internal verification process samples resubmissions. Internal verifiers check that the resubmission brief is targeted (not a full redo), that the submitted evidence addresses only the criteria identified in the targeted brief, and that the authentication is in order. Resubmissions where the authentication appears inconsistent with the standard of work produced, or where the evidence does not align with what a targeted brief should produce, are subject to investigation. The consequences of confirmed malpractice on a resubmission include potential removal of the grade for the entire unit, and in serious cases, removal from the qualification programme.

What is the most important thing to do before starting a BTEC resubmission? Obtain specific written feedback on which criteria were not met and why — not just the criterion codes, but the reason each was not awarded. Without understanding the specific failure mode for each criterion, the resubmission addresses the same content at the same level and produces the same result. The section below covers the academic integrity considerations specific to resubmission support.

BTEC Resubmission and Academic Integrity

The boundary between legitimate resubmission support and academic malpractice is specific and important. Legitimate support is any activity that develops the student's understanding of what the criteria require and how to produce evidence at the correct cognitive level — this directly enables the student to write their own work more effectively. Getting help to understand what "evaluate" means, what distinguishes evaluation from analysis, and what a creditable evaluative response looks like in the subject context is legitimate preparation for resubmission.

Illegitimate support is any arrangement where the submitted resubmission is produced by someone other than the student and presented as the student's own work. This constitutes academic malpractice under the authentication declaration regardless of whether the motivation is time pressure, anxiety, or a genuine desire to achieve a better grade. The distinction is between developing the student's capacity to write and writing on the student's behalf.

How many times can I resubmit a BTEC assignment?

One resubmission is available for internally assessed BTEC units following a Referral. If the resubmission also receives a Referral — failing to meet the targeted criteria a second time — no further attempt is available on that assignment. For externally assessed units, there is no resubmission route; the student must resit in the next available Pearson assessment window.

Can I improve from Pass to Merit or Distinction through resubmission?

No. Resubmission is only available following a Referral — when Pass criteria have not been met. A student who has already achieved a Pass grade on a unit cannot resubmit that unit to achieve a higher grade. Resubmission only applies to the recovery of a Referral result.

What happens if I miss the resubmission deadline?

The resubmission opportunity is typically forfeited. There is no automatic extension, and no further attempt is available on that assignment without exceptional circumstances procedures being invoked (which require documented evidence of the exceptional circumstance). If there is any risk of missing the resubmission deadline, contact the programme coordinator immediately — not after the deadline has passed.

Do I have to resubmit the entire assignment or just the parts that were referred?

Only the criteria that were not met. The targeted brief issued by your tutor specifies exactly which criteria the resubmission must address. Evidence that already met its criteria in the original submission stays in the portfolio and does not need to be resubmitted. Resubmitting already-awarded evidence risks inadvertently altering the quality of evidence that has been credited, without improving the unmet criteria.
