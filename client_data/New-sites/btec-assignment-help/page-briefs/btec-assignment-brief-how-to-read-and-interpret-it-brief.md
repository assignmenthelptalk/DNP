#### H2: The Structure of a BTEC Assignment Brief: What Each Section Contains
> **EAV to cover**: All 5 structural sections — unit header (including internal/external status), learning outcomes, criteria grid, task section, submission requirements
> **Word count**: 340–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Unit header: qualification, unit number/title, credit value, internal or external status — the internal/external status determines whether resubmission is available if referred; many students find this out too late
> - Learning outcomes: broad educational objectives — what the student will know/understand/do by end of unit; contextualise the criteria but are NOT what is marked
> - Criteria grid: the marking instrument — lists every P/M/D criterion with code and exact evidence requirement; this is what the tutor checks; plan against this section
> - Task section: what the student must produce per task, format requirements (report/portfolio/presentation), word count guidance, scenario information
> - Submission requirements: deadline, file format, academic integrity declaration — read before the deadline, not on the day of submission

---

#### H2: Reading the Criteria Grid: The Most Important Part of the Brief
> **EAV to cover**: 4 extraction points (verb, content, relationships, count), linked vs independent criteria, criterion mapping discipline
> **Word count**: 340–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Four things to extract from the criteria grid before writing: verb (command word = cognitive level), content (subject matter scope per criterion), relationships (linked vs independent), count (total P/M/D criteria)
> - Criterion verb: the command word defines cognitive level — identify verb before planning any response; writing analysis to an evaluation criterion earns Merit at best
> - Criterion content: the specific subject matter the criterion assesses — stay within scope; accurate content about adjacent topics does not satisfy a criterion if it is not what the criterion asks for
> - Linked criteria: P1/M1/D1 addressing the same learning outcome at increasing depth — one extended piece of evidence can satisfy all three if written at Distinction level throughout
> - Independent criteria: P1 and D2 may address entirely different learning outcomes — require separate evidence
> - Criterion count: count every criterion before writing; know how many P/M/D criteria exist; check every criterion by code before submitting

#### H2: Task Instructions: What the Tasks Actually Tell You About Evidence Format
> **EAV to cover**: Five task format types, word count guidance interpretation, scenario information in task instructions
> **Word count**: 300–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Written report: formal document with introduction, main body, conclusion — typically required at HNC and HND
> - Case study analysis: applying theory to provided scenario — scenario material must be engaged with specifically, not generically
> - Presentation with supporting notes: notes contain criterion evidence, not the slide deck; the slides are the delivery vehicle
> - Portfolio: collection of evidence from practical tasks — engineering, health and social care, sport; completeness of portfolio entries is the primary marker of quality
> - Research report: structured academic document following a methodology — most common at HND for Managing a Successful Business Project equivalent
> - Word count: indicative guidance, not strict limit; exists to signal expected depth and scope; 600 words for a 2,000-word assignment is almost certainly not meeting all criteria; exceeding word count by a reasonable margin does not attract penalties

#### H2: Submission Requirements: Evidence Format, Plagiarism, and Academic Integrity
> **EAV to cover**: Submission mechanics (deadline, file format), academic integrity declaration, Turnitin similarity, HNC/HND self-plagiarism rule
> **Word count**: 280–330 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Submission requirements: how and where to submit, file format requirements, deadline — missing the deadline may mean late submission is not accepted; incorrect file format may mean submission is not assessable
> - Academic integrity declaration: work is student's own, not submitted for another assignment, all sources acknowledged
> - At HNC/HND: Harvard referencing is part of academic integrity requirements; submitting Level 4/5 work without citations and reference list is not just poor practice but may constitute misconduct where sources have been used without attribution
> - Turnitin: high similarity score does not automatically constitute plagiarism — correctly cited quotations are distinct from copied text; paraphrase rather than quote wherever possible and cite consistently
> - Self-plagiarism: submitting work from a previous assignment as new original work is an integrity violation at HNC/HND; acknowledge prior work and explain how the current submission extends it differently

#### H2: Applying Scenario Information to Meet Merit and Distinction Criteria
> **EAV to cover**: How scenario/stimulus material connects to M/D criteria requirements, why generic theory fails Merit criteria, anchor technique (naming specific scenario elements), common scenario application error
> **Word count**: 300–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - BTEC Assignment Briefs frequently include a scenario or stimulus document — a case study description, company profile, organisational context, or statistical data — that Merit and Distinction responses are expected to engage with specifically
> - Why scenario application matters for Merit and Distinction: examiner marks are allocated for scenario engagement; generic responses that apply theory to an unnamed or hypothetical business fail Merit criteria even when the theory is accurately described and analysed
> - The anchor technique: before writing any Merit or Distinction response, identify the specific elements of the scenario relevant to that criterion — named company, named person, specific percentage from the data, specific policy or product — and write those names into every paragraph of the M/D response
> - Specific example: a Merit criterion "Analyse the factors affecting the marketing strategy of a named business" requires analysis of the specific business described in the brief; analysing the factors affecting business marketing strategy generally — however accurately — does not satisfy the criterion
> - Common error: students write accurate, analytical Merit-level content then add one sentence at the end connecting to the scenario; the scenario application must be integrated throughout the analysis, not appended as an afterthought
> - At Distinction level: scenario specificity requires making the evaluative judgement in the context of the specific organisation, not just applying an evaluation framework generically

#### H2: The Five-Step Assignment Brief Reading Strategy
> **EAV to cover**: Five steps — criteria grid first, highlight verbs, criterion checklist, map criteria to tasks, pre-submission criterion check — with time investment justification
> **Word count**: 300–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - 30–45 minutes before writing begins; return on investment in terms of preventing referrals is significant
> - Step 1: Read criteria grid first — the target; task instructions are the vehicle to reach the target; knowing the target before reading the vehicle changes how you interpret every task instruction
> - Step 2: Highlight the verb in every criterion — mark command verbs; note cognitive level per verb; creates rapid overview of evidence type required at each level before planning any content
> - Step 3: Create criterion checklist — list every criterion by code (P1, P2, M1, D1) with brief note of required evidence; note where in submission each will be addressed; this becomes criterion tracking document
> - Step 4: Map criteria to tasks — identify which task produces evidence for which criteria; where mapping is explicit follow it; where must be inferred, use learning outcomes and task description; prevents criterion evidence appearing in wrong task section
> - Step 5: Pre-submission criterion check — after completing submission but before submitting, return to criterion checklist and verify each; for each criterion: can you point to a specific sentence in the submission that explicitly satisfies it?

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is the most important section of a BTEC Assignment Brief?**
The assessment criteria grid. It is what the tutor marks against. Read it before the task instructions, plan against it, and check against it before submitting.

**Q: What does "internal" and "external" mean on a BTEC Assignment Brief?**
Internal = tutor set and marks, one resubmission if referred. External = Pearson set and marks on a fixed date, no resubmission — resit only.

**Q: Can I ask my tutor to explain what each criterion means before I start writing?**
Yes. Ask specific, targeted questions about criteria you find ambiguous — not "what should I write for Distinction?" but "does this criterion require comparison between two approaches or is a single evaluative analysis sufficient?"

**Q: What is the difference between learning outcomes and assessment criteria in a BTEC Assignment Brief?**
Learning outcomes describe broad educational objectives — what you'll know and do by the end of the unit. Assessment criteria (the P/M/D grid) specify the exact evidence required for each grade level. Learning outcomes provide context; criteria determine the grade.
