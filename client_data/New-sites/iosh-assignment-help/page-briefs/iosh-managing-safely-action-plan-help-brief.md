## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: IOSH Managing Safely project action plan
**Main Attributes**: action plan purpose, SMART objective criteria, prioritisation method, action plan entry components, common failures, scoring weight
**Derived Attributes**:
- **SMART objectives**: Specific (what exactly will be done — names the hazard, the action, the location); Measurable (how you will know the action is complete — a quantifiable or observable outcome); Achievable (realistic within the organisation's resources and authority — not "eliminate all risk" but "install non-slip matting in Zone B"); Relevant (directly addresses a risk identified in the hazard identification and risk assessment sections — not a generic safety improvement); Time-bound (specific completion date — not "soon" or "within a reasonable timeframe" but "by 30 April 2026")
- **Action plan entry components**: Action description (SMART), Person responsible (named role or individual — not "management" or "HSE officer" generically), Resources required (budget, equipment, training cost — what is needed to implement), Target completion date (specific date), Success measure (how you will verify the action is complete and effective — e.g., "risk rating reduced from High to Low following installation, confirmed by re-assessment")
- **Prioritisation from risk assessment**: Actions should address Very High and High risk ratings first. Medium risks follow. Low risks last. This demonstrates that the candidate understands risk prioritisation — a key IOSH concept. Action plan should include at least 3–5 actions drawn from the hazard identification and risk assessment sections.
- **Common failures**: Vague action descriptions ("improve safety," "raise awareness," "train staff" — these are not SMART); No responsible person named (or named as "everyone" or "management"); No specific target date; Actions that do not link back to identified hazards; Action plan that repeats what the control measures section has already stated (action plan is about ADDITIONAL improvements, not restating existing controls); SMART written as an acronym without applying it (stating SMART in the text without the objective actually meeting SMART criteria)
- **Scoring**: Action plan is the final scored section. Assessors look for: clear link to risk assessment findings, correct SMART format, realistic and achievable objectives, evidence of risk prioritisation. Vague objectives score 0 for that entry.

**Values per Attribute**:
- Minimum number of action plan entries: typically 3–5 (check provider guidance — some require more)
- SMART acronym: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound
- Format: table format is standard and expected — columns for Action, Responsible Person, Resources, Target Date, Success Measure
- Risk prioritisation order: Very High → High → Medium → Low
- Common word count for action plan section: 300–500 words (or equivalent table entries)

#### H2: Writing SMART Objectives for Your IOSH Project
> **EAV to cover**: All 5 SMART criteria with IOSH-specific worked examples
> **Word count**: 320–380 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Specific**: Names the hazard, the action, and the location. NOT: "Improve housekeeping." YES: "Remove all oil and fluid spillages from the factory floor in Zone B within 24 hours of occurrence using the existing absorbent granule kit stored in cabinet A3."
> - **Measurable**: Has an observable or quantifiable outcome. NOT: "Reduce slipping accidents." YES: "Zero slip incidents recorded in Zone B for three consecutive months following implementation, as measured by the accident log."
> - **Achievable**: Realistic given the organisation's resources, authority, and budget. Does not require external regulatory change or budget that the candidate's organisation cannot allocate.
> - **Relevant**: Directly addresses a specific risk identified and rated in the risk assessment section. If your action plan addresses a hazard not in your risk assessment — it will fail the relevance check.
> - **Time-bound**: States a specific calendar date. NOT: "As soon as possible." NOT: "Within a reasonable timeframe." YES: "By 30 May 2026."
> - Worked example for slip hazard in food processing area: "Install 5m × 2m anti-slip flooring in the food preparation area by 15 June 2026. Responsible: Facilities Manager. Budget: £800 from maintenance allocation. Success measure: risk rating reduced from High (15) to Low (4) on re-assessment."

#### H2: The Complete Action Plan Entry — What Every Row Must Include
> **EAV to cover**: All required components of an action plan table entry
> **Word count**: 260–310 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Standard action plan table columns: Action (SMART description), Person Responsible, Resources Required, Target Completion Date, Success Measure
> - **Action**: The SMART objective — specific description of what will be done
> - **Person Responsible**: Named role (e.g., "Health and Safety Manager," "Site Supervisor," "Facilities Manager") or named individual. NOT "management" or "everyone" — a single accountable person must be named.
> - **Resources Required**: What is needed to implement the action — materials (e.g., "anti-slip matting, approx. £800"), training (e.g., "manual handling refresher training for 12 operatives — £450"), time (e.g., "2 hours installation time during planned maintenance shutdown")
> - **Target Completion Date**: Specific date in format DD/MM/YYYY or Month Year
> - **Success Measure**: How you will verify the action is complete and working — e.g., "risk rating reduced from High to Medium on post-implementation re-assessment" or "maintenance inspection checklist signed off by site supervisor"
> - Missing any component = incomplete entry = reduced score for that row

#### H2: Prioritising Actions From Your Risk Assessment
> **EAV to cover**: How to sequence actions by risk rating, prioritisation logic, linking action plan to risk ratings
> **Word count**: 230–270 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Actions should appear in descending risk rating order: Very High (20–25) first → High (15–19) → Medium (7–14) → Low (1–6)
> - Assessors expect to see the highest-rated risks addressed with the earliest target dates — if your Very High risk has a target date 12 months away but a Low risk is addressed immediately, this shows poor risk prioritisation understanding
> - Each action plan entry should reference the risk rating from the risk assessment section — e.g., "Action addresses Hazard 2 (Manual Handling — Risk Rating 16 — High)"
> - Not all hazards need to appear in the action plan — focus on the highest-rated risks that require improvement. Hazards already at Low residual risk after control measures may not need an action plan entry.
> - If you have 6+ hazards, select the 4–5 with the highest residual risk ratings for the action plan

#### H2: Common IOSH Action Plan Mistakes That Lose Marks
> **EAV to cover**: Most frequent errors, zero-scoring patterns, what assessors reject
> **Word count**: 250–300 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Vague objectives**: "Improve safety awareness" or "Train all staff" score zero — no Specific action, no Measurable outcome, no Time-bound date

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How many action plan entries does the IOSH Managing Safely project require?**
Most IOSH Managing Safely providers require 3–5 action plan entries. Check your specific provider's project guide — some require a minimum of 4, others give no fixed number but expect the highest-priority risks to be addressed. Each entry must be a separate SMART objective addressing a specific hazard from your risk assessment.

**Q: Can I include PPE as an action plan entry?**
PPE can appear in the action plan only if it represents a new improvement — for example, if your risk assessment identified that no PPE was in use for a specific hazard. If you already listed PPE as a control measure in the control measures section, you cannot repeat it as an action plan entry. The action plan must be for additional improvements beyond existing controls.

**Q: What is the difference between control measures and the action plan?**
Control measures describe what is currently being done or what you are recommending to reduce risk during the assessment. The action plan describes additional improvement actions — new steps to be taken after the project is submitted, with a responsible person, resources, date, and success measure. The two sections must not repeat each other.

**Q: What happens if my SMART objective is not measurable?**
An action plan entry that cannot be measured fails the Measurable criterion of SMART and will score reduced or zero marks for that entry. Assessors expect to see how you will verify the action is complete — not just that something will be "improved." Replace vague outcomes like "improve safety" with quantifiable ones: "risk rating reduced from High to Low on re-assessment" or "zero incidents recorded in this area in the 3 months following implementation."
