#### H2: How to Write a Hazard Description That Scores Marks
> **EAV to cover**: Hazard description (entity) → specificity components (attribute) → hazard agent, location/task, mechanism, exposure group, existing control failure (values)
> **Word count**: 300–380 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Present the before/after comparison explicitly:
>   - WEAK (will not score): "Electrical hazard in the workplace"
>   - STRONG (will score): "Exposed 240V single-phase wiring on south wall of maintenance Room 12, accessed daily by non-electrical maintenance staff during equipment checks — no lockout/tagout isolation procedure is in place"
> - Explain why the strong version is better: it identifies the hazard agent (240V wiring), the location (Room 12), the exposure mechanism (daily access), the people at risk (non-electrical staff), and the existing control failure (no lockout procedure)
> - A good description answers five questions: What is the hazard? Where does it occur? During which task or activity? Who is exposed? What existing control is absent or failing?
> - This level of specificity is what allows the risk matrix rating to be justified — "likelihood: 3 (Possible)" requires a plausible exposure scenario, not a generic label
> - Second before/after example: WEAK "Manual handling hazard" vs STRONG "Warehouse operatives manually lifting incoming stock pallets weighing 30–40kg to shelf height at 1.8m — no mechanical handling aid provided and no team lift procedure in place"

#### H2: Common Hazard Identification Mistakes That Cost Marks
> **EAV to cover**: Hazard identification failures (entity) → description vagueness, insufficient variety, fabricated hazards, over-similar hazards, missing categorisation (attributes) → specific marking consequences per failure type (values)
> **Word count**: 280–350 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Vague descriptions**: "poor housekeeping" is not a hazard — "floor contaminated with hydraulic oil in the area between Machinery Bay 3 and the emergency exit, creating a slip hazard for all production staff during shift changes" is a hazard. Vague descriptions cannot be rated on the risk matrix.
> - **Insufficient number**: submitting only 4 hazards when a provider awards marks for 6–8 results in lost marks in this section and in the risk assessment section that follows
> - **All hazards from one category**: selecting 6 physical hazards only demonstrates a one-dimensional understanding of workplace risk — marks are awarded for range
> - **Fabricated or industry-inappropriate hazards**: selecting hazards that do not plausibly exist in the candidate's stated workplace undermines credibility with the assessor
> - **Using generic labels as descriptions**: "chemical hazard," "fire hazard," "biological hazard" — these are categories, not descriptions
> - **Failing to link hazards to legislation**: where legislation is applicable (e.g., COSHH for chemical hazards, WAHR for working at height), naming it in the hazard identification section demonstrates legal competence

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How many hazards do I need to identify in the IOSH Managing Safely project?**
Most IOSH Managing Safely providers require a minimum of 4 hazards for a pass. Identifying 6 to 8 hazards from varied categories (Physical, Chemical, Biological, Ergonomic, Psychosocial) is recommended to achieve higher mark bands, as more hazards give the risk assessment and control measures sections more substantive content to assess.

**Q: Can I use hazards from a different workplace if mine does not have many hazards?**
Hazards must be genuine to your own workplace — assessors are experienced health and safety professionals who verify that identified hazards are plausible for your industry and role. Fabricating or borrowing hazards from an unrelated environment risks project failure. If your workplace has limited hazards, broaden your scope to include all five categories and examine tasks, not just fixed locations.

**Q: What is the difference between a hazard and a risk in the IOSH project?**
A hazard is the source of potential harm — for example, exposed machinery guarding. A risk is the likelihood and severity of harm occurring from that hazard given current controls. The IOSH project requires both: hazard identification in Section 1, risk quantification (using the Likelihood × Severity matrix) in Section 2. Conflating the two terms in the project report loses marks.

**Q: Does each hazard need to reference a specific piece of legislation?**
Referencing applicable legislation in the hazard identification section demonstrates legal awareness and is rewarded in most IOSH Managing Safely marking schemes. Each hazard category has specific regulations: Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992 for manual handling hazards, COSHH Regulations 2002 for chemical hazards, Display Screen Equipment Regulations 1992 for ergonomic DSE hazards, and Working at Height Regulations 2005 for height-related physical hazards.
