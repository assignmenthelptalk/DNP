#### H2: Meaning Analysis in CELTA Assignment 2: Denotation, Connotation, and Appropriacy
> **EAV to cover**: Meaning attribute × denotation, connotation, appropriacy, semantic prosody values
> **Word count**: 300–370 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Denotation: the core, referential meaning of the item — what it refers to in the world. For "give up": the act of abandoning or ceasing an activity. State denotation in simple, precise terms that could be used as a dictionary definition — avoid synonyms that themselves require explanation.
> - Connotation: associated meaning beyond the literal — the attitudinal and cultural associations carried by the item. "Give up" carries a negative connotation (failure, defeat, lack of persistence) in most contexts. However, connotation can shift with collocates and context: "give up smoking" has a positive connotation. Candidates must note the dominant connotation AND acknowledge context-dependency where relevant.
> - Appropriacy: who uses this item, in what situations, and whether it is suitable for formal writing, informal speech, or both. "Give up" is informal to neutral — appropriate in speech and informal writing, less typical in formal academic writing where "abandon" or "cease" would be preferred.
> - Semantic prosody: the consistent attitudinal colouring of a word across its typical collocates in corpus data. Example: "cause" consistently collocates with negative outcomes (cause problems, cause damage, cause concern) — its semantic prosody is negative, even though the word itself is neutral. Candidates should note semantic prosody where it affects teaching (learners may use "cause" with positive collocates inappropriately).
> - For grammar items: meaning analysis focuses on the temporal/aspectual meaning of the structure. For "used to + V": meaning = past habitual action or state that is no longer true in the present. Must distinguish from "would + V" (past habit but NOT past states) and "be used to + gerund" (accustomed to, present meaning).

#### H2: Phonology Analysis in CELTA Assignment 2: Word Stress, Connected Speech, and IPA Transcription
> **EAV to cover**: Phonology attribute × word stress (primary/secondary), connected speech (elision/assimilation/linking/weak forms), IPA transcription values
> **Word count**: 380–460 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Word stress: mark primary stress with ˈ before the stressed syllable. For multi-syllable words, identify secondary stress (ˌ). Example: "education" = /ˌedjʊˈkeɪʃən/ — secondary stress on first syllable, primary stress on third. State which syllable carries the stress and why it matters for learner production.
> - Connected speech — elision: sounds omitted in fast, natural speech. Examples: "want to" → /wɒnə/; "going to" → /ˈɡəʊnə/; "used to" → /ˈjuːstə/; word-final /t/ or /d/ is often elided before a consonant: "next day" → /neks deɪ/. Candidates must identify which sounds in their chosen language item are subject to elision in connected speech.
> - Connected speech — assimilation: a sound changes to become more like an adjacent sound. Example: "ten boys" → /tem bɔɪz/ (alveolar /n/ assimilates to bilabial /m/ before bilabial /b/); "in pain" → /ɪm peɪn/. Place and manner of articulation of adjacent consonants determines the direction of assimilation.
> - Connected speech — linking: consonant-to-vowel linking in connected speech. Example: "far away" → /fɑːrəweɪ/ (intrusive /r/); "turn it off" → /tɜːnɪtɒf/ (consonant links to following vowel-initial word). Linking prevents the glottal stop pattern common in learners.
> - Weak forms: function words (auxiliaries, prepositions, articles, pronouns) are typically reduced in connected speech. "Have" in present perfect → /həv/ or /əv/: "I should have known" → /aɪ ʃʊdəv nəʊn/. Candidates must identify weak forms for any auxiliary verbs in their grammar item.
> - Full IPA transcription: provide an IPA transcription of the language item in a typical sentence in connected speech — not just the isolated item. Example for "used to": "I used to go there every day" → /aɪ ˈjuːstə ɡəʊ ðər ˈevri deɪ/
> - Common errors: providing only a phonemic transcription of the isolated word without connected speech context; marking only primary stress without secondary stress in multi-syllable items; failing to identify elision or assimilation patterns; omitting the phonology section entirely

#### H2: How to Write Concept Checking Questions (CCQs) for CELTA Assignment 2
> **EAV to cover**: CCQ entity × CCQ design rules, question format, sequencing, example CCQs for grammar and lexical items
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Definition: CCQs (Concept Checking Questions) are simple questions a teacher asks to check whether learners understand the MEANING of a language item — without using the target language in the question itself.
> - Rule 1 — No target language: the CCQ must not include the item being checked. For "used to + V": asking "Did you USE TO do this?" is invalid — the target language appears in the question. Correct form: "Do you still do this?" / "Did you do this regularly in the past?" / "Is it finished now?"
> - Rule 2 — Binary or short-answer format: CCQs should be answerable with "yes/no" or a short factual answer (a number, a time reference, a name). Avoid open-ended questions that do not verify comprehension.
> - Rule 3 — Check meaning, not use or form: CCQs verify semantic comprehension, not grammatical knowledge. A CCQ is not a grammar transformation task.
> - Rule 4 — 3–4 CCQs per item, sequenced logically: begin with broader comprehension questions and narrow to the specific semantic feature being checked. For "used to + V" checking the "no longer true in the present" feature: Q1: "Did I do this in the past?" Yes. Q2: "Do I still do it now?" No. Q3: "Did I do it once or regularly?" Regularly. Q4: "Is it a habit I have now?" No.
> - For lexical items: CCQs check denotation and connotation. For "give up": Q1: "Did this person succeed?" No. Q2: "Did they stop before they finished?" Yes. Q3: "Did they want to stop?" (checks connotation — the answer reveals whether connotation of defeat is understood).
> - Common CCQ errors in Assignment 2: using the target language in the question; writing questions with multiple correct interpretations; asking questions about form ("What tense is this?") rather than meaning; writing only one or two CCQs.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is the FMU+P framework in CELTA Assignment 2?**
Form, Meaning, Use, and Phonology (FMU+P) is the analytical framework required for CELTA Assignment 2. Form covers grammatical structure in affirmative, negative, and interrogative sentences — using notation and naming auxiliary verbs and morphological features. Meaning covers denotation (core meaning), connotation (associated meaning), and appropriacy (suitable contexts of use). Use covers register, frequency, typical collocations, and when speakers choose this form over alternatives. Phonology covers primary and secondary word stress marked in IPA, connected speech features (elision, assimilation, linking, weak forms), and a full IPA transcription of the item in connected speech context.

**Q: How do I write CCQs for CELTA Assignment 2?**
Concept Checking Questions (CCQs) must follow four rules: (1) never use the target language in the question itself; (2) format questions as binary (yes/no) or short-answer only; (3) check meaning — not form or use; (4) write 3–4 questions sequenced from general to specific. For "used to + infinitive," correct CCQs include: "Do you still do this?" (No), "Did you do this regularly in the past?" (Yes), "Is it finished now?" (Yes). Asking "Did you USE TO do this?" violates Rule 1 because the target language appears in the question.

**Q: Why do most CELTA Assignment 2 referrals happen?**
The most common causes of Assignment 2 referral are: (1) omitting the phonology section or providing only a partial phonology analysis (e.g., stating which syllable is stressed without providing IPA transcription or connected speech features); (2) writing CCQs that include the target language in the question; (3) failing to provide all three structural forms — affirmative, negative, AND interrogative — in the Form section; (4) not distinguishing the chosen grammar structure from a commonly confused alternative in the Meaning or Use sections.

**Q: What does Pass B standard look like for CELTA Assignment 2?**
Pass B Assignment 2 demonstrates complete FMU+P analysis with no omissions. Phonology includes full IPA transcription, identified connected speech features (elision, assimilation, or linking specific to the item), and at least two anticipated phonological problems with solutions. CCQs follow all four construction rules with 3–4 questions per item. The Meaning section distinguishes the item from commonly confused alternatives. The anticipated problems section addresses form, meaning, AND phonology problems with specific, actionable solutions for each.
