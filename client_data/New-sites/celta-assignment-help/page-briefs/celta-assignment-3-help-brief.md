## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: CELTA Assignment 3 — Skills-Related Task
**Main Attributes**: assignment purpose, receptive skills analysis, productive skills analysis, lesson staging methodology, sub-skills targeting, Pass B criteria
**Derived Attributes**:
- **Assignment purpose**: Candidates demonstrate ability to select and analyse a skills-based text or task and design a lesson that develops a specific skill. Shows Cambridge that the candidate understands how to teach receptive skills (reading, listening) and productive skills (speaking, writing) through principled lesson staging.
- **Receptive skills — reading sub-skills**: Skimming (reading quickly for gist/overall meaning — first pass), Scanning (reading quickly for specific information — e.g., finding a date or name), Reading for detail (close reading — understanding precise meaning of specific sections), Reading for inference (understanding implied meaning — what is not stated explicitly)
- **Receptive skills — listening sub-skills**: Listening for gist (general understanding — what is the conversation about?), Listening for specific information (identifying specific details — numbers, names, locations), Listening for detail (understanding precisely what was said), Listening for attitude/opinion (understanding the speaker's viewpoint or emotional stance)
- **Processing models for reading/listening**: Top-down processing (using background knowledge, context, and prediction to aid comprehension — schema activation); Bottom-up processing (decoding individual words and sounds before constructing meaning — important for lower-level learners); Interactive processing (combination of both — most skilled readers/listeners use both simultaneously)
- **Pre-task stage**: Lead-in (activate schemata — background knowledge about the topic), vocabulary pre-teaching (select vocabulary that would block comprehension if unknown — not all new words, only blocking words), prediction tasks (using title, images, or first line to predict content — engages top-down processing)
- **While-task stage**: Gist task first (one or two broad comprehension questions — global understanding), then detail/specific information task (more focused questions — confirms engagement with text content). Two stages are standard at CELTA level.
- **Post-task stage**: Production activity — speaking or writing task that uses the text's theme, topic, or language as a springboard. Must be communicative — not another comprehension task. Example: after a reading about travel, a speaking task where learners plan a trip using information from the text.
- **Productive skills (speaking)**: Accuracy vs fluency — accuracy activities focus on correct use of language (controlled practice); fluency activities focus on communicating without stopping to correct (freer practice). CELTA favours fluency-focused post-tasks.
- **Productive skills (writing)**: Genre — what type of text? (email, report, narrative, description). Process writing (stages: planning, drafting, revising, editing) vs product writing (focus on the end product quality). At CELTA level, process writing is more commonly referenced.
- **Schemata**: Bartlett (1932) — prior knowledge structures that help us interpret new information. Schema activation is the theoretical basis for lead-in activities.

**Values per Attribute**:
- Number of while-reading/listening tasks: minimum 2 (gist + detail) at CELTA level
- Pre-task vocabulary selection principle: only pre-teach words that would block comprehension if unknown — not all new vocabulary
- Post-task production: must be communicative — not a grammar exercise or another comprehension check

#### H2: Reading Sub-Skills — What Your Text Develops
> **EAV to cover**: Skimming, scanning, reading for detail, reading for inference — definitions with text-level examples
> **Word count**: 280–330 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Skimming**: Reading quickly to get the gist — overall topic and main idea. Readers skip detail and focus on topic sentences, headings, first and last sentences of paragraphs. While-task: a single broad question answered after 90 seconds of rapid reading (e.g., "What is this article mainly about?")
> - **Scanning**: Reading quickly to locate specific information — a name, a date, a statistic. Eyes move over text without reading every word. While-task: "Find the year mentioned in paragraph 2" or "Which country does the writer recommend?"
> - **Reading for detail**: Close reading — understanding precisely what is written in a specific section. Requires engagement with every word. While-task: true/false/not given questions about specific factual claims in the text.
> - **Reading for inference**: Understanding implied meaning — what is NOT explicitly stated. Requires interpreting tone, connotation, and between-the-lines meaning. While-task: "How does the writer feel about X? What words suggest this?"
> - In Assignment 3, candidates must identify which sub-skill their while-task targets — and justify why this sub-skill is appropriate for the level and purpose of the lesson.

#### H2: Top-Down and Bottom-Up Processing — Why Pre-Tasks Exist
> **EAV to cover**: Top-down processing, bottom-up processing, schema theory (Bartlett 1932), lead-in rationale
> **Word count**: 260–300 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Top-down processing**: Readers/listeners use prior knowledge (schemata), context, predictions, and topic familiarity to understand text before decoding individual words. Competent readers process texts primarily top-down.
> - **Bottom-up processing**: Decoding text word by word, sound by sound — building meaning from linguistic units upward. Used more heavily by lower-level learners or when encountering unfamiliar vocabulary.
> - **Schema theory** (Bartlett 1932): Prior knowledge structures (schemata) activate when we encounter a topic — they create expectations that speed comprehension. This is the theoretical basis for lead-in activities: activating schemata before reading/listening reduces cognitive load and improves comprehension.
> - **In Assignment 3**: Candidates must explain WHY their lead-in activates schemata and facilitates top-down processing — not just describe that they "showed pictures to introduce the topic." The rationale must be theory-backed.
> - **Interactive processing**: Most skilled readers use both simultaneously — CELTA candidates should acknowledge this rather than presenting top-down and bottom-up as binary opposites.

#### H2: Designing the Pre-Task Stage
> **EAV to cover**: Lead-in activity, vocabulary pre-teaching (selection principle), prediction task, activating schemata
> **Word count**: 280–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Lead-in**: Activity that activates schemata about the topic before learners encounter the text. Examples: discussing related personal experience, looking at images connected to the topic, watching a short video clip (30 seconds), brainstorming what they know. Must be communicative (generates learner output) not passive (teacher lectures about the topic).
> - **Vocabulary pre-teaching**: Only pre-teach words that would BLOCK comprehension if unknown — not all new vocabulary. The selection principle: if a learner could understand the gist of the text without knowing this word, do not pre-teach it (context will aid understanding). If not knowing this word would prevent gist comprehension, pre-teach it. In Assignment 3, candidates must name which specific words they would pre-teach and justify why those words are blocking vocabulary.
> - **Prediction task**: Using the title, an image, or the first line to predict content. Engages top-down processing before reading. Example: "Look at the title and the photo — what do you think happens in this story? Discuss with your partner."
> - Do NOT pre-teach all new vocabulary — this removes the authentic reading/listening challenge and reduces motivation. This is a common CELTA candidate error.

---

#### H2: Designing the While-Task Stage
> **EAV to cover**: Gist task first (why), detail task second (why), task design principles, number of while-tasks
> **Word count**: 250–290 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Two while-tasks are standard at CELTA level: gist task (global) → detail task (specific)
> - **Gist task first**: Sets a single, achievable question before the first read/listen. Guides attention, reduces anxiety. Learners read/listen once quickly. Gist question must be answerable after a single rapid read — if it requires detailed comprehension, it is NOT a gist question.
> - **Detail/specific information task second**: After learners have gist comprehension, a more focused task — true/false, multiple choice, specific information retrieval. Learners read/listen again (second exposure). More questions, more specific.
> - Why two readings/listenings? Multiple exposures build deeper comprehension and model real-world reading/listening behaviour (we re-read/re-listen when we need more information).
> - In Assignment 3: candidates must name the specific sub-skill their while-tasks target (e.g., "the gist task targets skimming; the detail task targets reading for specific information") and justify the sequence.

---

#### H2: Designing the Post-Task Stage
> **EAV to cover**: Communicative production task, connection to text theme, fluency vs accuracy, why comprehension checks are not post-tasks
> **Word count**: 230–270 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Post-task: a speaking or writing activity that uses the text's theme, topic, or context as a springboard for communicative production. Must require learners to produce language — not answer more comprehension questions.
> - **Communicative**: Learners have a real communicative purpose — sharing an opinion, making a recommendation, telling a story, debating a topic connected to the text theme.
> - **Fluency focus**: Post-tasks at CELTA level favour fluency (communicating a message without excessive self-correction) over accuracy (producing error-free language). Feedback on errors comes after the task, not during.
> - Examples: after a reading about social media, a speaking task — "Discuss with a partner: what are the benefits and risks of social media for teenagers?"; after a listening about travel experiences, a writing task — "Write a 100-word description of a memorable journey."
> - What a post-task is NOT: another comprehension check, a vocabulary matching exercise, a grammar drill unrelated to the text theme.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Which text should I choose for CELTA Assignment 3?**
Authentic texts work best — a newspaper article, a podcast excerpt, a short video clip, a magazine feature. The text must be appropriate for your learners' level (matching or slightly above their current CEFR level). Avoid texts that are too long (more than 400–500 words for a reading at B1), too culturally specific, or that contain language requiring extensive pre-teaching.

**Q: Do I need to write a full lesson plan for Assignment 3?**
Assignment 3 requires a lesson outline or staging plan — not a full detailed lesson plan. You must show the pre/while/post stages with timing, materials, and rationale for each stage. The rationale — explaining WHY each stage exists using methodology terminology (schema activation, gist comprehension, top-down processing) — is more important than detailed procedural steps.

**Q: How many while-tasks does Assignment 3 need?**
Two while-tasks are the standard at CELTA level: a gist task (global comprehension — one broad question) followed by a detail or specific information task (more targeted questions). A single while-task is generally insufficient. More than three while-tasks risks making the lesson feel like a comprehension exercise rather than a communicative skills lesson.

**Q: What is the most common mistake in CELTA Assignment 3?**
Pre-teaching too much vocabulary before the text is the most common error — candidates often pre-teach every new word rather than selecting only blocking vocabulary. This removes the authentic reading/listening challenge, reduces learner engagement, and demonstrates a misunderstanding of the purpose of the pre-task stage. The second most common error is designing a post-task that is another comprehension check rather than genuine communicative production.
