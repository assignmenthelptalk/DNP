#### H2: How to Profile a Learner for CELTA Assignment 1
> **EAV to cover**: Learner profiling attribute × L1, educational context, learning aims, CEFR level assessment, learning preferences
> **Word count**: 300–370 words
> **Specific content**:
> - The learner profile is built from a one-to-one interview — candidates should prepare 8–12 questions covering: L1 and any other languages spoken; country of origin and English learning history; current use of English (work, study, travel); learning aims and timeframe; previous English language learning experience (self-taught, school, private tuition); perceived strengths and weaknesses in English

> - Note: candidates must use a real learner from their TP class — invented or composite learner profiles are not permitted and are detectable in external moderation.

#### H2: Error Analysis for CELTA Assignment 1: Identifying and Categorising Learner Errors
> **EAV to cover**: Error analysis attribute × identification methodology, error categories (grammar/vocabulary/pronunciation), classification (L1 interference vs developmental)
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Error identification: collect real examples from interaction with the learner — jot down exact utterances during conversation. Alternatively, collect a short writing sample (email, paragraph) from the learner. The assignment requires real data — not invented examples.
> - Error categorisation: classify each error into one of three categories: (1) Grammar errors — tense errors (wrong tense used), aspect errors (simple vs continuous confusion), agreement errors (subject-verb agreement: "*She go to school"), article errors (omission or wrong use of "a/the"), copula errors, word order errors; (2) Vocabulary errors — wrong word choice (selecting a word with similar but incorrect meaning), collocation errors (choosing a non-native collocate: "*do a mistake" instead of "make a mistake"), false cognate use; (3) Pronunciation errors — phoneme substitution (replacing an English phoneme absent in L1 with the nearest L1 equivalent), word stress errors (placing stress on the wrong syllable), intonation pattern transfer from L1
> - Error classification — L1 interference vs developmental: a developmental error is one that appears across all learner groups regardless of L1 (e.g., overgeneralisation of past tense -ed: "*goed," "*buyed") — it reflects a stage of acquisition, not L1 transfer. An L1 interference error is specific to the learner's first language: an Arabic speaker omitting the copula "be" is showing L1 interference because Arabic has no present tense copula. Candidates must classify each error correctly — simply labelling everything as "L1 interference" is a marker of below-standard analysis.
> - For Pass B, each error must be: (a) stated as an exact observed error (using asterisk notation for errors: "*She teacher"); (b) categorised precisely (copula omission — grammar); (c) classified correctly (L1 interference for Arabic L1); (d) explained with reference to the L1 grammatical system (Arabic present equative sentences have no copula verb).

#### H2: Applying L1 Interference Theory in CELTA Assignment 1
> **EAV to cover**: L1 interference attribute × Spanish, Arabic, Mandarin, French examples with specific grammatical explanations
> **Word count**: 380–460 words
> **Specific content**:
> - L1 interference (also called negative transfer) occurs when the grammatical rules or patterns of a learner's first language are transferred into English production, producing errors that a native speaker would not make developmentally.
> - Spanish L1 interference: (1) Article omission with abstract nouns — Spanish uses the definite article for generic reference with abstract nouns ("La vida es bella" = "Life is beautiful") but English does not use the article in the same generic way — Spanish speakers sometimes omit the article in English or insert it inappropriately; (2) Question word order — Spanish allows declarative word order with rising intonation in questions ("¿Tú hablas inglés?") — learners transfer this pattern as "*You speak English?" without the inversion; (3) Ser/estar conflation — both translate to "be" in English but have distinct functions in Spanish (ser for permanent states, estar for temporary states) — learners may produce unnatural overuse or avoidance of certain "be" constructions
> - Arabic L1 interference: (1) Copula omission — Arabic does not require a copula verb in present tense equative sentences — "Ana mudarris" (literally "I teacher") transfers directly as "*I teacher" in English; (2) /p/ vs /b/ substitution — Arabic has no /p/ phoneme — Arabic speakers substitute /b/ for /p/ consistently: "paper" → "*baber"; (3) Definite article handling — Arabic uses the prefixed definite article "al-" (assimilating to the following consonant) — learners may transfer article behaviour inconsistently to English
> - Mandarin L1 interference: (1) Past tense -ed omission — Mandarin uses no tense morphology; temporal meaning is conveyed by adverbs and context — the English past tense marker -ed is regularly omitted: "*Yesterday I go to the market"; (2) Article omission — Mandarin has no equivalent to "a" or "the" — both articles are frequently omitted or used randomly; (3) Classifier transfer — Mandarin uses different counting classifiers for different noun categories (e.g., 一本書 — "one flat-thing book" for flat-spined books) — learners may produce overly specified or misapplied partitive expressions in English
> - French L1 interference: (1) False cognates — "sensible" in French means sensitive, not sensible (reasonable); "actuel" means current, not actual; "éventuel" means possible, not eventual; (2) Gender agreement — French nouns are gendered and adjectives agree — no direct transfer to English grammar, but learners may hypercorrect or confuse adjective position; (3) Pronoun formality — French tu/vous distinction may produce over-formalisation in English address forms

#### H2: Recommending Materials in CELTA Assignment 1: What Counts as a Good Recommendation
> **EAV to cover**: Material recommendation attribute × published materials, authentic materials, justification criteria, named resources, level alignment
> **Word count**: 280–340 words
> **Specific content**:
> - The material recommendation must be: (1) appropriate for the learner's identified CEFR level; (2) directly linked to one of the specific language errors identified in the error analysis — the recommendation must solve a specific problem, not be a general resource; (3) a named, published material with author and publisher, or a named authentic resource (e.g., a specific BBC Learning English series, a named podcast)
> - Named published EFL materials: "English Grammar in Use" (Raymond Murphy, Cambridge University Press) — grammar reference and practice; specify edition and unit: 4th edition Unit 86 for copula "be." "Headway" series (Soars, Oxford) — integrated skills coursebook, specify level (Elementary, Pre-Intermediate, etc.). "English Vocabulary in Use" (McCarthy & O'Dell, Cambridge) — vocabulary reference; "New English File" (Oxenden, Oxford) — communicative approach. "Streamline English" (Hartley & Viney, Oxford) — structured conversation practice.
> - Graded readers: Cambridge English Readers, Penguin Readers, Oxford Bookworms — specify title, level (Starter to Level 6), and why the authentic-but-graded text addresses the learner's needs (e.g., reading practice for B1 learner who needs exposure to narrative past tense structures)
> - Justification: the recommendation must explain WHY this resource addresses the identified error. "I recommend English Grammar in Use because the learner needs grammar practice" is not sufficient. "I recommend English Grammar in Use 4th edition Units 86–87 (am/is/are; I am doing/I do) because the learner's copula omission error (L1 Arabic interference) requires explicit form presentation followed by controlled production practice, which these units provide in a structured sequence" is Pass B standard.

#### H2: L1 Interference vs Developmental Errors — Why the Distinction Matters
> **Content**: Deeper treatment of the L1 interference vs developmental error distinction for candidates who are unsure how to classify their learner's errors. Developmental errors appear in learners of all L1 backgrounds at similar stages of acquisition (overgeneralisation: "*goed," "*mouses"; learning strategies: "*more good" for "better"). L1 interference errors are statistically more frequent in learners of specific L1s. The distinction matters for Assignment 1 because: (1) incorrectly classifying a developmental error as L1 interference reduces the analytical credibility of the assignment; (2) correctly identifying L1 interference demonstrates that the candidate understands the learner as an individual with a specific linguistic background — not a generic "English learner."

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is the Focus on the Learner in CELTA Assignment 1?**
Focus on the Learner is the official Cambridge title for CELTA Assignment 1. The assignment requires candidates to interview a learner from their TP class, build a learner profile (L1, educational background, CEFR level, learning aims), analyse specific language errors collected from real interaction with that learner, explain those errors using L1 interference theory, and recommend a named published material justified against the identified language needs. The assignment tests the candidate's ability to identify individual learner needs and respond with appropriate teaching resources.

**Q: What L1 interference examples should I include in CELTA Assignment 1?**
L1 interference examples must be specific to the learner's first language grammatical system. For Arabic L1 learners, copula omission in present tense equative sentences is a predictable L1 interference pattern — Arabic has no present tense copula verb. For Mandarin L1 learners, past tense -ed omission reflects Mandarin's aspect-based (not tense-based) grammatical system. For Spanish L1 learners, question word order errors reflect Spanish's allowance of declarative word order with rising intonation. The linguistic explanation must name the specific L1 grammatical rule that produces the English error — not a general observation about the learner's country or culture.

**Q: How many errors do I need to analyse in CELTA Assignment 1?**
A minimum of 3–4 specific errors is standard for Assignment 1. Pass B requires that each error is presented as an exact observed utterance (marked with an asterisk), categorised precisely (e.g., copula omission — grammar), classified correctly (L1 interference or developmental), and explained with specific reference to the L1 grammatical system. Providing 6–8 errors with shallow analysis is weaker than providing 3–4 errors with full L1 linguistic explanation and precise categorisation.

**Q: What materials can I recommend in CELTA Assignment 1?**
Named published EFL materials are required — general categories do not constitute a recommendation. Acceptable recommendations include: "English Grammar in Use" (Murphy, Cambridge, 4th edition — specifying which unit addresses the identified error), "English Vocabulary in Use" (McCarthy & O'Dell, Cambridge), "Headway" or "New English File" series (specifying level), graded readers from Cambridge English Readers or Oxford Bookworms (specifying title and level). Each recommendation must be explicitly justified against the specific error identified — not offered as a general improvement resource.
