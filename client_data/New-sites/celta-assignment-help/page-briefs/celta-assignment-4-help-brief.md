## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: CELTA Assignment 4 — Lessons from the Classroom
**Main Attributes**: assignment purpose, critical reflection vs description, reflective models, pattern identification, TP incident selection, Pass B criteria, connection to teaching practice
**Derived Attributes**:
- **Assignment purpose**: Candidates demonstrate the ability to reflect critically on their own teaching practice (TP), identify patterns in their development, analyse root causes, and propose forward-looking action. Shows Cambridge that the candidate is a developing reflective practitioner — a fundamental EFL teacher competency.
- **Critical reflection vs description**: The most common failure — candidates narrate what happened in a lesson (description) rather than analysing WHY it happened and what it reveals about their teaching (critical reflection). Description: "I introduced the grammar using the board and then students practised in pairs." Critical reflection: "My grammar presentation stage used only a written deductive approach which may have disadvantaged kinaesthetic learners — I could have included a discovery activity. Scrivener (2011) argues that inductive approaches generate deeper processing of form."
- **Schon (1983) — Reflection-in-Action and Reflection-on-Action**: Reflection-in-Action = adjusting mid-lesson in response to learner feedback (noticing students are confused and changing approach on the spot). Reflection-on-Action = critical analysis after the lesson, identifying what worked, what didn't, and why. Assignment 4 is primarily Reflection-on-Action — but Pass B candidates demonstrate awareness of Reflection-in-Action moments during TP.
- **Kolb (1984) — Experiential Learning Cycle**: Concrete Experience (a specific TP incident) → Reflective Observation (what happened, what I noticed) → Abstract Conceptualisation (what theory or principle explains this) → Active Experimentation (what I will try next time — the action commitment). Assignment 4 should move through all four stages for each incident.
- **Pattern identification**: Pass B requires candidates to identify PATTERNS across multiple TP lessons — not just report individual incidents. Pattern example: "Across four TP lessons, I noticed that my instruction-giving was consistently unclear — learners frequently asked for clarification before tasks. This pattern suggests I am not checking concept before setting tasks." A pattern is not: "In Lesson 3, my instructions were unclear."
- **TP incident selection**: Candidates should choose incidents that are rich in learning — not incidents where everything went well, and not incidents so catastrophic they cannot be analysed objectively. Best candidates: moments of uncertainty, unexpected learner responses, decisions made under pressure, or recurring difficulties.
- **Theory integration**: EFL theory must be integrated into the analysis stage — not just named. Krashen's Input Hypothesis (1982) — comprehensible input (i+1); Vygotsky's ZPD (1978) — task difficulty pitched appropriately; Harmer's ESA framework — how the lesson was or wasn't structured; Scrivener (2011) — classroom management and learner-centredness; Thornbury — vocabulary and grammar teaching principles.

**Values per Attribute**:
- Minimum TP incidents: 2–3 (Pass B candidates typically analyse 3 with pattern linking)
- Recommended structure: Introduction → Incident 1 analysis → Incident 2 analysis → Incident 3 analysis → Pattern analysis across incidents → Forward action commitments
- Pass B criteria: patterns identified across TP lessons (not individual incidents), theory named and applied (not just referenced), forward action specific and realistic
- Word count: typically 1,500–2,500 words depending on CELTA centre

**Attributes on THIS page**: Assignment 4 purpose and requirements, description vs critical reflection distinction, Schon reflection-in-action/on-action, Kolb's ELC applied to TP, pattern identification methodology, theory integration, Pass B criteria
**Attribute depth warning**: Do not reduce Assignment 4 to "use Gibbs' cycle." CELTA specifically centres on Schon and Kolb for TP reflection — Gibbs is more common in nursing and care qualifications. Writers should name Schon (1983) and Kolb (1984) with specific TP applications, not import the Gibbs framework from other qualifications.

#### H2: Schon's Reflection-in-Action and Reflection-on-Action in CELTA TP
> **EAV to cover**: Both reflection types (1983) with specific CELTA TP applications for each
> **Word count**: 270–310 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Reflection-in-Action** (Schon 1983): Thinking on your feet during the lesson — adjusting approach in real time in response to learner confusion or unexpected responses. Example: "During a listening task, I noticed learners had misheard a key word — I paused the audio and clarified before continuing rather than pressing on and losing the class." In Assignment 4, documenting Reflection-in-Action moments demonstrates awareness of responsive teaching.
> - **Reflection-on-Action** (Schon 1983): Thinking about the lesson after it has happened — analysing what occurred and why. This is the primary mode of Assignment 4. Example: "On reflection, my instruction-giving in Lesson 2 was unclear — learners began the wrong task. Reviewing my lesson plan, I notice I had not included a concept check before setting the task."
> - Pass B assignment shows BOTH types: Reflection-in-Action moments demonstrate responsiveness during TP; Reflection-on-Action demonstrates the depth of post-lesson analysis.
> - Both must be applied to real TP incidents — not as theoretical definitions.

#### H2: Using Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle for Assignment 4
> **EAV to cover**: All 4 Kolb stages (1984) with a complete TP incident worked example
> **Word count**: 280–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (1984): Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Conceptualisation → Active Experimentation
> - Applied to a TP incident:
>   - **Concrete Experience**: "In TP Lesson 4, I set a speaking task in groups but most learners spoke in their L1 and did not produce English."
>   - **Reflective Observation**: "I observed that the task design gave learners no communicative pressure to use English — they could complete the task more easily in their L1. My monitoring was also infrequent."
>   - **Abstract Conceptualisation**: "Willis (1996) argues TBL tasks must have an information gap or genuine communicative need to generate target language use. My task lacked this. Krashen's (1982) Affective Filter theory also suggests that learners produce more when anxiety is low — group monitoring may have increased self-consciousness."
>   - **Active Experimentation**: "In my next TP lesson, I will redesign the speaking task to include an information gap (Student A and Student B have different information) and will monitor by listening at a distance rather than standing directly over the group."
> - Each TP incident in Assignment 4 should follow this structure.

#### H2: Identifying Patterns Across Multiple TP Lessons
> **EAV to cover**: What a pattern is (vs a single incident), how to identify patterns, how Pass B candidates present patterns
> **Word count**: 250–290 words
> **Specific content**:
> - A pattern is a recurring behaviour, difficulty, or development area that appears across multiple TP lessons — not a single incident.
> - How to identify patterns: review tutor feedback across all TP lessons. Look for recurring comments — e.g., "board work unclear," "monitoring too close," "instructions not checked," "student talking time low." Recurring tutor comments = patterns.
> - Examples of patterns: "Across four TP lessons, tutor feedback consistently noted that my error correction was over-immediate — I corrected errors as they occurred during fluency activities rather than noting them for delayed feedback." Pattern: overcorrection during fluency tasks.
> - How Pass B candidates present patterns: "This pattern of [X] appeared in Lessons 2, 3, and 4. I believe this is connected to [theoretical explanation]. My action commitment is [specific, realistic next step]."
> - A single incident is NOT a pattern — do not present a one-off occurrence as a development theme.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How many teaching practice lessons should I refer to in Assignment 4?**
Assignment 4 typically requires reference to 2–3 specific TP incidents. Pass B candidates demonstrate patterns across 3 incidents — showing development over time, not just a single reflection. Some CELTA centres specify a minimum number of TP references; check your centre's Assignment 4 brief for guidance.

**Q: Can I write about a lesson that went well in Assignment 4?**
Positive TP incidents can appear but should not dominate. Assignment 4 is a critical reflection — Cambridge expects candidates to identify areas for development, not just celebrate successes. If you include a positive incident, the critical element should be: why did this work? What would I do differently to make it even more effective? Or how can I replicate this consistently?

**Q: Is Gibbs' Reflective Cycle appropriate for CELTA Assignment 4?**
Kolb (1984) and Schon (1983) are the reflective frameworks most closely aligned with CELTA teaching practice reflection. Gibbs (1988) is used in nursing, healthcare, and CACHE qualifications. Some CELTA centres accept Gibbs but Schon and Kolb are the methodologically appropriate choices for EFL teacher training reflection — they were developed in professional practice contexts (Schon) and experiential learning theory (Kolb), both of which directly apply to TP.

**Q: What is the difference between Assignment 4 and a lesson evaluation?**
A lesson evaluation describes what happened in a lesson and whether objectives were met — it is a procedural record. Assignment 4 is a critical reflection — it analyses WHY things happened, what they reveal about teaching development patterns, and what theoretical frameworks explain the observations. Lesson evaluations describe; Assignment 4 analyses and theorises.
