#### H2: HNC Concepts of Health and Wellbeing Assignment: Medical Models, Social Models, and Critical Analysis
> **EAV to cover**: medical model of health (biomedical, disease-focused, curative), social model of health (bio-psychosocial, determinants-focused, preventive), Dahlgren and Whitehead rainbow model of health determinants, Marmot Review social determinants, P/M/D criteria for critical evaluation
> **Word count**: 300–380 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Concepts of Health and Wellbeing is a foundational HNC HSC unit examining how health is defined, measured, and understood across different theoretical frameworks
> - Pass criteria: describe the medical and social models of health; identify key determinants of health using the Dahlgren and Whitehead model; describe health inequalities in the UK population
> - Merit criteria: analyse the strengths and limitations of the medical and social models; explain how different determinants of health interact to create health inequalities; compare the approaches of different health promotion models
> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate the medical and social models — assess which provides a more complete explanation of health inequalities in contemporary UK society; synthesise insights from the Dahlgren and Whitehead model, the Marmot Review, and relevant sociological theory into a coherent critical analysis of health inequality
> - Medical model: focuses on disease, diagnosis, and treatment — the dominant model in acute NHS settings; limitations include its neglect of social and environmental causes of ill health
> - Social model: focuses on the broader determinants of health — income, housing, education, environment (Dahlgren and Whitehead rainbow model, 1991); the Marmot Review (2010) provides the evidence base for social determinants of health in UK policy
> - Distinction error: students who describe both models and conclude "both have strengths and weaknesses" without evaluating their relative explanatory power for specific health inequalities will not reach Distinction criteria

#### H2: HNC Sociological Perspectives Assignment: Applying Theory to Health and Social Care
> **EAV to cover**: functionalism (Parsons' sick role — rights: exempted from normal duties, cared for; obligations: want to get better, seek medical help), conflict theory (Marxist analysis of health inequality, capitalism and health), symbolic interactionism (Goffman's stigma theory, labelling in mental health), feminist sociology (gendered health experiences), P/M/D criteria for synthesis and critical evaluation
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Sociological Perspectives is one of the most academically demanding HNC HSC units — it requires application of sociological theory to health and social care contexts, which is new to most care workers
> - Pass criteria: describe at least three sociological perspectives (functionalism, conflict theory, interactionism) and explain how each applies to a health or social care context
> - Merit criteria: compare and contrast the sociological perspectives — where do they agree on the causes of health inequality? Where do they fundamentally disagree? Apply each perspective to a specific health issue (e.g., mental health, poverty and health, disability)
> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate the sociological perspectives — assess which perspective provides the most useful analytical framework for understanding a specific aspect of UK health and social care; synthesise insights from multiple perspectives into a coherent sociological analysis of health inequality
> - Parsons' sick role (1951): functionalist theory — people who are ill are temporarily exempted from normal social obligations (work, family duties) in exchange for two obligations (wanting to recover and seeking medical help); Distinction requires critical evaluation of this model — does it apply to chronic illness? Does it reflect power imbalances between doctors and patients?
> - Goffman's stigma theory (1963): interactionist perspective on how society labels and stigmatises those with mental illness, disability, or social deviance — highly relevant to mental health care contexts; Distinction requires critical evaluation of how stigma theory explains barriers to mental health help-seeking in contemporary UK care
> - Feminist sociology: analysis of gendered health experiences — women's greater use of health services, gendered diagnosis patterns (hysteria, medicalisation of childbirth), feminist critiques of the biomedical model

> - Merit criteria: analyse how communication barriers affect specific service user groups (elderly service users, people with learning disabilities, people with mental health conditions); analyse how anti-discriminatory communication practice is implemented in care settings; justify the choice of communication methods for specific service user needs

#### H2: HNC Research Methods in Health and Social Care: Methodology, Ethics, and Literature Review
> **EAV to cover**: primary research methods (interviews, questionnaires, observation), secondary research methods (literature review, systematic review, document analysis), quantitative vs qualitative approaches, research ethics (informed consent, confidentiality, anonymisation, REC approval), literature review structure, P/M/D criteria
> **Word count**: 280–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Research Methods at HNC Level 4 requires students to design a research proposal or evaluate existing research — not just describe research methods but justify methodology choices
> - Pass criteria: describe primary and secondary research methods; identify quantitative and qualitative approaches; describe key ethical principles in health and social care research
> - Merit criteria: analyse the strengths and limitations of different research methods for a given HSC research question; compare quantitative and qualitative approaches and justify which is more appropriate for studying a specific aspect of health or care practice
> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate a research methodology — assess the validity and reliability of the research approach; evaluate the ethical implications for vulnerable service user populations; synthesise research methods knowledge into a justified research proposal with acknowledged limitations
> - Research ethics: informed consent (service users must understand what they are agreeing to), confidentiality (data storage and anonymisation), special considerations for vulnerable populations (mental health service users, elderly patients, children) — Distinction requires critical evaluation of these ethical requirements, not just a list
> - Qualitative vs quantitative: qualitative approaches (interviews, focus groups) provide rich contextual data — appropriate for understanding lived experience; quantitative approaches (surveys, statistical analysis) provide generalisable data — appropriate for measuring prevalence; at Distinction, students must justify their choice for the specific research question
> - Literature review: identifying, evaluating, and synthesising existing research — students must evaluate the quality of sources (peer-reviewed journals vs grey literature vs government reports) and synthesise findings into a coherent argument

#### H2: Writing a Reflective Account for HNC Health and Social Care: Gibbs' Cycle to Distinction
> **EAV to cover**: Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988) — six stages (Description, Feelings, Evaluation, Analysis, Conclusion, Action Plan), Distinction-level Analysis stage requirement, difference between description and critical reflection, professional practice evidence in academic writing
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Reflective accounts are a central assessment format in HNC Health and Social Care — used in Communication, Concepts of Health and Wellbeing, and several other units
> - Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988) is the most commonly required reflective framework at HNC Level 4:
>   - Stage 1 — Description: what happened? (factual account of the event)
>   - Stage 2 — Feelings: how did you feel during and after the event?
>   - Stage 3 — Evaluation: what was good and bad about the experience?
>   - Stage 4 — Analysis: what sense can you make of the situation? (connect to theory and legislation)
>   - Stage 5 — Conclusion: what else could you have done?
>   - Stage 6 — Action Plan: what will you do differently if this situation arises again?
> - Pass criteria: complete all six stages in sequence; demonstrate awareness of the situation and personal response
> - Merit criteria: analyse the experience using relevant theory or legislation — connect the Communication stage to Care Act 2014 requirements; connect the Analysis stage to a sociological or psychological framework
> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate the professional practice — assess what assumptions were operating in the situation; evaluate the effectiveness of the approach against research evidence and legislative requirements; synthesise multiple theoretical perspectives in the Analysis stage to produce a comprehensive critical reflection
> - The Analysis stage is where Merit and Distinction criteria are concentrated — students who only describe the event at this stage will not access higher grades
> - Common failure: stopping at Evaluation (Stage 3) without reaching Analysis (Stage 4) — the structural distinction between evaluation (what was good/bad) and analysis (what theory explains why) is the key grade differentiator

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is Gibbs' Reflective Cycle and how do I use it in an HNC HSC assignment?**
Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988) is a six-stage reflective framework used in HNC Health and Social Care assignments: Description (what happened), Feelings (your emotional response), Evaluation (what was good and bad), Analysis (what theory or legislation explains the situation), Conclusion (what else could have been done), and Action Plan (what will change in future practice). The Analysis stage is where Merit and Distinction criteria are located — it requires connecting the practice experience to sociological theory, legislation (Care Act 2014, Mental Capacity Act 2005), or research evidence. Students who stop at Evaluation without reaching the analytical stage will not access Merit or Distinction grades.

**Q: Which sociological theories do HNC Health and Social Care assignments require?**
HNC Health and Social Care assignments typically require application of three to four sociological perspectives. The core frameworks are: functionalism (Parsons' sick role — 1951), conflict theory (Marxist analysis of health inequality), symbolic interactionism (Goffman's stigma theory — 1963), and feminist sociology (gendered health experiences). Distinction criteria require critical evaluation of these frameworks — assessing which perspective best explains a specific health inequality or care practice issue — not just description and application of each theory in isolation.

**Q: Do I need Harvard referencing for HNC Health and Social Care assignments?**
Harvard referencing is required for all HNC Health and Social Care assignments at Level 4. Academic sources for HSC include: sociological and health promotion textbooks, peer-reviewed health journals (Journal of Advanced Nursing, BMJ), NHS policy documents, NICE guidelines, and UK legislation (cited by title and year). Government reports and NHS documents are cited as organisational publications. General health websites and Wikipedia are not acceptable sources at Level 4. Distinction-level assignments typically draw on 8–12 well-selected academic and policy references.

**Q: Can I use my own professional care experience as evidence in HNC assignments?**
Professional care experience can be referenced in HNC HSC assignments — particularly in reflective accounts and case study analyses — but must be connected to theoretical frameworks and legislative requirements to meet Merit and Distinction criteria. Describing what you did in your care role achieves Pass criteria. Merit requires analysis of why that approach was appropriate, connected to theory. Distinction requires critical evaluation of the practice against research evidence and legislation. Professional experience is the starting point, not the endpoint, of a Level 4 reflective account.
