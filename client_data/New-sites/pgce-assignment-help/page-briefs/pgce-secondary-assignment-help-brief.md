## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: PGCE Secondary qualification assignments
**Main Attributes**: PGCE Secondary structure, assignment types, Teachers' Standards (TS1–TS8), subject pedagogy requirements, reflective writing expectations, behaviour management, lesson study, subject knowledge audit
**Derived Attributes**:
- **PGCE Secondary programme structure**: One-year full-time postgraduate qualification (60 Masters-level credits). Alternates between university seminars (educational theory, pedagogy, professional development) and school placements (60–70% of the programme in school). Assessment: typically 3–4 written assignments + school placement mentor reports + subject knowledge audit. Assignments must connect educational theory to teaching practice — not describe lessons or retell classroom events.
- **Teachers' Standards (TS1–TS8)**: The framework against which all trainee teachers in England are assessed for qualified teacher status (QTS). Published by the Department for Education (2011, updated 2021). Must be evidenced through school placement practice and referenced in PGCE assignments. The 8 standards: TS1 — Set high expectations which inspire, motivate and challenge pupils; TS2 — Promote good progress and outcomes by pupils; TS3 — Demonstrate good subject and curriculum knowledge; TS4 — Plan and teach well-structured lessons; TS5 — Adapt teaching to respond to the strengths and needs of all pupils; TS6 — Make accurate and productive use of assessment; TS7 — Manage behaviour effectively to ensure a good and safe learning environment; TS8 — Fulfil wider professional responsibilities. PGCE Secondary assignments require explicit TS referencing — claiming "TS4: Plan and teach well-structured lessons" with specific lesson examples and theoretical justification.
- **Subject pedagogy assignments**: PGCE Secondary trainee teachers must demonstrate subject-specific pedagogical content knowledge — not just general teaching skill. Shulman (1986) coined "pedagogical content knowledge" (PCK) — knowing not just a subject but knowing how to teach it to specific learners. Subject pedagogy assignments at Secondary level require: understanding of subject-specific misconceptions (e.g., common pupil misunderstandings in Mathematics — proportional reasoning, negative numbers); subject-specific assessment strategies; National Curriculum subject requirements; disciplinary literacy in the subject (how reading and writing work specifically in History vs Science vs English).
- **Reflective writing expectations at PGCE Secondary level**: Critical reflection must connect classroom observations or teaching incidents to educational theory — not just describe what happened. Gibbs (1988) or Schon (1983) applied to a specific classroom incident. Example of insufficient reflection: "In my lesson on Shakespeare, some students were disengaged. I changed activity and they became more engaged." Example of sufficient reflection: "When engagement dropped during the Shakespeare close reading, I shifted from whole-class discussion to a collaborative task — a decision informed by Vygotsky's (1978) ZPD concept, which suggests peer dialogue can scaffold understanding where teacher explanation alone creates passive learning. This aligns with TS5 — adapting teaching to respond to learner needs."
- **Behaviour management in PGCE Secondary assignments**: TS7 is the most anxiety-inducing standard for Secondary trainees — managing behaviour in a secondary school classroom is the most commonly cited challenge. Assignment treatment of behaviour management must reference evidence-based approaches: Rogers (2006) — behaviour management models and assertive discipline; Delaney (2009) — secondary classroom management strategies; Low-level disruption vs serious incidents — different responses required; Restorative approaches vs sanctions; Understanding that behaviour is often communication — seeking to understand root causes.
- **Lesson study as a PGCE Secondary assignment format**: Lesson study is a collaborative teacher inquiry model originating in Japan (Stigler and Hiebert 1999 — The Teaching Gap). For PGCE Secondary: three trainees co-plan a "research lesson" around a shared teaching question, one teaches while the others observe focusing on 3 "case pupils," the group discusses what was learned, and the lesson is revised and retaught. PGCE assignment asks for written analysis of the lesson study process — including: the research question, what was observed in the three case pupils, what was learned about subject-specific pedagogy, and implications for future practice.
- **Subject knowledge audit (SKA)**: A self-assessment tool used at the start of PGCE programmes to identify subject knowledge gaps. The SKA assignment asks trainees to: identify 3–5 areas of weaker subject knowledge; demonstrate how they addressed these gaps (reading, subject association resources, mentor guidance); show evidence of improvement. Not a confession of what the trainee doesn't know — a demonstration of professional self-regulation and subject knowledge development.

#### H2: Critical Reflective Writing in PGCE Secondary Assignments
> **EAV to cover**: What critical reflection means at PGCE level, Gibbs and Schon applied to classroom incidents, description vs analysis
> **Word count**: 260–300 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Critical reflection connects classroom experience to educational theory — analysis of WHY something happened, what theory explains it, and what will change in future practice
> - Insufficient (description): "In my lesson on the Cold War, some students struggled to understand the causes of tension. I gave them a worksheet and this seemed to help."
> - Sufficient (critical reflection): "When students struggled with the causal chain of Cold War tensions, my initial instinct was to provide a structured worksheet — a scaffolding decision informed by Bruner's (1960) concept of scaffolding within Vygotsky's (1978) ZPD. However, on reflection, the worksheet reduced cognitive demand and may have inhibited deeper historical reasoning. Counsell (2000) argues that historical causation requires genuine analytical engagement, not procedural completion. In future, I will use a card-sort causation activity that requires pupils to justify their sequencing decisions, addressing TS4 and TS5."
> - Schon (1983) — reflection-in-action (mid-lesson adjustment) and reflection-on-action (post-lesson analysis). Both must appear in PGCE Secondary assignments — reflection-in-action moments demonstrate responsive teaching (TS5); reflection-on-action demonstrates critical self-evaluation (TS8)

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How do I reference the Teachers' Standards in a PGCE Secondary assignment?**
Teachers' Standards are referenced by number and descriptor: "This lesson planning decision demonstrates TS4 — Plan and teach well-structured lessons — through the deliberate sequencing of retrieval practice before new content introduction, supported by Rosenshine's (2012) Principles of Instruction." Use the standard number (TS4, TS7, etc.) not just a vague reference to "professional standards." Each written assignment should reference multiple relevant standards — most PGCE assignments touch on at least 3–4 standards across their argument.

**Q: What is pedagogical content knowledge and why does it matter for PGCE Secondary assignments?**
Pedagogical content knowledge (Shulman 1986) is the ability to understand not just a subject but how to teach it to specific learners — including knowing where pupils commonly go wrong, which analogies and representations work for specific concepts, and how to sequence content appropriately. PGCE Secondary assignments require PCK demonstration because TS3 (Subject and curriculum knowledge) specifically assesses this. A history trainee demonstrating PCK shows awareness of historical misconceptions and how to teach causation analytically; a maths trainee demonstrates awareness of common pupil errors around proportional reasoning and how to address them.

**Q: Why are PGCE Secondary assignments at Masters level?**
PGCE programmes award 60 Masters-level credits (Level 7 on the RQF) because they are postgraduate qualifications. Masters-level (Level 7) means: critical analysis rather than description, synthesis of multiple theoretical perspectives, evaluation of competing arguments, and original reasoned argument construction supported by evidence. A PGCE Secondary assignment that describes a lesson, reports what happened, and concludes "this went well" is not meeting Level 7 standards — it must analyse why, connect to named theory, evaluate critically, and argue for a theoretical position.

**Q: How much of a PGCE Secondary assignment should reference classroom experience?**
PGCE assignments balance theory and practice — neither should dominate. Practice examples (from school placement) provide the concrete evidence that theoretical principles apply in real classrooms. Theory (named researchers, educational frameworks) explains why the practice works or what it reveals. A strong PGCE Secondary assignment uses classroom examples throughout but always connects them to theory: "When I...this was informed by...which connects to TS...because..." Purely theoretical assignments with no practice examples, or purely descriptive accounts with no theory, both fail to meet PGCE assessment criteria.
