#### H2: NEBOSH Construction Hazards You Must Cover in NC Assignments
> **Context vector**: Construction-specific hazard categories — the range that NC2 risk assessment must cover, with technical values
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - **Working at height** (WAH Regs 2005): leading cause of fatal injuries in UK construction — three-stage hierarchy: avoid (can the task be done at ground level?); prevent (collective protection — scaffolding, guard rails, MEWPs, working platforms, edge protection); minimise consequences (personal fall protection — harness and fall arrest systems) — PPE used only where collective measures are not reasonably practicable
> - **Excavations and ground works**: collapse risk (angle of repose, battering, shoring, trench boxes); contact with underground services (CAT scanning, permit to dig, hand-dig exclusion zones); flooding; inhalation of contaminated soils
> - **Manual handling**: MSDs (musculoskeletal disorders) are the most common non-fatal injury type in construction; TILE assessment (Task, Individual, Load, Environment); mechanical handling aids (telehandlers, hoists, block and tackle) preferred
> - **COSHH hazards**: silica dust — WEL = 0.1 mg/m³ (8-hour TWA) — generated by cutting, drilling, or grinding concrete, masonry, stone; asbestos — fibres in ACMs (asbestos-containing materials) in pre-2000 buildings — control limit = 0.1 fibres/cm³ (4-hour TWA); licensed removal required for most licensable asbestos work; cement — contains chrome VI (chromate) — causes occupational contact dermatitis; LEV, RPE, and wet methods as controls
> - **Plant and machinery** (PUWER 1998, LOLER 1998): struck by reversing vehicles (segregation of pedestrians and plant, banksman, reversing alarms); lifting operations (LOLER — thorough examination of lifting equipment every 6 months for person-carrying, 12 months otherwise; appointed person required for complex lifts); safeguarding requirements for excavators, compactors
> - **Electricity**: overhead power lines (exclusion zones, goal posts, permit to work); buried cables (CAT scanning, hand-dig exclusion); temporary site electrical supplies (110V site tools, RCDs, PAT testing)
> - **Demolition and structural stability**: temporary works design requirement; sequence of demolition (progressive, piecemeal); structural survey required before demolition commences

#### H2: FAQ — NEBOSH Construction Certificate Representative Query Extensions

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. **Is the NEBOSH National Construction Certificate the same as the NGC?** → Answer: No, though they share the same assessment format (OBE + practical risk assessment) and grading bands. The NGC (National General Certificate) covers health and safety management across all industries, applying HSWA 1974, MHSWR 1999, and general risk assessment methodology. The NCC is construction-specific — it applies CDM Regulations 2015, Working at Height Regulations 2005, LOLER 1998, and construction-specific hazard categories (silica dust, asbestos in existing structures, excavation risks, plant and machinery). The NCC is intended for construction industry professionals; the NGC serves a broader audience.
2. **What does CDM 2015 require the Principal Contractor to do in NEBOSH assignments?** → Answer: CDM 2015 requires the Principal Contractor to plan, manage, monitor, and coordinate health and safety during the construction phase. Key specific duties include: producing the Construction Phase Plan before work starts, ensuring all workers receive adequate site induction, managing cooperation between contractors, maintaining a safe site (welfare facilities, emergency procedures, traffic management), and ensuring the site is secure against unauthorised access. NEBOSH NC assignments expect students to name and apply these duties to the construction scenario provided.
3. **What are the most common construction hazards examiners expect to see in NC2?** → Answer: NEBOSH NC2 examiners expect broad hazard coverage across all construction hazard categories: working at height (falls from scaffolding, ladders, roof edges); excavation (collapse, underground services, flooding); plant and machinery (struck-by, crushing, overturning); COSHH (silica dust, asbestos, cement dermatitis); manual handling (MSDs from repetitive or heavy lifting); electricity (overhead lines, buried cables, temporary supplies). A risk assessment limited to the most visible hazards on a walk-through will not achieve Credit or Distinction — background and process hazards must also be identified.
4. **When does CDM 2015 require HSE notification?** → Answer: CDM 2015 requires notification to HSE (on Form F10) for projects that will last longer than 30 working days with more than 20 workers simultaneously on site at any point, or that will exceed 500 person-days of work. The Principal Designer is responsible for notifying HSE before the construction phase begins. This threshold is regularly tested in NC1 OBE tasks — students must know the specific criteria, not just that "large projects" require notification.
