## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: NEBOSH National Diploma (ND) qualification and assignments
**Main Attributes**: Diploma structure (DN1/DN2/DN3), academic level, assessment format per unit, grading thresholds, Distinction criteria, CMIOSH pathway, distinction from IGC/NGC
**Derived Attributes**:
- **Diploma structure — three units**: DN1: Health and Safety Management Systems — Plan (hazard identification, risk assessment, legal framework, management system planning); DN2: Health and Safety Management Systems — Do and Check (implementing controls, monitoring performance, measuring safety outcomes, audit); DN3: Health and Safety Management Systems — Act (reviewing performance, continual improvement, corrective action, applying management review). The structure follows the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) cycle, also known as the Deming cycle.
- **RQF level**: NEBOSH National Diploma is at Level 6 on the Regulated Qualifications Framework — equivalent to a Bachelor's degree. This distinguishes it sharply from the IGC (Level 3) and NGC (Level 3). The academic and analytical demands are significantly higher.
- **Assessment format**: DN1 and DN2 are assessed by written assignment (coursework). DN3 is assessed by a written examination. Candidates submit DN1 and DN2 assignments to their learning provider who marks against NEBOSH marking criteria before external moderation. Written assignments at Diploma level require: critical analysis of health and safety management concepts, application of legislation and standards to complex workplace scenarios, synthesis of research sources with Harvard referencing, and evaluation of competing approaches or frameworks.
- **Harvard referencing**: Required at Diploma level — candidates must cite legislation, HSE guidance, British Standards (BS OHSAS 18001 now superseded by ISO 45001:2018), academic texts, and other sources. Reference lists and in-text citations must follow Harvard format throughout.
- **Grading thresholds**: Distinction 70%+ (note: different from IGC/NGC — Diploma uses 70% not 80% for Distinction); Credit 55–69%; Pass 40–54%; Fail below 40%
- **Distinction criteria at Diploma level**: Critical evaluation — not just describing a framework but evaluating its strengths and limitations; synthesis of multiple sources; application to complex multi-site or multi-hazard scenarios; original argument construction supported by evidence; analysis of competing theoretical positions (e.g., Heinrich's Triangle vs more recent epidemiological models)
- **CMIOSH pathway**: NEBOSH Diploma → IOSH Graduate membership → CMIOSH (Chartered Member of IOSH). Candidates with the NEBOSH Diploma and 5+ years' health and safety management experience can apply for Chartered status via the IOSH Continuing Professional Development (CPD) assessment route. The Diploma is the qualification gateway — without it, Chartered status requires an alternative Level 6 qualification.
- **DN1 key topics**: Legal framework for health and safety in Great Britain (HSW Act 1974, Management Regulations 1999, Construction Design and Management Regulations 2015, Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002); Risk assessment methodologies (qualitative, semi-quantitative, quantitative — HAZOP, FMEA, bow-tie analysis); Health and safety management systems (ISO 45001:2018 — Plan stage); Organisational factors influencing health and safety culture
- **DN2 key topics**: Implementing control measures across the hierarchy; Monitoring health and safety performance — leading and lagging indicators; Health and safety auditing (active and reactive monitoring); Investigating accidents and near misses (fault tree analysis, root cause analysis methodologies); Occupational health — workplace exposure limits, health surveillance
- **DN3 key topics**: Performance review processes; Corrective and preventive action (CAPA); Management review of the health and safety management system; Continual improvement frameworks; Reporting health and safety performance to senior management

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How does the NEBOSH Diploma differ from the NEBOSH IGC?**
The NEBOSH Diploma is a Level 6 qualification on the RQF — equivalent to a Bachelor's degree. The NEBOSH IGC is a Level 3 qualification. The Diploma requires Harvard referencing, critical evaluation, synthesis of academic and legislative sources, and analysis of complex multi-hazard management systems. The IGC requires clear application of health and safety knowledge to workplace scenarios. They are not comparable qualifications — the Diploma is the advanced post-IGC route for professionals seeking Chartered IOSH status.

**Q: Do I need to complete the IGC before the NEBOSH Diploma?**
The NEBOSH Diploma does not formally require the IGC as a prerequisite. However, NEBOSH recommends that candidates have a solid foundation in health and safety before attempting the Diploma, and most candidates hold the IGC or NGC before enrolling. The Diploma assumes working knowledge of risk assessment, legislation, and health and safety management — candidates without practical experience typically find it significantly more demanding.

**Q: What is the difference between DN1, DN2, and DN3?**
DN1 covers the Plan stage of the health and safety management cycle — legal framework, risk assessment methodologies, and management system planning. DN2 covers Do and Check — implementing controls, monitoring performance, auditing, and accident investigation. DN3 covers Act — performance review, corrective action, and continual improvement. DN1 and DN2 are assessed by written assignments; DN3 is assessed by written examination.

**Q: What Harvard referencing sources should I use in NEBOSH Diploma assignments?**
Primary sources for NEBOSH Diploma Harvard referencing include: legislation (HSW Act 1974, Management Regulations 1999, CDM 2015, COSHH 2002), HSE guidance documents (HSG65: Managing for Health and Safety, HSG48: Reducing Error and Influencing Behaviour), ISO 45001:2018 standard, academic journals (Safety Science, Journal of Safety Research, International Journal of Occupational Safety and Ergonomics), and IOSH technical guidance. Examiners value breadth of sourcing — candidates who cite only the course textbook score lower than those who integrate legislation, HSE guidance, and academic research.
