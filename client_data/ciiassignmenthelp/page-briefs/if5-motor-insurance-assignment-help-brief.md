##### H3: The Road Traffic Act 1988 — Compulsory Motor Insurance
> **Specific content**:
> - **RTA 1988 s.143**: It is illegal to use, cause, or permit the use of a motor vehicle on a road or other public place without insurance that at minimum covers third-party death, bodily injury, and emergency treatment costs.
> - **RTA 1988 s.151**: Insurers must satisfy court judgements against the insured (up to the policy limit) even where the policy has been avoided for non-disclosure or misrepresentation — protecting innocent third parties.
> - **Certificate of Motor Insurance**: Evidence of compulsory insurance; must be produced on request by police.
> - **Motor Uninsured Vehicles**: Keeper liability — Continuous Insurance Enforcement (CIE) requires vehicles to be insured unless formally declared SORN (Statutory Off Road Notification).
> - **Relevant exam angle**: Scenarios often test whether s.151 applies when the insurer would otherwise avoid the policy — students must know the insurer pays the third party but may recoup from the insured.

##### H3: Cover Types — TPO, TPFT, and Comprehensive
> **Specific content**:
> - **Third Party Only (TPO)**: Minimum legal cover. Covers death and bodily injury to third parties, damage to third-party property. Does NOT cover own vehicle damage, fire, theft, or own injury.
> - **Third Party Fire and Theft (TPFT)**: TPO scope PLUS own vehicle loss or damage from fire or theft (not accidental damage to own vehicle).
> - **Comprehensive**: All TPO and TPFT risks PLUS accidental damage to own vehicle, windscreen, personal accident benefits, and (subject to policy wording) legal expenses and courtesy car.
> - **Exam tip**: MCQ scenarios distinguish TPFT from Comprehensive by whether own accidental damage is covered — theft of own vehicle is covered under both TPFT and Comprehensive.

##### H3: MIB and MID — Protecting Victims of Uninsured and Untraced Drivers
> **Specific content**:
> - **Motor Insurers Bureau (MIB)**: Non-profit body funded by member insurers — all UK motor insurers must be members. Compensates victims where at-fault driver is uninsured or untraced.
> - **Uninsured Drivers Agreement**: MIB satisfies unsatisfied judgements against uninsured at-fault drivers. Conditions: victim must report accident to police within 5 days; MIB excess applies to property damage claims; not available if victim knew the driver was uninsured.
> - **Untraced Drivers Agreement**: Compensates death and personal injury (not property damage beyond £300) caused by a driver who cannot be identified. Application must be made within 3 years of accident.
> - **Motor Insurance Database (MID)**: Central record of all UK motor insurance policies. Enables police (via roadside ANPR cameras) and DVLA to identify uninsured vehicles. Insurers must update MID within 7 days of policy inception or change.

#### H2: No Claims Discount — How NCD Works in IF5
> **EAV to cover**: NCD scale, protection clause, step-back, fleet NCD
> **Word count**: 250–300 words
> **Instruction**: Give the precise NCD percentage scale and mechanics — not a general description

> **Specific content**:
> - **NCD Scale (typical personal lines)**: 0 years = 0%; 1 year = 30%; 2 years = 40%; 3 years = 50%; 4 years = 60%; 5+ years = 65–70% (insurer-specific ceiling).
> - **Earned on**: Number of consecutive claim-free years on a personal motor policy.
> - **Basis**: NCD is earned per driver (not per vehicle). When moving insurer, the NCD certificate must be provided.
> - **Step-back clause**: On a first claim where NCD protection is purchased, the NCD remains at its current level. On a subsequent claim (without protection), the NCD steps back by typically 2 years — e.g., 5 years NCD steps back to 3 years NCD after one unprotected claim.
> - **NCD Protection**: Available at 4+ years NCD in most schemes. Allows one claim (sometimes two in a rolling 3-year period) without loss of accumulated NCD. Does NOT protect the premium level — insurers may still increase premiums after claims.
> - **Fleet NCD**: Commercial fleets often use experience-rated pricing (claims cost as proportion of premium over 3–5 year period) rather than a step NCD structure.

#### H2: Fleet and Telematics Underwriting in IF5
> **EAV to cover**: Fleet rating factors, telematics data usage, premium adjustment
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Fleet rating factors**: Number of vehicles, vehicle types and values, driver ages and occupations, territorial use, annual mileage, claims experience (frequency and severity), business use class.
> - **Fleet policy structure**: Any driver or named driver; open or closed fleet (closed = named drivers only, cheaper if managed). Annual policy — not per-vehicle renewal.
> - **Telematics (black box)**: Device installed in vehicle (OBD-II port or hardwired) records speed, time of day, braking intensity, cornering, acceleration, mileage. Data transmitted to insurer.
> - **Telematics rating**: At inception, provisional premium set. At renewal or mid-term review, premium adjusted based on telematics score. Safe drivers receive discount; high-risk behaviour triggers surcharge or policy cancellation warning.
> - **Use in MCQ**: IF5 scenarios test whether telematics constitutes a rating factor (yes) and whether the insurer can cancel for telematics-evidenced reckless driving (yes, with appropriate notice under policy terms and FCA ICOBS requirements).

#### H2: Motor Claims Handling in IF5
> **EAV to cover**: FNOL, total loss valuation, subrogation, hire car, fraud indicators
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **FNOL (First Notification of Loss)**: Claims notification must be made promptly — delay may allow insurer to reject claim. Insurer records: date, circumstances, third parties involved, police report number (if any).
> - **Total Loss Valuation**: Own vehicle written off when repair cost exceeds market value (or when repair not economically viable — typically 60–80% threshold varies by insurer). Market value = replacement cost of equivalent vehicle at date of loss (not new car value). CAT A: crush. CAT B: bodyshell crush, parts salvageable. CAT S/N: structural or non-structural damage — can be repaired and re-registered.
> - **Betterment**: Where old parts are replaced by new, insurer may apply betterment deduction from settlement.
> - **Subrogation in motor**: After paying own damage claim, insurer steps into insured's shoes to recover from at-fault third party. Must pursue in insured's name. Insured must cooperate — failure to cooperate is a breach voiding the right.
> - **Hire Car**: Under fault claims, insured's insurer may provide courtesy car. Under third-party credit hire, the claimant hires a replacement at market rates from a credit hire organisation — insurer of at-fault driver is liable for reasonable hire costs.
> - **Fraud indicators**: Staged accident patterns (low speed, multiple passengers, soft-tissue injury claims), ghost broking (fraudulent policy documentation), fronting (parent named as main driver when young driver is actual main user — material non-disclosure).

> **EAV to cover**: Scenario elimination technique, common IF5 traps, time management
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Content**:
> - IF5 uses scenario-based MCQ: a fact pattern (accident/claim/cover type question) followed by 4 options (A–D). One correct, three plausible distractors.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Is IF5 compulsory for the CII Certificate in Insurance?**
IF5 is an optional unit within the Certificate in Insurance. It is not a compulsory core unit but is commonly chosen by students working in personal lines motor roles.

**Q: What is the pass mark for IF5?**
The CII indicative pass mark for IF5 is 65%. Confirm the current pass mark on the official CII unit syllabus page before sitting the exam.

**Q: Does IF5 cover commercial vehicle insurance?**
IF5 primarily covers personal motor insurance. Commercial and fleet vehicles are touched at the margins. Students seeking deeper commercial motor content should consult the M86 (Commercial and Industrial Insurances) unit at Diploma level.

**Q: What is the difference between the MIB Untraced and Uninsured Drivers Agreements?**
The Uninsured Drivers Agreement covers situations where the at-fault driver is identified but has no valid insurance. The Untraced Drivers Agreement covers situations where the at-fault driver cannot be identified. The Untraced Agreement only compensates death and personal injury — property damage is only covered above £300 and is limited.
