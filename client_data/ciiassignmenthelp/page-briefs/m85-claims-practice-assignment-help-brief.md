**Derived Attributes**:
- **Claims investigation management**: Loss adjuster appointment (large property losses exceeding insurer's in-house handling threshold; complex liability claims with contested causation; catastrophe events requiring field adjusting capacity); engineers (machinery breakdown, structural damage, construction defects — appointed where technical cause of damage requires specialist report); forensic accountants (business interruption claims, fidelity guarantee claims, financial crime — appointed to verify turnover, gross profit rate, and loss of revenue figures); solicitors (subrogation recovery actions; large liability claims with litigation; policy coverage disputes requiring legal opinion; fraud investigations requiring civil recovery action). Decision criteria for specialist appointment: size of claim (cost-benefit of specialist fee vs claim complexity); technical complexity (cause of loss requires expert report); contested coverage (need for legal opinion on policy interpretation); fraud indicators (SIU referral requires documented referral justification).
- **Large and complex claims handling**: Multiple parties (insured, third-party claimants, co-insurers, reinsurers, specialists — all require coordinated communication and information flow); long-tail claims (liability claims, latent disease — asbestos, industrial deafness — where claims arise years or decades after the causative event; reserve must reflect current exposure not historical premium income); catastrophe response (activation of catastrophe response plan; field adjuster deployment; triage of claims by severity; use of agreed settlement values for low-value high-frequency losses; coordination with reinsurers on catastrophe notifications)
- **Coverage analysis at practice level**:
  - **Proximate cause determination**: The dominant, effective, or operative cause of the loss. Wayne Tank & Pump Co v Employers Liability [1974] CA: where two concurrent causes operate simultaneously — one insured, one excluded — the entire loss is excluded if the excluded cause is the proximate cause. Contrast with the Wayne Tank principle: if the insured peril and an excluded peril both contribute to the loss, the excluded peril does not automatically defeat the claim unless it is the proximate cause.
  - **Policy interpretation at practice level**: Contra proferentem rule — ambiguity in the insurer's policy wording is construed against the insurer who drafted it. Ejusdem generis — general words following a list are limited to the same category as the listed items. Conditions precedent vs conditions subsequent — breach of a condition precedent defeats the claim; breach of a condition subsequent may limit but not automatically defeat it (post-Insurance Act 2015).
- **Business interruption claims methodology**:
  - **Turnover basis of settlement**: The standard basis for BI claims in UK commercial insurance. Indemnity = (Rate of Gross Profit × reduction in turnover during the indemnity period) + Additional Increased Cost of Working (AICOW) — limited to the lesser of the actual AICOW or the saved Gross Profit.
  - **Rate of Gross Profit**: (Gross Profit ÷ Turnover) × 100 for the accounting period immediately preceding the loss — adjusted for any trend. Gross Profit in BI insurance = Turnover − Uninsured Working Expenses (variable costs that cease when the business interrupts, e.g., raw materials, packaging, direct labour on piece-work — but NOT fixed overheads, which continue).
  - **Maximum Indemnity Period (MIP)**: The maximum period of insurance cover for the BI loss — selected at inception (typically 12, 18, 24, or 36 months). The MIP must be long enough to cover full recovery of turnover to pre-loss levels — underinsurance of the MIP is a common cause of claim underpayment.
  - **Actual vs projected turnover**: The assessor compares actual turnover achieved during the interruption period with the turnover that would have been achieved but for the loss — the projected turnover. The projected turnover is adjusted for trend (growth or decline in the business independent of the insured event) using the Trends Clause.
  - **Trends Clause**: Adjusts the standard turnover figures (pre-loss turnover used to calculate the Rate of Gross Profit; projected post-loss turnover) to reflect trends and other circumstances which, had the damage not occurred, would have affected the business for better or worse. Example: a business in long-term decline would have lower projected turnover even without the loss event — the Trends Clause reduces the indemnity accordingly.
  - **Additional Increased Cost of Working (AICOW)**: Expenditure incurred by the insured during the indemnity period, beyond normal expenditure, to avoid or reduce the turnover reduction. Covered only to the extent that it reduces the BI loss (i.e., it must be economic — the cost must not exceed the loss it avoids). Dual basis BI policies also cover Increased Cost of Working (ICW) without the economic limit.
- **Liability claims management**:
  - **Third-party bodily injury (BI) claims**: The claimant (injured third party) must establish: liability (the insured was negligent and the negligence caused the injury); causation (the injury was caused by the breach of duty); quantum (the extent of financial and non-financial loss).
  - **Personal injury valuation — general damages**: Non-financial losses (pain, suffering, and loss of amenity — PSLA). Assessed using the Judicial College Guidelines (JCG) — bracket system by injury type and severity. Example: severe traumatic brain injury with permanent significant disability: JCG bracket £282,010–£403,990 (as per current edition). Moderate back injury, period of recovery but good outcome: JCG bracket £12,510–£27,760.
  - **Personal injury valuation — special damages**: Financial losses (past and future earnings, medical expenses, care costs, adaptation of accommodation). Future loss of earnings calculated by Ogden Tables method: annual loss × multiplier. Ogden Tables (8th Edition): discount rate set at -0.25% (as updated by Lord Chancellor under the Damages Act 1996 s.1; applied since August 2019). The discount rate reflects the expected real return on investment of a lump sum — a negative discount rate reflects current low-yield investment environment, resulting in higher lump sums than under positive discount rate.
  - **Periodical Payment Orders (PPOs)**: For very large personal injury claims with long-term care needs, courts can award PPOs instead of a lump sum — periodic payments index-linked to the ASHE (Annual Survey of Hours and Earnings) index for care workers, continuing for the claimant's lifetime. PPOs remove investment risk for the claimant but create long-tail reserving uncertainty for the insurer.
- **Fraud management at professional level**:
  - **Organised fraud networks**: Professional fraud rings operating across multiple insurers simultaneously — e.g., staged motor accidents (induced collisions — 'cash for crash'); ghost broking (false insurance certificates); bogus employers' liability claims using fabricated employment history. SIU (Special Investigations Unit) referral criteria: multiple indicators present (inconsistent accident description, multiple witnesses with same address, claimant legal representation initiated before notification to insurer, previous fraud history on insurance industry database — Claims and Underwriting Exchange (CUE), Motor Insurers' Database (MID)).
  - **Insurer remedies for fraud**: Void ab initio (if there was fraudulent misrepresentation at inception — the policy is treated as if it never existed; no claims are payable; premium may be forfeited). Rescission (policy set aside from inception for material non-disclosure or misrepresentation — under Insurance Act 2015 and Consumer Insurance Act 2012, proportionate remedy may apply rather than automatic avoidance). Repudiation of claim (specific claim denied on fraud grounds — policy itself continues). Under the Insurance Act 2015 (consumer: Insurance Fraud) and the common law, a fraudulent claim allows the insurer to forfeit the entire claim including any legitimate portion of the same claim.
- **Claims reserving methodology**:
  - **Case reserve**: Individual reserve set for each reported claim at first notification — based on claims handler's assessment of likely settlement cost given the information available at notification stage. Must be reviewed and updated as new information emerges.
  - **IBNR (Incurred But Not Reported)**: Claims that have occurred within the policy period but have not yet been reported to the insurer. Calculated by actuarial methods (development triangle analysis — comparing the development pattern of paid claims across successive underwriting years to project the ultimate incurred cost for immature years). Significant in: employers' liability (latent disease — asbestos, HAVS); professional indemnity (claims-made vs occurrence basis distinction for IBNR calculation); casualty reinsurance.
  - **ULAE (Unallocated Loss Adjustment Expenses)**: The claims handling costs that cannot be attributed to specific individual claims — overhead costs of the claims department, IT systems, management costs. Must be reserved for separately alongside case reserves and IBNR.
  - **Claims inflation adjustment**: Reserves must be adjusted for expected future claims inflation (medical treatment cost inflation — typically higher than RPI; legal cost inflation; care cost inflation — ASHE index). Failure to apply adequate claims inflation loading to reserves results in reserve deficiency as claims mature.
- **Subrogation recovery management**: Decision to pursue recovery (cost-benefit analysis — recovery prospect × probability of success vs legal costs; creditworthiness of potential defendant; time limits — limitation period 3 years for personal injury, 6 years for contract and property damage); managing recovery in the insured's name (court proceedings are brought in the insured's name, not the insurer's name; insured must cooperate — failure to cooperate is a condition precedent breach); instructing subrogation solicitors; negotiation vs litigation strategy; recovery proceeds allocation (if recovery is partial — excess loss recovery first, then insurer recoups its payment pro rata with the insured for any element not fully indemnified)

#### H2: Claims Investigation Management — Appointing the Right Specialist — Major Query Need: specialist appointment in M85
> **EAV to cover**: Loss adjuster, engineer, forensic accountant, solicitor — when and why each is appointed; decision criteria
> **Word count**: 350–400 words

##### H3: Loss Adjusters — Appointment Criteria and Role
> **Specific content**:
> **When loss adjusters are appointed**: Large property claims exceeding the insurer's internal handling threshold (typically claims above £25,000–£50,000 — varies by insurer); complex claims requiring on-site investigation (cause of loss determination; scope of damage assessment; contractor oversight); claims with multiple interested parties (landlord/tenant, mortgagee, co-insurer); catastrophe events where the insurer's in-house capacity is overwhelmed; claims with suspected fraud indicators requiring documented field investigation.
>
> **The loss adjuster's role**: The loss adjuster acts as the insurer's agent for investigation purposes. They: attend the loss location; prepare a scope of damage report; assess the cause of loss and whether it is covered under the policy; verify the sum insured adequacy (checking for underinsurance — if the sum insured is below the reinstatement value, the average clause may reduce the settlement proportionately); agree the scope of reinstatement works with the insured's contractor; certify interim and final payments.
>
> **Loss adjuster instruction — key requirement for M85**: The instruction letter to the loss adjuster must specify: the coverage issues to investigate (e.g., whether the cause of loss is covered under the policy; whether any exclusions apply); the reserve the insurer has set (so the adjuster can advise if the reserve is inadequate); any fraud indicators already identified; whether legal advice is required alongside adjusting. A loss adjuster appointed without clear instructions produces a report that does not address the coverage questions — a common source of claims management failure.

##### H3: Engineers, Forensic Accountants, and Solicitors — When Each is Used
> **Specific content**:
> **Engineers**: Appointed for machinery breakdown claims (to determine whether the failure mode is covered — sudden and unforeseen breakdown vs gradual deterioration or wear and tear, which is excluded); construction defect claims (to assess whether the defect constitutes a covered latent defect or excluded faulty workmanship); structural damage claims (to assess whether the structural failure was caused by an insured peril or pre-existing condition); fire origin and cause investigations (alongside forensic fire investigators — to determine whether the fire was caused by electrical fault, arson, or excluded process).
>
> **Forensic accountants**: Essential for BI claims of any size — the forensic accountant verifies the insured's trading accounts, calculates the Rate of Gross Profit, assesses the actual turnover during the interruption period and the projected turnover (adjusted for trend), and checks the Additional Increased Cost of Working figure for economic validity. Also appointed for fidelity guarantee claims (employee theft — verification of missing funds) and financial crime claims.
>
> **Solicitors**: Appointed for: subrogation recovery actions (pursuing the third party responsible for the loss in the insured's name); large liability claims where litigation is likely (defending personal injury claims, employer's liability claims with serious injury); coverage disputes requiring legal advice on policy interpretation (where a complex coverage question cannot be resolved between insurer and insured without legal opinion); fraud investigation civil recovery actions (Civil Recovery Unit — recovering the fraudulent claim payment from the fraudster).

#### H2: Coverage Analysis at Practice Level — Proximate Cause and Policy Interpretation — Major Query Need: coverage determination in M85
> **EAV to cover**: Wayne Tank concurrent causes rule, contra proferentem, conditions precedent in claims context
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

##### H3: Proximate Cause — Wayne Tank and Concurrent Causes
> **Specific content**:
> **Proximate cause at practice level**: In M85, candidates must apply proximate cause to multi-cause scenarios — not just state the doctrine. The proximate cause is the dominant, effective, or operative cause — not the most recent or first in a chain.
>
> **Wayne Tank & Pump Co v Employers Liability [1974] QB 57 — the concurrent causes rule**: Where two causes operate simultaneously and one is insured while the other is excluded, the exclusion prevails if the excluded peril is one of the proximate causes. In Wayne Tank, a duct overheated (excluded — faulty design) and ignited a fire (insured peril). Because both the insured and excluded perils were concurrent proximate causes, the Court of Appeal held the claim was excluded. The significance for M85: an insured cannot recover where an excluded peril is a proximate cause alongside an insured peril — even if the insured peril also qualifies as proximate.
>
> **Contrast with the single proximate cause chain**: Where a chain of events has a single proximate cause (the first cause in efficiency), subsequent events in the chain are considered consequences of that proximate cause. If the proximate cause is an insured peril, all consequential losses are covered — even if an intermediate event in the chain would have been excluded had it occurred independently (Leyland Shipping v Norwich Union [1918]).
>
> **M85 exam application**: Coverage questions in M85 present scenarios with two or more causes of loss. The candidate must: identify each cause; classify it as insured, excluded, or neutral; determine whether causes are sequential (single proximate cause analysis) or concurrent (Wayne Tank analysis); conclude on coverage. A candidate who states "the proximate cause is the dominant cause" without applying the concurrent causes analysis to the scenario will not score well at M85 level.

##### H3: Policy Interpretation — Contra Proferentem and Conditions Precedent
> **Specific content**:
> **Contra proferentem**: Where a policy term is genuinely ambiguous, the ambiguity is resolved against the insurer who drafted the policy. The ambiguity must be real — courts will not create ambiguity by reading policy wording in an artificial or strained way. The contra proferentem rule is a last resort interpretation tool — only applied after all other rules of construction have failed to resolve the ambiguity.
>
> **Conditions precedent to the claim**: Obligations the insured must satisfy before the insurer's liability to pay is triggered. Examples: prompt notification of loss (within 30 days of discovery, or "as soon as practicable"); submission of a completed claim form; provision of all books and records for BI claims. Breach of a condition precedent is an absolute bar to the claim — even if the breach caused no prejudice to the insurer (pre-Insurance Act 2015 position; now modified — post-2015 for commercial policyholders, the insurer can only rely on a condition precedent breach if it was prejudiced by the breach, by operation of Insurance Act 2015 s.13A implied term).

#### H2: Business Interruption Claims — Full Methodology — Major Query Need: BI claims calculation in M85
> **EAV to cover**: Turnover basis, Rate of Gross Profit, MIP, actual vs projected turnover, Trends Clause, AICOW
> **Word count**: 400–450 words

##### H3: The Turnover Basis of Settlement — Core Framework
> **Specific content**:
> **The standard BI indemnity formula**:
> Indemnity = (Rate of Gross Profit × Reduction in Turnover during the Indemnity Period) + Additional Increased Cost of Working (subject to the economic limit)
>
> **Gross Profit in BI insurance**: Turnover MINUS Uninsured Working Expenses. Uninsured Working Expenses are the variable costs that cease when the business is interrupted — they must be specifically listed in the policy schedule. Examples: purchases of raw materials, packaging materials, direct labour on piece-work contracts. Uninsured Working Expenses do NOT include: wages of permanent staff, rent, rates, insurance premiums, loan interest — these fixed overheads continue during interruption and are therefore covered as part of the BI indemnity.
>
> **Rate of Gross Profit**: Calculated from the insured's last full accounting year before the loss: Rate of Gross Profit = (Gross Profit ÷ Turnover) × 100. Example: Gross Profit = £600,000; Turnover = £1,500,000. Rate of Gross Profit = 40%.
>
> **Reduction in Turnover**: The difference between the projected turnover (what the business would have achieved but for the loss) and the actual turnover achieved during the interruption period. Example: Projected turnover for 6-month interruption period = £750,000; Actual turnover achieved = £300,000 (partial trading from undamaged area). Reduction in turnover = £450,000. Indemnity (before AICOW) = 40% × £450,000 = £180,000.

##### H3: Trends Clause — Adjusting for Business Trends
> **Specific content**:
> The Trends Clause adjusts the standard turnover figures to reflect any trend or other circumstance affecting the business. Two applications:
>
> 1. **Adjusting the Rate of Gross Profit**: If the business was growing (or declining) in the period before the loss, the Rate of Gross Profit from the last accounting year may overstate (or understate) the rate that would have applied during the interruption period. Example: a growing business increased turnover by 15% year-on-year — the forensic accountant applies a trend adjustment to the Gross Profit calculation to reflect the rate that would have applied in the interruption period, not just the historical rate.
>
> 2. **Adjusting projected turnover**: The projected turnover must reflect what the business would actually have achieved during the interruption period but for the loss — including any trend affecting the business independently of the insured event. Example: if the business was in long-term decline due to market changes unrelated to the fire, projected turnover must be adjusted downward to reflect that trend — the insurer does not indemnify the insured for the decline that would have occurred anyway.
>
> **M85 exam point**: Trends Clause questions in M85 require the candidate to identify the specific trend in the scenario and explain its adjustment direction (upward for growing business, downward for declining) and its effect on the calculated indemnity. A candidate who mentions the Trends Clause without applying it to the scenario facts will not achieve full marks.

##### H3: Maximum Indemnity Period and AICOW
> **Specific content**:
> **Maximum Indemnity Period (MIP)**: The period during which the BI policy provides indemnity — selected by the insured at inception (typically 12, 18, 24, or 36 months). The MIP must be long enough to cover full recovery of turnover to pre-loss levels — for complex manufacturing businesses or highly specialist commercial properties (e.g., a specialist printing works requiring bespoke machinery), 36 months is often the minimum adequate MIP. Underinsurance of the MIP is a frequent cause of BI claim underpayment — if the business has not recovered to pre-loss turnover by the end of the MIP, indemnity ceases at the MIP end regardless of ongoing loss.
>
> **Additional Increased Cost of Working (AICOW)**: Expenditure beyond normal that the insured incurs during the indemnity period to reduce the turnover loss. AICOW is covered only to the extent that it is economic — the insured cannot claim for expenditure that costs more than the BI loss it saves. Example: an insured spends £50,000 on emergency temporary premises and additional transport costs. This expenditure reduces the projected BI loss by £80,000. The AICOW is economic (£50,000 cost saves £80,000 loss) and is recoverable in full. If the expenditure had cost £90,000 to save £80,000, only £80,000 would be recoverable (economic limit applies).

#### H2: Liability Claims — Personal Injury Valuation at Practice Level — Major Query Need: PI valuation methodology in M85
> **EAV to cover**: General damages (JCG), special damages (Ogden Tables), Ogden discount rate, PPOs
> **Word count**: 350–400 words

##### H3: General Damages — Judicial College Guidelines
> **Specific content**:
> **General damages**: Compensation for non-financial losses — pain, suffering, and loss of amenity (PSLA). Assessed by reference to the Judicial College Guidelines (JCG), which provide bracket ranges by injury type and severity. JCG brackets are not binding but are the standard reference in settlement negotiations and litigation.
>
> **Key JCG bracket examples for M85 exam preparation** (candidates should know the general order of magnitude for major injury categories, not memorise precise figures — but should cite the relevant JCG bracket by injury category name):
> - Severe traumatic brain injury (permanent significant disability, requires full-time care): highest bracket in Chapter 1
> - Moderate back injury with full recovery: lower middle bracket
> - Minor whiplash/soft tissue (full recovery within 3 months): lowest bracket
>
> **M85 exam application**: A PI valuation question will present a claimant's injuries and ask the candidate to calculate or estimate general damages. The candidate must: identify the injury category; state the relevant JCG bracket by name (not specific figures from memory — the exam does not require memorisation of exact figures); apply aggravating or mitigating factors (age, pre-existing condition, impact on amenity — all affect where within the bracket the award falls); state the PSLA figure with justification.

##### H3: Special Damages — Ogden Tables and the Discount Rate
> **Specific content**:
> **Special damages**: Financial losses — past losses (loss of earnings from date of injury to trial, medical expenses incurred, care costs incurred) and future losses (future loss of earnings, future care costs, future medical expenses, future transport costs).
>
> **Future loss calculation — Ogden Tables method**:
> Future loss = Annual loss × Ogden multiplier
> The annual loss is the net annual loss of earnings (after tax, National Insurance) or net annual cost of care.
> The Ogden multiplier reflects: the claimant's life expectancy (from actuarial tables by age and gender); the discount rate (the assumed real rate of return on investment of the lump sum by the claimant).
>
> **The discount rate**: Set by the Lord Chancellor under the Damages Act 1996 s.1. Current rate: -0.25% (set August 2019 — prior rate was -0.75%, prior to that +2.5%). A negative discount rate reflects the low-yield investment environment — a claimant investing a lump sum conservatively is expected to earn less than inflation, so the lump sum must be larger to fully compensate future losses. A negative discount rate produces higher multipliers and therefore higher lump sum awards than a positive rate.
>
> **Periodical Payment Orders (PPOs)**: Available for very large personal injury claims with long-term care needs. Under a PPO, the court orders periodic payments instead of a lump sum — typically indexed to ASHE 6115 (the care worker wage index) to protect the claimant against care cost inflation. PPOs remove the investment risk from the claimant but create long-term reserving uncertainty for the insurer — the total cost is unknown at the time of settlement, as it depends on the claimant's actual life expectancy. M85 candidates must understand that PPO reserves must include a life expectancy loading and that periodic payment inflation is linked to ASHE, not RPI or CPI.

#### H2: Fraud Management at Professional Level — Major Query Need: fraud investigation and remedies in M85
> **EAV to cover**: Organised fraud indicators, SIU referral thresholds, insurer remedies (void ab initio, rescission, repudiation), fraud deterrence
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

##### H3: Identifying Organised Fraud at Professional Level
> **Specific content**:
> **Organised fraud indicators**: Staged motor accident (cash for crash) — multiple claimants in one vehicle; all claimants represented by the same firm of solicitors before notification to insurer; CCTV footage shows no contact between vehicles at the alleged accident location; accident occurs at a location identified as a fraud hotspot on the Claims and Underwriting Exchange (CUE) database; vehicle hire and storage charges disproportionate to the vehicle value. Property fraud indicators: serial claimant — insured appears multiple times on CUE for similar losses at different addresses; items claimed are new with no evidence of ownership (receipts, photographs, warranties); loss occurred immediately after policy inception (first 30 days) or before renewal; claims figures presented initially are significantly higher than subsequently evidenced losses.
>
> **SIU referral thresholds**: The insurer's Special Investigations Unit must be instructed when: two or more fraud indicators are present simultaneously; the CUE database shows prior fraud history for the insured or any named claimant; the claim value exceeds the insurer's internal referral threshold combined with at least one indicator; organised ring activity is suspected across multiple claims. The referral must be documented — the claims handler records the specific indicators observed and the basis for the SIU referral.

##### H3: Insurer Remedies for Fraud — Void Ab Initio, Rescission, Repudiation
> **Specific content**:
> **Void ab initio**: The policy is treated as if it never existed — applied where there was fraudulent misrepresentation at inception (the policyholder deliberately provided false information to obtain the policy). No claims are payable under a void policy; the insurer may retain the premium paid. Applicable where inception-stage fraud is proven.
>
> **Rescission**: The policy is set aside from inception due to material non-disclosure or misrepresentation. Under the Insurance Act 2015 (business) and Consumer Insurance Act 2012 (consumer), proportionate remedies apply before avoidance is considered — the insurer must demonstrate that the misrepresentation was deliberate or reckless to avoid without return of premium. Non-deliberate misrepresentation triggers a proportionate remedy (reduce claim, adjust terms), not automatic rescission.
>
> **Repudiation of the claim**: The specific fraudulent claim is denied, but the policy itself continues to cover other (legitimate) claims. Under the common law fraud principle (confirmed in Versloot Dredging BV v HDI Gerling [2016] SC) and the Consumer Insurance Act 2012 s.12: a fraudulent claim allows the insurer to repudiate the entire claim — including any legitimate portion of the same claim — and may also give grounds to avoid the policy going forward. However, Versloot Dredging modified the position on fraudulent devices (exaggerating but not fabricating a claim) — the Supreme Court held that a fraudulent device used to support an otherwise valid claim does not forfeit the claim if the exaggeration was immaterial to the insurer's decision to pay.
>
> **M85 exam application**: A fraud scenario in M85 requires the candidate to identify the indicators, recommend the appropriate remedy (with justification for which remedy is applicable given the specific scenario), and advise on any evidential steps required before the remedy is applied.

#### H2: Claims Reserving Methodology — Major Query Need: reserving in M85
> **EAV to cover**: Case reserve, IBNR, ULAE, claims inflation adjustment — with specific calculation methods
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> **Case reserve**: Set at first notification based on the claims handler's assessment of the likely settlement cost, using the information available at the time. Must reflect: likely liability (probability of claim succeeding multiplied by the maximum exposure); likely quantum (estimated settlement figure including general and special damages for PI claims); legal costs (defendants' and claimants' solicitors); loss adjustment expenses. Case reserves must be reviewed and updated as the claim develops — a reserve that is not updated as new information emerges will become stale and unreliable.
>
> **IBNR — Incurred But Not Reported**: The liability for claims that have occurred within the policy period but have not yet been notified to the insurer. Calculated actuarially using the development triangle method — comparing how claims for previous underwriting years have developed over time (from notification to settlement) to project the ultimate incurred cost for years where claims are still immature. IBNR is most material for: employers' liability (latent disease — mesothelioma claims may arise 30–40 years after the asbestos exposure); professional indemnity (claims-made policies have lower IBNR than occurrence policies because coverage is triggered when the claim is made, not when the error occurred); casualty reinsurance (long development tail on underlying claims).
>
> **ULAE — Unallocated Loss Adjustment Expenses**: The overhead costs of the claims function that cannot be allocated to specific individual claims — claims department salaries, IT systems, management, training. Must be included in the overall claims reserve calculation alongside case reserves and IBNR.
>
> **Claims inflation**: Reserves must be adjusted for expected future inflation in claims costs — particularly: medical treatment cost inflation (often above CPI); care cost inflation (linked to ASHE — wage growth for care workers, which exceeds CPI in most periods); legal cost inflation (solicitors' hourly rates, court fees). Failure to apply adequate claims inflation loading is a systematic source of reserve deficiency.

#### H2: Subrogation Recovery Management Strategy — Major Query Need: recovery strategy in M85
> **EAV to cover**: Decision to pursue recovery, recovery in insured's name, cooperation requirement, proceeds allocation
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Specific content**:
> **Decision to pursue recovery**: Cost-benefit analysis — is the expected recovery (probability of success × likely recovery amount) greater than the legal costs of pursuit? Factors: creditworthiness of the potential defendant (a defendant with no assets or in insolvency produces no recovery even with a judgment); limitation period (3 years from discovery for personal injury; 6 years from damage for contract and property claims — proceedings must be issued before the limitation period expires); availability and quality of evidence (does the insured have documentation to prove the third party's negligence caused the loss?). Where the cost-benefit is marginal, negotiated settlement without litigation may recover a lower amount at lower cost.
>
> **Conducting recovery in the insured's name**: Court proceedings in subrogation are brought in the insured's name — the insured must cooperate fully (provide documents, attend court as witness, give instructions to solicitors on instruction from the insurer). The insured's obligation to cooperate is typically a condition of the policy — breach of the cooperation obligation allows the insurer to cease funding the recovery action.
>
> **Recovery proceeds allocation**: Where recovery is only partial (e.g., defendant is partly at fault or partly solvent), the proceeds are allocated by priority: any uninsured excess or deductible paid by the insured is recovered first; the insurer then recovers its claim payment from the balance. If recovery is insufficient to satisfy both the insured's excess and the insurer's payment, the proceeds are shared pro rata.

> **EAV to cover**: M85-specific written answer technique for complex claims scenarios
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Content**:
> M85 extended answer questions present a complex, multi-issue claim — a large commercial property loss with BI implications and fraud indicators; a catastrophe event with multiple claimants and coverage disputes; a serious personal injury claim with quantum, liability, and PPO considerations. The candidate must manage the claim, assess coverage, appoint the right specialists, and advise on reserve and recovery strategy — all in a structured written answer.
>
> **Short-answer questions (10–15 marks)**: Define the concept and apply it to the scenario. For BI: state the formula, calculate the indemnity using scenario figures, and identify any Trends Clause adjustment required. Do not describe BI claims in general terms — apply the method to the numbers provided.
>
> **Extended answer questions (25 marks)**: Structure for complex claims scenario: (1) Coverage assessment — identify the proximate cause(s); apply Wayne Tank if concurrent causes are present; state coverage conclusion. (2) Specialist appointment — identify which specialist(s) are required; justify the appointment with specific reference to the claim characteristics. (3) Quantum assessment — for BI: calculate the indemnity step-by-step. For PI: identify JCG bracket and calculate Ogden multiplier approach for future losses. (4) Reserve recommendation — state case reserve and flag any IBNR, ULAE, or inflation considerations. (5) Recovery and fraud — identify any fraud indicators; state appropriate remedy; assess subrogation recovery prospects.

(2) Calculating BI indemnity without applying the Trends Clause adjustment when the scenario indicates a business trend. #### H2: Frequently Asked Questions about M85

> FAQ targets representative query variants — not repetition of main content.

**Q1: How hard is M85?**
Answer direction: M85 is one of the more demanding Diploma optional units because it combines technical methodology (BI calculations, PI valuation tools, actuarial reserving) with professional judgment requirements (specialist appointment decisions, fraud investigation strategy, coverage analysis using case law). The most common failure reason is producing generic claims management answers without applying the specific tools required — candidates who cannot calculate a BI indemnity step-by-step or apply the Wayne Tank concurrent causes rule to a scenario will not pass. Practising BI calculations with worked examples and PI valuation using Ogden Tables is essential preparation.

**Q2: How long does M85 take to study?**
Answer direction: Most candidates with a claims background take 90–120 hours across 10–15 weeks. Key study priorities are BI methodology (the full turnover basis calculation including Trends Clause — this is almost always tested), personal injury valuation (Ogden Tables discount rate and JCG brackets — tested in every PI scenario), and fraud remedies (the distinction between void ab initio, rescission, and repudiation is a consistent examiner focus). Candidates without a BI or liability claims background typically need additional preparation time for the calculation-heavy sections.

**Q3: How is M85 different from IF4?**
Answer direction: IF4 (Certificate, Level 3, 15 credits, MCQ) introduces the claims handling process — notification, investigation, settlement, recoveries at a foundational level. M85 (Diploma, Level 4, 20 credits, written exam) requires the candidate to apply professional claims tools — BI methodology with full turnover basis calculation, personal injury valuation using Ogden Tables and JCG, Wayne Tank coverage analysis, SIU fraud referral and insurer remedies, IBNR reserving. IF4 tests awareness; M85 tests competence in applying the tools.

**Q4: What written answer structure does M85 require?**
Answer direction: M85 extended answers must work through a scenario systematically: coverage assessment first (is the loss covered? — proximate cause analysis, conditions precedent check), then quantum (BI calculation or PI valuation as required), then specialist appointment recommendations, then reserve and recovery strategy. Answers that jump to quantum without addressing coverage will lose significant marks. Answers that describe the claims process without applying the methodology to the scenario figures will not demonstrate the professional judgment M85 requires.

**Q5: Is there a pass mark for M85?**
Answer direction: The indicative pass mark for M85 is approximately 55%. The CII uses scaled scoring. Candidates should ensure competence across all major syllabus areas — BI methodology, PI valuation, fraud management, and reserving are all tested. Concentrating only on BI at the expense of PI or fraud topics creates a significant risk of failing to score adequately in those question areas.
