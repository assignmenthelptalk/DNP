- DIL (Difference in Limits): local policy limit is lower than the multinational standard — master policy tops up the difference; critical in countries with compulsory lower limits

- Captive types: single-parent captive (100% owned by one parent organisation); group captive (multiple unrelated organisations pool risk); rent-a-captive (using a cell within another's captive structure for a fee); protected cell company (PCC — legally separate cells within one structure, each cell's assets ring-fenced — domiciles: Guernsey, Isle of Man, Bermuda, Cayman)
- Fronting arrangement: a locally licensed insurer (fronter) issues the policy to comply with local regulation; the fronter cedes substantially all the risk (typically 90–95%) to the captive via a reinsurance arrangement; the fronter charges a fronting fee (0.5–5% of premium) for its regulatory capital and licence exposure
- Captive feasibility threshold: typically minimum £500k–£1m annual premium equivalent to justify formation and operating costs; minimum capital and surplus requirements by domicile (e.g., Bermuda Class 1 captive: minimum capital $120,000)
- Parametric insurance basis risk: the trigger event (e.g., wind speed at a weather station) occurs but the actual loss differs from the index payout — the insured receives a payout that does not match the actual loss; basis risk is the primary objection to parametric solutions and must be addressed in programme design
- FCA IDD remuneration disclosure: brokers must disclose nature of remuneration (fee, brokerage, or combination) to commercial clients on request; conflicts of interest must be documented and managed
- TCFD for brokers: Task Force on Climate-related Financial Disclosures — large FCA-regulated brokers are subject to TCFD reporting requirements; brokers must understand physical and transition climate risk to advise corporate clients

#### H2: What Does 930 Advanced Insurance Broking Cover? — Major Query Need: syllabus
> **Word count**: 350–400 words

##### H3: Multinational Insurance Programme Design
> **Specific content**: A multinational corporation (MNC) operating in 30 countries cannot simply buy a single insurance policy — each country has its own regulatory requirements, compulsory covers, and premium tax rules. The advanced broker must design a programme structure that achieves: (1) compliance with each country's insurance regulations, (2) consistent coverage standards across all territories, (3) premium tax efficiency, (4) clarity of claims recovery procedures. The master policy (issued in the parent's home country) provides the corporate-level cover and the DIC/DIL top-up mechanism. Local policies are issued in each operating country by locally licensed insurers. DIC (Difference in Conditions): where the local policy terms are narrower than the master programme standard — the master pays the difference; e.g., if the local policy excludes flood but the master programme includes it, the master responds under the DIC section for flood losses. DIL (Difference in Limits): where the local policy limit is lower than the master standard — the master pays the difference in limit up to the programme standard. Admitted vs non-admitted: Must-admit countries (Brazil, China, India) require that policies covering risks in those countries are issued by locally licensed insurers — the broker must identify locally licensed partners and manage fronting arrangements.

##### H3: Captive Insurance Management
> **Specific content**: A captive is an insurance company owned by the insured to underwrite the parent organisation's own risks. The advanced broker's role includes: feasibility analysis (assessing whether captive formation is economically justified — minimum premium threshold typically £500k–£1m), domicile selection (Bermuda, Cayman, Guernsey, Isle of Man, Dublin — each with different regulatory capital requirements, supervisory approach, and tax treatment), fronting arrangement design (fronter issues the policy, cedes risk to captive via reinsurance), captive programme optimisation (deciding which risks to retain in the captive vs transfer to market). Types: single-parent captive (fully owned subsidiary — most common); protected cell company (PCC — multiple cells, each ring-fenced from others, cost-efficient for smaller programmes — dominant in Guernsey and Isle of Man).

##### H3: Alternative Risk Transfer
> **Specific content**: Parametric insurance triggers payment on the occurrence of a defined physical parameter (e.g., wind speed exceeding 120mph at a specified weather station) rather than on actual loss. No loss adjustment is required — payment is automatic on trigger confirmation. Basis risk is the key challenge: the trigger event may occur while the insured's actual loss is minimal (false trigger), or the actual loss may be large while the trigger is not met (basis risk gap). The broker's role is to design the trigger to minimise basis risk — selecting the right index, the right geographic reference point, and the right threshold.

#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions about 930

**Q1: How hard is 930?**
Answer direction: 930 is one of the three hardest CII units because it requires genuinely strategic thinking — not just knowledge of broking processes but the ability to evaluate strategic choices, compare programme structures, and apply regulatory requirements to complex scenarios. The written exam penalises candidates who describe how broking works rather than analysing strategic decisions. Candidates with senior client-facing experience find the content more accessible but often need support structuring their analytical written answers.

**Q2: How long does 930 take to study?**
Answer direction: 120–150 hours across 15–20 weeks is a typical preparation timeline for 930. The most common challenge is the shift from practical broking knowledge to written analytical responses — candidates who work in broking often know the content but struggle to articulate it at the required academic level without structured preparation.

**Q3: What is the difference between admitted and non-admitted insurance?**
Answer direction: Admitted insurance means the policy is issued by an insurer locally licensed in the country where the risk is located — required in must-admit jurisdictions (Brazil, China, India, Saudi Arabia). Non-admitted insurance means the policy is issued by an insurer not locally licensed — permitted in many countries but may create regulatory exposure for the insured and the broker. For multinational programmes, correctly identifying which countries require admitted policies is a core broking competency tested in 930.

**Q4: What is DIC/DIL and why does it matter?**
Answer direction: DIC (Difference in Conditions) and DIL (Difference in Limits) are the mechanisms in a multinational master policy that close the gap between local policy terms/limits and the multinational standard. Without DIC/DIL, a local policy with narrower terms or lower limits creates an unintended coverage gap for the multinational insured. The master policy's DIC/DIL section fills those gaps — ensuring the multinational maintains consistent coverage standards globally regardless of local market variations.

**Q5: Do I need 930 to become ACII?**
Answer direction: ACII status requires completing the CII Advanced Diploma in Insurance, which includes the three core units (820, 930, 960) and sufficient optional units. 930 is the designated core unit for the broking route — it is required for candidates pursuing the Advanced Diploma through the broking pathway. Candidates who have already completed M81 at Diploma level will find 930 content builds directly on that foundation at a strategic level.
