#### H2: Group Life Assurance — Death in Service Benefits
> **EAV to cover**: Benefit design, trust structure, expression of wishes, P11D
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Group life assurance (death in service)**: Lump sum paid to the estate or nominees of an employee who dies while in employment. Employer pays the premium; the benefit is paid to beneficiaries.
> - **Benefit basis**: Multiple of salary (e.g., 2× or 4× annual salary) or fixed lump sum. The multiple is set by the employer at scheme design stage. Senior employees may have higher multiples under a tiered scheme structure.
> - **Trust structure**: Group life benefits are typically written in trust — the benefit does not form part of the deceased's estate and is therefore not subject to inheritance tax or probate delay. The trustees (typically employer representatives) have discretion over who receives the benefit.
> - **Expression of wishes**: The employee nominates preferred beneficiaries via an expression of wishes form. This is not legally binding on the trustees but is influential — trustees use it as primary guidance.
> - **Free Cover Limit (FCL)**: The maximum benefit level per member that the insurer will cover without requiring individual medical evidence. Members whose benefits exceed the FCL must provide medical evidence (individual underwriting) before the excess is covered. The FCL is set by the insurer at scheme inception based on scheme size and average benefit.
> - **P11D treatment**: Under HMRC rules, employer-paid group life assurance premiums are generally exempt from P11D (benefit in kind) reporting for employees — they do not create a personal tax liability. Group income protection premiums paid by employers are subject to P11D reporting (taxable benefit).

#### H2: Group Income Protection — Replacing Employee Income During Long-Term Absence
> **EAV to cover**: Deferred period, benefit basis, definition of disability, rehabilitation, benefit integration with SSP
> **Word count**: 350–400 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Group Income Protection (GIP)**: Employer-funded insurance that replaces a proportion of an employee's salary during long-term absence due to illness or injury. It bridges the gap between SSP (28 weeks maximum) and the employee's return to work or retirement.
> - **Benefit basis**: Typically 50–75% of pre-absence salary. The employer may integrate the GIP benefit with other income (e.g., SSP already paid, state benefits) so total income does not exceed a proportion of full salary.
> - **Deferred period**: The waiting period before GIP benefits commence — options typically 13 weeks, 26 weeks, or 52 weeks. The employer's own sick pay scheme and/or SSP covers the deferred period. Longer deferred periods = lower premium cost for the employer.
> - **Definition of disability**: Three common tests:
>   - **Own occupation**: Unable to perform the specific duties of their own job. Most generous to the claimant — most expensive for the insurer.
>   - **Any occupation**: Unable to perform any occupation for which they are reasonably suited by education, training, and experience. Most restrictive — cheapest premium.
>   - **Activities of daily work (ADW)**: Unable to perform a defined list of work-related activities (e.g., walking, lifting, communication). Intermediate definition.
> - **Statutory Sick Pay (SSP) integration**: SSP pays £116.75 per week (2024-25 rate) for up to 28 weeks. Employer may integrate GIP benefit so that it commences at the point SSP expires (if deferred period is aligned to 26 weeks). Employer cannot recover SSP from HMRC under current rules (SSP rebate scheme was abolished).
> - **Rehabilitation services**: Modern GIP insurers provide vocational rehabilitation — physiotherapy, psychological support, occupational health referrals — funded by the insurer to facilitate earlier return to work. Early intervention reduces the overall claim duration and cost.
> - **Benefit cessation**: GIP benefits cease on: return to work, death of the employee, reaching normal retirement age, or end of the policy benefit period.

#### H2: Group Critical Illness — Lump Sum on Diagnosis
> **EAV to cover**: ABI model definitions, core covered conditions, lump sum application
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Group Critical Illness (GCI)**: Pays a lump sum to the employee on diagnosis of a specified serious illness — typically written as a multiple of salary or fixed sum. The employee uses the lump sum as they choose — treatment costs, mortgage clearance, life adjustment.
> - **ABI Model Definitions**: The Association of British Insurers publishes standard condition definitions used across the industry to ensure consistency. The core three conditions by claim volume are: cancer (excluding less advanced cases meeting specified criteria), heart attack (meeting specified ECG and enzyme criteria), and stroke (resulting in permanent neurological deficit lasting a minimum period).
> - **Core and extended conditions**: Standard GCI policies cover 5–7 conditions. Extended policies cover 30–50 conditions. The additional conditions (e.g., multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's, major organ transplant, total permanent disability) vary by insurer.
> - **Survival period**: Most policies require the insured to survive a minimum period (typically 14 or 30 days) post-diagnosis before the benefit is paid.
> - **GCI vs group life**: GCI pays on a living diagnosis event; group life pays on death. They are complementary products in an employer-sponsored benefits package.

#### H2: Employer Statutory Obligations — SSP and Equality Act 2010
> **EAV to cover**: SSP rate and duration, Equality Act disability definition, reasonable adjustments, group risk as complementary
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Statutory Sick Pay (SSP)**: Employers must pay SSP to eligible employees who are too ill to work. Eligibility: average weekly earnings above the Lower Earnings Limit (£123/week 2024-25). Rate: £116.75/week (2024-25). Duration: up to 28 weeks in any linked period of incapacity. After 28 weeks, SSP ceases and the employee may claim Employment and Support Allowance (ESA) from the state.
> - **Equality Act 2010 — Disability Definition**: A person has a disability if they have a physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on their ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities. "Long-term" means it has lasted or is expected to last at least 12 months.
> - **Reasonable Adjustments**: Employers have a legal obligation to make reasonable adjustments for disabled employees — modified working hours, adapted equipment, phased return to work, reallocation of duties. Failure to make reasonable adjustments constitutes disability discrimination.
> - **Group risk as employer obligation support**: GIP benefits support employers in meeting their obligations to disabled employees — funding phased return arrangements, providing rehabilitation services, and ensuring income continuity beyond SSP. In GR1 exam scenarios, the student must understand the interaction between statutory obligations and employer-funded group risk schemes.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Is GR1 the same as the IF unit series?**
GR1 is a separate unit within the CII Certificate framework, specifically designed for the group risk sector. It covers employee benefit schemes rather than general insurance products.

**Q: What is the free cover limit in group risk?**
The free cover limit (FCL) is the maximum benefit per member that the insurer will cover without requiring individual medical evidence. Members needing benefits above the FCL must undergo individual underwriting for the excess amount.

**Q: How does group income protection integrate with SSP?**
SSP pays £116.75/week for up to 28 weeks. GIP has a deferred period (typically 13–52 weeks) before benefits start. Where the deferred period is 26 weeks, the GIP benefit commences shortly after SSP exhausts, providing continuity of income.
