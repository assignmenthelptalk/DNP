#### H2: Demand for Insurance — Economic Analysis
> **EAV to cover**: PED, income elasticity, adverse selection, moral hazard
> **Word count**: 350–400 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)**: Measures the responsiveness of quantity demanded to a change in price. Formula: PED = % change in quantity demanded ÷ % change in price. PED > 1 = elastic (consumers are price-sensitive); PED < 1 = inelastic (consumers are not price-sensitive).
> - **Insurance PED applications**:
>   - **Motor TP (compulsory)**: Highly price-inelastic — consumers cannot opt out of TP insurance (legal requirement). A price increase causes a small reduction in quantity demanded (switching to cheaper insurer rather than abandoning insurance).
>   - **Travel insurance (discretionary)**: More price-elastic — consumers may choose not to buy or to reduce cover level in response to price increases. Demand is sensitive to price.
>   - **Commercial insurance (B2B)**: Elasticity varies by class and size. Large commercial buyers (experienced, price-aware, access to alternatives including captives) are more price-elastic than small businesses.
> - **Income Elasticity**: Insurance demand rises as income rises — insurance is a normal good (income elasticity > 0). As incomes grow, individuals and businesses accumulate more insurable assets and have greater affordability for protection. This is why insurance penetration rises with GDP per capita.
> - **Adverse Selection (information asymmetry)**: Individuals know more about their own risk level than the insurer. Higher-risk individuals have a stronger incentive to seek insurance. If the insurer charges an average premium, it attracts a disproportionate number of high-risk individuals — the pool becomes riskier than average. Solution: risk-based pricing (charging premiums that reflect individual risk profile) and underwriting (screening risks through proposal forms and medical evidence).
> - **Moral Hazard**: Once insured, individuals may take less care to prevent loss — because the insurer bears the financial consequences. Moral hazard is an information problem (the insurer cannot monitor behaviour post-policy inception). Solutions: policy excesses/deductibles (align insured's financial interest with loss prevention), co-insurance (insured bears a proportion of every claim), no-claims discounts (reward claim-free behaviour), exclusions for deliberate or reckless acts.

#### H2: Supply of Insurance — Capacity, Reinsurance, and Regulation
> **EAV to cover**: Insurance capacity definition, reinsurance effect on capacity, Solvency II capital constraints
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Insurance supply (capacity)**: The total amount of risk that the insurance market is willing and able to accept at a given point in time. Capacity is limited by:
>   - **Capital availability**: Insurers must hold sufficient capital (Solvency II SCR) to absorb unexpected losses. When capital is scarce (after large losses or investment downturns), capacity falls.
>   - **Risk appetite**: Insurers may withdraw from unprofitable classes or geographies — reducing supply in those markets.
>   - **Reinsurance availability**: Reinsurance enables primary insurers to write more risk by transferring a portion to reinsurers. When reinsurance capacity falls (post-catastrophe), primary capacity also falls.
> - **Hard market supply**: Following a significant loss event (natural catastrophe, pandemic, large market loss), reinsurers raise prices and restrict terms. Primary insurers face higher reinsurance costs and reduced capacity — they respond by raising premiums and reducing cover scope. Supply falls. Prices rise.
> - **Solvency II and supply**: Capital requirements under Solvency II create a minimum threshold — insurers that cannot meet the SCR after large losses face regulatory intervention, reducing their ability to write new business. Capital requirements constrain supply during periods of market stress.

#### H2: The Insurance Underwriting Cycle
> **EAV to cover**: Soft market characteristics, trigger events, hard market characteristics, recovery phase
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **The underwriting cycle** (also called the insurance cycle or pricing cycle): A recurring pattern of alternating soft and hard market conditions driven by changes in capacity, profitability, and competitive behaviour.
> - **Soft market phase**: Excess capital in the market → insurers compete aggressively for premium income → premiums fall → underwriting standards relax → policies written at inadequate rates → combined ratios rise (loss ratios deteriorate). Characteristics: falling premium rates, broad coverage terms, high insurer capacity, high competition, new entrant growth.
> - **Trigger events**: An event that breaks the soft market cycle — typically one or more of: a large catastrophe loss (hurricane, flood), a significant investment market downturn (reducing capital), or accumulation of large liability losses (latent disease, mass tort).
> - **Hard market phase**: Losses deplete capital → reinsurance capacity falls → primary capacity contracts → premiums rise sharply → underwriting standards tighten → restrictive terms and exclusions applied → combined ratios improve → profitability returns. Characteristics: rising premium rates, restricted coverage, high excess levels, insurer selectivity, reduced capacity, some insurers exit.
> - **Recovery phase**: Profitability in the hard market attracts new capital (new entrants, existing insurer capital raising) → capacity builds again → competitive pressure returns → soft market begins to re-emerge.
> - **Insurance implications**: Understanding the cycle is critical for M92 financial analysis questions and 530 economic analysis questions — exam scenarios may describe a market situation and ask whether it is a soft or hard market, or what event would trigger a transition.
> - **Catastrophe impact**: A single catastrophe event (e.g., Hurricane Katrina 2005, COVID-19 2020) can rapidly convert a soft market to a hard market by removing capacity and increasing reinsurance costs simultaneously.

#### H2: Macroeconomic Factors Affecting Insurance in 530
> **EAV to cover**: GDP, inflation (claims inflation), interest rates (investment return impact), unemployment
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **GDP and insurance demand**: Rising GDP increases economic activity — more vehicles on the road (motor insurance demand), more construction (property insurance demand), more trade (marine, cargo, trade credit demand). Insurance penetration (premiums as % of GDP) is correlated with income levels. Recession reduces demand for discretionary insurance and affects premium affordability.
> - **Inflation — claims inflation**: As construction costs, medical costs, and wage costs rise, the cost of claims rises. Claims inflation in motor bodily injury, household rebuild costs, and liability settlements must be factored into premium rate adequacy. Underestimating claims inflation leads to inadequate pricing and deteriorating loss ratios.
> - **Inflation — reserve adequacy**: For long-tail lines (liability, workers' compensation), claims are settled years after the loss event. Inflation erodes the real value of reserves — insurers must account for future inflation in their IBNR reserves.
> - **Interest rates — investment income**: Insurers invest the premium float. Rising interest rates increase investment income, which can subsidise underwriting losses (explaining why combined ratios above 100% can be sustainable in high-rate environments). Falling interest rates reduce investment income, putting pressure on underwriting profitability.
> - **Interest rates — discounting reserves**: Some accounting standards permit discounting of long-tail reserves at current interest rates. Higher rates reduce the present value of future claims payments, lowering reported reserve levels — potentially masking inadequate reserving.
> - **Unemployment**: Rising unemployment reduces premium affordability (motor, household may be cancelled or downgraded); increases moral hazard in liability policies (employers' liability — laid-off workers may have higher propensity to file occupational injury claims); affects demand for commercial lines (fewer businesses = fewer insurable risks).

#### H2: Business Strategy Applied to Insurance — Porter's Five Forces
> **EAV to cover**: All five forces with insurance-specific examples, economies of scale
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Porter's Five Forces applied to insurance**:
>   1. **Competitive rivalry**: High in personal lines (price comparison websites → commoditisation of motor and household; high insurer churn rates). Moderate in commercial lines (relationship-based; specialist markets less commoditised).
>   2. **Threat of new entrants**: Moderate — capital requirements, regulatory authorisation, and distribution network create barriers. However, InsurTech (digital-first) insurers have lowered entry barriers in personal lines. MGA/coverholder model reduces capital barriers for new specialists.
>   3. **Threat of substitutes**: Self-insurance and captive insurers are the primary substitutes for large corporate buyers. Uninsurance (going without) is a substitute for compulsory insurance only in illegal use cases.
>   4. **Buyer power**: High for large commercial buyers (sophisticated, price-aware, negotiate directly with Lloyd's market; can threaten to move to captive). Low for individual consumers (information asymmetry; switching costs; complexity).
>   5. **Supplier power**: Reinsurers are key suppliers of capacity. In hard market conditions, reinsurer bargaining power increases — they can impose price increases and coverage restrictions. Lloyd's managing agents are suppliers of specialist underwriting capacity to broker/client.
> - **Economies of scale in insurance**: Large insurers benefit from: spreading IT infrastructure costs over a larger premium base; claims processing efficiency (volume discounts with repair networks, medical providers); regulatory compliance costs (fixed cost spread over more business); brand recognition reducing acquisition costs per policy. Explains M&A activity and the consolidation of personal lines market.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Is 530 assessed by MCQ or written exam?**
530 is assessed by written examination — not MCQ. Economic concepts must be explained and applied to insurance scenarios in structured written answers.

**Q: What is the insurance underwriting cycle?**
The underwriting cycle is a recurring pattern where insurance market conditions alternate between soft phases (falling premiums, high capacity, relaxed underwriting) and hard phases (rising premiums, restricted capacity, tight underwriting). Transitions between phases are typically triggered by large loss events, catastrophes, or investment downturns that deplete insurer capital.

**Q: How does inflation affect insurance in the 530 exam?**
530 tests claims inflation (rising costs of claims settlement) and investment income (rising interest rates benefit insurers' investment returns). Students must distinguish between these two separate effects of inflation on insurance profitability and reserve adequacy.

**Q: What is adverse selection and how do insurers address it?**
Adverse selection occurs when higher-risk individuals are more likely to seek insurance than lower-risk individuals, making the insurer's pool riskier than average. Insurers address this through risk-based pricing (charging premiums based on individual risk profile) and underwriting (screening applicants via proposal forms and medical evidence).
