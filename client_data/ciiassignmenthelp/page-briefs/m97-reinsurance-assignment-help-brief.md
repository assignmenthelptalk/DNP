##### H3: Why Reinsurance Exists — The Five Functions
> **Specific content**:
> 1. **Capital protection**: An insurer writing a £50m property risk retains capital against the full exposure. Reinsurance transfers part of that exposure, releasing the capital for other business uses — improving return on equity.
> 2. **Capacity provision**: Without reinsurance, an insurer can only write risks up to its net retention limit. Reinsurance enables writing risks that individually exceed the insurer's capacity — particularly relevant for large commercial, marine hull, or offshore energy risks.
> 3. **Result stabilisation**: A catastrophe year without reinsurance could eliminate an insurer's annual profit or threaten solvency. Reinsurance smooths the loss ratio across years — particularly relevant for cat-exposed classes.
> 4. **Spread of risk**: Reinsurers accept business from cedants across multiple countries and classes — this geographic and class diversification gives the reinsurer a more stable overall book, and the cedant access to a reinsurer willing to accept concentrated exposures.
> 5. **Expertise acquisition**: Specialist reinsurers — particularly in marine, aviation, and offshore energy — bring underwriting expertise and pricing data that the cedant can access through the reinsurance relationship.

##### H3: Proportional Treaty Reinsurance — Quota Share and Surplus
> **Specific content**:
> **Quota share**: The cedant cedes a fixed percentage of every risk in the defined class. A 30% quota share means: the cedant retains 70% of the premium and pays 70% of every loss; the reinsurer receives 30% of the premium and pays 30% of every loss. The ceding commission paid by the reinsurer to the cedant represents the reinsurer's contribution to the cedant's acquisition and management costs. Quota share is simple to administer but inflexible — it cedes the same proportion of profitable small risks as of large ones. Main use: new classes, classes with volatile results, or where the cedant needs broad capacity support.
> **Surplus treaty**: The cedant retains a fixed monetary amount per risk (the "line" — e.g. £500,000). The surplus above that retention, up to a maximum number of lines (e.g. 9 surplus lines = 9 × £500,000 = £4.5m maximum cession per risk), is ceded to the treaty. On a £2m risk with a £500,000 line and 9 surplus lines: cedant retains £500,000 (25%), cedes £1,500,000 (75%) to the surplus. On a £400,000 risk: cedant retains the whole risk (below the line). Surplus is more flexible than quota share — small risks are retained in full; large risks are partially ceded in proportion to their size above the line.

##### H3: Non-Proportional Treaty Reinsurance — Excess of Loss
> **Specific content**:
> **Risk XL (per-risk excess of loss)**: The reinsurer pays losses on any single risk that exceed the cedant's retention up to the reinsurance limit. Structure expressed as "limit xs retention" — e.g. £1m xs £500k means the reinsurer pays the portion of any single-risk loss between £500k and £1.5m. If the loss is £800k, the cedant pays £500k and the reinsurer pays £300k. If the loss is £2m, the cedant pays £500k, the reinsurer pays £1m (exhausting the limit), and the cedant retains the excess above £1.5m unless a second layer applies.
> **Cat XL (catastrophe excess of loss)**: Per-event cover. The reinsurer pays the aggregate of all losses from a single catastrophe event that exceed the cedant's event retention, up to the cat XL limit. Critical concept: an "event" must be defined in the treaty — typically using hours clauses (e.g. 72 hours for windstorm, 168 hours for flood). Reinstatement provisions specify how the limit is reinstated after a loss — usually one automatic reinstatement at 100% or pro-rata additional premium.
> **Aggregate XL**: The reinsurer pays when the cedant's aggregate losses for a defined class across the policy year exceed an annual retention, up to a limit. This protects against an accumulation of attritional losses in a bad year, even if no single loss or event is large. Less common than risk XL or cat XL.
> **Stop loss**: The reinsurer pays when the cedant's combined loss ratio for a class exceeds a defined level — e.g. reinsurer pays losses above a 90% loss ratio up to a 120% loss ratio. Rarely used in practice due to moral hazard concerns; primarily used in agriculture and specialist lines.

##### H3: Burning Cost Method — Worked Example
> **Specific content**: A risk XL reinsurer pricing a £1m xs £500k property layer. Past 5 years of loss data — reinsurer requires at least 5 years, ideally 7–10. Gross losses to the layer: Year 1: £0; Year 2: £650k (recoverable £150k); Year 3: £0; Year 4: £800k (recoverable £300k); Year 5: £0. Total recoverable losses: £450k over 5 years = average £90k per year. Subject premium (total premium income for the class across all years adjusted to current rate level): £500k per year on average. Burning cost = £90k ÷ £500k = 18% burning cost. Apply loading factor (e.g. 1.3 for expenses and profit): rate = 18% × 1.3 = 23.4% of subject premium. Key limitation: 5 years may not capture a major loss — insufficient for cat-exposed classes.

##### H3: Rate on Line and Payback Period
> **Specific content**: Rate on line expresses the reinsurance premium as a percentage of the limit. If the annual reinsurance premium for a £10m cat XL layer is £500k: Rate on line = £500k ÷ £10m × 100 = 5%. Payback period = £10m ÷ £500k = 20 years. The payback period represents the theoretical number of years required for the reinsurer to recover the full limit from premium income. For high-frequency layers, a short payback period (e.g. 3–5 years) reflects the reinsurer's pricing assumption that the layer will be hit regularly. For remote catastrophe layers (return period 1-in-100 years), the payback period may exceed 50 years — reflecting the low probability of attachment.

#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions about M97

**Q1: How hard is M97?**
Answer direction: M97 is widely regarded as one of the most technically demanding optional Diploma units because it requires both conceptual understanding of reinsurance structures and the ability to perform calculations (burning cost, rate on line). The 30-credit weighting means the written exam is longer and more demanding than 20-credit units. Candidates with practical reinsurance experience find the conceptual content accessible but often struggle with structured written answer technique. Candidates without reinsurance backgrounds typically need 120+ hours of study to reach exam standard.

**Q2: How long does M97 take to study?**
Answer direction: The CII recommends approximately 120–150 study hours for a 30-credit unit. M97 candidates typically spread this over 12–20 weeks. The most common feedback from tutors is that candidates underestimate the time needed to master reinsurance pricing mechanics — burning cost calculation, rate on line, and payback period require practice with numerical examples, not just reading.

**Q3: What is the difference between quota share and surplus reinsurance?**
Answer direction: Both are proportional treaties, but the key difference is flexibility in the proportion ceded. In quota share, the cedant cedes the same fixed percentage of every risk in the class — profitable small risks and loss-making large risks alike. In surplus, the cedant retains each risk up to a defined line and only cedes the surplus above that line — so small risks below the line are retained in full, while large risks are ceded proportionally. This makes surplus more suitable for cedants with a wide range of risk sizes, because it avoids ceding premium for small risks the cedant can afford to retain.

**Q4: What written answer structure does M97 require?**
Answer direction: M97 written answers require analytical structure: define the concept precisely, explain the mechanics (ideally with a brief numerical example), evaluate the advantages and limitations in context, and connect to the scenario presented. Purely descriptive answers — "quota share means the cedant and reinsurer share losses" — typically fail to score at the required level. Examiners reward the application of technical knowledge to specific scenarios and the demonstration of judgement, not just knowledge recall.

**Q5: Is M97 relevant if I work in broking rather than underwriting?**
Answer direction: M97 is highly relevant for reinsurance brokers, who are involved in designing and placing reinsurance programmes on behalf of cedants. The unit's content on treaty structure, pricing methodology, and programme design directly applies to reinsurance broking practice. Candidates working for wholesale broking firms or London market specialists often choose M97 alongside M97 as preparation for the 930 Advanced Insurance Broking unit at Advanced Diploma level.
