#### H2: Insurance Contract Formation — Key Elements in M05
> **EAV to cover**: Offer, acceptance, consideration, capacity, legality in the insurance context
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Who makes the offer?**: In insurance contract formation, the INSURED makes the offer (by completing the proposal form). The INSURER accepts or counter-offers (by issuing a quotation or policy). This is the opposite of most commercial transactions — important for M05 exam answers.
> - **Consensus ad idem**: "Meeting of the minds" — both parties must agree on the same terms. In insurance, material non-disclosure or misrepresentation prevents genuine consensus.
> - **Consideration**: The premium paid by the insured; the insurer's promise to indemnify is the insurer's consideration.
> - **Capacity**: Both parties must have legal capacity to contract — insurance companies must be authorised under FSMA 2000; individuals must have legal capacity (age, mental capacity).
> - **Legality**: The subject matter and purpose of the contract must be legal — an insured cannot insure against the consequences of their own criminal act (Gray v Barr 1971 — principle of public policy).

#### H2: Policy Terms Classification — Conditions, Warranties, and Insurance Act 2015 Reform
> **EAV to cover**: Pre-2015 warranty rule, basis of contract clauses, Insurance Act 2015 s.9–s.11 reform
> **Word count**: 350–400 words

##### H3: Conditions Precedent and Conditions Subsequent
> **Specific content**:
> - **Condition precedent**: Must be satisfied before the insured has any rights under the policy. Breach automatically suspends or eliminates the insurer's liability — without any requirement to show prejudice. Examples: prompt notification of a claim within a defined period, submission of a proof of loss form, obtaining the insurer's consent before admitting liability.
> - **Condition subsequent**: An ongoing obligation during the policy period. Breach does NOT automatically void the policy. It may give the insurer a right to terminate or limit liability, but only if the breach causes prejudice. Examples: maintaining agreed security measures, maintaining the insured property in good repair.
> - **Distinction is critical**: M05 exam scenarios frequently test whether a specific clause is a condition precedent or subsequent — and the legal consequences of breach of each type.

##### H3: Warranties — Pre and Post Insurance Act 2015
> **Specific content**:
> - **Pre-Insurance Act 2015 warranty rule**: Any warranty breach — however trivial, however unrelated to the loss — automatically and permanently discharged the insurer from liability from the date of breach onwards (Dawsons v Bonnin [1922] AC 413). The basis of contract clause converted all proposal form answers into warranties — so an inaccurate answer about a trivial matter could void the entire policy.
> - **Insurance Act 2015 s.9 — Abolition of basis of contract clauses**: For business insurance, basis of contract clauses are abolished — proposal form answers are no longer automatically warranties. Applies to contracts entered into on or after 12 August 2016.
> - **Insurance Act 2015 s.10 — Warranties as suspensive conditions**: A warranty breach no longer permanently discharges insurer liability. Instead, insurer liability is SUSPENDED during the period of the breach. If the breach is remedied, insurer liability is reinstated. If the loss occurs when the warranty is not breached, the insurer must still pay.
> - **Insurance Act 2015 s.11 — Limitation on reliance on terms**: Insurers cannot rely on a term to exclude or limit liability for a loss if the term could not have increased the risk of the loss that actually occurred.

#### H2: Insurance Act 2015 — Fair Presentation and Proportionate Remedies
> **EAV to cover**: s.3 fair presentation duty, s.4–7 proportionate remedies for breach
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **s.3 — Duty of Fair Presentation (business policyholders)**: The insured must disclose every material circumstance which the insured knows or ought to know, or give the insurer sufficient information to put a prudent insurer on notice that it needs to make further enquiry. Presentation must be in a reasonably clear and accessible manner (not "information dump").
> - **Knowledge**: The insured "knows" what its senior management knows, and what would be revealed by reasonable search of information held by the insured's organisation.
> - **s.4–7 — Proportionate Remedies for Breach of Fair Presentation**:
>   - If breach is deliberate or reckless: insurer may AVOID the contract, retain the premium, and refuse all claims.
>   - If breach is not deliberate or reckless, the remedy depends on what the insurer would have done if presented with the true information:
>     - If the insurer would not have written the risk at all: the insurer may avoid the contract (with return of premium).
>     - If the insurer would have written on different terms (other than premium): the contract is treated as if those terms applied from inception.
>     - If the insurer would have charged a higher premium: the insurer reduces the claim settlement proportionately (by the ratio of premium actually charged to the premium that would have been charged).
> - **Consumer insurance**: The Consumer Insurance (Disclosure and Representations) Act 2012 applies to individual consumers (not business). Similar proportionate remedy structure but aligned to consumer context.

#### H2: Subrogation and Contribution at Diploma Depth
> **EAV to cover**: Subrogation timing, Lord Napier, cooperation duty; contribution methods
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Subrogation**:
>   - Only arises after the insurer has paid full indemnity — not before.
>   - Insurer steps into the insured's legal shoes to pursue the third party tortfeasor in the insured's name.
>   - Insured must cooperate — failure to cooperate may be a condition precedent breach.
>   - Lord Napier and Ettrick v Hunter [1993]: established that in reinsurance, the reinsurer's subrogation rights arise against the original insured's recovery from the third party — the insured cannot keep the recovery if the reinsurer has paid the claim.
>   - Haynes v Harwood [1935]: Subrogation is available even where the third party tortfeasor is another insured under the same policy (e.g., employer and employee — however, s.151 RTA may modify this in motor contexts).
> - **Contribution**:
>   - Arises when two or more policies cover the same risk, same insured, same peril, and the same interest.
>   - **Independent liability method**: Each insurer pays the proportion of the claim that represents its liability as a proportion of the total independent liabilities of all insurers (most commonly used in the UK).
>   - **Maximum liability method**: Each insurer pays a proportion based on its policy limit as a proportion of total limits. Used in some marine and commercial contexts.

#### H2: M05 Written Exam Answer Technique — Diploma Level
> **EAV to cover**: Structured answer format, statute citation, case law citation, analytical depth
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Content**:
> - M05 is assessed by written exam — answers must demonstrate knowledge, analysis, and application. MCQ technique does NOT transfer to this format.
> - **Short-answer questions (10–15 marks)**: Typically ask you to define a concept, explain a statutory provision, or compare two legal positions. Answer in clear paragraphs — definition, explanation, example/case authority, application.
> - **Extended answer questions (25 marks)**: Present a scenario and ask you to advise the parties. Structure: identify the legal issue, state the applicable rule (statute/case law), apply the rule to the scenario facts, state the conclusion.
> - **Statute citation**: Always cite by section — "Under Insurance Act 2015 s.3..." not "Under the Insurance Act..." Vague citations lose marks.
> - **Case law citation**: Include case name and year — e.g., "In Dawsons v Bonnin [1922], the House of Lords held that..." Stating the principle without the case loses marks at Diploma level.
> - **Common M05 exam failures**: (1) Describing the pre-2015 warranty rule without stating the post-2015 reform. (2) Confusing conditions precedent (breach = automatic discharge) with conditions subsequent (breach may give termination right, not automatic void). (3) Applying subrogation before indemnity has been paid.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Is M05 assessed by MCQ or written exam?**
M05 is assessed by written examination — short-answer and extended answer questions. It is NOT assessed by MCQ. This is a key distinction from Certificate-level IF units. Written answer technique, statute citation, and case law application are all required.

**Q: What is the difference between a condition precedent and a warranty in M05?**
A condition precedent must be satisfied before the insured has any rights under the policy — breach automatically eliminates insurer liability. A warranty (post-Insurance Act 2015) is now a suspensive condition — insurer liability is suspended during the breach but reinstated if the breach is remedied. Pre-2015, a warranty breach permanently discharged insurer liability regardless of materiality.

**Q: Does M05 cover the Consumer Insurance Act 2012 or only the Insurance Act 2015?**
M05 covers both. The Consumer Insurance Act 2012 applies to consumer (individual) policyholders. The Insurance Act 2015 applies to business (commercial) policyholders. The exam requires students to know which Act applies based on the scenario's policyholder type.

**Q: How many credits is M05 and what level is it?**
M05 is a 25-credit Level 4 unit — significantly more demanding than Certificate-level (15-credit Level 3) units. It requires written exam technique, not MCQ technique.
