CII insurance qualification assignments are formally assessed components of the Chartered Insurance Institute qualification framework — a Regulated Qualifications Framework (RQF) structure spanning Certificate in Insurance (Level 3), Diploma in Insurance (Level 4–6), and Advanced Diploma in Insurance (Level 6–7). Each qualification requires students to pass individually assessed units, taking the form of MCQ scenario-based examinations at Certificate level or written examinations and structured coursework at Diploma and Advanced Diploma level. This service provides expert CII assignment help across all three qualification tiers, for every unit, for UK and international students.

### What Is a CII Insurance Qualification Assignment?

<!-- INFOGRAPHIC: cii-qualification-levels-comparison-infographic.svg | Type 3 Side-by-Side | Subject: CII Insurance Qualification Assessment | Place immediately after this H3, before prose -->
<!-- figcaption: CII qualification levels compared — Certificate in Insurance (Level 3), Diploma in Insurance (Level 4–6), and Advanced Diploma in Insurance (Level 6–7) — assessment formats, pass marks, and marking standards. -->

A CII insurance qualification assignment requires a student to demonstrate applied professional knowledge within a defined assessment format, under the marking standards set by the Chartered Insurance Institute. The precise format — and therefore the nature of the challenge — differs between qualification levels.

At Certificate in Insurance level, assessments are MCQ scenario-based examinations: each question presents a realistic professional insurance situation and the student must identify the correct professional action, policy interpretation, or legal position from four answer options. The pass mark is 65%. Rote memorisation of insurance definitions does not meet this standard — the examination requires applied judgment in a live professional context.

At Diploma in Insurance and Advanced Diploma in Insurance level, assessments are written examinations and structured coursework. The pass mark is 50%. Written assessments are marked independently on four criteria: **Knowledge** (accurate technical content from the unit syllabus), **Analysis** (reasoned argument that applies knowledge to the specific question or scenario — not restatement of theory), **Structure** (logical sequencing with a clear introduction, developed argument, and conclusion), and **Examples** (relevant case law citations, regulatory references, or verified industry examples that support the analytical points). Most students who fail CII written assessments do so not because their Knowledge is insufficient, but because their Analysis is underdeveloped and their Examples are too general to score marks.

Unit enrolment windows are 12–18 months from registration. A student who misses their scheduled assessment sitting must re-enrol and pay the unit registration fee again.

#### MCQ Scenario-Based Exams — Certificate in Insurance Assessment Format

Certificate in Insurance units (IF1–IF11, GR1, LM3, R05, and international variants) are each assessed by a single MCQ scenario-based examination worth 15 credits at RQF Level 3. Each question in the examination presents a described professional insurance scenario — a claims notification, a policy dispute, a regulatory obligation, a coverage interpretation — and the student must select the correct professional response from four options (A–D).

This format tests applied professional judgment, not factual recall. A student who has read the IF3 syllabus and memorised the definition of an insurance warranty will not automatically know which of four scenario outcomes correctly reflects the effect of an Insurance Act 2015 section 10 breach under the given fact pattern. The scenarios require the student to reason from principle to outcome — and to do so under examination conditions, within the time limit, without reference materials.

Each IF unit covers a discrete professional practice area. IF3 (Insurance Underwriting Process) requires scenario-level application of risk assessment, proposal evaluation, policy terms construction, and the effect of material non-disclosure. IF4 (Claims Handling Process) requires scenario application of the FNOL process, coverage determination, settlement calculation, subrogation timing, and contribution between co-insurers. IF5 (Motor Insurance) requires knowledge of Road Traffic Act 1988 obligations, cover type distinctions, MIB scheme conditions, and NCD mechanics — applied to fact patterns. Students cannot pass these units by understanding the topics in the abstract. The scenario must be read correctly, the correct rule must be identified, and the correct outcome must be selected.

#### Written Examinations and Coursework — Diploma and Advanced Diploma Assessment Format

<!-- INFOGRAPHIC: cii-diploma-marking-criteria-infographic.svg | Type 1 Icon Grid 2×2 | Subject: CII Diploma Written Assessment Marking Criteria | Place immediately after this H4, before prose -->
<!-- figcaption: The four independently weighted marking criteria for CII Diploma and Advanced Diploma written assessments — Knowledge, Analysis, Structure, and Examples — with Analysis the most commonly failed criterion. -->

Diploma in Insurance and Advanced Diploma in Insurance units are assessed by written examination or structured coursework, with a pass mark of 50%. The four marking criteria — Knowledge, Analysis, Structure, and Examples — are applied separately and weighted independently.

**Knowledge** requires accurate technical content drawn from the unit syllabus. For M05 Insurance Law and Practice, this means accurate statements of insurance law principles and the statutory provisions that govern them. For 530 Economics and Business, this means correct application of economic theory (demand elasticity, the underwriting cycle, macroeconomic factors) to the insurance market.

**Analysis** requires the student to reason from the knowledge to a conclusion — to identify what the rule means for the specific scenario, what its implications are, and why one outcome follows from the applicable principle rather than another. Answers that describe insurance law without analysing its application to the given facts score adequately on Knowledge but fail on Analysis. This is the most commonly failed criterion at Diploma and Advanced Diploma level.

**Structure** requires a logical argument sequence: a clear introduction that defines the legal or commercial question, a developed body that addresses each element in turn, and a conclusion that states the position with reasons. Answers that begin directly with technical content without framing the question, or that end without a stated conclusion, lose marks on Structure.

**Examples** require specific, verifiable citations: named case law with year, named regulatory provisions by reference, or named industry practice examples. "Relevant case law supports this position" is not an Example. "In *Castellain v Preston* [1883], the Court of Appeal held that subrogation arises only after the insurer has paid the full amount of the insured's loss" is an Example that scores marks.

### CII Certificate in Insurance — Assignment Types and Units

Each unit within the CII Certificate in Insurance covers a distinct professional insurance discipline and is assessed by a scenario-based MCQ examination worth 15 credits at RQF Level 3. The Certificate requires accumulation of units to achieve the award — students may combine IF series units, specialist units (GR1, LM3, R05), and approved international variant units (WCE, WCA, WUE, WUA, W04, IMU, IMP) to build their qualification.

The IF unit series covers the core disciplines of general insurance practice: insurance law and regulation, the general insurance business environment, the underwriting process, claims handling, and a range of product-specific areas (motor, household, healthcare, commercial packages, broking). The full unit set available within the Certificate includes:

- **IF1** — Insurance: Legal and Regulatory — the six principles of insurance law (insurable interest, utmost good faith, indemnity, subrogation, contribution, proximate cause), FCA dual regulatory structure, ICOBS conduct of business rules, Insurance Distribution Directive CPD obligations
- **IF2** — General Insurance Business — the structure of the insurance market, underwriting cycle, policy documentation sequence, roles of market participants
- **IF3** — Insurance Underwriting Process — risk assessment methodology, proposal form evaluation, Insurance Act 2015 s.10 warranty provisions, rating factor application, policy terms construction
- **IF4** — Insurance Claims Handling — FNOL process, coverage determination, loss investigation, settlement calculation, subrogation right and timing, contribution between co-insurers, fraud indicators
- **IF5** — Motor Insurance — Road Traffic Act 1988 s.143 and s.151 obligations, TPO/TPFT/Comprehensive cover distinctions, MIB Untraced and Uninsured Drivers Agreements, Motor Insurance Database, No Claims Discount scale, fleet and telematics underwriting, total loss valuation
- **IF6** — Household Insurance — buildings reinstatement basis and average clause, contents new-for-old vs indemnity, standard perils with triggering conditions, non-standard construction and location underwriting, flood zone and subsidence risk
- **IF7** — Healthcare Insurance — private medical insurance (PMI) structure, group and individual schemes, moratorium vs full medical underwriting, hospital cash plans, dental insurance
- **IF8** — Packaged Commercial Insurances — combined commercial policies, public liability, employers' liability, commercial property, business interruption
- **IF9** — Customer Service in Insurance — Treating Customers Fairly (TCF) six outcomes, complaints handling process, vulnerable customer obligations, FOS referral rights
- **IF10** — Insurance Broking Fundamentals — FCA authorisation, ICOBS demands and needs, suitability, remuneration disclosure (ICOBS 4.6), broker's legal position as agent of client
- **IF11** — Risk and Insurance — pure vs speculative risk, hazard/peril/risk definitions, risk management process (IIMM), risk financing options (transfer, avoidance, reduction, retention), insurable risk criteria
- **GR1** — Group Risk — death in service, group income protection (deferred period, disability definition, SSP integration), group critical illness (ABI model definitions), employer statutory obligations under Equality Act 2010
- **LM3** — London Market Broker — Lloyd's Act 1982, Lloyd's market structure (syndicates, managing agents, Lloyd's brokers, Funds at Lloyd's), slip-based placing process, lead and following market subscription, binding authority and coverholders
- **R05** — Financial Protection — life assurance types (level/decreasing/increasing term, whole of life), income protection (deferred period, own/any occupation definitions), critical illness (ABI model definitions, survival period), trust arrangements and IHT implications

#### IF Units — Core Certificate in Insurance Modules

IF1 through IF11 each address a distinct professional insurance discipline, and all are assessed by the same MCQ scenario-based format at 15 credits, RQF Level 3. The scenario format is consistent across units — but the subject matter domain, the applicable rules, and the professional context shift entirely between units.

IF3 (Insurance Underwriting Process) is not simply about "underwriting in general." It tests scenario-level application of the risk assessment process — how a physical hazard affects a risk rating, how a material non-disclosure should be treated under Insurance Act 2015 s.10, how a warranty breach suspends (not discharges) insurer liability under the post-2015 reform, and how underwriting authority limits determine when a risk must be referred. IF4 (Claims Handling Process) tests the FNOL process step-by-step: what the insurer must do on receipt of a claim, how coverage is determined, when a loss adjuster must be appointed, how subrogation timing works (the insured must receive full indemnity before subrogation rights arise), and when contribution between co-insurers applies. Students seeking CII assignment help for specific IF units require support that goes to this level of applied specificity — not general guidance on insurance topics.

IF1 (Insurance: Legal and Regulatory) is the foundational legal unit — it tests the six principles of insurance law. These six principles appear on the IF1 examination in scenario format: a given fact pattern requires identification of which principle applies (insurable interest — *Macaura v Northern Assurance* [1925]: a shareholder has no insurable interest in company assets held in the company's name), what its legal basis is, and what the correct professional response is. IF5 (Motor Insurance) tests the RTA 1988 compulsory cover obligation — s.143 (the legal duty to insure) and s.151 (the insurer's obligation to satisfy judgements against insured parties even where the policy might otherwise be avoided). The NCD scale, MIB scheme conditions, and total loss categorisation (CAT A/B/S/N) are all examined at this applied level.

#### Specialist and International Certificate Units

GR1 (Group Risk Insurance) is designed for professionals in employee benefits, group risk broking, and HR roles. It covers employer-sponsored group risk schemes: group life assurance (death in service — trust structures, free cover limits, expression of wishes), group income protection (deferred period options of 13, 26, or 52 weeks; own occupation vs any occupation vs activities of daily work disability definitions; integration with Statutory Sick Pay at £116.75 per week for up to 28 weeks), and group critical illness (ABI model definitions — cancer, heart attack, and stroke as the three highest-volume claim conditions; survival period requirements). Employer statutory obligations under the Equality Act 2010 (the disability definition, the 12-month threshold for "long-term" impairment, and the reasonable adjustments duty) are specifically tested.

LM3 (London Market Broker) is designed for London market and Lloyd's professionals. It covers Lloyd's market structure — the Society of Lloyd's under the Lloyd's Act 1982, syndicates and managing agents, Lloyd's brokers (distinct from standard FCA-authorised brokers — only Lloyd's-accredited firms can place directly at Lloyd's), Funds at Lloyd's (FAL — the capital deposited by syndicate members), and the Lloyd's Central Fund (the mutual reserve that supports all Lloyd's policies). The slip-based placing process is examined at the level of a London market practitioner: the broker prepares a risk presentation slip, the lead underwriter sets terms and signs, following underwriters subscribe their proportionate shares, and the LPSO processes the completed slip.

The international variant units (WCE/WCA, WUE/WUA, W04) cover the same core content as their IF series equivalents — but they replace UK-specific statute references with general good faith and disclosure principles applicable across non-UK insurance jurisdictions. IMU (India Motor Underwriting) and IMP (India Motor Practice) are distinct from UK IF5 Motor Insurance — they are assessed under the IRDAI regulatory framework, reflecting Indian motor insurance law, the Motor Vehicles Act 1988 (India), IRDAI detariffication of Own Damage rates (2007), and Indian motor claims processes including the Surveyor and Loss Assessor requirement. Students in India, GCC markets, or other non-UK jurisdictions sitting these units need support that reflects the correct regulatory context — not UK regulatory content transposed to their scenario.

### CII Diploma in Insurance — Core and Optional Module Support

Three compulsory core units form the foundation of the CII Diploma in Insurance — M05 Insurance Law and Practice, M92 Insurance Business and Finance, and 530 Economics and Business. All three must be passed before the Diploma award is conferred. These core units are assessed by written examination at a materially higher level of analytical demand than Certificate-level MCQ units — they require structured argument, case law citation, and demonstration of professional-level reasoning across all four marking criteria.

**M05 Insurance Law and Practice** carries 25 credits at RQF Level 4. It demands precise application of English contract law principles — the six elements of contract formation, the rules on misrepresentation and non-disclosure (Consumer Insurance Act 2012 for consumers; Insurance Act 2015 for business policyholders), classification of policy terms (conditions precedent vs conditions subsequent, and the transformation of warranties by Insurance Act 2015 s.9–s.11 from absolute discharge triggers into suspensive conditions), and the remedies for breach at each level of culpability.

**M92 Insurance Business and Finance** carries 25 credits at RQF Level 4. It demands understanding of how insurance companies operate financially — premium setting (pure premium formula), financial performance metrics (loss ratio = claims paid and outstanding ÷ earned premiums; expense ratio; combined ratio = loss ratio + expense ratio, where below 100% signals underwriting profit), reinsurance structures (proportional: quota share and surplus; non-proportional: excess of loss per risk, catastrophe XL, and aggregate XL), Solvency II's three-pillar framework (Pillar 1: quantitative capital requirements — SCR and MCR; Pillar 2: governance and ORSA; Pillar 3: disclosure), and insurance market distribution channels.

**530 Economics and Business** carries 30 credits at RQF Level 6. It applies microeconomic and macroeconomic theory to the insurance market — demand for insurance (price elasticity of demand, income elasticity, adverse selection, moral hazard), supply of insurance (capacity, reinsurance availability, Solvency II capital constraints), the insurance underwriting cycle (soft market → trigger event → hard market → recovery), macroeconomic factors (GDP growth, claims inflation, interest rate effects on investment income and reserve discounting), and business strategy applied to insurance (Porter's Five Forces — buyer power, supplier power, threat of substitutes including captives and self-insurance, threat of new entrants including InsurTech, and competitive rivalry including price comparison website commoditisation of personal lines).

Students seeking CII assignment help for Diploma units must receive support that operates at the level of the actual marking criteria — not general explanations of insurance concepts. Analysis of specific M05 scenarios requires identification of the applicable doctrine, citation of the relevant statutory provision (by section number), citation of the relevant case (by name and year), application of the rule to the scenario facts, and a stated conclusion.

#### M05 Insurance Law and Practice — The Most Examined Diploma Unit

<!-- INFOGRAPHIC: m05-key-cases-infographic.svg | Type 6 Comparison Table | Subject: M05 Insurance Law — Five Key Cases | Place immediately after this H4, before prose -->
<!-- figcaption: The five cases most frequently examined in M05 Insurance Law and Practice — each establishing a distinct doctrine required for the Examples marking criterion. -->

M05 Insurance Law and Practice demands the most intensive use of named case law of any unit in the CII Diploma. Five cases in particular are cited frequently across M05 assessments and must be understood at the level of the doctrine each establishes — not merely as names:

- ***Castellain v Preston* [1883]** — Establishes the subrogation doctrine: after an insurer pays a claim in full, the insurer acquires the right to stand in the insured's place and recover from any third party responsible for the loss. The insured cannot profit — if the insured has already recovered from the third party, the insurer can reclaim the amount it paid.
- ***Macaura v Northern Assurance* [1925]** — Establishes the insurable interest requirement: a shareholder does not have an insurable interest in the assets owned by the company in which they hold shares, even if they are the sole shareholder. Insurable interest must be a legally recognised financial interest in the subject matter itself, not in the entity that owns it.
- ***Leyland Shipping v Norwich Union Fire Insurance Society* [1918]** — Establishes the proximate cause doctrine: where a loss results from a chain of causes, the cause that is proximate in efficiency (not necessarily proximate in time) determines whether the loss falls within the scope of the insured peril.
- ***Carter v Boehm* [1766]** — Lord Mansfield's foundational statement of the duty of utmost good faith (uberrimae fidei): both parties to an insurance contract must disclose all material facts that would affect the other's decision — the insurer knows the general probability of risk; the insured knows the specific circumstances.
- ***Pan Atlantic Insurance v Pine Top Insurance* [1994]** — Establishes the materiality test for disclosure: a fact is material if a prudent underwriter would have wished to know it before deciding whether to accept the risk and on what terms. It does not need to have been decisive — it merely needs to have been relevant to the prudent underwriter's assessment.

Each of these cases establishes a distinct doctrine. M05 exam scenarios test whether students can correctly identify which doctrine applies, cite the authority, and reason from it to a conclusion about the scenario outcome.

### CII Advanced Diploma in Insurance — Strategic Assignment Support

Unlike the Diploma in Insurance, the Advanced Diploma in Insurance requires candidates to demonstrate strategic management capability at RQF Level 6 and Level 7 — not practice-level operational description. The three compulsory core units at Level 6 (820 Advanced Claims Management, 930 Advanced Insurance Broking, and 960 Advanced Underwriting) require the student to reason and argue as a senior insurance professional operating at portfolio, strategy, or director level. The Level 7 optional units (991, 993, 994) carry 50 credits each — the single highest credit weighting of any unit in the entire CII qualification framework — and require sustained strategic analysis and argument at executive or board level.

Students who complete the Advanced Diploma in Insurance and meet the CII's Chartered status requirements become eligible for the ACII (Associate of the Chartered Insurance Institute) designation — the recognised professional credential in the UK and international insurance market for experienced practitioners.

The shared core between the Diploma and Advanced Diploma (M05, M92, and 530) must be passed before the Advanced Diploma can be awarded. Students who have not yet completed these Diploma core units require Diploma-level support before progressing to the Advanced Diploma core units.

#### Core Units 820, 930 and 960 — Advanced Claims, Broking and Underwriting

**820 Advanced Claims Management** (30 credits, Level 6) requires strategic management of complex claims portfolios — not operational claims handling. Assignments at this level require argument at the level of a claims director: how does a portfolio of large loss claims affect the organisation's combined ratio and reinsurance recovery position? What litigation strategy is appropriate for a coverage dispute claim exceeding delegated authority thresholds? How should a major property loss involving multiple insurers, reinsurers, and a loss adjuster be coordinated at strategic level? Written assignments for 820 must demonstrate that the student understands claims management as a strategic and financial function of an insurance organisation — not as a process to be described step by step.

**930 Advanced Insurance Broking** (30 credits, Level 6) requires senior professional reasoning about corporate client broking — placement strategy decisions, market access choices, regulatory compliance architecture at a senior level, and corporate client relationship management. Assignments must reflect strategic broking judgment: why does a corporate client with a captive arrangement require a different placement approach than a corporate client in the open market? How should a complex multiline corporate programme be structured across Lloyd's and the company market? What FCA compliance obligations apply at senior intermediary level for a large commercial book?

**960 Advanced Underwriting** (30 credits, Level 6) requires portfolio underwriting strategy — the design of an underwriting risk appetite framework, the construction of a reinsurance programme that aligns with that framework, pricing model design at the class of business level, and strategic decision-making about which risks to pursue, which to price selectively, and which to decline. Assignments must argue at the level of an underwriting director: what does the combined ratio performance of a commercial property book indicate about the adequacy of the current pricing model, and what strategic changes are required?

All three of units 820, 930, and 960 demand strategic reasoning and evidence of senior-level professional judgment. CII assignment help for these units must be positioned as expert strategic analysis support and analytical review — not exam technique coaching.

M05 Insurance Law and Practice is the most frequently requested CII Diploma unit for assignment help — because its assessment intensity is the highest of the three core units. Every M05 written answer that scores on the Examples criterion requires specific case law citation. The most commonly examined M05 cases are *Castellain v Preston* [1883] (subrogation — the insurer's right to recover from the third party after paying the claim in full), *Macaura v Northern Assurance* [1925] (insurable interest — a shareholder has no insurable interest in company assets), and *Leyland Shipping v Norwich Union* [1918] (proximate cause — the cause proximate in efficiency, not in time, determines the insured peril). Knowing these cases at this level of doctrinal specificity is what the M05 Examples criterion requires — and what distinguishes an assignment that passes from one that does not.

For M92 Insurance Business and Finance, help covers combined ratio calculation (loss ratio + expense ratio, with interpretation of what a ratio above or below 100% means for underwriting profitability), reinsurance structure analysis (quota share and surplus proportional treaties; excess of loss per risk and catastrophe XL non-proportional treaties), and Solvency II three-pillar framework application (SCR and MCR under Pillar 1; ORSA under Pillar 2; SFCR disclosure under Pillar 3).

For 530 Economics and Business, help covers the insurance underwriting cycle phase analysis, PED application to different insurance classes, adverse selection and moral hazard with insurer solutions, and Porter's Five Forces applied specifically to the insurance market.

#### India Market — IMU, IMP and IRDAI-Aligned Units

IMU (India Motor Underwriting) and IMP (India Motor Practice) are CII Certificate-level units specifically designed for the Indian insurance market. These are not variants of UK IF5 (Motor Insurance) — they are distinct units assessed under the IRDAI (Insurance Regulatory and Development Authority of India) regulatory framework, reflecting the specific legal and market structure that governs motor insurance in India.

IMU and IMP cover: the Motor Vehicles Act 1988 (India) s.146 compulsory third-party cover obligation; IRDAI's regulatory role including the detariffication of Own Damage rates (liberalised from April 2007 while third-party rates remain IRDAI-controlled); the Insured Declared Value (IDV) calculation using the IRDAI depreciation schedule; the No Claim Bonus (NCB) scale applicable in India (20% after 1 claim-free year through to 50% after 5+ years — distinct from the UK NCD scale); IRDAI's 2018 mandate requiring 5-year long-term third-party policies for new private cars; the Solatium Fund as India's equivalent of the UK MIB Untraced Drivers Agreement; and Indian motor claims processes including the requirement for a licensed Surveyor and Loss Assessor above specified claim thresholds.

Students in India seeking CII assignment help for IMU and IMP need support that operates within the IRDAI regulatory framework — not UK RTA 1988 content applied to an Indian scenario.

#### Arabic-Speaking Students and Takaful Unit 590

Unit 590 (Takaful Insurance) is the most commonly requested CII unit among Arabic-speaking students in Gulf Cooperation Council (GCC) markets — Saudi Arabia, UAE, Kuwait, Bahrain, Qatar, and Oman. It is available with an Arabic-language examination variant, making it both linguistically accessible and professionally relevant for insurance practitioners in markets where Takaful is a primary insurance structure.

Takaful operates on Shariah-compliant principles that are structurally distinct from conventional insurance indemnity: **tabarru** (the mutual contribution model — participants donate to a mutual pool from which losses are paid, removing the element of monetary exchange between insurer and insured that raises issues under Islamic finance principles); **wakalah** (the agency model — the Takaful operator acts as agent of the participants and charges a management fee for operating the pool); and **mudarabah** (the profit-sharing model — the operator manages the investments of the pool and takes a share of the surplus). Different Takaful markets in the GCC use different model combinations — wakalah-dominant (common in UAE, Saudi Arabia), mudarabah-dominant, and hybrid structures.

Assignment help for unit 590 requires genuine understanding of these models — not surface awareness that Takaful is "Islamic insurance." Arabic-language tutoring support is available for 590 WUA and 590 Arabic variant students.

### Frequently Asked Questions

**What is the difference between a CII written examination and a CII MCQ scenario-based exam?**

CII MCQ scenario-based examinations are used at Certificate in Insurance level. Each question presents a realistic professional insurance scenario — a claims dispute, an underwriting decision, a regulatory obligation — and the student must select the correct professional outcome from four answer options. The pass mark is 65%. The examination tests applied professional judgment: the ability to identify the correct rule and apply it to a described professional situation. It does not test recall of definitions in isolation.

CII written examinations are used at Diploma in Insurance and Advanced Diploma in Insurance level. The student writes structured answers to questions or analyses scenarios, marked independently on four criteria: Knowledge, Analysis, Structure, and Examples. The pass mark is 50%. The two formats require fundamentally different preparation — scenario-based MCQ requires practised applied interpretation; written examinations require structured argument construction supported by case law and regulatory citation. CII assignment help for each format is calibrated to these different demands.

**What do CII Diploma examiners look for in a written assignment answer?**

Diploma written assignments are marked on four independently weighted criteria. **Knowledge** requires accurate technical content from the unit syllabus — correct statements of insurance law, regulatory provisions, financial concepts, or economic models. **Analysis** requires the student to reason from that knowledge to a conclusion about the specific question or scenario — not to restate theory, but to apply it and explain what it means for the situation described. **Structure** requires logical sequencing: a clear introduction that frames the question, a developed body that addresses each element in order, and a conclusion that states the student's position with supporting reasons. **Examples** require specific, verifiable citations — named case law with year and outcome (*Macaura v Northern Assurance* [1925]: shareholder has no insurable interest in company assets), named regulatory provisions by reference (Insurance Act 2015 s.3: duty of fair presentation), or named industry practice examples. Diploma answers that demonstrate adequate Knowledge but underdeveloped Analysis and insufficient Examples consistently fail — because Analysis and Examples carry substantial independent marking weight. CII assignment help for Diploma units is specifically designed to address this pattern.
