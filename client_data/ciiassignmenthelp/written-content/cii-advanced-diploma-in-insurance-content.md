## MAIN CONTENT

### What Is the CII Advanced Diploma in Insurance?

The CII Advanced Diploma in Insurance is the apex qualification of the Chartered Insurance Institute's general insurance pathway — awarded at RQF Level 6 for its core and most optional units, and at RQF Level 7 for the three extended optional units 991, 993, and 994. The Chartered Insurance Institute confers the ACII (Associate of the Chartered Insurance Institute) designation upon completion of the Advanced Diploma — the professional post-nominal that distinguishes holders as having achieved the highest standard of recognised insurance qualification in the UK market.

The Advanced Diploma requires completion of three mandatory core units at Level 6 — 820 Advanced Claims Management (30 credits), 930 Advanced Insurance Broking (30 credits), and 960 Advanced Underwriting (30 credits) — alongside the three shared core units from the Diploma in Insurance: M05 Insurance Law and Practice (25 credits), M92 Insurance Business and Finance (25 credits), and 530 Economics and Business (30 credits). Students who completed M05, M92, and 530 at Diploma level have these credits recognised toward Advanced Diploma requirements and proceed directly to 820, 930, and 960.

The most consequential characteristic of the Advanced Diploma is its assessment character. Advanced Diploma written examinations demand strategic and senior-level analysis — examiners expect evidence of leadership-level thinking, portfolio-level decision-making, and market-level strategic reasoning. A student who answers 820 by describing how a claims handler processes a claim will fail; the examiner requires analysis of why a claims reserving strategy creates a Solvency II capital exposure and what portfolio-level response is appropriate. For students who need support across the full qualification hierarchy, [CII assignment help across all qualification levels](/cii-assignment-help) covers every level from Certificate through Advanced Diploma.

#### CII Advanced Diploma vs CII Diploma — The Strategic Threshold

The CII Diploma in Insurance at RQF Level 4 tests legal reasoning, financial analysis, and economic theory application — operational and technical knowledge within defined subject areas. The CII Advanced Diploma at RQF Level 6 and Level 7 tests strategic portfolio-level decision-making — the ability to analyse why operational outcomes at scale create strategic risks and what senior-level responses are appropriate. Where a Diploma student explains the legal elements of an insurance contract (M05), an Advanced Diploma student analyses why a pattern of coverage disputes across a claims portfolio indicates a systemic policy wording risk and recommends a board-level risk management response. Operational insurance experience is necessary context for Advanced Diploma exams, but does not substitute for the strategic analytical framework those exams require.

#### ACII Designation — What It Confers and Why It Matters

ACII — Associate of the Chartered Insurance Institute — is the professional post-nominal conferred by the Chartered Insurance Institute upon completion of the Advanced Diploma in Insurance. Holders use "ACII" after their name in professional communications (e.g., Jane Smith ACII). The ACII designation is recognised by UK insurers, Lloyd's market participants, FCA-regulated insurance distribution firms, and major composite insurers as the benchmark for senior professional qualification in general insurance. Many Lloyd's managing agents and major composite insurers specify ACII as a minimum qualification for underwriting authority above defined premium thresholds, for appointment as a senior claims handler, and for FCA-registered functions at senior management level. The ACII post-nominal enables progression to Fellowship of the Chartered Insurance Institute (FCII) — the Chartered Insurer designation that represents the highest level of CII professional recognition.

### The Three Core Units of the CII Advanced Diploma — 820, 930, and 960

Three core units define the CII Advanced Diploma in Insurance: 820 Advanced Claims Management, 930 Advanced Insurance Broking, and 960 Advanced Underwriting. Each carries 30 credits at RQF Level 6 and is assessed through written examination requiring strategic analysis of the subject area's most complex decision-making scenarios — not a description of operational procedures.

#### 820 — Advanced Claims Management (30 Credits, RQF Level 6)

820 Advanced Claims Management carries 30 credits at RQF Level 6 and requires strategic analysis of claims function management at senior level — not description of individual claims processes.

The strategic subject areas 820 tests include claims reserving strategy — specifically the management of IBNR (Incurred But Not Reported) reserves, IBNER (Incurred But Not Enough Reserved) adjustments, and case reserve adequacy in the context of the insurer's Solvency II capital position. Large loss management and subrogation at portfolio level, counter-fraud strategy and investigation frameworks, rehabilitation case management for long-tail liability claims, and claims portfolio performance metrics — combined ratio contribution, claims frequency versus severity trend analysis — all form the examined content. The written examination requires students to analyse how claims decision-making at scale affects the insurer's financial position, Solvency II SCR (Solvency Capital Requirement) exposure, and strategic reserve adequacy.

The distinction between 820 and Diploma claims content is one of analytical type, not merely difficulty. Diploma claims units (M85, M86) test coverage decisions and claims settlement principles at the operational level; 820 tests why those decisions create portfolio-level financial consequences and what board-level strategy is appropriate in response. [820 Advanced Claims Management assignment help](/820-assignment-help) provides targeted strategic framing for this unit's written examination.

#### 930 — Advanced Insurance Broking (30 Credits, RQF Level 6)

930 Advanced Insurance Broking carries 30 credits at RQF Level 6 and demands senior-level broking strategy analysis — covering London Market placement, FCA regulatory obligations at senior level, and complex insurance programme design.

The strategic subject areas 930 assesses include senior broking strategy for complex commercial accounts and multinational risks, London Market placement strategy across Lloyd's syndicates and company market carriers, FCA regulatory obligations at senior broking level — specifically ICOBS (Insurance Conduct of Business Sourcebook) compliance, IDD (Insurance Distribution Directive) obligations, and conflicts of interest management at firm level — client risk assessment at insurance programme level, design of bespoke insurance programmes for complex risks including captive structures and parametric products, and reinsurance market access strategy for large broker accounts.

The written examination requires evidence that the student understands why specific placement strategies are appropriate for defined risk characteristics — not a description of how a policy is placed. ICOBS and IDD are regulatory entity-value pairs that signal Level 6 broking knowledge depth; students who cannot reference these frameworks by name operate below the examiner's expectation for 930. [930 Advanced Insurance Broking assignment help](/930-assignment-help) covers the regulatory and strategic content specific to Level 6 broking examination.

#### 960 — Advanced Underwriting (30 Credits, RQF Level 6)

960 Advanced Underwriting carries 30 credits at RQF Level 6 and requires strategic underwriting portfolio analysis — operating at the level of portfolio management and board-level risk appetite, not individual risk assessment.

The strategic subject areas 960 tests include underwriting portfolio management — target market definition, portfolio segmentation by risk class, profitability analysis at portfolio level — pricing strategy development including technical pricing methodology, commercial pricing decisions relative to risk appetite, and rate adequacy monitoring. Risk appetite framework design and board-level communication of underwriting strategy, treaty reinsurance strategy selection (quota share, surplus, excess of loss, and the strategic rationale for each), underwriting cycle management and strategic response to soft and hard market conditions, catastrophe exposure management including PML (Probable Maximum Loss) and EML (Estimated Maximum Loss) modelling principles, and the Solvency II SCR (Solvency Capital Requirement) implications of underwriting portfolio decisions all feature in the written examination.

SCR, PML, and EML are the technical entity-value pairs that distinguish 960 from Diploma-level underwriting content. Students who answer 960 scenarios using Diploma-level risk assessment logic — evaluating individual risks rather than portfolio strategy — fail to demonstrate the strategic analytical framework the Level 6 examiner requires. ### How Does the CII Advanced Diploma Require Strategic Rather Than Operational Knowledge?

Advanced Diploma written examinations require a fundamentally different type of answer than Diploma examinations — and the distinction is specific enough to describe concretely.

A Diploma student writing about claims reserving explains how a claims reserve is calculated: the estimated ultimate cost of a claim, set based on known facts and likely development. An Advanced Diploma student writing about the same subject analyses why a systemic pattern of under-reserving across the motor liability claims portfolio creates a Solvency II SCR exposure, how the actuarial IBNR calculation methodology affects the insurer's reported profit, and what board-level strategic response — reserve strengthening schedule, reinsurance programme adjustment, portfolio repricing — is appropriate given the insurer's current capital position.

Advanced Diploma written exam scenarios present an insurer, broker, or underwriter at senior level facing a strategic decision: "The underwriting director is reviewing the commercial property portfolio. The combined ratio has deteriorated from 96% to 112% over two consecutive years. Advise on the strategic options." The expected response demonstrates awareness of trade-offs, market context, financial implications, and regulatory constraints — not a list of what the underwriting director could do, but a structured analysis of why specific strategic options are appropriate given the described portfolio performance data.

Assessment criteria at Level 6 reward quality of strategic reasoning, evidence of senior professional perspective, accuracy of market and regulatory context (Solvency II, FCA framework, Lloyd's market structure), and structured presentation of recommendations. Operational insurance experience provides the context to make strategic arguments credible — but it does not generate the analytical framework the examiner expects. For students making this transition, [CII Diploma in Insurance written exam support](/cii-diploma-in-insurance-assignment-help) provides context on the Diploma-level knowledge that feeds into Advanced Diploma strategic analysis.

### Level 6 and Level 7 Optional Units in the CII Advanced Diploma

Beyond the three mandatory core units, the CII Advanced Diploma includes Level 6 optional units at 30 credits and Level 7 optional units at 50 credits — a credit weight almost double that of the core units, reflecting the research depth these qualifications demand.

Level 6 optional units, assessed through timed written examination, include 590 (Introduction to Lloyd's Market, providing advanced-level Lloyd's-specific market knowledge for practitioners in the Lloyd's environment) and 990 (Risk Management at advanced level, covering enterprise risk management frameworks and risk governance in an insurance entity context).

The three Level 7 optional units represent the highest qualification level within the entire CII framework:

**991 Strategic Risk Management (50 credits, RQF Level 7)** is assessed through research-based written submission — not timed examination. The unit requires engagement with enterprise risk management frameworks including ISO 31000 (the international standard for risk management principles, framework, and process) and the COSO ERM Framework (Committee of Sponsoring Organizations Enterprise Risk Management), ORSA (Own Risk and Solvency Assessment) requirements under the Solvency II Directive, board-level risk governance structures, and regulatory risk reporting obligations for Solvency II regulated entities. The research submission demands independent research, referenced strategic analysis, and policy recommendations at board level. [991 Strategic Risk Management assignment help](/991-assignment-help) provides research submission support at ISO 31000 and ORSA depth.

**993 Advanced Reinsurance (50 credits, RQF Level 7)** is assessed through research-based written submission covering global reinsurance market structures, catastrophe bond and ILS (Insurance-Linked Securities) mechanisms, retrocession strategies, facultative and treaty reinsurance pricing cycle dynamics, PML modelling in catastrophe reinsurance contexts, and alternative risk transfer mechanisms. The research submission format requires engagement with current reinsurance market developments at a depth that timed examination cannot assess. [993 assignment help](/993-assignment-help) provides specialist reinsurance market research support.

**994 Advanced Financial Planning in Insurance (50 credits, RQF Level 7)** is assessed through research-based written submission addressing strategic financial planning within an insurance entity — capital allocation strategy, investment policy design within the Solvency II framework, M&A analysis in insurance contexts, and strategic financial decision-making at board level. [994 assignment help](/994-assignment-help) covers the financial strategy content at the depth Level 7 research submissions require.

#### Choosing Between Level 6 and Level 7 Advanced Diploma Optional Units

Level 6 optional units — 30 credits, timed written examination — are appropriate for students building advanced specialism within a defined area of the insurance market. Level 7 optional units — 50 credits, research-based submission — are appropriate for students targeting the FCII Fellowship pathway or senior management and C-suite insurance careers. Selecting a Level 7 unit signals a commitment to qualification depth beyond the ACII threshold; it is not a requirement for ACII designation itself, but provides significantly greater credit weight toward the qualification's total credit requirement and demonstrates the highest available standard of CII qualification engagement.

### The Shared Core Units in the CII Advanced Diploma — M05, M92, and 530

M05 Insurance Law and Practice (25 credits), M92 Insurance Business and Finance (25 credits), and 530 Economics and Business (30 credits) are required components of the CII Advanced Diploma in Insurance — the same three mandatory core units that define the CII Diploma in Insurance.

The Chartered Insurance Institute recognises these units for students who completed them at Diploma level. Students who hold M05, M92, and 530 from their Diploma in Insurance do not resit them for the Advanced Diploma — the credits carry forward and those units are treated as satisfied. Students enrolling directly for the Advanced Diploma without prior Diploma completion must complete M05, M92, and 530 as part of their Advanced Diploma programme before or alongside the core Advanced Diploma units (820, 930, 960).

At Advanced Diploma level, the legal reasoning established by M05 provides the contractual and regulatory foundation for arguments required in 820 (claims coverage decisions), 930 (FCA regulatory compliance obligations), and 960 (policy terms and underwriting conditions). The financial analysis framework from M92 — combined ratio, loss ratio, Solvency II capital — applies directly in 820 and 960 written examinations. The macroeconomic reasoning from 530 provides the market context for 930 and 960 strategic analysis.

### From CII Diploma to Advanced Diploma — The Qualification Step-Up

The transition from CII Diploma in Insurance to CII Advanced Diploma involves three specific differences that determine how students must approach preparation.

First, the Diploma tests legal analysis, financial analysis, and economic theory at the operational and technical level; the Advanced Diploma tests strategic portfolio-level decision-making, where legal and financial knowledge is the assumed foundation — not the primary assessed output. Second, Diploma optional units operate at Level 4; Advanced Diploma optional units operate at Level 6 or Level 7, with Level 7 units assessed through research-based submissions rather than timed written examination. Third, the Advanced Diploma confers the ACII designation upon completion — a professional outcome that the Diploma alone does not provide.

Students who hold the Diploma can proceed to Advanced Diploma core units (820, 930, 960) with M05, M92, and 530 credits already recognised. ### Career Seniority and the CII Advanced Diploma — ACII in Practice

The ACII designation qualifies holders for senior professional roles in the UK general insurance market where the Chartered Insurance Institute's highest insurance qualification standard is required or expected. Senior underwriters managing commercial lines accounts with significant premium volumes at Lloyd's syndicates, company market insurers, and specialist insurers frequently hold ACII as a minimum qualification for their underwriting authority level. Heads of claims at major composite insurers specify ACII for managers overseeing complex claims portfolios. Broking directors at FCA-regulated insurance intermediaries in the commercial and London markets increasingly require ACII for client-facing senior roles. Risk managers at Solvency II regulated insurance entities, insurance company directors registered with the FCA as approved persons, and Lloyd's syndicate underwriters operating above defined underwriting capacity thresholds commonly hold ACII. Some Lloyd's managing agents specify ACII as a prerequisite for underwriting stamp authority above a defined gross written premium threshold — a concrete professional requirement that distinguishes ACII from a general career aspiration.

### Beyond ACII — The Chartered Insurer (FCII) Pathway

ACII holders can progress to Fellowship of the Chartered Insurance Institute (FCII) — the highest designation within the CII professional framework. Fellowship confers the title "Chartered Insurer," recognised as the peak standard of professional attainment in UK general insurance. The FCII pathway requires ACII holders to meet the CII's Fellowship criteria — demonstrating senior-level professional contribution, continuing professional development at the highest level, and commitment to the CII's ethics and professional standards. The Chartered Insurer designation is held by senior insurance professionals at board level, by insurance company directors, and by leading practitioners in specialist insurance markets. Students who complete the Advanced Diploma and achieve ACII designation are positioned to pursue Fellowship as the subsequent career-qualification milestone.

**Do I need to re-sit M05, M92, and 530 if I already completed them for the CII Diploma?**
No — M05 Insurance Law and Practice (25 credits), M92 Insurance Business and Finance (25 credits), and 530 Economics and Business (30 credits) completed as part of the CII Diploma in Insurance are recognised as credit toward the CII Advanced Diploma requirements. Students who hold these units from their Diploma proceed directly to the Advanced Diploma core units — 820, 930, and 960. **What is the difference between a Level 6 optional unit and a Level 7 unit in the Advanced Diploma?**
Level 6 optional units — such as 590 (Lloyd's Market) and 990 (Risk Management) — carry 30 credits and are assessed through timed written examinations in the same format as core units 820, 930, and 960. Level 7 optional units — 991 (Strategic Risk Management), 993 (Advanced Reinsurance), and 994 (Advanced Financial Planning in Insurance) — carry 50 credits each and are assessed through research-based written submissions rather than timed examinations. Research submissions require independent research, referenced strategic analysis, and policy-level recommendations. Level 7 units represent the highest qualification standard within the CII framework, and their research submission format requires a different preparation approach from timed written examination support.
