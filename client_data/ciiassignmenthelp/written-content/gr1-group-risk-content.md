## Group Life Assurance — Death in Service Benefits

Group life assurance is the employer-sponsored protection product that pays a lump sum to the nominees or estate of an employee who dies while employed. The GR1 exam tests the structure of the benefit, the trust mechanism through which it is administered, the free cover limit concept, and the tax treatment — all areas where precise definitions are required.

### Benefit Design

Group life assurance (death in service) benefit is typically expressed as a multiple of the employee's annual salary — for example, 2× or 4× salary. The employer sets the multiple at scheme design stage. Schemes may use a tiered structure where senior employees receive a higher multiple: 4× salary for directors, 2× salary for all other employees, for instance. Alternatively, a scheme may pay a fixed lump sum rather than a salary multiple — common where the employer wishes to provide equal benefit regardless of salary level. The employer pays the entire premium; the benefit is payable to the beneficiaries upon the employee's death.

### Trust Structure

Group life benefits are written in trust. This means the benefit does not form part of the deceased employee's estate and is therefore not subject to inheritance tax or probate delay. The trustees — typically employer representatives — hold the benefit on trust for the beneficiaries. The trustees have discretion over who receives the benefit. A trust structure speeds up payment to dependants at the time of bereavement and protects the benefit from the deceased's estate creditors.

### Expression of Wishes

The employee nominates preferred beneficiaries via an expression of wishes form — specifying who they wish to receive the benefit and in what proportions. This document is not legally binding on the trustees. It is, however, the primary guidance the trustees use when exercising their discretion. Trustees will depart from an expression of wishes only where there are strong reasons to do so — for example, where circumstances have changed since it was completed (such as a divorce not reflected in an outdated nomination). LM3 exam scenarios frequently test whether the expression of wishes is binding — it is not. The trustees have final discretion.

### Free Cover Limit (FCL)

The Free Cover Limit is the maximum benefit per member that the insurer will cover without requiring individual evidence of health. Members whose benefit entitlement falls at or below the FCL are covered automatically — no medical underwriting is needed. Members whose benefit entitlement exceeds the FCL must provide individual medical evidence (full medical underwriting) for the excess amount before that excess is covered under the scheme. The FCL is set by the insurer at scheme inception, based on the scheme's total membership size and average benefit level. A larger scheme with a higher average benefit typically attracts a higher FCL. The FCL concept is directly linked to the efficiency of group risk schemes — it allows large numbers of employees to be brought into cover without individual health assessment.

### P11D Tax Treatment

Under HMRC rules, employer-paid group life assurance premiums are generally exempt from P11D (benefit in kind) reporting for employees — they do not create a personal income tax liability for the employee. This is because group life benefits are paid through a discretionary trust and not directly to the employee. Group income protection premiums paid by employers, however, are subject to P11D reporting — they constitute a taxable benefit in kind for the employee. The GR1 exam tests this distinction: group life = generally P11D exempt; group income protection = P11D reportable for the employee as a taxable benefit.

## Group Income Protection — Replacing Employee Income During Long-Term Absence

Group income protection (GIP) is employer-funded insurance that replaces a proportion of an employee's salary during a long-term absence caused by illness or injury. It provides continuity of income beyond the statutory sick pay period and beyond the employer's own occupational sick pay scheme.

### Benefit Basis

GIP benefits are typically set at 50–75% of the employee's pre-absence salary. The benefit is not designed to replace full salary — a proportion of income replacement preserves the financial incentive to return to work. The employer may integrate the GIP benefit with other income the employee receives during absence — for example, state benefits or any residual employer sick pay — so that total income during absence does not exceed an agreed percentage of working salary. Integration ensures the scheme remains cost-effective and aligned with its purpose of income replacement rather than income enhancement.

### Deferred Period

The deferred period is the waiting period that must expire before GIP benefits commence. Standard options are 13 weeks, 26 weeks, and 52 weeks. The employer's occupational sick pay scheme and/or Statutory Sick Pay (SSP) covers the employee during the deferred period. The deferred period chosen has a direct impact on premium cost: a 52-week deferred period costs significantly less than a 13-week deferred period, because the insurer is exposed to fewer and shorter claims. Employers with a generous occupational sick pay scheme may choose a longer deferred period to align GIP commencement with the point at which their own sick pay entitlement runs out.

### Definition of Disability

The definition of disability used in the policy determines when the GIP benefit triggers. Three definitions are commonly used:

| Definition | Description | Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Own occupation | Unable to perform the specific duties of the employee's own job | Most generous to the claimant — highest premium cost |
| Any occupation | Unable to perform any occupation for which they are suited by education, training, and experience | Most restrictive — lowest premium cost |
| Activities of daily work (ADW) | Unable to perform a defined list of functional work activities (e.g., walking, lifting, communication) | Intermediate position |

The own occupation definition is the most commonly used in employer-sponsored GIP schemes — it is aligned to the employee's actual role, making it the most straightforward to administer. The any occupation definition creates disputes in practice because the insurer may argue the claimant is capable of a different type of work even if they cannot return to their previous role.

### Statutory Sick Pay Integration

Statutory Sick Pay pays £116.75 per week (2024-25 rate) for a maximum of 28 weeks to eligible employees. Eligibility requires average weekly earnings above the Lower Earnings Limit of £123 per week (2024-25). Where the GIP deferred period is set at 26 weeks, GIP benefits commence shortly after SSP exhausts — providing continuity of income without a gap. A 52-week deferred period, however, creates a gap between SSP exhaustion at week 28 and GIP commencement at week 52 — the employee would need to claim Employment and Support Allowance (ESA) from the state during that gap. Employers designing a scheme must understand this SSP/GIP interaction to avoid gaps in employee income protection.

### Rehabilitation Services

Modern GIP insurers provide vocational rehabilitation services — physiotherapy, psychological support (particularly relevant for mental health-related absences), occupational health referrals, and workplace adjustment advice — funded by the insurer and triggered at an early stage of absence. Early rehabilitation reduces total claim duration, which reduces the insurer's claim cost. Insurer-funded rehabilitation is now considered a standard feature of GIP policies and is a GR1 syllabus area — students should understand that rehabilitation is insurer-initiated, insurer-funded, and motivated by claim cost management as well as employee welfare.

### Benefit Cessation

GIP benefits cease on: the employee's return to work, the employee's death, the employee reaching normal retirement age under the scheme, or the expiry of the policy's maximum benefit period. Some policies cap benefit payment to a defined period (e.g., 5 years) rather than running to retirement — the GR1 exam may test the conditions under which GIP benefits stop.

## Group Critical Illness — Lump Sum on Diagnosis

Group critical illness (GCI) pays a lump sum to the employee on diagnosis of a specified serious illness. Unlike group income protection, which replaces ongoing income, GCI provides a one-time payment that the employee uses as they choose — for treatment costs, mortgage clearance, lifestyle adjustments, or family support.

### ABI Model Definitions

The Association of British Insurers (ABI) publishes standardised condition definitions that insurers use across the industry to ensure consistency. The ABI Model Definitions specify exactly what clinical criteria must be met before a condition qualifies for a claim. The three core conditions by claim volume are:

- **Cancer**: Malignant tumour characterised by uncontrolled growth and spread of malignant cells, meeting specified staging criteria — less advanced cancers are excluded under the model definition.
- **Heart attack**: Death of heart muscle due to coronary artery blockage — must be evidenced by specific ECG changes and cardiac enzyme elevation meeting stated thresholds.
- **Stroke**: Cerebrovascular event causing permanent neurological deficit lasting a minimum specified period — transient ischaemic attacks (TIAs) that resolve fully are excluded.

Standard GCI policies cover 5–7 conditions. Extended GCI policies cover 30–50 conditions, adding diagnoses such as multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's disease, major organ transplant, total permanent disability, and loss of limbs or sight.

### Survival Period

Most group critical illness policies include a survival period — typically 14 or 30 days from the date of diagnosis — that the employee must survive before the benefit is paid. This prevents the policy from paying a simultaneous death benefit and critical illness benefit in cases where death follows immediately from the diagnosed condition.

### GCI vs Group Life

GCI and group life are complementary products. Group life pays on death; GCI pays on a living diagnosis event — surviving a serious illness. An employer's benefits package that includes both products provides dual protection: a living benefit to help the employee manage the consequences of a serious illness while alive, and a death benefit to protect the employee's family.

## Employer Statutory Obligations — SSP and Equality Act 2010

The GR1 exam does not treat employer statutory obligations as background context. It tests them as substantive content — specifically, the interaction between the statutory sick pay baseline and employer-funded group risk schemes, and the Equality Act 2010 disability provisions that shape how employers must treat employees with long-term health conditions.

### Statutory Sick Pay

Employers must pay SSP to eligible employees who cannot work due to illness. The eligibility threshold is average weekly earnings above the Lower Earnings Limit (£123 per week, 2024-25). SSP pays £116.75 per week (2024-25 rate) for a maximum of 28 weeks in any linked period of incapacity. When SSP exhausts at week 28, the employer's SSP obligation ends. The employee may then apply to the state for Employment and Support Allowance (ESA). Employers cannot recover SSP payments from HMRC — the SSP rebate scheme was abolished, and SSP is a direct employer cost.

### Equality Act 2010 — Disability Definition

Under the Equality Act 2010, a person has a disability if they have a physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on their ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities. The "long-term" threshold is met where the impairment has lasted, or is expected to last, at least 12 months. The "substantial" threshold means more than minor or trivial. Both thresholds must be satisfied.

### Reasonable Adjustments

Employers have a legal obligation under the Equality Act 2010 to make reasonable adjustments for employees with disabilities. Reasonable adjustments may include: modified working hours, provision of adapted equipment, phased return to work following an absence, reallocation of specific duties that the employee cannot perform, or provision of a dedicated workspace. Failure to make reasonable adjustments constitutes disability discrimination — the employer is not required to remove all disadvantage, but must take steps that are reasonable in the circumstances.

### Group Risk as Employer Obligation Support

GIP benefits support employers in meeting their Equality Act obligations in practice. Insurer-funded rehabilitation services under a GIP policy can fund the phased return arrangements and occupational health assessments that reasonable adjustment compliance requires. GR1 exam scenarios regularly present situations where an employee's long-term absence intersects with Equality Act obligations — the student must identify both the employer's statutory position and the role of the GIP policy in supporting compliance.

## Scheme Design — Free Cover Limit, Membership, and Tax Treatment

How do scheme design decisions affect the cost, accessibility, and tax position of a group risk programme? GR1 exam scenarios test the student's ability to apply scheme design principles to employer situations.

### Actively at Work Clause

Group risk schemes typically include an "actively at work" clause — a member must be actively at work (at work and capable of performing all duties) on the date of scheme commencement, or on the date they join the scheme, to be covered from that date. An employee who is absent due to illness on the scheme commencement date will not be covered under the new scheme until they return to active employment. This prevents adverse selection — employers cannot enrol sick employees into a new scheme at inception to access immediate claim benefits.

### Membership Criteria

The employer sets the eligibility criteria for scheme membership: typically all employees working a minimum number of hours per week after a qualifying period (e.g., three months' employment). Some schemes restrict membership by category — permanent employees only, or excluding workers on fixed-term contracts of less than one year. The GR1 exam tests how membership criteria interact with the FCL and with individual underwriting requirements.

### Tax Treatment Summary

| Product | Employer Premium — P11D? | Employee Benefit — Tax? |
|---|---|---|
| Group life assurance | Generally exempt — not a P11D benefit | Benefit paid under discretionary trust — generally not subject to inheritance tax |
| Group income protection | P11D reportable — taxable benefit for employee | GIP payments received by employee are taxable income — treated as employment income |
| Group critical illness | P11D reportable in most cases | Lump sum received — broadly no income tax but specific advice required |

The P11D distinction between group life (generally exempt) and group income protection (taxable benefit) is directly tested in GR1 exam scenarios. Students must not apply a blanket rule — the treatment differs by product.

## How to Pass the GR1 MCQ Exam — Exam Technique

GR1 scenarios present employer benefit design situations or individual employee claim scenarios. The student must identify the correct benefit outcome, apply the statutory baseline, or determine the regulatory position. The following patterns appear consistently.

**Confusing own occupation with any occupation:** The disability definition directly determines whether a specific employee qualifies for GIP benefit. A scenario presenting an employee who cannot do their previous job but could theoretically do lighter work will trigger differently under own occupation (entitled to benefit) versus any occupation (possibly not entitled). Read the definition stated in the scenario before reaching a conclusion.

**Assuming GIP benefits begin at the point SSP ends:** GIP has a deferred period that must expire before benefits commence. If the deferred period is 52 weeks and SSP runs for 28 weeks, there is a 24-week gap during which neither SSP nor GIP is payable from employer or insurer — the employee must claim state ESA. GR1 exam scenarios frequently test what happens during this gap.

**Treating expression of wishes as legally binding:** The expression of wishes is influential guidance to the trustees, not a binding legal direction. The trustees retain full discretion — they can pay the benefit to a different person or in different proportions if circumstances justify it. An expression of wishes completed before a divorce but not updated does not automatically bind the trustees to pay the ex-spouse.

**Assuming group life premiums are P11D reportable:** Group life assurance premiums paid by the employer are generally exempt from P11D — they do not generate a personal income tax liability for the employee. Group income protection premiums are P11D reportable and create a taxable benefit. This distinction is tested directly.

**SSP calculation:** Know the weekly rate (£116.75 for 2024-25) and the 28-week maximum duration. Scenarios may calculate the total SSP entitlement for a specific absence period, or test what happens when SSP exhausts before GIP commences.

## Frequently Asked Questions — GR1

**Q: Is GR1 the same as the IF unit series?**
GR1 is a separate unit within the CII Certificate framework, specifically designed for the group risk and employee benefits sector. It does not cover the general insurance products addressed in the IF series (motor, household, commercial). GR1 covers employer-sponsored group risk schemes — group life, group income protection, and group critical illness — assessed in the context of the employer-employee relationship and statutory obligations. Students in employee benefits roles take GR1 rather than the standard IF units because GR1 directly addresses their professional context.

**Q: What is the free cover limit in group risk?**
The free cover limit (FCL) is the maximum benefit per member that the insurer will cover without requiring individual medical evidence. Members whose benefit entitlement is at or below the FCL are automatically covered — no health questionnaire or medical examination is required. Members whose benefit exceeds the FCL must undergo individual underwriting for the excess amount. The FCL is set by the insurer based on the size of the scheme and the average benefit level. Larger schemes typically attract higher FCLs because the insurer's risk is spread across more lives.

**Q: How does group income protection integrate with SSP?**
SSP pays £116.75 per week for up to 28 weeks. GIP has a deferred period — typically 13, 26, or 52 weeks — before benefits commence. Where the deferred period is set at 26 weeks, GIP benefits commence at approximately the same time as SSP exhausts, providing continuity of income. Where the deferred period is 52 weeks, there is a gap of approximately 24 weeks between SSP exhaustion and GIP commencement during which neither statutory nor employer-arranged income replacement is available. Employees in that gap must claim Employment and Support Allowance from the state. The GR1 exam frequently tests this integration scenario with specific week calculations.
