## MAIN CONTENT

### Insurance Contract Formation — Key Elements in M05

Insurance contract formation requires the same six elements as any contract at common law, but each element operates distinctively in the insurance context. M05 written exam questions frequently present a scenario describing a proposed insurance transaction and ask which element is absent or defective — making precise understanding of how each element applies in insurance essential.

**Offer and acceptance in insurance** operate in the reverse of most commercial transactions. The insured makes the offer by completing and submitting the proposal form. The insurer accepts by issuing the policy, or counter-offers by proposing amended terms. This distinction is consistently tested in M05: if the exam scenario asks who makes the offer in an insurance contract, the answer is the insured — not the insurer. A quotation issued by the insurer in response to a proposal is a counter-offer, not an acceptance.

**Consensus ad idem** — meeting of the minds — requires both parties to be in genuine agreement on the same terms. In insurance, material non-disclosure or misrepresentation by the insured at the proposal stage prevents genuine consensus from forming, because the insurer is agreeing to a risk profile that does not reflect reality.

**Consideration** in insurance is the premium paid by the insured in exchange for the insurer's promise of indemnity. The insurer's consideration is the contractual promise to pay valid claims — not the actual payment of any claim. No consideration means no binding contract; a policy issued without agreement on premium is unenforceable.

**Capacity** requires both parties to have legal competence to contract. Insurance companies must be authorised under the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000 (FSMA 2000) to carry on insurance business in the UK — an unauthorised insurer cannot enforce the contract, though the insured may still be able to recover under the FSMA 2000 compensation scheme. Individual insureds must have legal capacity — relevant where a minor or a person lacking mental capacity has purported to enter an insurance contract.

**Legality** requires the subject matter and purpose of the contract to be lawful. An insured cannot insure against the financial consequences of their own criminal act — this is a public policy rule established in *Gray v Barr* [1971], where the court refused to allow recovery under a life policy where the insured had unlawfully killed the victim. The principle also prevents motor insurance covering damage caused during a deliberate criminal use of a vehicle.

### Policy Terms Classification — Conditions, Warranties, and Insurance Act 2015 Reform

The classification of a policy term determines the legal consequences of its breach. M05 requires students to distinguish between conditions precedent, conditions subsequent, and warranties — and to apply both the pre-Insurance Act 2015 common law position and the reformed position under the 2015 Act.

#### Conditions Precedent and Conditions Subsequent

A **condition precedent** must be satisfied before the insured acquires any rights under the policy. Breach of a condition precedent automatically eliminates the insurer's obligation to pay — the insurer does not need to show prejudice from the breach, nor does it need to show any causal connection between the breach and the loss. Common conditions precedent in insurance policies include: notification of a claim within a specified period (e.g., "notice must be given to the insurer within 30 days of the occurrence"); submission of a signed proof of loss form; obtaining the insurer's written consent before admitting liability to a third party; and the existence of insurable interest at the time of effecting the policy. M05 exam scenarios frequently present a situation where an insured has failed to comply with a notification time limit and ask whether the insurer can refuse to pay. Where the relevant clause is properly drafted as a condition precedent, the insurer can decline the claim without showing prejudice.

A **condition subsequent** is an ongoing obligation that applies during the currency of the policy. Breach of a condition subsequent does not automatically void the policy or discharge the insurer from all liability. It may give the insurer a right to terminate the policy prospectively, or to limit or reduce its liability for claims affected by the breach — but only where the breach has caused measurable prejudice to the insurer. Common conditions subsequent include: maintaining agreed security measures (e.g., an alarm that must be kept operational); maintaining the insured property in good repair; notifying material changes in risk during the policy period. The distinction is critical for M05: an insurer cannot refuse to pay a claim arising from an unrelated loss simply because the insured failed to comply with an ongoing condition subsequent — breach must be shown to affect the specific claim.

#### Warranties — Pre and Post Insurance Act 2015

The law of insurance warranties changed fundamentally when the Insurance Act 2015 came into force on 12 August 2016 for business insurance contracts. M05 requires students to know both the pre-Act position and the reformed position — exam scenarios will specify dates, and the applicable law depends on whether the contract was entered into before or after 12 August 2016.

**Pre-Insurance Act 2015 warranty rule (common law):** Any warranty breach — however trivial, however unrelated to the loss — automatically and permanently discharged the insurer from all liability from the date of breach onwards. This rule was established in *Dawsons v Bonnin* [1922] AC 413, where the House of Lords held that an incorrect statement about where a lorry was garaged (a trivial inaccuracy with no causal connection to the fire that destroyed the vehicle) permanently voided the insurer's liability. The harshness of this rule was compounded by the **basis of contract clause**: insurers routinely included a clause in the proposal form converting all of the insured's answers into warranties. Any inaccuracy in any proposal form answer — however minor — could therefore trigger the automatic discharge rule.

**Insurance Act 2015 s.9 — Abolition of basis of contract clauses:** For insurance contracts entered into on or after 12 August 2016, basis of contract clauses are abolished in business insurance. Insurers cannot convert proposal form statements into warranties by use of a basis of contract clause. This removes a major source of harsh outcomes that had drawn criticism from commercial policyholders and the Law Commission.

**Insurance Act 2015 s.10 — Warranties as suspensive conditions:** A warranty breach no longer permanently discharges insurer liability. Instead, insurer liability is **suspended** during the period of the warranty breach. If the breach is remedied before the loss occurs, insurer liability is fully reinstated. If the loss occurs while the warranty is in breach, the insurer may decline the claim. If the loss occurs after the breach has been remedied, the insurer must pay. This is a fundamental change from the common law position — the key word for M05 exam answers is "suspended," not "discharged."

**Insurance Act 2015 s.11 — Limitation on reliance on terms:** An insurer cannot rely on a term — whether styled as a warranty or a condition — to exclude or limit liability for a loss if the insured can show that the term could not have increased the risk of the loss that actually occurred. This provides an additional protection against insurers declining claims on technical term breaches where the breach had no possible relevance to the loss.

| | Pre-Insurance Act 2015 | Post-Insurance Act 2015 (from 12 August 2016) |
|---|---|---|
| Effect of warranty breach | Permanent discharge of insurer liability from date of breach | Suspension of insurer liability during period of breach |
| Basis of contract clause | Valid — converts all proposal answers into warranties | Abolished for business insurance |
| Relevance of breach to loss | Irrelevant — no causal connection required | s.11: insurer cannot rely on term if breach could not have increased risk of actual loss |

### Insurance Act 2015 — Fair Presentation and Proportionate Remedies

The Insurance Act 2015 replaced the pre-existing duty of utmost good faith (as applied to disclosure) with a more precisely defined **duty of fair presentation** for business insurance contracts. The old duty, derived from *Carter v Boehm* (1766) and codified in the Marine Insurance Act 1906, required the insured to volunteer all material facts — but provided only the blunt remedy of avoidance (treating the contract as void from inception) for any breach, however minor or innocent. The Insurance Act 2015 replaces this with a structured duty and a graduated remedy system.

**Insurance Act 2015 s.3 — The Duty of Fair Presentation:** The business insured must:
- Disclose every material circumstance which the insured knows or ought to know; or
- Give the insurer sufficient information to put a prudent insurer on notice that it needs to make further enquiry about those circumstances.
- Present the information in a reasonably clear and accessible manner — not by dumping information on the insurer in a form that obscures material facts.

The insured "knows" what its senior management knows, and what would be revealed by a reasonable search of information held by the organisation. The test for materiality remains the prudent insurer test: a circumstance is material if it would influence the judgment of a prudent insurer in determining whether to write the risk and on what terms.

**Insurance Act 2015 s.4–7 — Proportionate Remedies for Breach of Fair Presentation:**

The remedy for breach of the duty of fair presentation depends on the insured's state of mind and on what the insurer would have done if presented with the true information:

- **If the breach is deliberate or reckless:** The insurer may avoid the contract from inception, retain all premiums paid, and refuse all claims. This is the most severe outcome and is reserved for the most culpable conduct.
- **If the breach is not deliberate or reckless**, the remedy depends on the counterfactual — what would the insurer have done had it known the true position?
  - *If the insurer would not have written the risk at all:* The insurer may avoid the contract but must return the premium.
  - *If the insurer would have written the risk on different terms (other than premium):* The contract is treated as if those different terms had applied from inception. The insurer cannot avoid but can rely on terms it would have imposed.
  - *If the insurer would have charged a higher premium:* The insurer pays the claim but reduces the payment proportionately — by the ratio of the premium actually charged to the premium that would have been charged. Example: if the insurer would have charged £10,000 but was charged only £8,000, and the claim is £100,000, the insurer pays £80,000 (80% of the claim, reflecting that only 80% of the correct premium was paid).

This proportionate remedy structure — linking the remedy to what the insurer would have done — is the defining feature of the Insurance Act 2015 reforms and is heavily tested in M05 written exam scenarios.

**Consumer Insurance:** The Consumer Insurance (Disclosure and Representations) Act 2012 applies to consumer (individual) policyholders and introduced a similar proportionate remedy structure for consumer insurance before the Insurance Act 2015 applied to business insurance. M05 requires students to know which Act applies: the 2012 Act applies to individual consumers; the Insurance Act 2015 applies to business insureds.

### Subrogation and Contribution at Diploma Depth

#### Subrogation

Subrogation is the right of the insurer, following payment of a claim, to stand in the insured's shoes and pursue any third party tortfeasor responsible for causing the loss. The doctrine prevents the insured from recovering twice — once from the insurer and once from the third party — and enables the insurer to recover from the party actually responsible for the loss.

Three timing and procedural rules govern subrogation at Diploma level:

First, **subrogation only arises after full indemnity has been paid.** The insurer has no right to pursue the third party in the insured's name before paying the claim in full. This is a frequently tested point in M05 exam scenarios — an insurer who has paid only part of a claim cannot yet exercise subrogation rights.

Second, **the insurer sues in the insured's name.** The insurer does not sue in its own name — the action is brought as if it were the insured's action, using the insured's cause of action against the third party. The insured must cooperate fully with subrogation proceedings; failure to cooperate may amount to a condition precedent breach under the policy.

Third, *Lord Napier and Ettrick v Hunter* [1993] established that in reinsurance, the reinsurer's subrogation rights extend to any recovery the insured achieves from the third party — the insured cannot retain a recovery from the tortfeasor to the extent that the reinsurer has funded the payment. This prevents the insured from receiving both the reinsurer's payment and the tortfeasor's compensation for the same loss.

#### Contribution

Contribution applies where two or more policies cover the same risk for the same insured against the same peril and in respect of the same insurable interest. Each insurer is liable to contribute a rateable proportion of the claim — no insurer can be required to pay more than its proportion merely because another insurer is insolvent or refuses to pay.

Two methods are used to calculate each insurer's contribution share:

**Independent liability method (most common in UK practice):** Each insurer calculates its liability as if it were the only insurer. The contribution share is then the proportion that each insurer's individual liability bears to the total of all independent liabilities. Example: Insurer A is liable for £100,000 as sole insurer; Insurer B is liable for £150,000 as sole insurer. Total independent liability = £250,000. If the actual loss is £120,000, Insurer A pays £120,000 × (£100,000 ÷ £250,000) = £48,000; Insurer B pays £120,000 × (£150,000 ÷ £250,000) = £72,000.

**Maximum liability method:** Each insurer contributes in the ratio of its policy limit to the sum of all policy limits. Less commonly used in general insurance; more prevalent in some marine and commercial contexts. The method used must be stated in M05 exam answers — do not describe "the contribution method" without specifying which method and showing the calculation.

### Agency Law in Insurance — Broker Authority and Knowledge Imputation

#### Who Does the Broker Represent?

In insurance, the broker is generally the agent of the insured — not the insurer. The broker owes duties to the insured: to exercise reasonable skill and care in placing cover, to disclose all material facts to the insurer, and to advise on the adequacy of cover obtained. This position is important for M05 because it determines where liability falls when a broker provides incorrect information or fails to disclose a material fact.

The general rule that the broker is the insured's agent has one significant exception for disclosure purposes: where the insurer appoints a broker or agent to act on its behalf in completing proposal form questions or gathering information, knowledge acquired by that agent in that capacity is imputed to the insurer. This was established in *Pan Atlantic Insurance v Pine Top* [1995], where it was held that the insurer is treated as knowing what its own agent knows — an insurer cannot rely on non-disclosure of information that its own agent held.

#### Types of Broker Authority

**Actual authority:** The authority expressly or impliedly given to the broker by the insured or insurer. A broker with actual authority to bind the insurer can commit the insurer to coverage within that authority.

**Apparent (ostensible) authority:** Where an insurer has represented to a third party (the insured) that the broker has authority to act on its behalf, the insurer is bound by acts within that apparent authority even if no actual authority was given. This prevents insurers from denying the validity of cover issued by their agents when they have led policyholders to believe those agents have authority.

**Deemed authority:** Certain representations made by agents in the course of their duties are deemed to be made with the insurer's authority by operation of law — the insured does not need to prove actual knowledge of the agent's authority.

### Insurable Interest — Case Law Thread in M05

Insurable interest is the legal or equitable interest in the subject matter of insurance that the insured must hold to be entitled to effect a valid policy. Without insurable interest, an insurance contract is void or unenforceable — the insured cannot recover even if a genuine loss occurs.

The statutory basis for insurable interest differs by insurance class:
- **Life assurance:** Life Assurance Act 1774 requires insurable interest at the time of effecting the policy. A person has unlimited insurable interest in their own life; spouses and civil partners have unlimited insurable interest in each other's lives; beyond these categories, insurable interest must be demonstrated.
- **Marine insurance:** Marine Insurance Act 1906 s.5 defines insurable interest as any legal or equitable relation to the adventure or the insurable property that would cause the insured to benefit from its preservation or suffer prejudice from its loss.
- **Indemnity insurance (non-marine, non-life):** Insurable interest must exist at the time of the loss (not necessarily at inception). A vendor of property who has contracted to sell but not yet completed retains insurable interest until completion.

The key case law thread on insurable interest for M05 is:

*Macaura v Northern Assurance* [1925]: The House of Lords held that a shareholder has no insurable interest in the assets of the company whose shares he holds — even where the shareholder owns all the shares. The company is a separate legal entity from its shareholders; the shareholder's insurable interest is in the shares, not in the company's physical assets. Mr Macaura owned all the shares in a company that held timber; when the timber was destroyed by fire, he could not recover under a policy he had taken in his own name (not the company's name).

*Feasey v Sun Life Assurance* [2003]: The Court of Appeal held that a P&I Club had an insurable interest in the lives of its members and their employees — the Club's liability to pay members' claims gave it a pecuniary interest in the insured's survival (longer survival = lower claim liability). This extended the concept of insurable interest beyond traditional categories.

### M05 Written Exam Answer Technique — Diploma Level

M05 is assessed exclusively by written examination. MCQ technique — eliminating wrong options, working from recognition — does not transfer to M05 written answers. Every mark requires demonstration of knowledge, analysis, and application within a structured written response.

**Short-answer questions (10–15 marks):** These questions ask for a definition, an explanation of a statutory provision, or a comparison between two legal positions. The structure for each short-answer response is: (1) State the applicable rule with precision — cite the statute by section number and name, or the case by name and year. (2) Explain the rule in the insurance context. (3) Apply the rule to the scenario or give a specific example. Avoid generic statements: "Under Insurance Act 2015..." is not acceptable without the section number.

**Extended answer questions (25 marks):** These present a scenario — typically a described insurance transaction or a claim dispute — and ask students to advise the parties. The required structure is: (1) Identify the legal issue raised by the scenario. (2) State the applicable rule with full citation. (3) Apply the rule to the specific facts of the scenario. (4) State the conclusion — who wins, on what basis, with what remedy. This structure is known as IRAC (Issue, Rule, Application, Conclusion) and is the standard format for legal analysis at Diploma level.

**Statute citation rule:** Always cite by section number — "Under Insurance Act 2015 s.10..." not "Under the Insurance Act." Vague statutory references lose marks in Diploma written exams. Know the section numbers for: s.3 (fair presentation duty), s.4–7 (proportionate remedies), s.9 (basis of contract clause abolition), s.10 (warranties as suspensive conditions), s.11 (limitation on reliance on terms), s.13A (insurer's implied duty to pay within reasonable time).

**Case law citation rule:** State the full case name and year in square brackets — "In *Dawsons v Bonnin* [1922], the House of Lords held that..." Stating a legal principle without its case law authority loses marks at Diploma level. For M05, the minimum case law repertoire includes: *Gray v Barr* [1971] (legality/public policy); *Dawsons v Bonnin* [1922] (pre-Act warranty rule); *Carter v Boehm* (1766) (utmost good faith); *Pan Atlantic Insurance v Pine Top* [1995] (knowledge imputation); *Lord Napier and Ettrick v Hunter* [1993] (subrogation in reinsurance); *Macaura v Northern Assurance* [1925] (insurable interest — shareholder position); *Feasey v Sun Life Assurance* [2003] (extended insurable interest).

**Common M05 written exam failures:**
1. Describing the pre-2015 warranty rule (permanent discharge) without stating the post-2015 reform (suspensive condition). The exam expects both positions stated and contrasted.
2. Confusing conditions precedent (breach = automatic discharge, no prejudice required) with conditions subsequent (breach may give right to terminate, prejudice relevant). The distinction changes the answer completely.
3. Applying subrogation before indemnity has been fully paid — subrogation only arises after full payment.
4. Stating the Insurance Act 2015 proportionate remedy without specifying which of the four outcome scenarios applies to the facts given.

### FAQ — M05 Insurance Law Assignment Help

**Q: Is M05 assessed by MCQ or written exam?**

M05 is assessed exclusively by written examination — it is not MCQ. This is the most important structural distinction between M05 and Certificate-level IF units. The written exam combines short-answer questions (typically 10–15 marks each) requiring precise legal definitions and statutory citations, with extended answer questions (25 marks) requiring structured legal analysis of insurance scenarios in IRAC format. Students who have only experience of Certificate MCQ assessment must develop a different approach for M05.

**Q: What is the difference between a condition precedent and a warranty in M05?**

A condition precedent must be satisfied before the insured has any rights under the policy — breach automatically eliminates the insurer's obligation to pay, with no requirement to show prejudice or causal connection to the loss. A warranty, under the Insurance Act 2015 s.10 (for contracts from 12 August 2016), operates as a suspensive condition — insurer liability is suspended during the period of breach but reinstated if the breach is remedied before the loss. Pre-2015 at common law, a warranty breach permanently discharged insurer liability (*Dawsons v Bonnin* [1922]). The exam requires students to state both positions and identify which applies based on the contract date in the scenario.

**Q: Does M05 cover the Consumer Insurance Act 2012 or only the Insurance Act 2015?**

M05 covers both Acts. The Consumer Insurance (Disclosure and Representations) Act 2012 applies to individual consumer policyholders — it introduced the duty to take reasonable care not to make misrepresentations, replacing the previous disclosure duty for consumers. The Insurance Act 2015 applies to business (commercial) policyholders from 12 August 2016 — it introduced the duty of fair presentation and the proportionate remedy structure. M05 exam scenarios identify whether the policyholder is a consumer or a business; students must apply the correct Act based on the scenario facts.

**Q: How many credits is M05 and what level is it?**

M05 carries 25 credits at RQF Level 4. This is significantly more demanding than Certificate-level IF units, which carry 15 credits at RQF Level 3. The Level 4 assessment requires written examination technique, statutory section citation, and case law application — none of which are required at Certificate level. The pass mark for M05 is set by the CII; students should confirm the current pass mark with the CII directly, as assessment standards are subject to periodic review.

**Q: What is the Insurance Act 2015 s.13A?**

Insurance Act 2015 s.13A implies a term into every insurance contract that the insurer must pay any sums due under a claim within a reasonable time. If the insurer breaches this implied term by failing to pay within a reasonable time without good cause, the insured may have a claim for damages for late payment — including consequential losses that flow from the delay. This provision applies to insurance contracts entered into after 4 May 2017 (when s.13A came into force). It is a distinct remedy from the proportionate remedy framework in s.4–7.
