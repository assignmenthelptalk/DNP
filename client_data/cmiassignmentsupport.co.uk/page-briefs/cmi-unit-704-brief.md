**Key frameworks — all requiring Critically Analytical engagement:**
- PESTLE analysis — Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Legal, Environmental — as an external environment scan
- Porter's Five Forces (1980) — competitive industry analysis: threat of new entrants, bargaining power of buyers, bargaining power of suppliers, threat of substitutes, competitive rivalry
- Resource-Based View (RBV) — Barney (1991); VRIO framework (Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, Organised) — internal capability analysis; what gives the organisation sustainable competitive advantage
- Ansoff Matrix (1957) — four strategic growth directions: market penetration, market development, product development, diversification — and their associated risk profiles
- Porter's Generic Strategies (1980) — cost leadership, differentiation, focus — and the "stuck in the middle" trap
- Balanced Scorecard — Kaplan and Norton (1992) — four perspectives (financial, customer, internal processes, learning and growth) as a strategic performance measurement framework
- Dynamic capabilities — Teece, Pisano and Shuen (1997) — the ability to integrate, build, and reconfigure internal and external competences in response to changing environments

**Common failure points:**
1. PESTLE listed as environmental factors without critical analysis of which are most strategically significant and why
2. Porter's Five Forces applied mechanically (ticking boxes) rather than Critically Analysed as a competitive framework with limitations
3. RBV described without VRIO application — what specifically makes the organisation's resources valuable, rare, inimitable, and organised?
4. AC3 (strategic direction) justified without connecting to AC1 and AC2 analysis — the strategy must logically follow from the environmental and capability analysis
5. No engagement with dynamic capabilities or the limitations of static frameworks (PESTLE, Porter) in rapidly changing environments
