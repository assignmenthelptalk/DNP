## What Is CMI Unit 502 and What Does It Cover?

CMI Unit 502 — Developing, Managing and Leading Individuals and Teams to Achieve Success focuses on the practical team leadership dimension of Level 5 management. It asks the student to evaluate what influences team performance, how effective managers develop and lead teams through their lifecycle, and how individual team members are managed differently from the team as a whole.

This is one of the most practically relevant units at Level 5. Most middle managers and operational leaders spend the majority of their management work leading teams — motivating individuals, managing team dynamics, developing team capability, and responding to conflict or underperformance within the team context. CMI 502 asks the student to evaluate the theoretical frameworks that underpin this work, not just describe what they do in practice.

The unit is assessed against three Assessment Criteria:

- **AC1** — Evaluate the factors that influence team dynamics and performance
- **AC2** — Evaluate approaches to developing, managing and leading teams to achieve success
- **AC3** — Evaluate approaches to managing and leading individuals within a team

The distinction between AC2 and AC3 is significant. AC2 focuses on the team as a unit — how the team is developed, led, and managed through its performance lifecycle. AC3 focuses on individuals within the team — how individual team members with different roles, skills, and development needs are managed differently. Students who treat AC2 and AC3 as the same question produce reports that miss one criterion entirely.

### CMI 502 Assessment Criteria — What the Assessor Is Marking

**AC1 — Evaluate the factors that influence team dynamics and performance**

Team dynamics are shaped by multiple interacting factors: team composition (the mix of skills, personalities, and roles), team size, team maturity (how long the team has been working together and through what challenges), the clarity of team goals and accountability, the psychological climate of the team, and the leadership style applied. AC1 requires evaluation of these factors — not a list of them. Which factors have the greatest influence? Under what conditions? With what evidence?

**AC2 — Evaluate approaches to developing, managing and leading teams to achieve success**

This criterion requires evaluation of the theoretical frameworks that guide team development — Tuckman's model in particular, alongside high-performance team frameworks. Evaluate means applying criteria (effectiveness in developing cohesion, applicability across different team types, practical usability for a manager) and reaching a reasoned conclusion about which approaches best support team success in the defined organisational context.

**AC3 — Evaluate approaches to managing and leading individuals within a team**

This is the individual dimension. Within any team, individual members have different levels of skill, commitment, confidence, and development need. Hersey and Blanchard's situational leadership (introduced in Unit 501) is directly applicable here — the manager adapts their approach to each individual's development level, not to the team as a whole. Belbin's team roles framework also applies: understanding what role each team member naturally occupies helps the manager leverage individual strengths and address gaps in team composition.

## Key Theories and Models in CMI Unit 502

CMI 502 requires evaluation of frameworks for team development and individual management. The following are the most commonly applied in submitted assignments and the most expected by assessors.

### Tuckman's Team Development Model — Five Stages

Bruce Tuckman (1965) identified four stages of small group development — Forming, Storming, Norming, and Performing — later adding a fifth stage, Adjourning, in 1977 with Jensen. The model describes the typical progression a team moves through as it develops from a collection of individuals into a cohesive, high-performing unit.

**Forming:** The team assembles. Members are polite, cautious, and uncertain about their roles. They look to the leader for direction and structure. Dependency on the manager is high. The manager's primary task is to provide clarity — about goals, roles, and expectations.

**Storming:** Conflict emerges. Team members challenge each other, the leader, and the task structure. This is the most uncomfortable and the most important stage. Teams that avoid storming often produce superficial norming — apparent cohesion without genuine alignment. The manager's role is to manage conflict productively, not suppress it.

**Norming:** The team finds its working rhythm. Roles become clearer, relationships strengthen, and shared norms of behaviour emerge. Conflict gives way to co-operation. The manager's role shifts from directive to facilitative.

**Performing:** The team operates with high levels of interdependence and output. Members work without close supervision, resolve issues internally, and adapt to challenges without losing cohesion. The manager's role is to sustain performance conditions and develop individual capability.

**Adjourning:** The team disbands — project completion, restructuring, or role changes. This stage involves recognition of achievement and transition management. For permanent teams, Adjourning may be a temporary state before Reforming with new members.

**How to Evaluate Tuckman in CMI 502:** The criteria for evaluating Tuckman's model in a Level 5 management report might include: applicability across different team types, practical usability for a manager with limited time, and effectiveness as a developmental framework. Tuckman's model scores well on applicability — it is widely validated and directly usable. Its limitation (required for Distinction) is that team development is rarely linear. Teams may regress from Performing to Storming when membership changes or organisational pressures escalate — the model does not fully account for this cyclical reality.

### Belbin's Team Roles — Nine Roles and How to Apply Them in CMI 502

Meredith Belbin (1981) identified nine team roles through observational research at Henley Management College. Each role describes a pattern of behaviour a team member naturally tends toward — their contribution style, their strengths, and their allowable weaknesses. The nine roles are grouped into three orientations:

**Action-oriented roles:**
- **Shaper** — challenges the team, drives progress, thrives under pressure. Risk: aggression, insensitivity.
- **Implementer** — turns ideas into practical actions, reliable and organised. Risk: inflexibility.
- **Completer Finisher** — focuses on quality, detail, and deadline compliance. Risk: anxiety, reluctance to delegate.

**People-oriented roles:**
- **Co-ordinator** — clarifies goals, delegates effectively, promotes team decision-making. Risk: can be perceived as manipulative.
- **Teamworker** — supports others, diplomatic, averts friction. Risk: indecisive in conflict situations.
- **Resource Investigator** — explores external opportunities, builds external networks. Risk: loses momentum after initial enthusiasm.

**Thought-oriented roles:**
- **Plant** — generates creative ideas and novel solutions. Risk: ignores practicalities, poor communication.
- **Monitor Evaluator** — analyses options, makes sound judgements. Risk: perceived as overcritical, lacks drive.
- **Specialist** — provides expert knowledge in a specific domain. Risk: contributes only in a narrow field.

**How to Apply Belbin in CMI 502:** Belbin is most effectively used in two ways. First, for AC1 — team composition as a factor in team dynamics. A team without a Completer Finisher may meet milestones poorly; a team with multiple Shapers may experience chronic conflict. Second, for AC3 — individual management. Understanding a team member's natural role helps the manager leverage their contribution and adapt their management approach accordingly. A Plant needs creative space and tolerance for unconventional thinking; a Completer Finisher needs clarity on quality standards and deadlines.

**Limitation to acknowledge (required for Distinction):** Belbin's model has been criticised for its reliability and for the assumption that individuals have stable, fixed roles. Research by Broucek and Randell (1996) questions the psychometric validity of Belbin's Self Perception Inventory. In practice, team members often shift roles depending on the team composition and task context. Including this limitation and examining its implications for how a manager uses the model demonstrates Distinction-level critical engagement.

### High-Performance Teams — Katzenbach and Smith

Katzenbach and Smith (1993) define a real team as a small number of people with complementary skills, committed to a common purpose, performance goals, and approach, for which they hold themselves mutually accountable. Their research distinguishes between a working group (members who share information but are individually accountable) and a high-performance team (members who hold each other mutually accountable for collective output).

**How to use in CMI 502:** The Katzenbach and Smith framework provides criteria for AC1 (team dynamics factors) and AC2 (evaluating what constitutes high performance). It also provides a useful contrast to Tuckman — Tuckman describes how teams develop over time, Katzenbach and Smith describe what a high-performance team looks like when it arrives. Applied together, they provide a developmental trajectory (Tuckman) and a destination definition (Katzenbach and Smith).

### Psychological Safety — Amy Edmondson

Amy Edmondson (1999) demonstrated through empirical research that team performance is strongly associated with psychological safety — a team climate in which members feel safe to take interpersonal risks: to speak up, challenge ideas, admit mistakes, and ask questions without fear of embarrassment or punishment.

**How to use in CMI 502:** Psychological safety addresses AC1 directly — it is one of the most evidence-supported factors influencing team dynamics and performance. High-performing teams (Performing in Tuckman's model) are characterised by psychological safety. Managers who suppress dissent, punish mistakes, or create fear of challenge inadvertently prevent their teams from reaching the Norming and Performing stages. This makes psychological safety a bridge concept connecting AC1 (factors in team dynamics) and AC2 (approaches the manager takes to develop the team) with strong empirical backing.

## How to Evaluate Team Development Approaches in CMI 502

Evaluate is the primary command verb for both AC2 and AC3. The same requirements apply as in Unit 501 — but now applied specifically to team development frameworks and individual management approaches.

**The Evaluate structure for CMI 502:**
1. **Establish criteria** — what are you measuring team development approaches against? Criteria might include: effectiveness in moving teams from Storming to Norming, practical applicability for a manager without specialist HR support, adaptability across teams of different sizes and compositions.
2. **Apply each framework against those criteria** — with evidence. "Tuckman's model provides a sequenced framework that allows the manager to identify the team's current stage and select an appropriate leadership response (Tuckman, 1965)" is an evidenced application. "Tuckman helps managers" is not.
3. **Reach a reasoned conclusion** — which approach is most effective for the organisational context defined, and why. This conclusion must be defended, not hedged.

### What Does Not Count as Evaluate in CMI 502

**Narrating Tuckman's stages as a story:** "First the team forms, then there is conflict in the storming stage, then the team settles..." This is description. It does not evaluate Tuckman as a framework. It does not establish criteria, apply evidence, or conclude whether Tuckman is effective for the management context defined.

**Listing Belbin roles without connecting to performance:** Writing out all nine Belbin roles with their descriptions and then stating "this shows the importance of team role balance" is not Evaluate. The evaluative question is: what is the effect of role imbalance on team dynamics? What is the evidence? What does this mean for how the manager composes or compensates within the team?

**Personal preference without evidence:** "In my team, I have found transformational leadership most effective for developing people." Personal experience is not evidence at Level 5. It may accompany evidence but cannot replace it.

## Why CMI 502 Assignments Are Referred — The Most Common Mistakes

**1. Treating Tuckman as a narrative rather than a framework to evaluate**
The most common referral cause. Tuckman's stages are described chronologically rather than evaluated as an approach to team development. The criteria and evidence required for Evaluate are absent.

**2. Belbin listed but not applied**
Nine roles are presented as a taxonomy with no evaluative connection to team composition, team dynamics, or individual management approaches. The framework appears but does not answer the assessment question.

**3. AC2 and AC3 conflated**
Both criteria answered with team-level analysis. AC3's specific focus on individual management approaches — how individuals with different development needs, roles, and motivation levels are managed differently — is absent.

**4. No organisational context**
Team development frameworks discussed in the abstract. AC2 requires evaluation of approaches in a specific team and organisational context. Vague references to "my workplace" without defining the team, its stage, or its composition do not satisfy the contextual requirement.

**5. Executive summary written as an introduction**
A recurring format error across Level 5. The executive summary must be a standalone document that an assessor can read independently — summarising the key findings on team development approaches and the management recommendations. An introductory paragraph that introduces the topic does not fulfil this requirement.

## What Separates a Merit from a Distinction in CMI 502?

At **Merit**, all three Assessment Criteria are addressed with Evaluate depth. AC1 identifies and evaluates at least three factors influencing team dynamics with named evidence. AC2 evaluates Tuckman's model (and at least one other framework) against defined criteria in a specific organisational context, reaching a defended conclusion. AC3 evaluates individual management approaches (situational leadership and/or Belbin applied to individuals) with evidence. Recommendations are SMART and connected to the analysis.

At **Distinction**, the response does all of the above plus:

- **Acknowledges the limitations of Tuckman** — team development is not linear; teams regress; the model provides limited guidance on how a manager actually moves a team from Storming to Norming beyond generic "manage conflict" advice
- **Critiques Belbin's psychometric validity** — questions whether roles are as stable across contexts as the model implies; examines what happens when the manager's assessment of team roles differs from the team member's self-perception
- **Engages with competing perspectives** — for example, considers whether high psychological safety always improves performance, or whether some productive tension is necessary (Edmondson's own research acknowledges this nuance)
- **Defends a position on individual management** — which approach to managing individuals within the team is most effective for the defined context, and why, argued against the alternatives with evidence
