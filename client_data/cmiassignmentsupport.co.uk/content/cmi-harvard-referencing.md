# CMI Harvard Referencing Guide — In-Text Citations and Reference Lists

Harvard referencing is required on all CMI assignments from Level 3 to Level 7. The format is consistent across levels — the same author-date system applies whether you are writing a 2,000-word Level 3 reflective account or a 6,000-word Level 7 strategic paper. What changes across levels is the number of sources required, the quality of sources expected, and the depth at which they are integrated into your argument.

This guide covers the exact format for in-text citations and reference list entries for the source types CMI students actually use: books, journal articles, government reports, NHS publications, ACAS guidance, websites, and CMI's own publications.

## What Is Harvard Referencing?

Harvard referencing is a parenthetical author-date citation system. Every time you refer to, paraphrase, or quote another author's work in your assignment, you include a brief in-text citation — the author's surname and the publication year in brackets: (Author, Year). At the end of your document, a full reference list presents every cited source in alphabetical order by the first author's surname.

CMI uses Harvard referencing at all levels because it is the standard in UK management and business education. Your assessor uses the reference list to verify that your arguments are supported by appropriate sources and that the sources are cited accurately. Incorrect referencing — wrong format, incomplete information, in-text citations not matching the reference list — affects your grade.

The reference list is typically excluded from the word count, but it is assessed for quality.

## In-Text Citation Format

### Single Author

Place the author's surname and the year of publication in brackets after the paraphrased or summarised content:

*Transformational leaders motivate followers by appealing to higher-order needs rather than transactional reward mechanisms (Bass, 1985).*

If you mention the author's name in the sentence, include only the year in brackets:

*Bass (1985) argues that transformational leadership produces follower motivation beyond transactional exchange.*

### Two Authors

Use both surnames, joined by "and":

*(Kaplan and Norton, 1992)*

*Kaplan and Norton (1992) propose that financial metrics alone are insufficient for measuring strategic performance.*

### Three or More Authors

Use the first author's surname followed by "et al." (the abbreviation of "et alia" — and others):

*(Tuckman et al., 1977)*

Note: In your reference list, list all authors — "et al." is used only in in-text citations.

### Direct Quotation

When you reproduce an author's exact words, include the page number:

*(Edmondson, 2018, p. 23)*

Direct quotations should be used sparingly in CMI assignments. Paraphrase and integrate sources rather than quoting extensively — assessors are looking for your analytical writing, not transcribed text.

### No Identified Author — Corporate or Organisational Author

When a document has an organisation rather than a named individual as author, use the organisation's name:

*(ACAS, 2021)*
*(NHS England, 2023)*
*(CMI, 2022)*
*(CIPD, 2020)*

### No Author and No Identifiable Organisation

Avoid citing sources with no identifiable author or organisation. These cannot be verified and are not acceptable in CMI academic writing. If you cannot determine who produced a document, do not cite it.

### Secondary Citation

A secondary citation is used when you refer to a source that you found cited in another author's work — you have not read the original source directly.

*(Maslow, 1943, cited in Robbins, 2017)*

This tells your assessor that you encountered Maslow's 1943 work through Robbins' 2017 publication. Secondary citations are acceptable but should be used sparingly. CMI assessors at Level 5 and Level 7 expect you to locate and read primary sources — citing Maslow only through a textbook at Level 7 is a weakness. Where possible, access the original journal article or book.

## Reference List Format by Source Type

The reference list appears at the end of your assignment, on a new page titled "References" or "Reference List". Entries are listed alphabetically by the first author's surname. Use a hanging indent (the first line of each entry is flush left; subsequent lines are indented). Do not number entries.

### Book

**Format**: Author Surname, Initials. (Year) *Title of Book*. Edition (if not first). Place of publication: Publisher.

**Example**:
Kotter, J.P. (1996) *Leading Change*. Boston: Harvard Business School Press.

Bass, B.M. and Avolio, B.J. (1994) *Improving Organizational Effectiveness Through Transformational Leadership*. Thousand Oaks: Sage.

If citing a specific chapter in an edited book:

**Format**: Chapter Author Surname, Initials. (Year) 'Chapter title', in Editor Surname, Initials. (ed.) *Book Title*. Place of publication: Publisher, pp. page range.

**Example**:
Schein, E.H. (2010) 'Organisational culture and leadership', in Shafritz, J.M. (ed.) *Classics of Organisation Theory*. 7th edn. Boston: Wadsworth, pp. 311–320.

### Journal Article

**Format**: Author Surname, Initials. (Year) 'Title of article', *Name of Journal*, Volume(Issue), pp. page range.

**Example**:
Edmondson, A.C. (1999) 'Psychological safety and learning behavior in work teams', *Administrative Science Quarterly*, 44(2), pp. 350–383.

Barney, J. (1991) 'Firm resources and sustained competitive advantage', *Journal of Management*, 17(1), pp. 99–120.

If the article has a DOI, you may include it at the end: doi:10.1177/014920639101700108

### Government Report or Official Publication

**Format**: Issuing Organisation (Year) *Title of Report*. Place of publication: Publisher. Available at: URL (Accessed: date).

**Example**:
Department of Health and Social Care (2021) *Integration and Innovation: Working Together to Improve Health and Social Care for All*. London: DHSC. Available at: https://www.gov.uk/government/publications [Accessed: 12 January 2025].

HM Government (2010) *The Equality Act 2010*. London: The Stationery Office. Available at: https://www.legislation.gov.uk/ukpga/2010/15/contents [Accessed: 20 February 2025].

### NHS Publication

**Format**: Issuing Organisation (Year) *Title of Document*. Place of publication: Issuing Organisation. Available at: URL (Accessed: date).

**Example**:
NHS England (2023) *NHS Long Term Workforce Plan*. London: NHS England. Available at: https://www.england.nhs.uk/long-read/nhs-long-term-workforce-plan/ [Accessed: 15 March 2025].

Care Quality Commission (2023) *The State of Health Care and Adult Social Care in England 2022/23*. Newcastle: CQC. Available at: https://www.cqc.org.uk [Accessed: 10 January 2025].

### Website

**Format**: Author Surname, Initials. or Organisation Name (Year) *Title of webpage* [Online]. Available at: URL (Accessed: date).

**Example**:
ACAS (2021) *Code of Practice on Disciplinary and Grievance Procedures* [Online]. Available at: https://www.acas.org.uk/acas-code-of-practice-for-disciplinary-and-grievance-procedures [Accessed: 5 February 2025].

CMI (2022) *Management and Leadership Skills Report 2022* [Online]. Available at: https://www.managers.org.uk [Accessed: 22 January 2025].

### CMI Publication

CMI produces research reports, management articles, and guidance documents. These are acceptable CMI sources, particularly for practical context. They are not peer-reviewed academic journals.

**Format**: CMI (Year) *Title of Publication* [Online]. Available at: URL (Accessed: date).

**Example**:
CMI (2023) *Better Managed Britain: The Case for Management and Leadership Development*. London: CMI. Available at: https://www.managers.org.uk [Accessed: 1 April 2025].

## How Many Sources and What Quality by Level

The source requirements in CMI assignments increase as the level rises — both in number and in academic quality:

| CMI Level | Minimum Sources | Source Quality Expectation |
|---|---|---|
| Level 3 | 6–8 | Textbooks, reputable websites (ACAS, CMI, NHS England), government publications |
| Level 4 | 8–10 | Textbooks, professional body publications, some peer-reviewed journals |
| Level 5 | 10–12 | Mix of textbooks and peer-reviewed academic journals; government and official reports |
| Level 6 | 12–15 | Primarily peer-reviewed academic journals; authoritative reports to supplement |
| Level 7 | 15–20 | Predominantly peer-reviewed journals; textbooks as background; CMI and official reports as supplementary |

At Level 5, "peer-reviewed journal" means an academic journal where articles are reviewed by subject experts before publication. Examples relevant to CMI Level 5 include: *Journal of Management Studies*, *British Journal of Management*, *Human Relations*, *Journal of Organizational Behavior*, *Administrative Science Quarterly*.

At Level 7, your reference list should read like an academic paper — journals like *Academy of Management Review*, *Strategic Management Journal*, *Journal of Applied Psychology*, *Organization Science*, *Journal of Risk Research*.

Using only textbooks at Level 7 is a common reason assessments fail to achieve Merit. Textbooks summarise primary research — they are useful for orientation but are not the primary source of knowledge at Level 7.

## Common Mistakes and Edge Cases

**In-text citation doesn't match the reference list.** Every source cited in-text must appear in the reference list with the same author name and year. Every source in the reference list must have a corresponding in-text citation. Uncited references and uncited in-text mentions both indicate poor academic practice.

**Using "ibid." or "op. cit."** CMI Harvard referencing does not use these Latin abbreviations. Repeat the full in-text citation each time: (Kotter, 1996).

**Citing Wikipedia.** Wikipedia is not an acceptable CMI source. It is an editorial encyclopedia, not an academic publication. Use Wikipedia to understand a concept quickly, then locate the primary academic source and cite that.

**Citing LinkedIn articles.** LinkedIn articles are not peer-reviewed and are not acceptable academic sources. Find the underlying research or publication and cite that directly.

**No date available.** If a source has no publication date, use "no date" or "n.d.": (CMI, n.d.). In the reference list: CMI (n.d.) *Title of document* [Online]...

**Edition not first.** Include the edition for all books that are not first editions: *Leading Change*. 2nd edn. Many management textbooks are in their third or later edition — check your copy.

**Multiple works by the same author in the same year.** Differentiate with lowercase letters: (Schein, 1985a) and (Schein, 1985b). Use the same letters in the reference list.

**Organisation as author with long name.** You may abbreviate in-text after the first use if you introduce the abbreviation: The Department for Education (DfE, 2022) published guidance stating... Thereafter: (DfE, 2023). In the reference list, use the full name.

## Referencing and Source Quality

Good referencing is not just formatting correctly. It is demonstrating that your argument is built on appropriate evidence. At Level 7, an assessor reading your reference list can immediately tell whether you have engaged with the academic literature or relied on management textbooks and websites.

The goal is not to accumulate sources — it is to use sources purposefully. Each citation should support a specific analytical claim. A reference list with 20 sources where only three are actually integrated into the argument is weaker than a reference list with 16 sources where each one has a clear analytical function.

At Level 5 and above, assessors also look for source currency: research published within the last 10–15 years, with appropriate acknowledgement when you cite older foundational work (Maslow, 1943; Kotter, 1996) and connect it to more recent empirical evidence.

## FAQ

**Does CMI use Harvard referencing?**

Yes. Harvard referencing is required on all CMI assignments from Level 3 to Level 7. In-text citations use the author-date format — (Author, Year) — and a full alphabetically ordered reference list appears at the end of the document. The reference list is typically excluded from the word count but is assessed for source quality.

**How many references does a CMI assignment need?**

Level 3: 6–8 sources; Level 4: 8–10; Level 5: 10–12; Level 6: 12–15; Level 7: 15–20. Quality increases with level — Level 7 sources should be predominantly peer-reviewed academic journals. Using only textbooks at Level 7 is a common reason for not achieving Merit.

**How do I cite a government report in CMI Harvard referencing?**

Use the issuing organisation as the author: (NHS England, 2023). In the reference list: NHS England (2023) *NHS Long Term Workforce Plan*. London: NHS England. Available at: [full URL] (Accessed: [date]).

**What is a secondary citation in Harvard referencing?**

A secondary citation is used when you found a source referenced in another author's work rather than reading it directly: (Maslow, 1943, cited in Robbins, 2017). This should be used sparingly — CMI assessors expect you to locate and read primary sources, particularly at Level 5 and Level 7.

**Can I use websites as sources in CMI assignments?**

Reputable websites — government departments (GOV.UK), NHS England, ACAS, CMI, CIPD — are acceptable. Wikipedia, LinkedIn articles, and unsigned commercial websites are not. At Level 7, websites should supplement peer-reviewed journal articles, not replace them.

**Are CMI publications acceptable sources in CMI assignments?**

Yes. CMI research reports and management publications are acceptable sources, particularly for practical management context. They are not peer-reviewed academic journals. At Level 7, CMI publications should supplement journal articles, not serve as primary academic evidence.
