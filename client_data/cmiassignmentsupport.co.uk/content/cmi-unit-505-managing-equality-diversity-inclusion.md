## What Is CMI Unit 505?

CMI Unit 505 — Managing Equality, Diversity and Inclusion — is a Level 5 Diploma unit assessing how managers understand, implement, and Evaluate organisational EDI practice. The typical assessment criteria are:

- **AC1**: Evaluate legislation and organisational policies relating to equality, diversity and inclusion
- **AC2**: Evaluate the impact of inequality and discrimination on individuals and organisations
- **AC3**: Evaluate approaches to developing an inclusive culture

All three criteria use **Evaluate** as the command verb. At Level 5, Evaluate means: weigh the evidence, consider multiple perspectives, and reach a justified conclusion. It does not mean describe, list, or summarise.

The output is a management report of 3,500–4,500 words, supported by 10–12 sources with Harvard referencing. The report structure follows CMI management report conventions: executive summary, introduction, main analysis sections aligned to assessment criteria, conclusions and recommendations.

At Level 5, Evaluate means your analysis should include:
- Reference to relevant legislation and academic research
- Evidence of what works and what does not
- A justified conclusion — not "there are pros and cons" but "on the balance of evidence, approach X is more effective than approach Y because..."
- SMART recommendations that follow directly from your evaluation

## The Equality Act 2010 — What It Requires of Managers

The Equality Act 2010 is the primary legislative framework for equality in the UK. It consolidates and replaces earlier legislation (Sex Discrimination Act 1975, Race Relations Act 1976, Disability Discrimination Act 1995) into a single Act covering nine protected characteristics.

### The Nine Protected Characteristics

The Equality Act 2010 protects individuals from discrimination on the basis of:

1. **Age** — applies to all ages, not only older workers; includes younger workers and trainees
2. **Disability** — a physical or mental impairment that has a substantial and long-term adverse effect on a person's ability to carry out normal day-to-day activities
3. **Gender reassignment** — the process of transitioning from one gender to another, whether or not the individual has undergone medical treatment
4. **Marriage and civil partnership** — protection from discrimination in employment (note: this characteristic is more limited than others)
5. **Pregnancy and maternity** — protection during pregnancy and maternity leave periods
6. **Race** — colour, nationality, and ethnic or national origin
7. **Religion or belief** — any religion, lack of religion, or philosophical belief held with similar gravity
8. **Sex** — male or female
9. **Sexual orientation** — heterosexual, gay, lesbian, or bisexual

### The Four Forms of Prohibited Conduct

- **Direct discrimination**: treating someone less favourably because of a protected characteristic — for example, not shortlisting a candidate because of their name (race discrimination), or withdrawing a promotion offer when a pregnancy is disclosed
- **Indirect discrimination**: applying a provision, criterion, or practice (PCP) that appears neutral but puts people with a protected characteristic at a particular disadvantage — for example, requiring all staff to work Saturday shifts when this disproportionately affects some religious groups
- **Harassment**: unwanted conduct related to a protected characteristic that has the purpose or effect of violating someone's dignity or creating an intimidating, hostile, degrading, humiliating, or offensive environment
- **Victimisation**: treating someone less favourably because they have made (or are suspected of making) a complaint or raised a grievance under the Equality Act

### What Evaluating the Legislation Means at Level 5

Listing the nine protected characteristics and four prohibited conduct types is awareness — it is not Evaluation. Evaluating the Equality Act 2010 in AC1 means:

- Examining what the Act requires of managers specifically — not just HR departments
- Identifying where the Act creates obligations (reasonable adjustments for disabled employees, objective justification test for indirect discrimination) and where it relies on employer discretion
- Evaluating how your organisation's EDI policy extends beyond the Act's minimum requirements — or where it falls short
- Engaging with the Public Sector Equality Duty (PSED) if your organisation is a public authority — the active duty to advance equality of opportunity, not merely avoid discrimination

## Diversity, Equity, Inclusion, and Belonging — The Distinctions

These four terms are frequently used interchangeably in management practice. They are not equivalent, and conflating them leads to confused analysis in AC1 and AC3.

<!-- Alt text: "Equality equity diversity inclusion belonging — five distinct concepts from same treatment through to the felt experience of belonging" -->

- **Equality**: treating everyone the same. The floor of legal obligation — everyone gets the same access, the same process, the same rules. Equality alone cannot address disadvantage rooted in structural difference.
- **Equity**: adjusting treatment to account for difference — giving people what they need rather than the same thing. A disabled employee receiving a different workstation is equity, not preferential treatment.
- **Diversity**: the presence of people with different characteristics, experiences, and perspectives in an organisation. Diversity is about representation — the numbers. It can be measured: proportion of women in senior management, ethnic minority representation, disability disclosure rate.
- **Inclusion**: whether diverse people's contributions, perspectives, and experiences are valued, incorporated, and acted upon. An organisation can have a diverse workforce that is structurally excluded — tokenism, silenced voices, ideas attributed to others.
- **Belonging**: the felt sense — whether individuals experience the organisation as a place where they can be authentic, are valued, and do not have to conform to a dominant cultural norm to succeed (Shore et al., 2011). Belonging is the outcome that diversity and inclusion practices are trying to produce.

At Level 5, the most common analytical gap in AC1 is evaluating the organisation against equality (does it comply with the law?) rather than against inclusion and belonging (does it create a culture where diverse people thrive?).

## Unconscious Bias — What It Is and the Limits of Bias Training

Unconscious bias refers to the automatic, fast mental processes that influence perception, judgement, and behaviour without conscious awareness. Kahneman's (2011) dual process theory distinguishes System 1 thinking (fast, automatic, intuitive, prone to cognitive shortcuts) from System 2 thinking (slow, deliberate, analytical). Unconscious bias operates through System 1.

Common bias types relevant to management practice:

- **Affinity bias**: preferring people who are similar to you — same background, educational institution, ethnicity, gender; a significant driver of homogeneous leadership teams
- **Confirmation bias**: seeking out information that confirms existing beliefs and discounting contradictory evidence; affects performance assessment and promotion decisions
- **Halo/horns effect**: one positive characteristic (halo) or negative characteristic (horns) influencing overall judgement; affects interview decisions and appraisals
- **Attribution bias**: attributing success to personal factors for people like you (ability), and external factors for people unlike you (luck); differential attribution by gender is well-documented in performance review research

### The Limits of Bias Training

Unconscious bias training is the most widely deployed organisational response to bias — and the evidence for its effectiveness is weak. Dobbin and Kalev (2016) conducted a comprehensive review of 30 years of corporate diversity programmes in the United States and found that bias training had no significant impact on diversity outcomes and in some cases produced backlash — managers who resented being told their decisions were biased became more resistant to change.

The core problem with training-only approaches is that they address individual awareness without changing the structures that allow bias to operate. Awareness of one's biases does not consistently translate into changed behaviour, particularly under time pressure or cognitive load.

**Evaluating approaches to bias reduction** at Level 5 means engaging with this evidence. Recommending bias training without acknowledging its limitations is a Pass-level response. Evaluating the evidence on training versus structural intervention — and reaching a justified conclusion about what is more likely to change outcomes — is Merit to Distinction level.

## Inclusive Leadership — Building an Inclusive Culture

AC3 asks you to Evaluate approaches to developing an inclusive culture. The research on what makes leadership inclusive is increasingly clear.

Nembhard and Edmondson (2006) studied inclusive leadership in healthcare teams and found that team psychological safety — the belief that one will not be punished or humiliated for speaking up — was a key mechanism. Leaders who engaged in "inviting input" behaviours (asking questions, acknowledging their own fallibility, actively soliciting perspectives from less powerful team members) created conditions for inclusion that affected both wellbeing and performance outcomes.

Carmeli, Reiter-Palmon and Ziv (2010) identified inclusive leadership behaviours as: openness (receptivity to diverse perspectives), availability (accessibility and presence for team members), and accessibility (removing barriers to approach). These behaviours are not personality traits — they are practices that can be developed.

**What structural approaches have stronger evidence than training?**

- **Structured interviews with standardised criteria**: reduce the scope for affinity bias in hiring by requiring consistent questions and pre-defined scoring criteria evaluated independently before discussion
- **Blind recruitment**: removing name, address, and educational institution from initial screening; reduces race and socioeconomic bias in shortlisting
- **Sponsorship and mentoring programmes**: sponsorship (active advocacy for career advancement) has stronger evidence than mentoring (guidance and support) for improving representation in senior roles — sponsors use their political capital, mentors share information
- **Accountability mechanisms**: mandatory reporting (gender pay gap reporting, ethnicity pay gap reporting), diversity dashboards with line manager accountability, inclusion in management performance objectives
- **Inclusive meetings practices**: explicit facilitation to ensure all voices are heard; rotating who presents and chairs; follow-up on ideas that were dismissed or overlooked

## What Evaluate Requires Across AC1–AC3

**AC1 — Evaluate legislation and organisational policies**

Do not list what the Equality Act says. Evaluate what it requires of managers, where it creates genuine obligation versus discretion, and how your organisation's EDI policy compares — whether it extends beyond the Act's minimums and where it has gaps. Consider the Public Sector Equality Duty if relevant.

**AC2 — Evaluate the impact of inequality and discrimination**

Draw on research evidence. The psychological impact of discrimination — reduced self-esteem, heightened anxiety, lowered motivation (Jones et al., 2016) — is documented. The organisational impact — disengagement, attrition, litigation risk, reputational damage, reduced innovation from under-representation of diverse perspectives (Hunt et al., 2015, McKinsey) — is also evidenced. Evaluate the evidence, do not describe it.

**AC3 — Evaluate approaches to developing an inclusive culture**

Do not list EDI initiatives. Evaluate the evidence on what produces genuine inclusion. Engage with the Dobbin and Kalev (2016) findings on training. Examine structural interventions and their evidence base. Consider intersectionality — an approach that addresses gender but not race, or disability but not age, is not building an inclusive culture. Reach a justified conclusion about the priority approaches for your organisation's context.

## Common Mistakes in CMI 505

**AC1 becomes a list of protected characteristics.** Listing the nine characteristics is awareness content from Level 3. Evaluating the legislation means examining what it requires managers to do, where it creates employer obligations, and where organisational policy must go further than the legal minimum.

**AC2 describes rather than evaluates.** Stating that discrimination is harmful is description. Evaluating the impact means drawing on research evidence — psychological wellbeing research, organisational performance studies, tribunal and litigation data — and reaching a conclusion about the most significant impacts in your organisational context.

**AC3 recommends diversity training as the primary response.** The evidence on standalone diversity training is weak. An AC3 that recommends training without engaging with this evidence and examining structural alternatives will not achieve Merit. Evaluate the full range of approaches and justify your recommendations.

**No intersectionality.** Treating protected characteristics as separate dimensions misses the compound disadvantage experienced at multiple intersections. A Black woman faces different barriers than a white woman or a Black man — intersectionality (Crenshaw, 1989) requires that EDI approaches address overlapping characteristics, not each characteristic in isolation.

**Confusing diversity and inclusion.** An organisation can have diverse headcount and still have an exclusionary culture — if diverse employees are not included in decision-making, if their perspectives are not valued, if they must conform to a dominant cultural norm to advance. Distinction-level analysis distinguishes between diversity as representation and inclusion as belonging.

## Merit vs Distinction in CMI 505

A **Merit** in CMI 505 Evaluates the Equality Act 2010 with genuine engagement beyond listing protected characteristics, provides evidence-based analysis of discrimination's impact on individuals and organisations, and Evaluates EDI approaches with some engagement with the research evidence — for example, acknowledging the limits of bias training.

A **Distinction** does all of the above and engages with intersectionality, distinguishes diversity from inclusion and belonging using Shore et al. (2011) or equivalent, applies Dobbin and Kalev's (2016) critique of bias training explicitly, evaluates structural interventions with their evidence base, and reaches a justified conclusion about the priority approaches for the specific organisational context described — with SMART recommendations that follow directly from the evaluation.
