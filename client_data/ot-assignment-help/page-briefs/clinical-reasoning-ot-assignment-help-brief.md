#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions About Clinical Reasoning in OT Assignments — *Supplementary Content*

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **What is the difference between procedural and conditional reasoning in OT?**
   → Answer direction: Procedural reasoning asks "what is wrong and what should I do about it?" — it focuses on the disability, the diagnosis, and the indicated treatment. The OT using procedural reasoning identifies impaired performance skills, selects standardised assessments, and applies evidence-based interventions to specific deficits. Conditional reasoning asks "who is this person as a whole, and what does their future look like?" — it encompasses the client's life story, roles, values, and envisioned future occupational self. The OT using conditional reasoning sets goals that reflect the client's desired future rather than simply remedying the identified deficit. In a case study, excellent assignments use BOTH types: procedural reasoning identifies the problem; conditional reasoning ensures the solution is meaningful to this specific person. Assignments that use only procedural reasoning receive feedback such as "needs a more holistic, client-centred approach."

2. **How do I show clinical reasoning in a case study assignment rather than just describing what happened?**
   → Answer direction: Clinical reasoning becomes visible in an assignment when you explain WHY each clinical decision was made, not just WHAT you did. Use this test: for every clinical decision in your case study, add a "because" clause. "I assessed using the COPM" shows no reasoning. "I selected the COPM because conditional reasoning required an assessment that elicited the client's own occupational priorities — standardised performance assessments alone cannot capture what matters most to the client" shows explicit reasoning. Name the reasoning type. Reference the theoretical framework or evidence that supports the decision. State the specific clinical rationale. Examiners are trained to look for these three components in every clinical decision paragraph: the decision, the reasoning type, and the justification.

3. **What is narrative reasoning and how do I use it in an OT essay?**
   → Answer direction: Narrative reasoning involves understanding the client's occupational story — the account of who they were before illness or injury, what occupations gave their life meaning, and what they hope to return to. It is accessed through listening to the client's narrative rather than observing their functional deficits. In an OT assignment, narrative reasoning appears when you: (a) discuss the client's pre-morbid occupational identity (roles, meaningful occupations, occupational history); (b) interpret OPHI-II results in terms of the client's life story; (c) connect the CMOP-E spirituality construct to occupational meaning as expressed in the client's own words. Example sentence: "Narrative reasoning, applied through the occupational history section of the OPHI-II, revealed that the client had defined herself through the role of primary school teacher for 28 years — a role whose loss constituted the central occupational disruption of her stroke experience."

4. **What is the Dreyfus model and how do I apply it in a reflective OT essay?**
   → Answer direction: The Dreyfus Novice-to-Expert model describes 5 stages of skill acquisition: Novice (rule-governed, no context-discernment), Advanced Beginner (beginning to recognise meaningful patterns), Competent (deliberate planning, responsibility-aware), Proficient (holistic situational perception), and Expert (intuitive grasp, fluid action). In a placement reflective essay, apply it by: (a) identifying which stage best characterises your current reasoning (typically Novice to Advanced Beginner for BSc students); (b) providing specific examples from placement that illustrate your current stage; (c) identifying what experience and reflection are needed to move toward the next stage; (d) discussing what expert OT reasoning looks like and why it cannot simply be taught but must be developed through supervised practice. The Dreyfus model should be cited — Dreyfus and Dreyfus (1980) for the original; Schell and Schell (2008) or Benner (1984) for healthcare applications.

5. **How do interactive and pragmatic reasoning differ, and when does each appear in OT assignments?**
   → Answer direction: Interactive reasoning concerns the therapeutic relationship — what is happening between therapist and client in the moment of intervention. It involves therapeutic use of self, adjusting communication style, responding to non-verbal cues, and maintaining rapport. In an assignment, interactive reasoning most commonly appears in reflective journals and practice placement essays: "Interactive reasoning led me to shift from a structured task-based approach to a conversational format when I noticed the client's body language suggesting anxiety about task performance." Pragmatic reasoning concerns real-world constraints — time, resources, setting policies, funding, carer availability, and environmental barriers. In an assignment, pragmatic reasoning appears in discharge planning sections, community OT essays, and case study modification sections: "Pragmatic reasoning identified that the 2-week CIMT protocol was not feasible in the outpatient clinic given the 45-minute session limit — modified CIMT (1 hour daily) was therefore substituted, with evidence supporting comparable outcomes for less impaired clients."
