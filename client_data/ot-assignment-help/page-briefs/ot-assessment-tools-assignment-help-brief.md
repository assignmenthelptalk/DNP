#### H2: OT Assessment Categories — Standardised, Performance-Based, Self-Report and Screening — *Major Query Need: Understanding assessment categories for OT assignments*
> **Context vector**: Assessment classification framework — establishes the organising taxonomy before individual tools
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Performance-based/observational: OT observes actual task performance; avoids self-report bias; examples: AMPS, Kitchen Task Assessment, Barthel (partially); more valid for clients with cognitive impairment or communication difficulties
> - Self-report: client rates their own performance, satisfaction, or impairment; examples: COPM, DASH; high face validity and client-centred; validity compromised when client has significant cognitive impairment
> - Proxy-report: carer, family member, or clinician rates on behalf of the client; examples: MOHOST, Barthel in moderate-severe dementia; reduces self-report validity concerns; introduces carer perspective bias
> - Standardised: validated tool with published psychometric properties, specific administration protocol, scoring manual, and population norms; examples: all five tools on this page; required for comparisons across clients and research
> - Non-standardised: informal clinical assessment, adapted tools, unstructured clinical observation; appropriate when standardised tools are unavailable or contraindicated; lower evidence weight in assignments
> - Screening: brief tool identifying presence/absence of difficulty; examples: MoCA, ACLS; precedes comprehensive assessment
> - Comprehensive: detailed tool describing nature and extent of difficulty; examples: AMPS, Vineland; used after screening indicates area of concern
> **Predicate to use**: "categorises" — classification framework predicate
> **First word of first paragraph**: "Occupational therapy assessments categorise into performance-based, self-report, and proxy-report formats..." — classification predicate from first clause
> **Writer note**: Students frequently use the wrong assessment category for a given client in assignments. Cover the matching principle: performance-based assessments are more valid for clients with cognitive impairment or communication difficulties; self-report is more valid for cognitively intact clients with client-centred occupational goals. This is the clinical reasoning element behind tool selection.
> **Word count target**: 280–360 words
> **Internal links**: None

#### H2: COPM — Client-Centred Occupational Performance Measure — Full Scoring and Application — *Major Query Need: How to use and document COPM in OT assignments*
> **Context vector**: COPM as the primary client-centred OT assessment — full attribute coverage
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - COPM purpose: identify occupational performance problems, prioritise by importance, score performance and satisfaction; client-centred because the client defines the problems
> - Three occupation categories: self-care (personal care, functional mobility, community management), productivity (paid/unpaid work, household management, play/school), leisure (quiet recreation, active recreation, socialisation)
> - Importance scale: 1–10; used during problem identification; client rates how important each identified occupational problem is; top 5 most important problems are selected for scoring
> - Performance scale: 1–10 (1 = not able to do it at all; 10 = able to do it extremely well); rated by the client
> - Satisfaction scale: 1–10 (1 = not satisfied at all; 10 = extremely satisfied); rated by the client
> - COPM score calculation: sum of performance scores for selected problems ÷ number of problems = mean performance score; same for satisfaction
> - Clinically significant change: ≥2 points change on performance or satisfaction scale = clinically meaningful improvement
> - Administration: semi-structured interview; problem identification first; then importance rating; then prioritisation to top 5; then performance and satisfaction scoring; 20–40 minutes
> - Population: validated across multiple populations — stroke, dementia, mental health, paediatrics, acquired brain injury
> - Proxy report: for moderate-severe dementia or clients with severe communication difficulties, a carer can complete with OT guidance; must be documented in assignment as a proxy administration
> **Predicate to use**: "identifies" — COPM identifies occupational performance problems through client report
> **First word of first paragraph**: "COPM identifies occupational performance priorities through a client-directed semi-structured interview..." — tool purpose and method in opening clause
> **Writer note**: Students most commonly misrepresent COPM by treating it as only a performance scale. Cover all three scales (importance, performance, satisfaction) and explain the role of importance scoring in problem prioritisation. The MCID of ≥2 points is essential for any assignment that uses COPM for pre/post outcome comparison.
> **Word count target**: 350–430 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: COPM Score Calculation and Clinically Significant Change — What OT Assignments Must Report
> **Attribute + Value**: Score calculation: sum all selected problem performance scores ÷ number of selected problems = mean performance score (e.g., five problems scored 3, 4, 2, 5, 4 → sum 18 ÷ 5 = 3.6 COPM performance score); same calculation for satisfaction; clinically significant change = ≥2 point increase in mean performance OR satisfaction score between pre- and post-intervention administrations; without the ≥2 threshold, a 1-point change may be within measurement error
> **Sequence modeling note**: "A COPM performance score of 3.6 indicates..." — specific calculated value in subject position; demonstrates the calculation being interpreted
> **Writer note**: Include a brief calculation example. Students who report "the client's COPM score improved" without stating the numeric change and comparing it to the ≥2 threshold lose clinical reasoning marks.

#### H2: AMPS — Assessment of Motor and Process Skills — Logit Scores and Certified Administration — *Major Query Need: How to document and interpret AMPS in OT assignments*
> **Context vector**: AMPS as the gold-standard performance-based OT functional assessment — Rasch-based, certified rater, cross-diagnostic
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - AMPS purpose: measures quality of ADL motor and process skill performance through direct observation; not task completion but HOW tasks are performed
> - Motor skills: 16 items — examples include posture, mobilises, stabilises, aligns, positions, walks, reaches, bends, grips, manipulates, coordinates, moves, lifts, calibrates, flows, endures
> - Process skills: 20 items — examples include paces, attends, heeds, chooses, uses, handles, inquires, initiates, continues, sequences, terminates, searches/locates, gathers, organises, restores, navigates, notices/responds, accommodates, adjusts, benefits
> - Quality basis: each skill is rated on how efficiently and safely the person performs the ADL task — errors, effort, safety, and independence are considered
> - Rater requirement: AMPS administration requires completion of a certified AMPS training course; students on placement cannot administer AMPS independently — assignments must reflect this
> - Rasch analysis: raw AMPS skill ratings are converted to logit measures using Rasch analysis; this creates an equal-interval scale allowing meaningful comparisons across different tasks and populations
> - Interpretation thresholds: ADL motor ≥2.0 logits = independent functional ADL motor performance; ADL process ≥1.0 logits = independent functional ADL process performance
> - Cross-diagnostic: AMPS is validated across populations (stroke, dementia, autism, mental health, physical disability); this is its primary advantage over condition-specific tools
> - Assignment use: in OT case study assignments, AMPS results are reported as logit scores with interpretation (above or below threshold); motor and process scores are reported separately
> **Predicate to use**: "measures" — AMPS measures quality of skill performance; maintains measurement context
> **First word of first paragraph**: "AMPS measures the quality of ADL motor and process skill performance..." — tool and measurement object in opening clause
> **Writer note**: The certified rater requirement is the most commonly misrepresented AMPS attribute in student assignments. A student who writes "I administered the AMPS to assess the client's functional performance" has made a professional scope of practice error — on placement, AMPS is administered by a certified OT, not a student. Assignments must describe AMPS as administered by the supervising OT or as a hypothetical future tool pending certification.
> **Word count target**: 350–450 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: Interpreting AMPS Logit Scores — Below Threshold and Above Threshold in OT Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**: ADL motor <2.0 logits = difficulty with physical aspects of ADL performance (e.g., moving, reaching, gripping — requires OT intervention for motor-based ADL goals); ADL motor ≥2.0 logits = independent motor performance (OT may still intervene for process deficits); ADL process <1.0 logits = difficulty with organisation, sequencing, and adaptive aspects of task performance (e.g., initiating, problem-solving during tasks — requires OT intervention for cognitive/process-based ADL goals); in assignments, students report both motor and process scores and interpret what each threshold means for the specific client's occupational goals
> **Writer note**: The dual threshold structure (different values for motor and process) is frequently simplified in student assignments to a single score. Both thresholds must be reported and interpreted separately.

#### H2: MOHOST — Model of Human Occupation Screening Tool — Six Sections and Rating Scale — *Major Query Need: MOHOST scoring and mental health OT application*
> **Context vector**: MOHOST as the MOHO-derived observational screening tool for mental health OT settings
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - MOHOST derivation: based on the Model of Human Occupation (MOHO); aligns with MOHO's three performance components (volition, habituation, performance capacity) plus environment
> - Six sections: (1) Motivation for Occupation — occupational values, interests, personal causation; (2) Pattern of Occupation — roles, habits, routines; (3) Communication and Interaction Skills — interpersonal communication in occupation; (4) Process Skills — cognitive aspects of occupational performance; (5) Motor Skills — physical aspects of occupational performance; (6) Environment — space, objects, occupational forms, social groups
> - Rating scale: 4-point for each item: Facilitates (F) — the factor actively supports occupational participation; Allows (A) — the factor does not impede participation; Inhibits (I) — the factor interferes with participation; Restricts (R) — the factor severely limits participation
> - Total items: 24 items across 6 sections
> - Administration: 15–20 minutes; observational; can be completed from clinical notes, OT session observation, or multidisciplinary team information; does not require direct client interaction in the scoring phase
> - Primary setting: mental health OT; inpatient and community mental health; acute psychiatric units; rehabilitation units
> - Assignment application: MOHOST profiles the client's occupational functioning across all six domains; each section rating justifies OT goal-setting for that domain
> **Predicate to use**: "profiles" — MOHOST profiles occupational functioning across six domains
> **First word of first paragraph**: "MOHOST profiles occupational functioning across six domains using a four-point rating scale..." — tool and its action in opening clause
> **Writer note**: Students using MOHOST in assignments must connect each section rating to MOHO concepts. A Section 1 (Motivation for Occupation) rating of Inhibits must be connected to MOHO's volition construct — "the client's low personal causation (MOHO volition component) inhibits occupational engagement as shown in MOHOST Section 1 rating of I." This theory-to-tool connection is what examiners look for in mental health OT case studies.
> **Word count target**: 300–380 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: MOHOST Rating Scale — F, A, I, R — Applying the Four-Point Scale in OT Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**: Facilitates (F) = the factor actively facilitates or supports occupational participation — above the threshold; Allows (A) = the factor neither facilitates nor inhibits, within the typical range; Inhibits (I) = the factor begins to impede occupational participation — below the threshold, requires OT attention; Restricts (R) = the factor severely limits occupational participation — most clinically significant rating; in assignment documentation, each of the 24 items receives one of the four ratings with a brief justification from clinical observation
> **Writer note**: The F-A-I-R scale is frequently described in assignments without justification. Each rating must be supported by a specific clinical observation or documented behaviour. "Section 2 Pattern of Occupation rated Inhibits (I) because the client is unable to maintain consistent daily routines due to positive symptoms disrupting habitual occupational patterns" is a complete MOHOST documentation entry for an OT assignment.

#### H2: FIM — Functional Independence Measure — Motor and Cognitive Subtotals — *Major Query Need: FIM scoring and interpretation in OT rehabilitation assignments*
> **Context vector**: FIM as the rehabilitation outcome measure — 18-item, 7-point scale with motor/cognitive split
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - FIM purpose: measures the amount of assistance required for ADL performance in rehabilitation settings; used for admission-discharge outcome comparison
> - 18 items: 13 motor items (eating, grooming, bathing, upper body dressing, lower body dressing, toileting, bladder management, bowel management, transfers: bed/chair/wheelchair, transfers: toilet, transfers: tub/shower, locomotion: walk/wheelchair, locomotion: stairs) + 5 cognitive items (comprehension, expression, social interaction, problem solving, memory)
> - 7-point scale: 7 = complete independence (no helper, no device); 6 = modified independence (uses device or takes more time); 5 = supervision or setup (helper required but no physical assistance); 4 = minimal assist (patient provides ≥75% of effort); 3 = moderate assist (patient provides 50–74% of effort); 2 = maximal assist (patient provides 25–49% of effort); 1 = total assist (patient provides <25% of effort)
> - Motor subtotal: 13 items × up to 7 = range 13–91
> - Cognitive subtotal: 5 items × up to 7 = range 5–35
> - Total: 18–126
> - MIF (Minutes of Indirect Function): derived from FIM score — approximation of care minutes required per day; used in healthcare resource planning
> - Application: inpatient rehabilitation; OT contributes motor FIM scoring (ADL performance items); cognitive FIM scored with MDT input
> **Predicate to use**: "quantifies" — FIM quantifies the level of assistance required; distinguishes from qualitative description
> **First word of first paragraph**: "The FIM quantifies the level of assistance required for ADL performance across 18 items..." — measurement purpose in opening clause
> **Writer note**: Students frequently confuse FIM and Barthel Index. The key distinction for assignments: FIM is more granular (7-point scale vs 0/5/10/15 Barthel scoring), separates motor from cognitive functioning, and is more sensitive to small functional changes during inpatient rehabilitation. Barthel is simpler and more widely used across UK NHS settings. Both may appear in a stroke case study — students must know when each is appropriate.
> **Word count target**: 320–400 words
> **Internal links**: None

#### H2: Barthel Index — Ten ADL Items, Scoring, and Interpretation Bands — *Major Query Need: Barthel Index scoring and interpretation for OT assignments*
> **Context vector**: Barthel Index as the most widely used OT outcome measure in UK stroke and rehabilitation — full item-level and interpretation-band coverage
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Barthel Index: 10 ADL items — feeding, bathing, grooming, dressing (upper body and lower body scored separately in some versions; combined in original), bowel control, bladder control, toilet use, transfers (bed to chair), mobility on level surface, climbing stairs
> - Scoring: 0/5/10/15 — not all items reach 15; most items score 0/5/10; mobility and transfers score up to 15; this unequal scoring means the maximum is 100 despite 10 items
> - Total score range: 0–100
> - Interpretation bands: 0–20 = total dependence (requires 24-hour nursing care); 21–60 = severe dependence (requires substantial help with most ADLs); 61–90 = moderate dependence (requires help with some ADLs); 91–99 = slight dependence (requires minimal help — possibly one task); 100 = full independence
> - UK context: standard outcome measure in NHS stroke units; Sentinel Stroke National Audit Programme (SSNAP) uses Barthel at admission and discharge as a key outcome metric
> - Admission-discharge use: Barthel score at admission and discharge quantifies functional gain during inpatient rehabilitation; in OT assignments, this comparison is used to evaluate intervention effectiveness
> - Limitations for OT case studies: Barthel does not capture IADL performance (only basic ADLs); does not capture quality of performance (only whether dependent); does not assess cognitive function; students should acknowledge these limitations in critical appraisal sections
> **Predicate to use**: "measures" — Barthel measures functional dependence across ten ADL items
> **First word of first paragraph**: "The Barthel Index measures functional dependence across ten ADL items using a 0/5/10/15 scoring system..." — tool and its measurement basis immediately
> **Writer note**: All five interpretation bands (0–20, 21–60, 61–90, 91–99, 100) must be named with their labels. Students who cite "a Barthel score of 65" without stating that this falls in the moderate dependence band have not completed the interpretation component of clinical reasoning.
> **Word count target**: 300–380 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: Barthel Index vs FIM — Choosing the Right Tool for OT Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**: Barthel Index — simpler, 10 items, 0–100, widely used UK NHS, appropriate for stroke and general rehabilitation OT; FIM — more detailed, 18 items (motor + cognitive split), 7-point scale, more sensitive to small changes in severe dependence range, more commonly used in formal rehabilitation programmes and research; choose Barthel when: UK NHS clinical context, stroke unit assignment, community OT post-discharge; choose FIM when: inpatient rehabilitation research context, need to capture cognitive functioning separately, admission-discharge change in severe dependence range; in assignments, justify the choice based on setting and the purpose of the assessment
> **Sequence modeling note**: "The Barthel Index is appropriate when..." — tool as subject with contextual condition; maintains parallel structure with FIM comparison
> **Writer note**: This H3 directly answers the representative query "Barthel vs FIM OT assignment." Students writing about both tools in the same assignment need to know when each is appropriate — justify tool choice as part of clinical reasoning marks.

#### H2: Psychometric Properties — Validity, Reliability and MCID in OT Assessment Assignments — *Major Query Need: Critical appraisal of OT assessment tools*
> **Context vector**: Psychometric properties as the academic critical appraisal framework for OT assessment tools
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Validity types:
>   - Content validity: does the tool measure all relevant aspects of the construct? (e.g., does the Barthel capture all ADLs that matter for rehabilitation outcome?)
>   - Construct validity: does the tool measure the theoretical construct it claims to? (e.g., does AMPS actually measure occupational performance quality?)
>   - Criterion validity: does the tool correlate with another established gold-standard measure of the same construct?
> - Reliability types:
>   - Inter-rater reliability: agreement between different raters scoring the same performance; ICC (Intraclass Correlation Coefficient) ≥0.75 = acceptable threshold
>   - Test-retest reliability: consistency of the same rater over time on the same client; important for outcome monitoring
>   - Internal consistency: consistency across items within the same tool (Cronbach's alpha ≥0.7 typical threshold)
> - Sensitivity: ability to detect true positives (people who have the difficulty); a highly sensitive tool misses few cases
> - Specificity: ability to correctly identify true negatives (people who do not have the difficulty); a highly specific tool generates few false positives
> - Sensitivity-specificity trade-off: increasing sensitivity often reduces specificity; important for screening tools; OT assignments at MSc level discuss this trade-off when justifying screening vs comprehensive assessment decisions
> - MCID (Minimum Clinically Important Difference): the smallest change in score that represents a meaningful difference to the patient; COPM = ≥2 points change; important for evaluating whether rehabilitation goals were meaningfully achieved
> **Predicate to use**: "determines" — validity determines whether the tool measures what it claims; "establishes" — reliability establishes consistency of measurement
> **First word of first paragraph**: "Psychometric properties determine whether an OT assessment tool produces valid and reliable measurements..." — properties as determinants of measurement quality
> **Writer note**: Students at Level 6 and Level 7 are required to critically appraise the assessment tools they use in assignments. "The Barthel Index is a valid and reliable tool" is not critical appraisal. "The Barthel Index demonstrates high inter-rater reliability (ICC ≥0.85 in stroke populations) but has limited content validity for IADL assessment and cognitive functioning, which are relevant OT domains" is Level 6 critical appraisal.
> **Word count target**: 300–380 words
> **Internal links**: None

#### H2: FAQ — OT Assessment Tools Assignment Questions
> **Critical rule**: Extend toward new representative query variants. Do not repeat main content.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **What is the clinically significant change for COPM and why does it matter in OT assignments?**
   → Answer direction: A clinically significant change on the COPM is ≥2 points on the performance or satisfaction scale — this means the change is large enough to be considered meaningful to the client, beyond measurement error. In OT assignments, this threshold matters because it is used to evaluate whether an intervention achieved a meaningful outcome. If a client's COPM performance score improved from 3.2 to 4.9 (a change of 1.7 points), this does not meet the ≥2 threshold — the OT must acknowledge this in the outcome evaluation section. If it improved from 3.2 to 5.5 (a change of 2.3 points), this is a clinically significant improvement that can be cited as evidence of intervention effectiveness.

2. **Can a student on placement administer the AMPS?**
   → Answer direction: No. AMPS administration requires completion of a certified AMPS training course delivered by an AMPS trainer. OT students on placement are not qualified to administer AMPS independently. In assignments, students should describe AMPS as administered by the supervising (certified) OT, or note that AMPS assessment was recommended as a next step requiring a certified rater. Writing "I administered the AMPS" in a case study assignment when the student is a placement student misrepresents professional scope of practice and may be flagged by markers.

3. **What is the difference between MOHOST and COPM and when would you use each in an OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: COPM is a client-directed self-report tool — the client identifies their occupational performance priorities through an interview; it is most appropriate when the client has sufficient communication ability and insight to self-report. MOHOST is an observational screening tool derived from MOHO — the OT observes and rates the client's occupational functioning across six domains; it is appropriate in mental health settings where client insight may be limited or where a broad occupational profile across MOHO domains is needed. Both can be used in the same case study — COPM for client-centred goal identification and MOHOST for MOHO-based functional profiling — and both must be justified in terms of tool selection rationale.

4. **What does an ICC of ≥0.75 mean for OT assessment tool reliability in an assignment?**
   → Answer direction: ICC (Intraclass Correlation Coefficient) ≥0.75 is the accepted threshold for good inter-rater reliability in OT research — it means that when two different raters score the same client on the same tool, their scores agree at a level considered acceptable for clinical and research use. In OT assignments at Level 6/7, citing a specific ICC value when discussing a tool's reliability demonstrates psychometric literacy: "The Barthel Index demonstrates acceptable inter-rater reliability (ICC 0.87–0.99 in stroke populations), making it a reliable outcome measure for comparing admission and discharge functional status." ICC values below 0.75 indicate poor inter-rater agreement and would be a limitation to identify in critical appraisal sections.

5. **What are the limitations of the Barthel Index that I should acknowledge in an OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: The Barthel Index has four significant limitations relevant to OT assignments: (1) It measures only basic ADLs — IADL performance (cooking, managing finances, community mobility) is not captured, so functional independence beyond basic self-care is underestimated; (2) It measures independence level only — quality of performance, safety, and efficiency are not captured (contrast with AMPS which does capture these); (3) Ceiling effect — clients who score 100 on the Barthel may still have significant occupational performance difficulties in IADLs, work, and leisure; (4) Scoring granularity — the 0/5/10/15 scale means small incremental changes in moderate-to-severe dependence ranges may not be captured, making it less sensitive than FIM for detecting small functional gains during rehabilitation. Acknowledging at least two of these limitations in an assignment critical appraisal section demonstrates Level 6 analytical depth.
