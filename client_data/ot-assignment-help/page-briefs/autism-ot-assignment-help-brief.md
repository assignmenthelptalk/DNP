#### H2: OT Autism Assessments — Sensory Profile 2, MABC-2, Vineland and More — *Major Query Need: Which assessment tools to use in autism OT assignments*
> **Context vector**: Assessment tool selection and interpretation for autism OT case study and essay assignments
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Sensory Profile 2 (SP2): four-quadrant model — Seeker (high threshold + active self-regulation), Avoider (low threshold + active self-regulation), Sensor (low threshold + passive self-regulation), Bystander (high threshold + passive self-regulation); score interpretation: Much More Than Others (>2 SD above norm), More Than Others (1–2 SD), Similar To Others (within 1 SD), Less Than Others / Much Less Than Others (below norms); versions: Infant (birth–6 months), Toddler (7–35 months), Child (3–14 years 11 months), Adolescent/Adult (15+); caregiver report tool
> - MABC-2 (Movement Assessment Battery for Children, 2nd edition): standardised motor assessment; three bands by age (3–6 years, 7–10 years, 11–16 years); three components (manual dexterity, aiming and catching, balance); total score gives percentile; ≤5th percentile = movement difficulty requiring OT intervention
> - Vineland Adaptive Behaviour Scales (3rd edition): adaptive behaviour domains (Communication, Daily Living Skills, Socialisation, Motor Skills); used to document functional independence level in autism OT assignments; normed population includes autism spectrum
> - CAPE (Children's Assessment of Participation and Enjoyment): leisure participation frequency, involvement, enjoyment; useful in autism OT assignments to document restricted participation patterns and occupation-based goals
> - ADOS-2 (Autism Diagnostic Observation Schedule, 2nd edition): diagnostic tool; OT does not administer ADOS-2 independently but uses diagnostic findings as assessment context; in assignments, students should reference ADOS-2 diagnosis as the clinical background, not as an OT assessment output
> **Predicate to use**: "profiles" — Sensory Profile 2 specifically profiles sensory processing; use "measures" for MABC-2 and Vineland
> **First word of first paragraph**: "The Sensory Profile 2 profiles sensory processing across four quadrants..." — most OT-specific autism assessment leads
> **Writer note**: Students frequently confuse the ADOS-2 as an OT tool. Clarify: ADOS-2 is a diagnostic tool administered by psychologists or speech-language pathologists — OT uses the diagnosis as context, not as their own assessment output. This distinction is a common source of mark loss in assignments.
> **Word count target**: 350–450 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: Sensory Profile 2 — Four Quadrants Explained for OT Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**: Seeker profile — autistic individual actively seeks additional sensory input; behaviours appear as touching everything, seeking movement, making noise; OT implication = provide structured sensory opportunities; Avoider profile — actively avoids sensory input through behaviour; may appear as rule-making, rigidity, avoidance routines; OT implication = respect regulatory strategies, introduce sensory modification gradually; Sensor profile — passively over-responds; appears as distractibility, emotional reactivity to sensory events; OT implication = environmental modification to reduce sensory load; Bystander profile — passively under-registers; appears disengaged, misses sensory cues; OT implication = increase sensory salience, use alerting input

> **Writer note**: Students must not apply a single quadrant profile to all autistic individuals in assignments — every autistic person has a unique Sensory Profile 2 pattern, and many show mixed profiles across sensory systems. #### H2: OT Autism Interventions — From Ayres SI to CO-OP and AAC — *Major Query Need: Which interventions to include in an autism OT case study*
> **Context vector**: Intervention approaches for autism OT case studies — ranging from sensory integration to cognitive and participation-based interventions
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Ayres Sensory Integration (ASI): five fidelity criteria — (1) SIPT-informed assessment, (2) active child participation (child initiates and directs activity), (3) enhanced sensory opportunities (controlled sensory environment), (4) just-right challenge (activity graded to optimal level), (5) therapeutic alliance (relationship quality between therapist and child); requires certified rater/therapist; evidence Level 2 moderate with ongoing research
> - Sensory-based activities: lack formal fidelity criteria; often administered by non-SI-certified staff; different (lower) evidence base from true Ayres SI — this distinction is academically important
> - JASPER (Joint Attention Symbolic Play Engagement Regulation): targets joint attention, symbolic play, and social engagement; delivered by OT and SLT; useful in early childhood autism assignments
> - DIR/Floortime: four engagement levels — (1) shared attention and regulation, (2) engagement and relating, (3) two-way intentional communication, (4) complex problem-solving and shared social problem-solving; child-led relationship-based approach; OT uses Floortime in paediatric mental health and early intervention settings
> - CO-OP (Cognitive Orientation to daily Occupational Performance): metacognitive strategy training using Goal-Plan-Do-Check cycle; designed for motor skill acquisition; validated for use in autism for motor learning; teaches the child to solve their own motor challenges rather than therapist directing repetition
> - AAC (Augmentative and Alternative Communication): OT contributes to device selection, positioning, and access method; works alongside SLT; relevant in autism OT assignments when communication support is part of the occupational performance goal
> - Visual supports: visual schedules, task cards, environmental labels; OT designs and implements within IADL and school contexts; evidence base for reducing anxiety and supporting routine predictability
> - IADL skill training with task analysis: activity analysis used to break IADL tasks into sequential steps; each step graded for sensory demands; autism-specific adaptations include sensory modification and predictability scaffolding
> **Predicate to use**: "delivers" — OT delivers interventions; professional action context
> **First word of first paragraph**: "Ayres Sensory Integration delivers intervention through five fidelity criteria..." — most clinically specific autism OT intervention leads
> **Writer note**: The Ayres SI vs sensory-based activities distinction is academically critical — students who conflate them lose marks for clinical precision. This distinction belongs in main content here AND has a dedicated supplementary section for deeper treatment.
> **Word count target**: 400–500 words
> **Internal links**: None in this section

##### H3: CO-OP Approach — Motor Learning in Autism OT Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**: CO-OP = Cognitive Orientation to daily Occupational Performance; mechanism = metacognitive strategy training not motor repetition; the child identifies the problem, generates a plan, executes, and evaluates (Goal-Plan-Do-Check); validated for DCD and has research support for autism; OT assignment application = CO-OP is used when motor goals are present (handwriting, ball skills, dressing) and the child has sufficient language and metacognitive capacity; contrasts with motor repetition approaches (more appropriate when language and metacognition are limited)
> **Writer note**: CO-OP is increasingly cited in autism OT assignments at MSc level as an evidence-based alternative to repetition-based motor training. Including CO-OP with its mechanism (Goal-Plan-Do-Check) demonstrates current literature awareness.

##### H3: AAC as an OT Domain — What to Include in Autism Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**: AAC encompasses low-tech (PECS picture exchange communication system, communication boards) and high-tech (speech-generating devices, tablet-based AAC apps); OT role = positioning for AAC access, upper limb access assessment (pointing, eye gaze, switch access), ADL integration of AAC; AAC is a shared domain between OT and SLT — OT assignments must define the OT-specific contribution, not describe the entire AAC process; in autism OT assignments, AAC often appears in IADL sections when communication support is required for task participation
> **Writer note**: Students at Level 6 and 7 are expected to define OT's specific scope within AAC, not describe all of AAC as an OT domain. This nuance separates distinction from pass.

#### H2: FAQ — Autism Occupational Therapy Assignment Questions
> **Critical rule**: Extend toward new representative query variants. Do not repeat main content.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **What is the difference between Ayres Sensory Integration and sensory-based activities in an OT autism assignment?**
   → Answer direction: Ayres Sensory Integration (ASI) meets five specific fidelity criteria — SIPT-informed assessment, active child participation, enhanced sensory opportunities, just-right challenge, and therapeutic alliance. Only when all five criteria are present can the intervention be described as Ayres SI in an assignment. Sensory-based activities (weighted vests, sensory bins, brushing protocols) without adherence to these fidelity criteria are a different category of intervention with a weaker evidence base. Students who conflate the two receive mark deductions for clinical imprecision. Always specify which category the assignment is discussing and justify the evidence level accordingly.

2. **Should I use identity-first or person-first language in my autism OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: Acknowledge both perspectives in the assignment introduction — identity-first ("Autistic person") is preferred by many autistic self-advocacy organisations and reflects the neurodiversity paradigm; person-first ("person with autism") was historically preferred in clinical and educational settings and reflects person-centred practice origins. Current academic best practice in the UK is to use the preferred language of the individual; in academic case studies with fictional clients, acknowledge both approaches and state the language choice made. Avoid "suffering from autism," "autistic traits," or functioning labels ("high/low-functioning") in academic assignments.

3. **What does a Sensory Profile 2 Avoider score mean for OT intervention planning in an assignment?**
   → Answer direction: An Avoider profile = low sensory threshold + active self-regulation. The autistic individual has a lower-than-average threshold for sensory input (becomes over-stimulated more easily) and actively manages this through avoidance behaviours and rule-making (e.g., insisting on same route, refusing new foods, avoiding crowded places). In an OT assignment, an Avoider SP2 result directs the intervention plan toward: environmental modification to reduce sensory demands; gradual and respectful introduction of sensory experiences; collaboration with family to understand and accommodate regulation strategies; and IADL modifications that reduce sensory complexity without forcing exposure.

4. **Which NICE guideline applies to autism occupational therapy for adults?**
   → Answer direction: NICE NG228 "Autism in adults: diagnosis and management" (published 2023) covers autism in adults and is relevant to MSc-level OT assignments covering adult autism practice. NG142 applies to under-19s. Both guidelines support OT involvement; NG228 adds recommendations for mental health co-morbidity management (anxiety, depression) which are common in autistic adults and frequently appear in MSc-level OT case studies. Always check the NICE guideline version date when writing assignments — guidelines are updated periodically.

5. **What is the CO-OP approach and when should I use it in an autism OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: CO-OP (Cognitive Orientation to daily Occupational Performance) is a metacognitive strategy training approach that teaches motor skill acquisition through self-discovery rather than therapist-directed repetition. The Goal-Plan-Do-Check cycle enables the child to identify their own movement problem, generate a strategy, execute and evaluate it. CO-OP is used in autism OT assignments when: (1) the assignment goals include motor skills (handwriting, dressing, ball skills); (2) the client has sufficient language and metacognitive capacity; (3) the student wants to demonstrate a contemporary, evidence-based alternative to drill-based motor training. CO-OP has been validated for both DCD and autism populations, making it suitable for presentations where both co-occur.
