**Values per Attribute**:
- AT → ISO 9999 definition → "any product, instrument, equipment, or technology adapted or specially designed for improving the functioning of a person with a disability" — ISO 9999 is the international classification standard for AT; it provides the academic definition for OT assignment writing
- Low-tech ADL aids → examples → grab rails (bathroom, stair), bath boards/bath seats, shower stools, non-slip mats, dressing aids (long-handled shoehorn, dressing stick, button hook, sock aid), long-handled reachers, Dycem non-slip matting, key turners, jar openers, adapted cutlery (weighted, built-up handle), plate guards, dysphagia cups
- Mobility AT → categories → walking aids (walking frames — Zimmer frame, rollator, wheeled frame; crutches — axillary, elbow; walking sticks — single, quad); manual wheelchairs; powered wheelchairs; scooters (class 2 and class 3); stairlifts; through-floor lifts; vehicle adaptations
- AAC devices → range → low-tech: symbol boards (PECS), communication books, alphabet charts; mid-tech: recorded speech output devices (GoTalk, BIGmack); high-tech: speech-generating devices (SGD) — Tobii Dynavox, Grid software on dedicated devices; eye gaze technology; AAC is primarily SLT-led but OT contributes to mounting, access, and integration into daily occupational contexts
- Environmental controls → types → smart home technology (voice-activated assistants: Amazon Alexa, Google Home for environmental control); environmental control units (ECU) for power wheelchair users; automatic door openers; remote-controlled lighting; video entry systems; OT role: assessment, prescription, training, and integration into occupational routine
- Cognitive aids → types → medication dispensers (electronic reminders); digital calendars; GPS devices/apps (for people with dementia or ABI who become disoriented); digital photograph frames with automated prompts; cueing devices; memory notebooks; OT role: assessment, prescription, training in use as part of compensatory strategy approach
- Vision aids → types → magnifiers, large-print labels, high-contrast adaptations; OT in visual impairment: environmental modification for safety (contrast marking on stairs, lighting assessment)
- Wheelchair assessment → domains → (1) Postural assessment: seating system, trunk support, pelvis alignment, head support; (2) Seat dimensions: seat width, depth, height, backrest height; (3) Pressure mapping: interface pressure to prevent pressure injuries; (4) Propulsion ability: upper limb strength, endurance, hand function for manual; joystick control for powered; (5) Environment access: home dimensions, door widths, thresholds, turning circles; (6) Lifestyle and activity requirements
- DFG → Disabled Facilities Grant → mandatory grant in England, Wales, and Northern Ireland; up to £30,000 in England (higher amounts in Wales); administered by local authority; means-tested above a threshold; used for major home adaptations (through-floor lift, level access shower, ramp, widened doorways, stairlift)
- Minor vs major adaptations → distinction → minor adaptations (< £1,000): grab rails, handrails, bath board, threshold ramps; typically funded directly by local authority without DFG application; major adaptations (> £1,000): structural works — shower room conversions, ramp installations, stairlifts, extensions; DFG application required
- HOME FAST → Westmead Home Safety Assessment / Falls risk tool → 25-item checklist of home fall hazards; items include: floor surfaces, lighting, bathroom safety, stairs, bedroom safety, outdoor access; OT completes HOME FAST during home visit for older adults or neurological conditions; used in falls prevention OT assignments
- AT prescription process → steps → (1) Occupational assessment (identifying functional need, goals, context); (2) Goal-setting with the client (what is the AT intended to enable?); (3) AT trial (where possible — equipment loan, demonstration); (4) Prescription and ordering; (5) Training and integration; (6) Review (is the AT achieving its intended goal?; is it still needed?; has the person's condition changed?)
- Trusted Assessor model → definition → a model where trained non-OT staff (e.g., social care staff, housing officers) can assess for and prescribe minor AT adaptations (grab rails, bath boards) without individual OT assessment for each item; OT sets the parameters; used to reduce waiting times and OT caseload burden
- AT evidence → Cochrane reviews → Campbell et al systematic reviews on home safety assessment and modification for falls prevention in older adults — demonstrate moderate to strong evidence for OT-led home modification in reducing falls; key citation for community OT and falls prevention assignments

#### H2: FAQ — Assistive Technology in OT Assignments
> **Critical rule**: New representative query variants only.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. **What is the Disabled Facilities Grant and how is it relevant to OT?** → Answer direction: The DFG is a mandatory local authority grant of up to £30,000 (in England) for home adaptations that enable disabled people to live independently. OTs are typically the lead assessors for DFG applications — the OT's assessment and recommendation report is the primary evidence for the grant decision. DFG-funded adaptations include through-floor lifts, ramp construction, level-access showers, and door widening.
2. **What is the difference between major and minor home adaptations in OT?** → Answer direction: Minor adaptations (generally under £1,000) include grab rails, handrails, bath boards, and threshold ramps — typically funded directly by local authority without a full DFG application. Major adaptations (over £1,000) include structural works such as stairlifts, shower room conversions, ramp construction, and extensions — these require a DFG application, means-testing, and a formal OT assessment recommendation.
3. **What are the domains of an OT wheelchair assessment?** → Answer direction: Six domains: (1) postural assessment (trunk, pelvis, head support); (2) seat dimensions (width, depth, height, backrest height); (3) pressure mapping (pressure injury prevention); (4) propulsion ability (upper limb function for manual; control assessment for powered); (5) environment access (home dimensions, turning circles); (6) lifestyle and activity requirements (storage, transport, sports).
4. **What is a Trusted Assessor in OT?** → Answer direction: A Trusted Assessor is a trained non-OT professional (social care worker, housing officer, community worker) who has been trained to assess for and prescribe minor assistive technology (grab rails, bath boards, basic ADL aids) without requiring an individual OT assessment for each item. OTs establish the assessment criteria; Trusted Assessors apply them to reduce waiting times and OT caseload pressure.
5. **What evidence supports OT-led home modification for falls prevention?** → Answer direction: Campbell and colleagues' Cochrane systematic reviews provide the strongest evidence — demonstrating that OT-led home safety assessment and modification reduces fall rate, particularly in older adults at high risk of falls (vision impairment, previous falls history). NICE falls prevention guideline (NG161) recommends home safety assessment by OT as a component of multifactorial falls risk assessment. Students should cite Campbell et al and NICE NG161 when writing about home modification evidence in OT literature reviews or case studies.
