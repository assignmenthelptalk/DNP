#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions About Mental Health OT Assignments — *Supplementary Content*

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **How do I write an occupational formulation in a mental health OT case study?**
   → Answer direction: An occupational formulation is a synthesising narrative (typically 200–400 words) that integrates assessment findings to explain a client's occupational situation. It has four components: (1) Occupational Identity — who the client is/was as an occupational being (draw on OPHI-II life history or client's description of past roles and valued occupations); (2) Occupational Competence — current capacity to maintain occupational participation (reference MOHOST or OCAIRS scores directly); (3) Environmental Impact — how specific environmental factors enable or restrict participation (name specific physical, social, and institutional environment factors); (4) Recommendation — what OT intervention is indicated based on the formulation, explicitly justified by the three preceding statements. The formulation is NOT a list of problems — it is a clinical narrative that tells the story of the client's occupational situation and points logically toward intervention.

2. **What is the CHIME framework and how do I apply it in an OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: CHIME is a validated framework for understanding personal recovery in mental health, derived from a systematic review of recovery narratives (Leamy et al., 2011). The five dimensions are: Connectedness (social inclusion and relationships), Hope (belief in recovery and motivation for change), Identity (rebuilding a positive sense of self beyond illness), Meaning (finding purpose through occupation and relationships), and Empowerment (having control and agency over one's life). In an OT assignment, CHIME provides the theoretical justification for occupation-focused mental health interventions — each OT goal should be traceable to a CHIME dimension. For example: "the goal of re-engaging the client in a weekly community gardening group addresses the Connectedness and Meaning dimensions of recovery" (Leamy et al., 2011). Always cite the original source.

3. **Can I use both MOHOST and OCAIRS in the same case study assignment?**
   → Answer direction: Yes — MOHOST and OCAIRS can be used together in an assignment, but you must justify why both assessments are chosen and what each uniquely contributes. MOHOST provides a rapid observational screening of occupational functioning across 6 domains — useful in acute inpatient settings where client engagement with interview is limited. OCAIRS provides more detailed interview-based assessment of 12 MOHO-aligned domains — useful in community settings where interview time is available. In a case study, using both shows comprehensive assessment: screen with MOHOST during initial acute phase, then conduct OCAIRS in the community phase for goal setting and care planning. Always explain the clinical rationale for assessment selection — examiners expect you to justify choices, not just list tools used.

4. **How do I apply Dunn's Model of Sensory Processing in a mental health OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: Dunn's Model describes how individuals process sensory input using two axes: neurological threshold (how much sensory input is needed before the nervous system responds — high threshold = lots of input needed; low threshold = minimal input triggers a response) and behavioural response (passive = person allows sensory input to affect them; active = person actively adjusts their environment). These axes produce 4 quadrants: Low Registration (high threshold + passive — person seems unresponsive, flat, under-stimulated; OT: alerting activities); Sensation Seeking (high threshold + active — person seeks extra input; OT: providing sensory-rich opportunities); Sensory Sensitivity (low threshold + passive — person is easily overwhelmed; OT: calming strategies, sensory diet); Sensation Avoiding (low threshold + active — person actively avoids input; OT: structured, predictable environments). In an assignment, identify the client's quadrant using Sensory Profile 2 results or clinical observation, then justify sensory modulation interventions based on their profile.

5. **What evidence base should I cite for mental health OT interventions in assignments?**
   → Answer direction: For mental health OT assignments, cite the following evidence levels per intervention: (1) Occupational therapy for psychosis — Cochrane review evidence (moderate quality); (2) Supported employment for mental illness — IPS model has strong RCT evidence (National Institute for Mental Health in England guidelines; Bond et al. meta-analyses); (3) Sensory modulation in mental health — emerging evidence, mixed quality (Lee et al. systematic reviews; NICE does not yet formally recommend); (4) Behavioural activation for depression — strong evidence from RCTs (NICE CG90/CG91); (5) Occupational therapy in acute mental health — limited but growing MOHOST validation evidence. When using evidence in a mental health OT assignment, always identify the evidence level (systematic review > RCT > cohort study > case study) and acknowledge limitations where relevant.
