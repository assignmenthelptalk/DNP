**Values per Attribute**:
- Kawa → origin → Michael Iwama, Japanese-Canadian OT scholar; developed collaboratively with Japanese OT practitioners in 1990s–2000s; published 2006 (Kawa Model: Culturally Relevant Occupational Therapy, Elsevier)
- Kawa → theoretical purpose → developed as a critique of Western OT models (MOHO, CMOP-E) that embed individualistic, Western assumptions about identity, occupation, and health; designed for contexts where collective identity and interdependence are central values
- Mizu (water) → represents → life energy; occupational flow; the person's life and occupational engagement; when flowing freely = health and well-being
- Kawa (river) → represents → the person's life journey and context; life itself as a flowing river
- Iwa (rocks) → represents → life circumstances and problems; challenges, barriers, difficult relationships, financial problems, illness or disability as experienced by the person; rocks narrow the river channel
- Tokmoku (driftwood) → represents → personal attributes and resources; personal character traits, values, relationships, material assets — can be helpful (opening spaces for flow) or obstructive (adding to blockage)
- Sukima (spaces between rocks and river walls) → represents → occupational therapy — the spaces where water still flows despite obstacles; the OT finds and widens these spaces to increase life flow
- River sides and floor → represents → social and physical environment; the container within which life flows; family, culture, community, physical spaces
- Cross-cultural validity → evidence → validated and applied in Japan, East Asia, Indigenous communities, collectivist cultural contexts; used in Australia with Aboriginal communities; cross-cultural OT literature documents applications beyond Western contexts
- Cross-cultural critique of Western models → specific → MOHO's volition construct assumes individual agency and personal choice are the primary drivers of occupation; CMOP-E's spirituality construct assumes individual spiritual identity; both are less relevant in cultures where collective identity, family role, and interdependence are central
- Mental health OT application → value → externalising problems via metaphor (rocks rather than "symptoms") creates psychological distance and reduces self-blame; collaborative meaning-making process; culturally safe assessment for collectivist or Indigenous contexts
- Kawa assessment process → steps → (1) OT introduces river metaphor; (2) client draws or describes their river (either literally on paper or through discussion); (3) client identifies iwa (rocks/problems), tokmoku (driftwood/assets), sukima (spaces for OT); (4) OT and client collaboratively explore spaces for life flow improvement
- Kawa vs MOHO → comparison → MOHO: structurally explicit (volition, habituation, performance capacity, environment — distinct constructs); MOHO has psychometric assessment tools (MOHOST, OPHI-II); primarily developed in North American/Western context. Kawa: metaphorical and flexible; no standardised psychometric tools; designed for cross-cultural flexibility; externalises problems; more appropriate for collectivist contexts
- Kawa vs CMOP-E → comparison → CMOP-E: Canadian origin; person-environment-occupation triangle; spirituality at centre; COPM as linked assessment; assumes individual occupational performance as the primary construct. Kawa: collective life flow rather than individual performance; no equivalent standardised assessment; better suited for mental health contexts where externalising is therapeutic
- Critique of Kawa → limitations → lacks standardised assessment tools (no psychometric properties to report in evidence-based assignments); less structured than MOHO — makes systematic comparison or outcome measurement difficult; limited peer-reviewed evidence base compared to MOHO; some critics argue the river metaphor can be superficially applied without genuine cross-cultural depth

##### H3: Sukima (Spaces Between Rocks and River Walls) — Where OT Intervenes
> **Attribute + Value**: Sukima → the spaces where water still flows despite obstacles → the OT's role is to find these existing spaces and work to widen them, thereby increasing occupational flow. This is metaphorically and practically different from "fixing the problem" (medical model) or "restoring function" (rehabilitation model). In a mental health OT context, sukima represent the areas of life where the client can still engage meaningfully — the OT supports and expands these.

#### H2: FAQ — Kawa Model Questions from OT Students
> **Critical rule**: New representative query variants only.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. **What does "sukima" mean in the Kawa Model?** → Answer direction: Sukima means "spaces between" in Japanese. In the Kawa Model, sukima represents the spaces between the rocks (problems) and the river walls (environment) where water (life energy/occupation) still flows. This is where OT intervenes — finding and widening existing spaces of occupational flow, rather than removing all obstacles.
2. **Can the Kawa Model be used with non-Japanese clients?** → Answer direction: Yes — the Kawa Model was specifically designed to be cross-cultural. It has been applied with Indigenous Australian communities, in collectivist cultural contexts across East and South-East Asia, and in UK mental health OT practice. The river metaphor is flexible enough to have culturally contextualised meanings across different cultural groups. The OT must approach the metaphor collaboratively rather than imposing a single fixed interpretation.
3. **What is the difference between iwa and tokmoku in the Kawa Model?** → Answer direction: Iwa (rocks) represent life circumstances and problems — challenges that narrow the river flow, such as illness, difficult relationships, or social exclusion. Tokmoku (driftwood) represents personal attributes and resources — characteristics, relationships, and assets that can either help open spaces or add to blockage depending on their position. Iwa are primarily external circumstances; tokmoku are primarily personal characteristics.
4. **Why is the Kawa Model used in mental health OT?** → Answer direction: The Kawa Model is valued in mental health OT because the river metaphor externalises the person's difficulties — the rocks are problems, not the person. This creates psychological safety, reduces self-blame and stigma, and enables collaborative goal-setting without the client identifying themselves as the problem. This is particularly therapeutically valuable in mental health contexts.
5. **Does the Kawa Model have any standardised assessment tools?** → Answer direction: No — the Kawa Model uses the client-drawn river assessment, which is qualitative and collaborative by design. There are no standardised psychometric tools (no scoring scales, no normative data, no published reliability or validity statistics). This is a limitation for evidence-based practice assignments that require quantitative outcome measurement. For assignments requiring psychometric outcomes, MOHO-based tools (MOHOST, OPHI-II) are more appropriate.
