#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions About Paediatric OT Assignments — *Supplementary Content*

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **How do I interpret Sensory Profile 2 results in an OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: The Sensory Profile 2 provides percentile scores across 4 quadrants (Seeking, Avoiding, Sensitivity, Registration) and multiple sensory systems. Interpretation uses three bands: Typical Performance (16th–84th percentile), More Than Others (>84th percentile — the child processes sensory input in this pattern more than age peers), and Less Than Others (<16th percentile — less than peers). In an assignment, report the quadrant name, the percentile band, and the clinical interpretation. For example: "The child's Sensory Profile 2 (Child version) results indicated a More Than Others pattern in the Avoiding quadrant (87th percentile), suggesting a low neurological threshold with active avoidance of sensory input, particularly tactile and auditory stimuli. This pattern is consistent with the sensory processing challenges commonly reported in ASD." Always link the Sensory Profile 2 interpretation to the child's occupational performance difficulties.

2. **What is the MABC-2 cut-off score for diagnosing DCD?**
   → Answer direction: The MABC-2 does not diagnose DCD on its own — it identifies children with movement difficulties who may warrant further investigation. The cut-off for "Movement Difficulty" is a total test score at or below the 5th percentile. However, a DSM-5 diagnosis of Developmental Coordination Disorder additionally requires: (1) that motor performance is significantly below what is expected for chronological age (MABC-2 <5th percentile provides this evidence); (2) that motor difficulties significantly interfere with daily activities or academic achievement; (3) that the onset was in the early developmental period; and (4) that motor difficulties are not explained by intellectual disability, visual impairment, or another neurological condition. In an assignment, never state that MABC-2 alone diagnoses DCD — always situate it within the multi-criteria diagnostic framework.

3. **What is the difference between Ayres Sensory Integration and sensory-based activities?**
   → Answer direction: Ayres Sensory Integration (ASI) is a specific, fidelity-defined clinical intervention with 10 published fidelity criteria (Parham et al.). It requires: a certified ASI therapist, a sensory-enriched environment with specialised suspended equipment, child-directed (not therapist-directed) activity, just-right challenge, and use of the SIPT or equivalent standardised sensory processing assessment. Sensory-based activities (SBAs) are any activities that incorporate sensory input — weighted blankets, fidget tools, sensory bins — but without adherence to ASI fidelity criteria. In an assignment, this distinction is critical: ASI has a developing but increasingly supported evidence base; SBAs have limited evidence and cannot be referred to as "sensory integration therapy" without meeting fidelity criteria. NICE (for autism) and the Royal College of Occupational Therapists recommend using the term accurately.

4. **How do I write about CO-OP in a paediatric OT case study?**
   → Answer direction: In a CO-OP case study, structure your intervention section around the 4 components: Goal (present the child's self-identified goal — e.g., "learning to ride a bicycle" — obtained through COPM-C or child interview), Plan (describe how Goal-Plan-Do-Check was introduced as the global cognitive strategy; identify any domain-specific strategies the child discovered through guided discovery — e.g., "body position" for balance tasks), Do (describe how intervention sessions were structured as practice with coaching questions rather than demonstration — "What's your plan before you try?" "Did it work?"), Check (describe how the child evaluated whether the plan worked and how strategies were refined). Always justify CO-OP selection with its evidence base: Cochrane-level evidence supports CO-OP for DCD (Smits-Engelsman et al., 2018 review); CO-OP is recommended as a task-oriented approach in NICE DCD guidelines.

5. **What is the WeeFIM and how do I use it in a paediatric OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: The WeeFIM (Functional Independence Measure for Children) is a standardised measure of functional independence in children aged 6 months to 7 years (or up to 12 years for children with developmental disabilities). It assesses 18 items on a 7-point scale (1 = total assistance required to 7 = complete independence), producing a total score range of 18–126. It is divided into two subscales: Motor (13 items, range 13–91: self-care, sphincter control, transfers, locomotion) and Cognitive (5 items, range 5–35: communication and social cognition). In an assignment, report the total WeeFIM score, the motor and cognitive subscale scores, and contextualise them: "A WeeFIM total score of 42 out of 126 indicates substantial dependence in daily activities across motor domains, with particular difficulties in self-care (items 1–6 scored 1–2 indicating requiring maximal assistance)." WeeFIM is most commonly used in paediatric neurology, NICU follow-up, and CP case studies.
