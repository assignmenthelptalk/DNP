**Values per Attribute**:
- IPA (Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis) → qualitative methodology → developed by Jonathan Smith (1995); focuses on the individual's lived experience of a phenomenon; double hermeneutic process (researcher interprets the participant's own interpretation of their experience); purposive sampling (homogeneous, small samples of 3–10 participants); data collection: semi-structured interview (60–90 minutes); analysis stages: (1) reading and re-reading transcripts, (2) initial noting of descriptive, linguistic, and conceptual comments, (3) developing experiential themes, (4) searching for connections across themes, (5) moving to the next case; reporting: COREQ checklist; OT application: patient experience of OT intervention, occupational identity in chronic illness, meaning of occupation in recovery — IPA is the most frequently used qualitative methodology in OT dissertations in UK programmes
- Phenomenology (Husserl/Merleau-Ponty/Heidegger traditions) → qualitative methodology → descriptive phenomenology (Husserl/Giorgi) focuses on the essence of an experience — what it is like; interpretive/hermeneutic phenomenology (Heidegger/van Manen) focuses on meaning within a life-world context; OT application: describing the lived experience of disability, the meaning of occupation after loss of function; data: unstructured or semi-structured interview; analysis: epoché (bracketing prior assumptions) in descriptive; dwelling with data and writing phenomenologically in interpretive; suitable for OT dissertation topics: "the lived experience of returning to work after stroke for OT clients"
- Grounded Theory → qualitative methodology → Strauss and Corbin (1990); Charmaz (2014) constructivist version most commonly used in OT; purpose: generate theory grounded in the data (inductively); theoretical sampling (sampling continues until theoretical saturation); constant comparative analysis; final product: a substantive theory; OT application: understanding the process of occupational adaptation, developing a theory of OT client engagement; requires more participants than IPA (typically 15–30); most ambitious option for BSc dissertation — more achievable at MSc level
- Thematic Analysis → qualitative analytical method (not a standalone methodology — used within various methodological frameworks); Braun and Clarke (2006) as foundational reference (Thematic Analysis: A Practical Guide, updated 2022); 6 phases: (1) familiarise yourself with data, (2) generate initial codes, (3) search for themes, (4) review themes, (5) define and name themes, (6) write up; semantic vs latent themes; inductive vs theoretical coding; OT application: widely used for interview data in OT research; often combined with IPA or phenomenology; COREQ reporting required
- PRISMA 2020 → reporting standard → Page, McKenzie, et al. (2021, BMJ); 27-item checklist for systematic reviews and meta-analyses; key items: title/abstract (PRISMA declaration), introduction (rationale, objectives, PICO), methods (eligibility criteria, information sources, search strategy, selection process, data collection, assessment of risk of bias, certainty of evidence), results (study selection PRISMA flow diagram, study characteristics, risk of bias assessment, results per outcome), discussion (summary of evidence, limitations, conclusions); required for MSc systematic review dissertations; strongly recommended for Level 6 systematic review projects
- COREQ → reporting standard → Tong et al. (2007), International Journal for Quality in Health Care; 32-item checklist for qualitative studies; three domains: (1) Research Team and Reflexivity (personal characteristics, relationship with participants), (2) Study Design (theoretical framework, participant selection, setting, data collection), (3) Analysis and Findings (data analysis, reporting); required for qualitative dissertation reporting at MSc level and expected at Level 6; most OT students are unaware of COREQ — its presence signals methodological rigour

#### H2: Choosing a Research Methodology for Your OT Dissertation — *Major Query Need: How to select between qualitative, quantitative, and mixed methods for an OT research question*
> **Context vector**: Research methodology selection — the three paradigm options, which OT research questions suit each, how to justify the choice in the methodology chapter
> **EAV to cover**: Qualitative methodology → appropriate when the research question is about experience, meaning, perception, or process ("What is the experience of...?", "How do OT clients understand...?", "What factors influence...?"); interpretivist paradigm; produces rich descriptive data; methods: semi-structured interview, focus group, observation; analysis: IPA, thematic analysis, grounded theory, phenomenology; sample size: 3–30 participants depending on methodology; → Quantitative methodology → appropriate when the research question is about effectiveness, prevalence, association, or measurement ("Is intervention X effective for...?", "What is the prevalence of...among OT clients?"); positivist paradigm; produces numerical data; methods: RCT, cohort, cross-sectional survey, single-case experimental design (SCED — common in OT research due to small sample feasibility); statistical analysis: SPSS, R; → Mixed methods → appropriate when neither qualitative nor quantitative alone can answer the research question ("Is the intervention effective AND acceptable to participants?"); pragmatist paradigm; convergent design (collect qualitative and quantitative simultaneously), sequential explanatory (quantitative first, qualitative explains the numbers), sequential exploratory (qualitative first, quantitative tests the findings); more complex — typically appropriate for MSc Level 7; methodology selection → justified by the nature of the research question, the OT values of client-centredness (supports qualitative), the evidence hierarchy (supports quantitative for effectiveness), and feasibility (student access to participants)
> **Predicate**: "suits" — qualitative methodology suits experience-based research questions; "requires" — quantitative requires statistical analysis capability
> **First word of first paragraph**: "Research" — "Research methodology selection in an OT dissertation depends on the nature of the research question..."

##### H3: Qualitative OT Dissertations — IPA, Phenomenology, and Grounded Theory
> **Attribute + Value**: IPA → most commonly used qualitative methodology in UK OT dissertations; best for: "the lived experience of OT clients," "the meaning of occupation in recovery from X"; sample: 3–8 participants (homogeneous); interview: semi-structured, 60–90 minutes, audio-recorded; analysis: double hermeneutic (researcher interprets participant's interpretation); reporting: COREQ checklist; key references: Smith, Flowers, Larkin (2009) Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis: Theory, Method and Research; → Phenomenology → descriptive: Giorgi's adapted Husserlian method, epoché (bracketing), focus on the essence of the experience; interpretive: van Manen, hermeneutic circle, focus on meaning in context; OT topics: lived experience of disability, embodied experience of occupation; → Grounded Theory → Charmaz (2014) constructivist version; generates theory from data; theoretical sampling until saturation; constant comparative analysis; requires 15–30 participants; more suited to MSc level due to sample size and complexity; OT topics: developing theory of client engagement in OT; → Thematic Analysis (Braun & Clarke) → most flexible — used within IPA, phenomenology, or as standalone; 6-phase process; inductive coding recommended for OT student dissertations; not a standalone methodology but an analytical method — clarify this in the methodology chapter or markers will query the philosophical underpinning
> **Writer note**: The specific citation for each methodology (Smith et al. 2009 for IPA; Charmaz 2014 for constructivist GT; Braun & Clarke 2006/2022 for TA) is essential EAV depth. Students who write "I used IPA" without citing the foundational IPA text will lose marks on methodology rigour criteria. These citations must appear in the brief for the writer to include them. Word count: 300–360 words.

##### H3: Quantitative and Mixed Methods OT Dissertations — RCTs, Cohort Studies, and SCED
> **Attribute + Value**: RCT → gold standard for intervention effectiveness; random allocation to treatment and control; blinding where possible (assessor blinding is feasible in OT research; therapist/client blinding is not); CONSORT reporting checklist; most feasible at Level 7 or OTD; ethical requirement: NHS REC if NHS population; → Cohort study → prospective (follows participants forward from a defined starting point) or retrospective (looks back at existing records); no randomisation; OT application: following a group of stroke OT clients from inpatient admission through community discharge, measuring IADL independence at 6 weeks, 3 months, 12 months; STROBE reporting standard; → Cross-sectional survey → single time-point data collection; assesses prevalence or associations; OT application: survey of OT practitioners' use of standardised assessments in mental health settings; most feasible for BSc Level 6 (no NHS REC typically required if surveying OT practitioners); analysis: descriptive statistics, chi-square, Pearson correlation; → SCED (Single-Case Experimental Design) → strong fit with OT research because of small clinical populations and individual intervention tailoring; A-B-A design (baseline-intervention-withdrawal); A-B-A-B design (withdrawal-reintroduction); visual analysis of graphed data; statistical analysis: Tau-U; particularly appropriate for OT research with rare conditions or specialist populations; feasible at Level 6 with careful planning; → Mixed methods → most common designs in OT research: sequential explanatory (quantitative effectiveness data followed by qualitative experience data from same sample); convergent parallel (simultaneous)
> **Writer note**: SCED is an underknown but highly OT-relevant quantitative design — its inclusion demonstrates expertise specific to OT research contexts. The Tau-U statistic is specific enough to be a genuine EAV value for SCED. Word count: 260–320 words.

#### H2: Ethics Applications for OT Student Research — University Committee and NHS REC — *Major Query Need: When ethics approval is required and what the application involves*
> **Context vector**: Research ethics in OT student dissertations — types of ethics review, application processes, consent forms, data protection, HCPC research principles

##### H3: What Is a Participant Information Sheet and Consent Form in OT Research?
> **Attribute + Value**: Participant information sheet (PIS) → required component of ethics application; content: study title, purpose of the research, what participation involves (time commitment, interview questions nature), risks and benefits, confidentiality and anonymisation process, right to withdraw without giving a reason at any point, contact details for researcher and supervisor; language: must be in plain English, reading age approximately grade 8, no technical jargon without explanation; format: typically 2–4 pages for university ethics; → Informed consent form → separate one-page document; participant confirms: read the PIS, opportunity to ask questions, understands participation is voluntary, agrees to participation, agrees to data use as described; participant and researcher both sign and date; original retained by researcher; photocopy given to participant; digital consent: acceptable via secure online form for email-recruited participants (university OneDrive or REDCap platform); consent cannot be obtained retrospectively — must precede all data collection
> **Writer note**: Students frequently submit ethics applications with incomplete PIS documents (missing the right to withdraw statement, or failing to specify the data storage location and retention period). These are grounds for ethics rejection — naming the failure points is EAV depth that both validates expertise and is commercially useful. Word count: 180–220 words.

#### H2: FAQ — Supplementary Content — Micro-contexts and Minor Query Needs
> **Critical rule**: FAQ answers must NOT repeat Main Content. Target new representative query variants.
> Each answer: 80–120 words, self-contained.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **How do I know if my OT dissertation research requires NHS ethics approval?** → Answer direction: NHS Research Ethics Committee (REC) approval is required if your research involves: (1) NHS patients as research participants (including service users of NHS OT services), (2) NHS staff recruited as participants, (3) NHS patient records or data (even anonymised if sourced from NHS systems), or (4) research conducted on NHS premises. If your dissertation is a systematic literature review, secondary data analysis of publicly available data, or involves recruiting participants outside the NHS (e.g., university students, members of the public, non-NHS OT practitioners), university ethics committee approval is typically sufficient. When in doubt, contact your supervisor and the university's research ethics office before beginning participant recruitment.

2. **What is IPA and why is it commonly used in OT dissertations?** → Answer direction: Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis (IPA) is a qualitative research methodology developed by Jonathan Smith (1995) that investigates the meaning of lived experiences for individuals. It is particularly well-suited to OT dissertation research because occupational therapy is inherently concerned with how individuals experience and make sense of their daily occupations — and IPA's focus on individual meaning and experience aligns directly with this client-centred perspective. IPA uses small, purposively sampled participant groups (typically 3–8 participants), semi-structured interviews of 60–90 minutes, and a double hermeneutic analytical process. It is the most commonly used qualitative methodology in UK OT dissertations.

3. **What is the difference between a research proposal and a dissertation in occupational therapy?** → Answer direction: A research proposal is an academic assignment that plans and justifies a research study that will or could be conducted — it describes the aim, methodology, ethics approach, and timeline without collecting or presenting data. It is typically 4,000–8,000 words. A dissertation is the completed research product — it includes all sections of the proposal, plus the executed study: data collection, analysis, findings, and discussion. A dissertation is typically 8,000–25,000 words depending on the qualification level. Some OT programmes assign the research proposal as a separate assessment in Year 2 or the first semester of Year 3, and the dissertation as the Year 3/final year major project.

4. **Can I use COPM as a data collection tool in my OT dissertation?** → Answer direction: Yes — COPM (Canadian Occupational Performance Measure) is a validated, client-centred outcome measure used widely in OT research as a primary data collection tool. For a dissertation, COPM can be administered as a semi-structured interview to assess participants' self-reported occupational performance and satisfaction. Its 10-point performance and satisfaction scales provide quantitative data suitable for pre-post comparison in a pilot study, while the qualitative interview content can provide thematic data for an IPA or thematic analysis study. Ethics approval is required before administering COPM to research participants. If recruiting NHS OT clients as participants, NHS REC approval may be required (see ethics guidance above).

5. **Does an OT MSc dissertation need to include a PRISMA flow diagram?** → Answer direction: Yes, if your MSc dissertation uses a systematic review methodology. PRISMA 2020 (Page et al., 2021) is the current reporting standard for systematic reviews and requires a PRISMA flow diagram illustrating the four stages of the literature search: Identification (total records from all databases), Screening (records remaining after duplicate removal and title/abstract screening), Eligibility (full texts assessed with reasons for exclusion), and Included (studies meeting all inclusion criteria). The PRISMA flow diagram belongs in the Methodology or Results chapter, and the complete PRISMA 2020 checklist (27 items with page numbers) must be included in the Appendix. For qualitative MSc dissertations, COREQ (32 items) is the relevant reporting standard, not PRISMA.
