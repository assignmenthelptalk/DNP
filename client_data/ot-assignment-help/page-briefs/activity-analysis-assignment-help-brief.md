**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **How many OTPF demand categories do I need to cover in my activity analysis assignment?** → Answer direction: All 8 OTPF activity demand categories should be addressed in a comprehensive activity analysis assignment at Level 5 and above. At Level 4, some programmes accept 5–6 categories with greater depth on each. Check your assignment brief — if it specifies "all demand categories" or "comprehensive analysis," all 8 are required. The 8 categories are: (1) Objects and their properties, (2) Space demands, (3) Social demands, (4) Sequencing and timing, (5) Required actions and performance skills, (6) Required body functions, (7) Required body structures, and (8) Contextual factors. Omitting any category will typically result in mark deductions on comprehensiveness criteria.

2. **What is the difference between grading and adapting an activity in OT?** → Answer direction: Grading involves systematically changing one or more activity demand parameters within the standard method to increase or decrease the challenge — for example, reducing the number of cooking steps for a client in early rehabilitation (downgrading) or introducing a simultaneous dual-task for a client in later rehabilitation (upgrading). Adaptation involves modifying the task, tools, environment, or method to enable participation without the activity demand changing — for example, using a lightweight pan (tool adaptation), adjusting counter height to 80cm for a wheelchair user (environmental adaptation), or using a one-handed cooking technique (method adaptation). Both are required in a complete Level 5 activity analysis assignment.

3. **Can I analyse any activity for my OT assignment or does it have to be a specific one?** → Answer direction: Most OT activity analysis assignment briefs either specify the activity or give students a choice from a defined list. If you have a free choice, cooking (meal preparation) is recommended for Level 4–5 because it provides sufficient complexity across all 8 OTPF demand categories to produce a comprehensive analysis. Handwriting is recommended if your assignment has a paediatric or school-based focus. Dressing is a good choice for physical rehabilitation populations. Avoid activities that are too simple (e.g., picking up an object) as they will not generate sufficient demand analysis for a pass-grade assignment.

4. **Do I need to link my activity analysis to a specific client for the assignment?** → Answer direction: This depends on your qualification level and assignment brief. Level 4 activity analysis assignments often require analysis of the standard activity without client application — the task is to demonstrate understanding of demand categories. Level 5 and Level 6 assignments typically require client application — either a real anonymised client from your placement or a provided vignette. With client application, the demand analysis is completed first (what the activity requires), then linked to the client's specific assessment findings (what the client can and cannot do), then grading and adaptation recommendations are made based on the demand-to-client factor gap. This two-stage structure (activity demands → client factors → grading/adaptation) is the standard format at Level 5+.

5. **What are the most common errors OT students make in activity analysis assignments?** → Answer direction: The five most common errors are: (1) incomplete demand category coverage — omitting categories 3 (social) or 8 (contextual), which are less obvious than physical demands; (2) describing performance skills without naming the OTPF motor/process skill terms (e.g., writing "the person holds the pan" instead of "grips — the activity requires a palmar grasp with sustained grip force"); (3) grading recommendations without linking to a specific demand category (saying "make the task easier" without identifying which category is being downgraded); (4) adaptation recommendations without specifying the adaptation type (task/environment/method); (5) ignoring the occupation context category entirely — contextual factors (temporal, cultural, personal) are consistently underweighted in student assignments.
