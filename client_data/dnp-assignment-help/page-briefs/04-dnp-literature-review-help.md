## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: DNP Capstone Literature Review

**Main Attributes**: review type, databases, search strategy, PRISMA, appraisal tools, evidence synthesis, study count, gap identification

**Derived Attributes**:
- **Review Type**: integrative review (most common in DNP — combines quantitative and qualitative), scoping review (broad topic mapping, PRISMA-ScR), systematic review (narrow PICO question, Cochrane-level rigour — rarely feasible in DNP timelines)
- **Databases**: CINAHL Complete (nursing primary), PubMed/MEDLINE (biomedical), Cochrane Library (systematic reviews), EMBASE (pharmacology, clinical), PsycINFO (mental health) — selection based on PICOT topic
- **Search Strategy**: MeSH headings + CINAHL headings + free-text keywords; Boolean operators (AND/OR/NOT); date range filters (5–10 years typical); language filter (English); publication type filter (peer-reviewed primary studies)
- **PRISMA**: Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (2020 update) — 4-phase flow diagram (identification, screening, eligibility, included); required for systematic and integrative reviews
- **Appraisal Tools**: JBI Critical Appraisal Checklists (RCTs, cohort studies, systematic reviews, qualitative studies — tool selected by study design); CASP tools (similar function, alternative); GRADE framework (evidence quality — certainty rating: high/moderate/low/very low)
- **Evidence Synthesis**: not a study-by-study summary — a thematic synthesis that identifies patterns, consistencies, conflicts, and gaps across all included studies
- **Study Count**: 15–30 primary studies typical for DNP capstones; fewer than 15 may be acceptable for very narrow topics; more than 30 increases appraisal burden without proportional quality gain
- **Practice Gap**: final synthesis step — the logical conclusion of the literature review that identifies what is NOT yet done or known in the specific clinical context, thus justifying the DNP project

**Values per Attribute**:
- Integrative review: includes both experimental and non-experimental studies; appropriate when evidence base is mixed quality and type; most DNP capstone topics
- PRISMA flow: typically starts with 500–2000 identified records; screening removes duplicates and off-topic titles; eligibility assessment removes studies that don't meet inclusion criteria; final included count: 15–30
- JBI RCT checklist: 13 items; JBI cohort checklist: 11 items; JBI systematic review checklist: 11 items — student selects tool based on each included study's design
- GRADE certainty ratings: high = consistent RCT evidence; moderate = some limitations; low = observational studies; very low = expert opinion — most DNP evidence is moderate to low

The search methodology must be reproducible and the appraisal must be documented.

---

#### H2: What Type of Literature Review Does a DNP Capstone Require?
> **EAV to cover**: review type selection criteria, integrative vs scoping vs systematic, why systematic reviews are rarely feasible
> **Word count**: 280–340 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Integrative review**: most appropriate for most DNP capstones — combines quantitative studies (QI outcomes, RCTs, cohort studies) and qualitative studies (patient experience, staff perceptions) on the same practice topic; PRISMA 2020 reporting
> - **Scoping review**: used when the topic is broad or the evidence base is emerging — maps "what is known" without synthesising evidence quality; PRISMA-ScR reporting; appropriate for Nursing Informatics or Population Health capstones where evidence base is newer
> - **Systematic review with meta-analysis**: narrow PICO question, homogeneous studies, statistical pooling — rarely feasible within a 1–2 semester DNP timeline; most DNP programs accept integrative reviews as meeting the systematic review standard
> - Decision rule: if the evidence base is mixed (quantitative + qualitative, varied study designs) → integrative review. If mapping breadth not depth → scoping review. Full systematic reviews are typically reserved for PhD-level work.

---

#### H2: Choosing Your Databases: CINAHL, PubMed, Cochrane, and Beyond
> **EAV to cover**: database selection by clinical topic, why multiple databases are required
> **Word count**: 260–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **CINAHL Complete**: nursing and allied health primary database — always included in DNP literature searches; most DNP-specific content
> - **PubMed/MEDLINE**: biomedical and clinical research — essential for any clinical intervention topic; free access via NLM
> - **Cochrane Library**: systematic reviews and meta-analyses — used to identify existing synthesised evidence; Cochrane reviews often form Level I evidence in DNP proposals
> - **EMBASE**: European biomedical literature; more comprehensive than PubMed for pharmacological and device-related interventions; useful for CRNA, acute care topics
> - **PsycINFO**: psychology, psychiatry, and behavioral health — required for PMHNP capstones; mental health intervention research
> - **CINAHL + PubMed is the minimum**: all DNP proposals should search at least 3 databases; committee may require demonstration that the search was comprehensive
> - Clinical content-specific additions: ERIC (nursing education), AgeLine (geriatric populations), CINAHL Pediatric (PNP/NNP capstones)

---

#### H2: Building Your Search Strategy: MeSH Terms, Boolean Operators, and Filters
> **EAV to cover**: MeSH/CINAHL subject headings, keyword searching, Boolean operators, date/language filters
> **Word count**: 300–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **MeSH headings (PubMed)**: controlled vocabulary terms that map to PubMed's indexing system; example: "Urinary Tract Infections/prevention & control" for CAUTI prevention
> - **CINAHL Subject Headings**: equivalent controlled vocabulary for CINAHL; example: "Catheter-Related Infections" 
> - **Free-text keywords**: used alongside subject headings to catch articles not yet indexed — truncation (catheter*), phrase searching ("catheter-associated urinary tract infection")
> - **Boolean operators**: AND (narrows search — connects concepts); OR (broadens search — connects synonyms); NOT (excludes irrelevant terms — use sparingly)
> - **Date range filter**: typically 5–10 years from current date; rationale must be stated in the proposal (e.g., "publications from 2015–2025 were searched to capture evidence following the CDC 2009 CAUTI guideline update")
> - **Other filters**: peer-reviewed, English language, human subjects, adult/pediatric population — applied consistently
> - **Search strategy documentation**: the exact search string with filters must be documented in the proposal and appendix — committee may request it during defense

---

#### H2: PRISMA Flow Diagram: Tracking Your Search for the DNP Proposal
> **EAV to cover**: PRISMA 2020 phases, how to complete each box, typical numbers at each phase
> **Word count**: 260–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Phase 1 — Identification**: total records found across all databases (typically 500–2000 for a 3-database search on a focused PICOT topic) + additional records from citation searching
> - **Phase 2 — Screening**: remove duplicates; screen titles and abstracts for relevance to PICOT; reasons for exclusion recorded (wrong population, wrong intervention, wrong outcome, date outside range)
> - **Phase 3 — Eligibility**: full-text review of remaining articles; apply inclusion/exclusion criteria; record number excluded with specific reasons
> - **Phase 4 — Included**: final count of studies included in the review (target: 15–30 for most DNP capstones)
> - PRISMA flow diagram is typically Figure 1 in the proposal appendix or embedded in Chapter 2
> - Free tools: Rayyan for collaborative screening; PRISMA 2020 flow diagram template available at prisma-statement.org

---

#### H2: Critical Appraisal Tools for DNP Projects: JBI, CASP, and GRADE
> **EAV to cover**: JBI checklists by study design, CASP tools, GRADE certainty ratings
> **Word count**: 300–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **JBI Critical Appraisal Checklists**: published by the Joanna Briggs Institute — separate checklist for each study design (RCT: 13 items, cohort/observational: 11 items, systematic review: 11 items, qualitative: 10 items, prevalence study: 9 items); responses: Yes/No/Unclear/Not Applicable; total score interpreted as high/moderate/low quality
> - **CASP Checklists**: Critical Appraisal Skills Programme — similar function to JBI; alternative when university recommends CASP; available free at casp-uk.net
> - **GRADE Framework**: assesses the certainty of evidence across all included studies for a specific outcome — not individual study quality; outputs: High/Moderate/Low/Very Low; used in the synthesis narrative ("the evidence supporting this intervention is of moderate certainty")
> - For most DNP capstones: JBI is the most commonly required tool — verify with faculty advisor
> - Appraisal results are recorded in the evidence synthesis table (one row per included study) and summarised in the Chapter 2 narrative

---

#### H2: Constructing Your Evidence Synthesis Table
> **EAV to cover**: table structure, required columns, synthesis vs summary distinction
> **Word count**: 240–300 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Required columns: Author(s) & Year | Study Design | Sample (N, population, setting) | Intervention | Comparison | Outcomes (measures + results) | Level of Evidence | Limitations
> - Typically presented as an appendix (Appendix A or B) in the proposal
> - The table itself is NOT the synthesis — it is raw material for synthesis
> - Synthesis means: identifying patterns across studies (e.g., "7 of 10 studies found X intervention reduced Y outcome by 15–25%"), conflicts (e.g., "2 studies found no significant effect — both had small samples"), and levels of evidence (e.g., "evidence is predominantly Level III–IV; no RCTs were identified for this specific population")
> - The synthesis narrative in Chapter 2 should be organised by theme (outcome category, population subgroup, implementation setting) — not by individual study

---

#### H2: How to Identify the Practice Gap That Justifies Your DNP Project
> **EAV to cover**: practice gap definition, how it emerges from the synthesis, how it is written
> **Word count**: 220–280 words
> **Specific content**:
> - The practice gap is the last paragraph of Chapter 2 and the direct logical link to the PICOT question
> - It states: what the evidence shows is effective (or promising), what is missing or not yet tested in the specific clinical context, and why the student's proposed project is needed
> - Example: "Although the literature consistently supports structured fall prevention programs in acute care settings, no published studies were identified that tested [the specific intervention] in a [specific setting] with [the specific population]. This gap in implementation evidence justifies the proposed QI project at [clinical site]."
> - Common error: a practice gap that says "more research is needed" — this is a PhD-level conclusion, not a DNP-level conclusion. The DNP gap should say "this evidence-based intervention has not been implemented and evaluated in this practice setting."

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How many studies do I need for a DNP literature review?**
Most DNP capstone literature reviews include 15–30 primary studies. Fewer than 15 may be acceptable for very narrow or emerging topics where the evidence base is limited — the proposal should document the search comprehensiveness and acknowledge the limitation. More than 30 studies increases the appraisal burden without meaningfully strengthening the evidence case for most clinical topics.

**Q: What databases should I search for a DNP literature review?**
At minimum, CINAHL Complete and PubMed/MEDLINE must be searched. A comprehensive DNP literature search typically includes three or more databases — adding Cochrane Library for existing systematic reviews, PsycINFO for mental health topics, or EMBASE for pharmacological or device-related interventions. The databases searched and the rationale for selection must be documented in the proposal.

**Q: What is the difference between an integrative review and a systematic review for a DNP project?**
A systematic review follows a strict protocol to answer a narrow PICO question using homogeneous studies — typically RCTs. An integrative review includes studies of varied designs (quantitative and qualitative) on the same practice topic and is more appropriate for most DNP capstone topics, where evidence quality is mixed and the evidence base includes both experimental and observational studies. Most DNP programs accept an integrative review as meeting the systematic review standard.

## Writer Instructions Summary
1. Name all databases (CINAHL, PubMed, Cochrane, EMBASE, PsycINFO) with their specific relevance — do not write "academic databases."
2. PRISMA section must include typical record counts at each phase (500–2000 → 100–300 → 30–60 → 15–30).
3. JBI checklist names must be specific by study design — do not write "JBI appraisal tool" without specifying which checklist applies to which study type.
4. The practice gap section must include an example of a correctly written gap statement AND an example of the common incorrect version ("more research is needed").
5. Synthesis vs summary must be explicitly differentiated — not just mentioned.
