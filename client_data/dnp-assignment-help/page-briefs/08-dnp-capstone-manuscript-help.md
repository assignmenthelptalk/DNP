## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: DNP Capstone Final Manuscript

**Main Attributes**: manuscript structure, abstract requirements, methods section, results section, discussion section, implications for practice, APA 7 format, appendices, length

**Derived Attributes**:
- **Manuscript Structure**: Title Page → Abstract → Introduction → Review of Literature → Theoretical/Conceptual Framework → Methods → Results/Findings → Discussion → Implications for Practice → Limitations → Recommendations for Future Practice → Conclusion → References → Appendices
- **Abstract**: 250 words maximum (structured or unstructured depending on university); components: background/significance, purpose, methods/project design, results/outcomes, conclusions/implications; keywords (5–7 terms) required below abstract
- **Introduction**: restates background, significance, problem statement, PICOT question — condensed from Chapter 1 of proposal; 2–3 pages
- **Review of Literature**: condensed from Chapter 2; maintains synthesis structure; evidence synthesis table moved to appendix; 5–8 pages
- **Methods**: describes project design, setting, sample, intervention, data collection, and analysis in past tense — what was done, not what will be done; 4–6 pages; specific enough to be reproduced
- **Results**: presents data without interpretation; sample demographics first, then process fidelity measures, then outcome measures; tables and figures labelled per APA 7; 4–6 pages
- **Discussion**: interprets findings relative to the PICOT question; connects results to the literature review; applies the theoretical framework; explains unexpected or null findings; 4–6 pages
- **Implications for Practice**: addresses impact for staff practice, patient care, policy, education, and leadership — each domain addressed; 2–3 pages
- **Appendices**: IRB approval letter, data collection tools/instruments, staff education materials, consent documents/waiver, evidence synthesis table, Gantt chart — each labelled Appendix A, B, C...
- **Length**: 60–120 pages of text excluding appendices; most DNP manuscripts fall between 70–90 pages

**Values per Attribute**:
- Abstract keywords: MeSH terms preferred; 5–7 terms listed after "Keywords:" on the line below the abstract
- Methods section verb tense: past tense throughout ("Data were collected," "Staff education was delivered") — proposal uses future tense; manuscript uses past tense
- Discussion section structure: (1) restate PICOT and purpose, (2) summarise key findings, (3) compare findings to literature (supporting and contrasting), (4) apply theoretical framework, (5) explain unexpected findings, (6) acknowledge limitations
- Implications for Practice subsections: For Nursing Practice, For Education, For Policy, For Leadership, For Future Research — not all sub-headings required at every university; check rubric
- APA 7 student paper: no running head; page number in top-right header only; level headings (Level 1: bold centered; Level 2: bold left-aligned; Level 3: bold italic left-aligned; Level 4: bold italic indented; Level 5: italic indented)

#### H2: Writing the DNP Capstone Abstract: 250 Words That Frame Your Entire Project
> **EAV to cover**: abstract word count, structured vs unstructured, required components, keywords
> **Word count**: 260–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - 250-word maximum — no exceptions; word count includes all abstract text; keywords are listed separately and not counted in the 250 words
> - **Structured abstract** (most common for clinical/QI manuscripts): labeled sections — Background, Purpose, Methods, Results, Conclusions
> - **Unstructured abstract** (some universities, more narrative style): same content without labeled subsections
> - Required components in all abstracts: clinical problem context (1–2 sentences), purpose statement (1 sentence), project design and methods (2–3 sentences), key results (2–3 sentences including the primary outcome measure change), conclusions and clinical implications (1–2 sentences)
> - **Keywords**: 5–7 MeSH terms listed below the abstract on a new line starting with "Keywords:"; should match the primary PICOT elements (population, intervention, outcome)
> - Common error: an abstract that summarises the proposal rather than the completed project — must be written AFTER implementation is complete; must report actual results, not expected results

---

#### H2: Introduction and Background: Setting the Stage for Your Practice Change
> **EAV to cover**: introduction structure, condensing from Chapter 1, scope
> **Word count**: 200–260 words
> **Specific content**:
> - The introduction in the final manuscript is a condensed version of Chapter 1 of the proposal — same content, tighter writing, past tense where implementation has occurred
> - Must include: significance of the clinical problem (national/local data), purpose statement, PICOT question (restated in full), brief preview of the manuscript structure
> - Length: 2–3 pages; do not re-expand Chapter 1 to its full length — the literature review section carries the evidence burden
> - Common error: a weak introduction that does not quantify the clinical problem or does not restate the PICOT — committee members should be able to identify the PICOT without searching

---

#### H2: Methods Section: Writing Up Your Implementation With Reproducible Detail
> **EAV to cover**: methods structure, past tense, level of specificity required
> **Word count**: 300–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Written in past tense throughout — the project is complete; all verbs describe what was done, not what will be done
> - Subsections: Project Design (state the design: "A pre-post quality improvement project was implemented..."), Setting and Sample (specific unit, patient census, staff count, inclusion/exclusion criteria, final N), Intervention (step-by-step description of what was implemented — specific enough to reproduce), IRB/Ethics (state determination: QI non-research or review level obtained, IRB number), Data Collection Instruments (name each tool, reliability/validity data), Procedures (timeline, who collected data, how), Data Analysis (name the exact tests used — this matches Chapter 3 of the proposal)
> - Specificity standard: a reader should be able to replicate the project in a different setting based on the methods section alone
> - Do not include rationale or justification in the methods section — that belongs in the proposal; the methods section reports what happened

---

#### H2: Results Section: Presenting Pre-Post Data, Tables, and Run Charts
> **EAV to cover**: results section structure, neutral presentation, table format, figure format
> **Word count**: 280–340 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Written in past tense; presents data without interpretation or commentary — interpretation belongs in the discussion
> - Structure: (1) Sample characteristics (demographics table), (2) Process fidelity results (implementation compliance rates), (3) Primary outcome results (the PICOT O measure), (4) Secondary outcome results if applicable
> - Each result reported as a neutral statement: "Pre-implementation CAUTI rate was 3.4 per 1000 catheter days (SD=0.8). Post-implementation rate was 1.2 per 1000 catheter days (SD=0.4). A paired t-test demonstrated a statistically significant reduction (t(29)=-8.2, p<0.001, 95% CI [-2.7, -1.7])."
> - All tables labelled above (Table 1, Table 2) in APA 7 format; all figures labelled below (Figure 1, Figure 2)
> - Run chart or SPC chart included as a figure — shows trend over the implementation period
> - Do not state "the results show that our intervention was successful" in the results section — save that language for the discussion

---

#### H2: Discussion Section: Interpreting Findings and Connecting Back to Literature
> **EAV to cover**: discussion structure, comparison to literature, framework application, unexpected findings
> **Word count**: 340–420 words
> **Specific content**:
> - The discussion is where the student demonstrates doctoral-level thinking: why did the results occur, what do they mean relative to the PICOT question, how do they align or conflict with the literature?
> - **Structure**:
>   1. Restate the purpose and PICOT question (1 paragraph — not a summary of the results, which the reader just read)
>   2. Interpret the primary outcome: was the PICOT answered? Was the change clinically significant? Compare the observed change to the MCID for the outcome measure
>   3. Compare findings to literature: where do results support prior evidence? Where do they conflict? Name specific studies from the literature review: "These findings are consistent with [Author, Year], who found X in a similar QI implementation at Y setting"
>   4. Apply the theoretical framework: how did the framework stages manifest in the project? Did any stage present unexpected challenges? Did the framework predict the outcomes accurately?
>   5. Explain unexpected or null findings: if the outcome did not improve, what are the plausible explanations? (insufficient implementation period, sample size, staff compliance, confounding factors)
>   6. Address limitations: sample size, single site, implementation fidelity challenges, data source limitations
> - Do not introduce new information in the discussion — only interpret what was presented in the results

---

#### H2: Implications for Practice, Education, Policy, and Leadership
> **EAV to cover**: four implication domains, how to frame each, AACN Essentials connection
> **Word count**: 260–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **For Nursing Practice**: what does this finding mean for day-to-day clinical care? Should this protocol be adopted across other units or sites? What workflow changes are recommended?
> - **For Nursing Education**: what does this project suggest about how nurses should be trained or educated? Staff education design? Curriculum implications for DNP programs or graduate nursing education?
> - **For Policy**: should the practice change be formalised as a unit policy, departmental policy, or organisational policy? What institutional policy changes are recommended?
> - **For Nursing Leadership**: what leadership actions are needed to sustain this change? What monitoring structures should executive leadership establish?
> - Each domain addressed in 1–2 paragraphs; connect to AACN 2021 Essentials domains where applicable (Domain 5: Quality and Safety; Domain 7: Systems-Based Practice; Domain 9: Professionalism)
> - Do not write "more research is needed" as an implication — this is a PhD-dissertation conclusion; DNP implications should recommend specific practice changes or policy actions

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How long is a DNP capstone final manuscript?**
A DNP capstone final manuscript typically runs 60–120 pages of text, excluding references and appendices. Most manuscripts fall between 70 and 90 pages. Appendices add another 20–40 pages depending on the number of data collection tools, education materials, and supporting documents. The manuscript is substantially longer than most academic papers because it documents an entire implementation project from problem identification through outcome evaluation.

**Q: What does the discussion section of a DNP capstone manuscript include?**
The discussion section interprets the results relative to the PICOT question, compares findings to the literature, applies the theoretical framework to explain what occurred, addresses unexpected or null findings, and acknowledges limitations. It should not introduce new data or information. It should demonstrate doctoral-level analytical thinking by explaining WHY the results occurred and what they mean for clinical practice.

**Q: What are the implications for practice in a DNP manuscript?**
The implications for practice section addresses how the findings should change nursing practice, nursing education, healthcare policy, and nursing leadership. Most DNP capstones address all four domains — specifying what should change, for whom, and at what level of the healthcare system. This section directly reflects the AACN 2021 Essentials requirements and is a graded component at most universities.

## Writer Instructions Summary
1. Manuscript structure must be presented as the FULL ordered list of all sections — not just "introduction through conclusion."
2. Abstract section must include both structured and unstructured options with the same components clearly listed.
3. Methods section must emphasise past tense and reproducibility standard — these are the two most commonly missed requirements.
4. Discussion section structure must be presented as a numbered sequence, not a general description.
5. Implications section must address all four domains (practice, education, policy, leadership) with a sentence or two on each — not a generic statement about "improving patient care."
6. Do not use the phrase "write your final paper" — use "final scholarly manuscript" or "final project report" throughout.
