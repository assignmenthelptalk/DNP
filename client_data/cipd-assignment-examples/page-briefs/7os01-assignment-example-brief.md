#### H2: AC 1.2 — Employment Status and the Gig Economy — The Strategic HR Risk of Worker Misclassification — Major Query Need: employment status law, gig economy cases
> **Context vector:** Employment status as a three-category framework (employee / worker / self-employed); gig economy disruption of traditional employment relationships; Supreme Court case law establishing worker status where contracts deny it
> **EAV to cover:**
> - Three-status framework: employee (full ERA protections) → worker (working time, NMW, holiday pay, non-discrimination) → self-employed (no statutory protections)
> - Autoclenz Ltd v Belcher [2011] UKSC 41: Supreme Court — written contracts can be disregarded where the reality of the working relationship is inconsistent with contractual terms. Established the "true agreement" principle.
> - Uber BV v Aslam [2021] UKSC 5: Supreme Court — Uber drivers are workers not self-employed. The Court applied Autoclenz to find that Uber's contractual framework did not reflect the reality of control exercised over drivers.
> - Strategic HR risk: organisations using self-employed contractors who are economically dependent may face retrospective NMW liability, holiday pay claims, and worker rights claims
> - IR35 / off-payroll working rules: HMRC test for employment status in tax context — different from employment law test but creates convergent risk
> **Predicate:** "misclassify," "determines," "establishes," "exposes"
> **First word:** "Employment status" — define the three categories immediately
> **Writer note:** This is the highest-stakes topic in 7OS01 for HR practitioners. Make the strategic risk concrete — not abstract. A single worker misclassification in a gig workforce of 500 can trigger 500 individual claims.
> **Word count:** 400–480 words
> **Internal links:** None in this section

#### H2: AC 2.2 — Equality Law and the Forstater Principle — Evaluating Expanding Protected Belief — Major Query Need: Equality Act 2010, protected characteristics, gender-critical beliefs
> **Context vector:** Equality Act 2010 as the primary equality framework; case law development of "philosophical belief" under s.10; Forstater as a landmark decision expanding the scope of protected belief
> **EAV to cover:**
> - Equality Act 2010: nine protected characteristics; direct discrimination, indirect discrimination, harassment, victimisation
> - Philosophical belief protection under s.10 EA 2010: Grainger criteria (five-part test from Grainger plc v Nicholson [2010] IRLR 4)
> - Forstater v CGD Europe [2021] UKEAT 0105/20: gender-critical beliefs (the belief that sex is biological and immutable) are capable of protection as a philosophical belief under s.10 EA 2010 — they meet the Grainger criteria
> - Strategic HR implication: employers must manage potential conflicts between gender-critical belief (protected) and gender reassignment (protected) without unlawfully discriminating against either characteristic
> - Reasonable adjustments: the duty to make reasonable adjustments for disabled employees under s.20 EA 2010; PCP (provision, criterion, or practice) test; substantial disadvantage threshold
> **Predicate:** "protects," "requires," "establishes," "generates"
> **Writer note:** Forstater is a live and contested area. Write it precisely — the EAT held these beliefs are capable of protection; whether a specific act of discrimination occurred is a separate question. Do not overstate or understate the decision.
> **Word count:** 300–360 words

#### H2: AC 3.1 — Collective Employment Law — Trade Union Rights and Industrial Action Regulation — Major Query Need: collective employment law, trade union recognition, industrial action
> **Context vector:** Collective employment law as the framework governing union-employer relations and the regulation of collective action
> **EAV to cover:**
> - Trade union recognition: voluntary vs statutory (TULRCA 1992 as amended by ERA 1999); Central Arbitration Committee (CAC) procedure; majority support in an appropriate bargaining unit
> - Scope of collective bargaining: mandatory (pay, hours, holidays); discretionary (discipline, redundancy, development)
> - Industrial action immunity: s.219 TULRCA 1992 — immunity from tortious liability only where action is in contemplation or furtherance of a trade dispute, following a valid ballot
> - Trade Union Act 2016: 50% turnout threshold for all industrial action ballots; 40% support threshold in important public services (health, education, transport, fire, border security)
> - Unofficial action: no immunity; employer may dismiss participating employees without unfair dismissal liability
> **Predicate:** "governs," "restricts," "establishes," "immunises"
> **Writer note:** At Level 7, the strategic analysis is about how the UK's industrial action framework (among the most restrictive in Europe) shapes the HR-union relationship at organisational level. Connect to the employer's industrial relations strategy.

#### H2: AC 3.2 — Post-Brexit Employment Law — Retained EU Law and the Divergence Risk — Major Query Need: Brexit employment law, retained EU law, REUL Act 2023
> **Context vector:** The constitutional and practical status of EU-derived employment law post-Brexit; the divergence risk created by the REUL Act 2023; strategic implications for HR compliance monitoring
> **EAV to cover:**
> - EU (Withdrawal) Act 2018: converted EU-derived domestic legislation into retained EU law at exit (31 December 2020)
> - REUL (Revocation and Reform) Act 2023: gave the UK government power to amend or revoke retained EU law without EU legislative process; used to amend Working Time Regulations (holiday pay calculation rules)
> - Key retained EU employment law: Working Time Regulations 1998; TUPE 2006 (SI 2006/246); Part-Time Workers Regulations 2000; Fixed-Term Employees Regulations 2002; Agency Workers Regulations 2010
> - ECJ case law: pre-31 December 2020 decisions are retained domestic precedent; post-2020 CJEU decisions are persuasive only — UK courts may depart from EU jurisprudence
> - Divergence examples: holiday pay calculation (WTR amendments 2023 reversed Bear Scotland v Fulton [2015] EAT on rolled-up holiday pay); TUPE consultation rules
> - Strategic HR implication: HR practitioners must now actively monitor both UK legislative amendments AND CJEU developments to identify where the two frameworks are diverging in operationally significant ways
> **Predicate:** "retained," "diverges," "monitors," "assesses," "exposes"
> **Writer note:** This is the forward-looking section. At Level 7 the analytical task is evaluating the strategic risk of divergence — not just describing what happened. Which retained EU employment rights are most vulnerable to amendment? What monitoring approach does HR need?
