##### H3: Buildings Insurance — Reinstatement Basis and the Average Clause
> **Specific content**:
> - **What buildings insurance covers**: The physical structure of the property — walls, roof, floors, fixed fittings (kitchen units, bathroom suites), garages, outbuildings, and boundary walls. Does NOT cover contents, personal possessions, or garden furniture.
> - **Sum insured basis — reinstatement (rebuild) value**: Buildings sum insured must equal the full cost of demolishing and rebuilding the property to its current specification — NOT the market sale price of the property. For most residential properties, reinstatement value is significantly lower than market value in high-demand areas, but may exceed market value in low-demand areas.
> - **BCIS Rebuild Cost Calculator**: The Building Cost Information Service (BCIS), a division of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors (RICS), publishes regional rebuild cost indices. Insurers use BCIS figures to index-link the sum insured at renewal to account for construction cost inflation.
> - **Average Clause**: If the insured sum is less than the true reinstatement value, the insurer is only liable for a proportionate share of any claim. Formula: **Claim settlement = (Claimed loss × Sum Insured) ÷ True Reinstatement Value**. Example: Property worth £200,000 to rebuild, insured for £150,000. Fire causes £50,000 damage. Settlement = (£50,000 × £150,000) ÷ £200,000 = £37,500. The insured bears the remaining £12,500.
> - **Day One Reinstatement (commercial variant)**: The sum insured is declared at Day One values, but the policy provides an agreed uplift percentage (typically 50%) to cover inflation during the rebuilding period. Relevant where IF6 questions cross into commercial property at the border.

##### H3: Contents Insurance — New for Old vs Indemnity Basis
> **Specific content**:
> - **What contents insurance covers**: All moveable items within the home — furniture, clothing, electronics, appliances, food, and personal possessions. Personal possessions extension covers items away from home (specified or blanket all-risks).
> - **New for Old basis**: Insurer replaces lost/damaged item with an equivalent new item — no deduction for age, wear, or depreciation. Standard for most modern household contents policies.
> - **Indemnity basis**: Settlement equals the current second-hand market value of the item, accounting for its age and condition — a depreciation deduction is applied. Less common in personal lines but appears on older legacy policies and in IF6 exam scenarios.
> - **High-value items**: Items above a single article limit (typically £1,000–£2,000) must be separately specified. Unspecified items above the limit may be excluded from the claim settlement.
> - **Valuables limit**: Overall sublimit on jewellery, watches, art, antiques — typically 30–33% of total contents sum insured unless items are individually specified.

##### H3: Standard Household Perils — Definitions and Triggering Conditions
> **Specific content**:
> - **Fire**: Damage caused by combustion, including scorching from direct flame and smoke damage from the same fire event. Does NOT cover scorching from a heat source without flame (e.g., cigarette burn on carpet is typically excluded unless accidental damage is included).
> - **Storm**: Damage caused by violent wind (typically defined as winds above Beaufort Force 8 / 64 km/h+), heavy rain, hail, or snow. Does NOT cover damage pre-existing weak structures or damage caused by gradual deterioration.
> - **Flood**: Damage caused by the overflow of water from any natural watercourse, lake, or sea. Flooding from burst pipes is covered under "escape of water," not flood. EA flood zone classification (Flood Zone 1/2/3) determines insurer loading or decline.
> - **Escape of Water**: Damage from water escaping from fixed domestic plumbing or heating systems — burst pipes (from freezing or pressure), leaking washing machine hose, immersion heater failure. One of the most frequent household claims. Insurers typically include a frost damage clause requiring reasonable preventive measures.
> - **Subsidence**: Downward movement of the ground beneath the property foundations. Triggered by: shrinkage of clay soils in dry weather, extraction of tree roots during drought, historic mining activity, leaking drains causing soil erosion. Not covered: settlement (initial natural compaction in new buildings), coastal erosion, movement of retaining walls not connected to the main structure. Typically carries a £1,000 excess.
> - **Theft**: Theft or attempted theft accompanied by forcible and violent entry or exit. Does NOT cover theft by a person lawfully on the premises (e.g., contractor theft) unless the policy specifically extends to this.

#### H2: Underwriting Non-Standard Household Risks in IF6
> **EAV to cover**: Non-standard construction, high-risk locations, Let Property
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Non-standard construction** (increases risk profile):
>   - **Flat roof**: Greater risk of water ingress; typically requires specialist cover if flat roof exceeds 30% of total roof area. Standard policies may exclude flat roof sections.
>   - **Timber frame**: Fire risk and potential void in standard brick/mortar policies — must be declared.
>   - **Thatched roof**: High fire risk; requires specialist thatched property insurer. Standard domestic policies will not cover thatched properties.
>   - **Listed buildings**: Reinstatement must use original materials and craftsmanship — significantly higher rebuild cost. Heritage consent required for repairs.
> - **High-risk locations**:
>   - **Flood Zone 3a/3b**: Properties in Environment Agency Flood Zone 3 face significant loadings or are declined by standard insurers. Flood Re scheme (UK government-backed reinsurance pool) subsidises flood cover for properties built before 1 January 2009 that cannot obtain affordable cover.
>   - **Subsidence-prone postcodes**: Clay soil areas (London, Hertfordshire), ex-mining areas (South Wales, Yorkshire, Nottinghamshire) — require full subsidence history disclosure.
> - **Let Property endorsement**: If the insured rents the property to tenants, the standard policy must be endorsed or replaced with a specific landlord/let property product. Failure to disclose tenancy voids the household policy.

#### H2: Household Claims Handling in IF6
> **EAV to cover**: FNOL, loss adjuster appointment, settlement basis, betterment, subrogation
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **FNOL**: Insured reports loss promptly by telephone or online portal. Delay may allow insurer to question loss causation or extent.
> - **Loss Adjuster**: Appointed by the insurer for large or complex claims (typically above £5,000–£10,000 threshold). Independent professional who investigates circumstances, confirms coverage, and agrees settlement value. Different from a loss assessor (appointed by and paid by the insured to negotiate with the insurer).
> - **Buildings claim settlement**: Repair/reinstatement by approved contractor network (insurer-managed repair) or cash settlement at agreed reinstatement cost. Average clause applied if underinsured.
> - **Contents claim settlement**: New for old replacement via approved retailer, or cash equivalent. Indemnity applied only if policy basis is indemnity.
> - **Betterment**: Where an old or worn item is replaced with a new equivalent, insurer may deduct betterment — e.g., replacing a 10-year-old boiler with a new model; insurer pays cost minus a percentage reflecting the boiler's remaining useful life.
> - **Subrogation rights**: After settling a buildings claim caused by a third party (e.g., a contractor who causes a fire), insurer may pursue the third party in the insured's name to recover the claim cost.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Is IF6 compulsory for the CII Certificate in Insurance?**
IF6 is an optional unit within the Certificate in Insurance. It is commonly chosen by students working in personal lines household insurance roles.

**Q: What is the average clause and why is it important for IF6?**
The average clause reduces the claim settlement proportionally when the sum insured is less than the true reinstatement value. For IF6 exam purposes: settlement = (claimed loss × sum insured) ÷ true reinstatement value. It is one of the most frequently tested calculations in IF6 MCQ scenarios.

**Q: What is the difference between reinstatement value and market value?**
Reinstatement value is the cost to demolish and rebuild the property to its current specification — this is the basis for buildings insurance. Market value is the sale price of the property, which includes the land and reflects supply/demand conditions in the local property market. Buildings insurance must be based on reinstatement value, not market value.

**Q: Does IF6 cover flood risk in detail?**
Yes — IF6 covers the flood peril, Environment Agency flood zone classification, and the Flood Re scheme for properties unable to obtain standard flood cover. Students must know the difference between flood (external water overflow) and escape of water (internal plumbing failure).
