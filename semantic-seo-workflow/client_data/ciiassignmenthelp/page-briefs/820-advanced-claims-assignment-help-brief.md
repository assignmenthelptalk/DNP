#### H2: Claims Philosophy and Strategy — Major Query Need: understanding claims at senior strategic level
> **EAV to cover**: "Claims is the product" philosophy; claims cost as driver of underwriting profitability; TPA model vs in-house claims; outsourcing strategy and decision criteria
> **Word count**: 350–400 words
> **Instruction**: Frame every point at organisational policy level — this is a claims director deciding structure and philosophy, not a claims handler managing a file

##### H3: Claims as a Competitive Differentiator
> **Specific content**: The 820 syllabus positions the claims function not as a back-office cost centre but as the primary product delivery mechanism of an insurer. From the insurer's perspective, the premium is the price charged; the claim is the product delivered. Claims performance — speed, accuracy, customer communication, fair settlement — directly determines renewal rates, brand reputation, and policyholder loyalty. At claims director level, the strategic question is: how does claims performance create competitive advantage? Key dimensions: (1) Speed of settlement — direct impact on Net Promoter Score (NPS) and renewal retention; (2) Accuracy of coverage decisions — reduces litigation exposure and FOS referrals; (3) Fraud containment — directly reduces loss ratio; (4) Legal cost management — panel solicitor arrangements, early ADR strategy, and litigation expense budgets.
> At portfolio level, total claims cost drives the combined ratio. A combined ratio above 100% means the insurer is paying out more in claims and expenses than it earns in premium — sustained above-100% combined ratios lead to premium rate increases, reinsurance cost rises, and ultimately capital erosion. The claims director's mandate is to manage claims cost per policy in a way that sustains underwriting profitability.

##### H3: TPA Model vs In-House Claims — Outsourcing Strategy
> **Specific content**: Third party administrators (TPAs) are specialist claims handling organisations contracted by insurers to manage claims on their behalf. The TPA model is used when: (1) the insurer lacks specialist claims expertise for a particular class (e.g., complex liability, workers' compensation, aviation); (2) the insurer is entering a new market and lacks the infrastructure; (3) cost efficiency through TPA scale economies is demonstrable; (4) captive insurer arrangements where the parent company does not want to build a claims function.
> In-house claims management is preferred when: (1) the insurer's brand reputation is closely tied to claims handling quality and customer experience; (2) data from claims is proprietary and strategic (underwriting pricing data requires claims data intimacy); (3) the claims volume justifies the fixed infrastructure investment; (4) regulatory oversight (FCA) is more straightforwardly managed through a directly regulated in-house function.
> Decision criteria for outsourcing at senior level: total cost of claims handling (not just TPA fee but claims outcomes — do TPAs settle claims too quickly or too slowly?); quality metrics (customer satisfaction, FOS complaint rates, leakage rates); data security and GDPR compliance; regulatory accountability (the insurer remains responsible for claims conduct even under TPA delegation — FCA ICOBS standards apply).

#### H2: Complex Coverage Disputes — Strategic Analytical Level — Major Query Need: the hardest 820 exam area
> **EAV to cover**: Concurrent causation doctrine (Wayne Tank); aggregation and unifying event test (Barnard v Zurich); policy trigger disputes — occurrence vs claims-made; mesothelioma trigger (Fairchild); employer's liability trigger (Bolton MBC v Municipal Mutual)
> **Word count**: 450–500 words
> **Instruction**: Each doctrine must be stated as a legal rule, applied to an insurance context, and framed as a strategic decision the claims director must make — not a claims handler task. Name the cases and state what each decided.

##### H3: Concurrent Causation — Wayne Tank & Pump Co v Employers Liability [1972]
> **Specific content**: Concurrent causation arises when a loss has two or more proximate causes — one of which is covered and one of which is excluded. Wayne Tank & Pump Co v Employers Liability [1972] (Court of Appeal) established the rule for general liability: where there are two concurrent proximate causes, one insured and one excluded, the insurer is entitled to rely on the exclusion and deny the claim in its entirety. This is the "Wayne Tank rule" — the exclusion prevails.
> Strategic implications for a claims director: (1) Coverage investigations must identify all causes of loss, not just the most obvious one — a single excluded cause (defective workmanship, deliberate act) in a concurrent chain allows denial; (2) Wordings review — the Wayne Tank principle operates by default unless the policy explicitly states otherwise; some modern wordings include "non-vitiation" or "innocent co-insured" clauses that modify this outcome; (3) In composite (multi-insured) policies, the Wayne Tank rule is disapplied for innocent co-insureds — each party's coverage is assessed separately; (4) Post-Wayne Tank, the 820 exam tests whether candidates understand that the applicable test differs between property policies and liability policies — and between all-risks wordings and named perils wordings.

##### H3: Policy Trigger Disputes — Fairchild and Long-Tail Latent Disease
> **Specific content**: Coverage trigger questions are among the most complex and commercially significant issues in claims management. They determine which policy year (and therefore which insurer) is triggered to respond to a claim — critical when the insured has changed insurers across multiple years, or where the loss event is latent and spans multiple policy periods.
> Two trigger doctrines dominate UK insurance law:
> (1) **Occurrence trigger**: the policy is triggered when the loss event occurs, regardless of when it is discovered or when the claim is made. Used in most property policies and general liability policies.
> (2) **Claims-made trigger**: the policy is triggered when the claim is first made against the insured (or first reported to the insurer), regardless of when the underlying incident occurred. Used in professional indemnity, D&O, and some medical malpractice policies.
> **Fairchild v Glenhaven Funeral Services [2002]** (House of Lords): In mesothelioma cases caused by asbestos exposure across multiple employers and policy periods, it is impossible to prove which specific employer's breach caused the disease (because a single fibre can trigger mesothelioma). The House of Lords held that any employer who materially contributed to the risk of the claimant contracting mesothelioma is liable — the "material contribution to risk" test. This means each employer's EL insurer from the relevant exposure periods may be jointly and severally liable.
> **Bolton MBC v Municipal Mutual [2006]**: Applied the Fairchild principle to employer's liability insurance. Confirmed that the EL trigger is the date of exposure to the risk (not the date of diagnosis or manifestation). Each insurer on risk during exposure periods has a proportionate liability.
> Strategic implications: (1) At claims director level, the trigger question determines whether the current-year policy responds or whether the insured (and their historical insurers) must be traced — the EL Tracing Office (ELTO) database is the mechanism for tracing historical EL policies; (2) Reserving strategy must account for latent disease IBNR — claims that have been incurred but not yet reported because the disease has not yet manifested; (3) Reinsurance treaties must be reviewed for trigger alignment — a reinsurance treaty written on an occurrence basis may not respond to a claims-made primary policy trigger.

##### H3: Aggregation — Barnard & Ors v Zurich Insurance and the Unifying Event Test
> **Specific content**: Aggregation clauses allow an insurer to treat multiple individual claims as a single occurrence or event for the purpose of applying deductibles, limits, and reinsurance recoveries. The commercial effect is significant: instead of responding to each claim separately (with each triggering a separate deductible and responding separately under the reinsurance), all claims are aggregated into a single loss — one deductible, one limit, one reinsurance recovery.
> The aggregation test depends on the wording used: (1) **Single occurrence**: All claims must arise from a single event or occurrence — narrow, requires a close causal connection; (2) **Unifying cause** (or originating cause): Claims may be aggregated if they have a common underlying cause — broader, includes cases where the claims arise from the same original negligence or defect even if individual incidents are separated in time and place; (3) **Unifying event**: Intermediate test — there must be a single event from which all claims emanate.
> **Barnard & Ors v Zurich Insurance**: Applied the aggregation test to a professional indemnity context, analysing whether multiple claims arising from the same underlying negligence could be treated as a single claim for deductible and limit purposes. The court examined the policy wording carefully — the applicable aggregation test is always determined by the precise wording.
> Strategic implications at claims director level: (1) Aggregation strategy affects reinsurance recoveries — aggregate across policy years under a proportional treaty vs recover separately under an excess of loss layer; (2) Coverage counsel must advise on the correct aggregation test for each policy wording before making reservation decisions; (3) The claims director must ensure that coverage decisions on aggregation are made early and consistently — ad hoc aggregation decisions create inconsistency and litigation risk.

#### H2: Large Loss Management — Catastrophe Response and Lloyd's Operations — Major Query Need: CAT claims at senior level
> **EAV to cover**: Catastrophe response protocols; international loss coordination; Lloyd's Major Claims; IBNR at event level; cross-border jurisdiction
> **Word count**: 300–350 words
> **Instruction**: Frame at head of claims / chief claims officer level — strategic response decisions, not individual file management

##### H3: Catastrophe Response Protocols
> **Specific content**: A catastrophe event (hurricane, earthquake, flood, pandemic, large terrorism event) creates simultaneous mass-notification, triage, and reserving demands that cannot be met through normal claims processes. At claims director level, the catastrophe response plan must pre-define: (1) Trigger threshold — what event size or notification volume activates the CAT plan; (2) Incident command structure — claims director as incident commander, with defined escalation paths to CEO, CFO, and the board; (3) Emergency staffing protocol — deployment of claims staff from other classes, TPA surge capacity activation, specialist loss adjusters (CAT adjusters, forensic accountants); (4) Customer communication cascade — proactive outreach to policyholders in the affected area before claims are made; (5) Preliminary IBNR estimate — within 24–72 hours of a CAT event, the claims director must provide an initial IBNR estimate to the CFO and reinsurance team to begin reinsurance recovery notifications.
> IBNR at event level: Unlike attritional IBNR (normal development of known claims), catastrophe IBNR must be estimated from event data — total insured values in the affected zone, policy count, historical claims frequency from comparable events (derived from catastrophe models — RMS, AIR Worldwide). The CAT IBNR estimate is heavily model-dependent in the first 30–60 days before sufficient claims notifications arrive.

##### H3: Lloyd's Major Claims Operation and International Coordination
> **Specific content**: Lloyd's of London has a dedicated Major Claims operation — part of Lloyd's Performance Management Directorate — that coordinates claims handling across all syndicates exposed to a major loss event. Key functions: (1) Establishes a major loss code enabling all syndicates to aggregate claims under that event; (2) Produces market-level loss estimates and IBNR guidance; (3) Coordinates with the lead underwriter and lead claims adjuster to establish the overall market position on coverage disputes; (4) Manages Lloyd's relationships with Lloyd's claims handlers and appointed representatives.
> International loss coordination: Cross-border claims create jurisdiction complexity. Key issues: (1) Which law governs the policy — Rome I Regulation (EU) or contractual choice of law; (2) Service of proceedings — where must the insured sue the insurer; (3) Enforcement of judgments — the New York Convention applies to arbitral awards; for court judgments, enforcement depends on bilateral treaties or domestic enforcement rules; (4) Currency of settlement — fluctuating exchange rates between loss currency and policy currency create additional exposure.

#### H2: Fraud Strategy — SIU Design and the IFED Framework — Major Query Need: strategic fraud management
> **EAV to cover**: Organised vs opportunistic fraud; SIU design and management; IFED; Fraud Act 2006; ghost vehicle fraud; staged accident fraud typologies
> **Word count**: 300–350 words
> **Instruction**: Senior strategic framing — SIU as an organisational asset; IFED relationship management; policy and legal framework, not investigative technique

##### H3: Organised Fraud Networks vs Opportunistic Fraud — Strategic Response Distinction
> **Specific content**: The claims director must distinguish between two fundamentally different fraud typologies requiring different strategic responses:
> **Opportunistic fraud**: An individual policyholder exaggerates or fabricates a genuine claim. Relatively low value per incident. Managed through: claims validation processes, contra indicators on file (inconsistency between reported loss and physical evidence), vehicle data checks (DVLA, HPI), and cross-reference with fraud databases (CIFAS, IFB — Insurance Fraud Bureau). The primary tool is analytics — identifying statistical anomalies in claim patterns relative to risk profile.
> **Organised fraud networks**: Criminal enterprises that manufacture claims — ghost vehicle fraud (insuring a vehicle that does not exist, or has been scrapped, and claiming for an accident involving it), staged accidents (orchestrating a "crash for cash" scenario — a deliberate low-speed rear impact at a junction, typically involving multiple claimants for whiplash), and insurance identity fraud (using stolen identities to take out policies). Organised fraud is materially more damaging — the ABI estimates insurance fraud costs UK insurers over £1.1 billion annually. Organised fraud requires an entirely different strategic response: intelligence sharing (IFB IFCAS — Insurance Fraud Covert Action Service), IFED referrals, and criminal prosecution strategy.
> **Fraud Act 2006**: Creates three fraud offences relevant to insurance claims: (1) Fraud by false representation (s.2) — making a false statement knowing it to be false or misleading; (2) Fraud by failing to disclose information (s.3) — where there is a legal duty to disclose; (3) Fraud by abuse of position (s.4) — where the person occupies a position of trust and abuses it. Under the Act, the prosecution does not need to prove actual financial loss — only the dishonest intent to make a gain or cause a loss.

##### H3: SIU Design — Building a Special Investigations Unit
> **Specific content**: A Special Investigations Unit (SIU) is a dedicated team within the claims function responsible for detecting, investigating, and responding to suspected insurance fraud. At claims director level, the SIU design decisions include: (1) **In-house vs outsourced SIU**: In-house SIU provides better data integration and institutional knowledge of fraud patterns; outsourced SIU (specialist investigation firms) provides surge capacity and specialist surveillance capability; (2) **Referral criteria**: Clear, objective criteria for which claims are referred to the SIU — referral thresholds based on claim value, fraud indicator score (propensity model), or specific typology flags; (3) **SIU authority levels**: SIU investigators must have authority to commission surveillance, obtain voice recordings, instruct forensic accountants, and make IFED referrals — these authorities must be documented and regulated; (4) **IFED relationship**: The Insurance Fraud Enforcement Department is a dedicated police unit (City of London Police) funded by the insurance industry. IFED focuses exclusively on insurance fraud. Referral to IFED is appropriate for organised fraud rings where criminal prosecution is warranted — IFED has powers of arrest, search, and seizure that the insurer does not have.

#### H2: Claims Litigation Strategy — ADR, Arbitration and Litigation Funding — Major Query Need: managing claims through the legal process
> **EAV to cover**: ADR options (mediation, early neutral evaluation); LCIA arbitration; New York Convention; litigation funding (Burford Capital); without prejudice communications
> **Word count**: 300–350 words
> **Instruction**: Every option must be positioned as a claims director decision — when to use, what the strategic trade-off is, and what the risk is of getting it wrong

##### H3: Alternative Dispute Resolution — Mediation and Early Neutral Evaluation
> **Specific content**: ADR is the preferred strategic tool for resolving insurance coverage disputes and large liability claims without litigation. The claims director must have a pre-defined ADR strategy embedded in the claims litigation management framework.
> **Mediation**: A voluntary, confidential process in which an independent mediator assists the parties in negotiating a settlement. Not binding unless the parties reach agreement and execute a settlement agreement. Appropriate for: disputes where both parties have commercial reasons to settle (ongoing relationship, reputational risk, cost of litigation); cases where the quantum is agreed but liability is in dispute; cases where a compromise is commercially rational for both sides. Key tactical point: under the Civil Procedure Rules (CPR) Practice Direction Pre-Action Conduct, a party who unreasonably refuses to participate in mediation risks a costs sanction — even if it wins at trial.
> **Early Neutral Evaluation (ENE)**: A process in which an independent evaluator (typically a senior barrister or retired judge) gives a non-binding assessment of the likely outcome at trial. Used where one party needs an independent external view to accept the commercial reality of their position, or where the dispute turns on a legal point that can be assessed without full evidence.
> **Without prejudice**: Communications made "without prejudice" during settlement negotiations cannot be used as evidence at trial. The claims director must ensure that all settlement offers and negotiation correspondence are marked without prejudice. Without prejudice save as to costs ("WPSATC") — the most important variant — allows a Calderbank offer to be disclosed to the court after liability is determined, for the purpose of costs assessment. If the other side fails to beat the offer at trial, they bear costs from the date of the offer.

##### H3: LCIA Arbitration and the New York Convention
> **Specific content**: Arbitration is the primary dispute resolution mechanism for complex commercial insurance disputes, particularly in the London market and for cross-border claims. The London Court of International Arbitration (LCIA) is the principal arbitral institution used in London market insurance and reinsurance disputes.
> Key features of LCIA arbitration relevant to the 820 exam: (1) **Confidentiality** — unlike court proceedings, arbitral proceedings and awards are private, which protects both parties' commercial interests; (2) **Choice of arbitrator** — parties can nominate arbitrators with specialist insurance expertise — critical where the dispute involves highly technical insurance law (coverage trigger, aggregation); (3) **Speed** — LCIA's expedited procedure can produce a final award within 6–9 months vs 2–3 years for commercial court litigation; (4) **Finality** — rights of appeal from LCIA awards to the English courts are very limited (Arbitration Act 1996, s.69 — appeal on point of law only with court permission); (5) **International enforcement** — LCIA awards are enforceable in over 160 countries under the New York Convention on the Recognition and Enforcement of Foreign Arbitral Awards (1958). This is critical for cross-border insurance disputes where the defendant is in a foreign jurisdiction.
> **Strategic decision for claims director**: Arbitration is generally preferred for high-value (£1 million+) commercial insurance coverage disputes, international claims, and disputes where confidentiality is commercially important. Mediation should always be attempted first — cost and speed considerations favour early settlement over arbitration.

##### H3: Litigation Funding — Burford Capital and Third-Party Finance
> **Specific content**: Litigation funding (also called third-party funding or litigation finance) is a mechanism by which a specialist funder (Burford Capital, Harbour Litigation Funding, Vannin Capital) provides the capital to pursue or defend litigation in exchange for a share of any recovery. Historically associated with claimants — the funder pays the legal costs and receives 20–40% of any settlement or judgment. In insurance, litigation funding is increasingly used by: (1) large commercial policyholders disputing a coverage denial — the funded policyholder can pursue the insurer without bearing the legal costs risk; (2) claimants in mass tort actions (asbestos, PFAS, pharmaceutical); (3) insurers pursuing subrogation recoveries where the recovery value is high but upfront legal costs are prohibitive.
> Strategic impact on claims management: Litigation funding materially changes the settlement dynamics. A policyholder backed by a sophisticated funder is a substantially better-resourced opponent. The funder will have conducted a detailed merit assessment before committing funds — meaning a funded claim is one where a professional assessor has concluded it has significant merit. The claims director must factor funded litigation into reservation strategy (higher settlement cost probability) and early ADR strategy (resolve before the funder achieves full position).

#### H2: Claims Reserving — IBNR Methodology and Actuarial Strategy — Major Query Need: reserving at senior leadership level
> **EAV to cover**: IBNR development triangle method; Bornhuetter-Ferguson method; actuarial involvement; FCA and PRA reserve adequacy expectations; catastrophe reserve releases
> **Word count**: 300–350 words
> **Instruction**: Reserving is a strategic financial decision at this level — connect to balance sheet, capital adequacy, and regulatory expectations

##### H3: IBNR Methodology — Development Triangle and Bornhuetter-Ferguson
> **Specific content**: IBNR (Incurred But Not Reported) reserves represent the estimated cost of claims that have occurred but have not yet been reported to the insurer, plus the expected development of known claims. Two primary actuarial methods dominate:
> **Development triangle method** (chain-ladder method): A historical analysis of claims paid and reported across accident years. The actuary constructs a triangle of cumulative paid claims by accident year and development year. From historical patterns, development factors are derived — showing how claims in a given accident year grow from one development year to the next. These development factors are then applied to the most recent diagonal (current outstanding positions) to project ultimate claims cost. The development triangle method works well when historical patterns are stable and there is sufficient data. It produces unreliable estimates when there are changes in claims settlement speed, mix of business, or inflationary environment.
> **Bornhuetter-Ferguson (BF) method**: A credibility-weighted blend of the development triangle estimate and an a priori (expected) loss ratio estimate. The BF method is used when: (1) the accident year is immature (insufficient claims development to rely on the triangle alone); (2) a catastrophe event has produced abnormal development patterns that distort the triangle; (3) there is a new class of business with no meaningful historical triangle. The a priori loss ratio (the "expected" loss ratio from pricing or industry benchmark) is given greater weight in early development years, with the triangle estimate gaining weight as the accident year matures.
> **Actuarial involvement at claims director level**: The claims director does not perform actuarial calculations — but must be able to challenge actuarial reserve estimates, understand the assumptions driving them, and escalate material reserve deficiencies. Reserving workshops — quarterly or bi-annual meetings between claims, actuarial, finance, and underwriting — are best practice.

##### H3: FCA and PRA Reserve Adequacy — Regulatory Expectations
> **Specific content**: Both the FCA (conduct regulator) and PRA (prudential regulator) have expectations regarding claims reserving adequacy. Under Solvency II (and UK Solvency II post-Brexit): (1) Technical provisions must include a Best Estimate of liabilities (50th percentile — the amount that is equally likely to be too high as too low) plus a Risk Margin; (2) Insurers must be able to demonstrate that their Best Estimate uses appropriate actuarial methods and relevant data; (3) The PRA expects that claims reserves are neither systematically optimistic nor conservative — persistent patterns of over- or under-reserving attract supervisory scrutiny.
> Reserve releases: A reserve release occurs when a reserve set in a prior accident year is reduced because the claims have developed more favourably than expected. Reserve releases improve the reported combined ratio and boost pre-tax profit. The FCA and PRA are alert to insurers using reserve releases to mask deteriorating underwriting performance — this is a key risk management concern at board level. The claims director must maintain defensible reserve levels and avoid pressure from the finance function to release reserves prematurely.

#### H2: Emerging Claims — Cyber, Climate, and Litigation Funding Growth — Major Query Need: forward-looking strategic awareness
> **EAV to cover**: Cyber claims (ransomware, ICO 72-hour rule, business interruption from cyber); climate change claims (flood, subsidence, attritional vs CAT); litigation funding growth
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

##### H3: Cyber Claims Management — ICO 72-Hour Notification and Ransomware
> **Specific content**: Cyber claims are among the fastest-growing and most complex class of claims in the London market. At claims director level, the key strategic issues are:
> (1) **Ransomware**: The insured's IT systems are encrypted by a criminal actor who demands a ransom for the decryption key. Key decisions: pay or not pay (law enforcement generally advise against paying — OFAC sanctions risk if the ransomware operator is a sanctioned entity); engage incident response firm (specialist forensic IT — CrowdStrike, Kroll, Mandiant) immediately; notify affected parties; assess business interruption loss. The ransom payment itself is typically covered under cyber policy; the business interruption loss (revenue loss during system downtime) is covered under the BI extension.
> (2) **ICO notification**: Under UK GDPR Article 33, where a personal data breach is likely to result in a risk to individuals' rights and freedoms, the data controller must notify the Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach. Failure to notify on time is a regulatory breach — ICO can impose fines up to £17.5 million or 4% of annual global turnover (whichever is higher). The cyber claims director must ensure that the incident response process includes ICO notification tracking as a day-one priority.

##### H3: Climate Change Claims — Flood, Subsidence, and Emerging CAT Patterns
> **Specific content**: Climate change is shifting the frequency and severity of weather-related claims. Strategic implications: (1) **Flood**: Increased frequency of surface water flooding (as opposed to river flooding) in areas previously classified as low-risk — challenging existing flood zone classifications and policy exclusions; Flood Re (the government-backed reinsurance scheme) covers flood risk for eligible domestic properties, but not commercial properties — commercial flood claims are market-managed; (2) **Subsidence**: Extended dry summers increase soil shrinkage and subsidence claims in clay-heavy soil areas — structural damage to domestic and commercial property. Subsidence IBNR requires longer development assumptions; (3) **Attritional vs CAT events**: Climate change increases attritional frequency (many small weather events) in addition to intensifying discrete CAT events. The attritional trend affects the underlying loss ratio continuously — a strategic pricing and reserving concern distinct from CAT response.

#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions about 820 Advanced Claims

**Q1: Is 820 assessed by written exam or MCQ?**
820 Advanced Claims Management is assessed exclusively by written examination — not MCQ. The examiner expects structured analytical answers demonstrating strategic and senior-level thinking. Candidates who prepare for 820 using MCQ study methods consistently underperform — the exam rewards the ability to construct a reasoned claims strategy recommendation, not recall of isolated facts.

**Q2: What level of claims experience do I need before taking 820?**
820 is designed for candidates who already hold the CII Diploma in Insurance (Dip CII) and have experience operating at a senior level in claims — claims manager, technical claims specialist, or senior claims adjuster at minimum. The CII does not impose a mandatory experience threshold, but the syllabus assumes familiarity with claims processes. Candidates with only operational claims handling experience typically find the strategic framing of 820 challenging without additional preparation.

**Q3: What are the hardest areas of the 820 syllabus?**
The most technically demanding areas are: (1) complex coverage disputes — correctly applying Wayne Tank concurrent causation, policy trigger doctrine (Fairchild, Bolton MBC), and aggregation clauses requires precise legal knowledge; (2) IBNR reserving — distinguishing the development triangle from the Bornhuetter-Ferguson method and knowing when to use each; (3) claims litigation strategy — knowing when to use mediation vs LCIA arbitration vs litigation, and how litigation funding changes the strategic calculation.

**Q4: How long does 820 take to study?**
Most candidates with senior claims experience take 150–180 hours of focused study. Candidates moving into claims management from a different insurance specialism may need 180–200 hours. Study should cover all syllabus areas — do not over-focus on coverage disputes at the expense of fraud strategy and reserving, which carry significant exam weight.

**Q5: How does 820 connect to the ACII qualification?**
820 is one of the three compulsory core units required for the CII Advanced Diploma in Insurance. Passing 820, 930, and 960 (and holding or completing M05, M92, and 530) satisfies the qualification requirements for the Advanced Diploma, which confers ACII (Associate of the Chartered Insurance Institute) designation. ACII is the primary senior-level professional qualification in the UK general insurance market.
