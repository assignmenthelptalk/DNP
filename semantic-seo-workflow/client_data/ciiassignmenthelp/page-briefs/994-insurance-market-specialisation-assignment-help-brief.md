#### H2: Energy Insurance in 994 — Major Query Need: energy market content
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

##### H3: Upstream Energy Insurance — Exploration, Production and Offshore
> **Specific content**: Upstream energy covers risks associated with oil and gas exploration and production activities. Key policy sections: well control/blowout cover (costs of regaining control of a runaway well — well-killing costs, re-drilling costs, seepage, pollution); Operator's Extra Expense (OEE — consequential costs of a well control incident including loss of production income); physical damage to platforms, FPSO (floating production, storage and offloading) vessels, subsea equipment, pipelines. Key underwriting factors: well type (exploration vs development), reservoir depth and pressure, operator experience and track record, age of offshore installation (asset values and maintenance standards), geographic location (Gulf of Mexico vs North Sea vs West Africa — regulatory and environmental liability frameworks differ significantly). EML (Estimated Maximum Loss) for offshore platforms can reach $1bn+ for major installations. London market is the global centre for upstream energy insurance.

##### H3: Downstream Energy Insurance — Refineries and Processing Plants
> **Specific content**: Downstream energy covers petrochemical refineries, LNG liquefaction/regasification terminals, oil refineries, and gas processing facilities. Policy structure: material damage (all-risk or named perils on plant and machinery, buildings, and stock) and business interruption (indemnity period often 24–36 months given complexity of reconstruction/repair). EML analysis is the core underwriting tool: the maximum foreseeable loss from a single event at the facility — typically a major fire or explosion at the highest-value process unit. EML calculation considers: process hazard analysis, fire and explosion modelling, separation of high-value process units, fire protection quality (suppression, detection, water supply). DSU (Delay in Start-Up) is sometimes added to cover additional financing costs and lost revenue during construction delays caused by insured events.

#### H2: Construction Insurance in 994 — Major Query Need: construction market content
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

##### H3: CAR Policy Structure and JCT Contract Forms
> **Specific content**: Contractors' All Risks (CAR) insurance covers the contract works during the construction period. Standard CAR structure: Section 1 — Contract Works (material damage to the works being constructed — all-risk basis, sum insured = contract value); Section 2 — Third Party Liability (injury to third parties or damage to their property caused by the contract operations); optional Section 3 — Employer's Liability. The policy period runs from commencement of works to practical completion and the maintenance/defects liability period (typically 12 months post-completion). JCT contract forms define risk allocation between employer and contractor: under JCT Design and Build, the contractor bears risk for design and construction; under JCT Standard Building Contract, the employer retains design risk while the contractor bears construction risk. The CAR insured party depends on the JCT form — contractor-arranged, employer-arranged, or joint-name policies.

##### H3: DSU — Delay in Start-Up Insurance
> **Specific content**: DSU (Delay in Start-Up) insurance is the construction-phase equivalent of Business Interruption. It covers financial losses resulting from a delay to the start of commercial operations caused by an insured event under the contract works (material damage) section. Covered losses: anticipated revenue loss during the delay period, additional financing costs (interest on construction loans), fixed overhead and operating costs that cannot be avoided during the delay. The indemnity period begins from the original target start-up date and runs for the actual delay period, capped at the maximum indemnity period. Underwriting key factors: total project value, construction timeline, revenue projections, construction risk quality (contractor experience, design complexity), and the maximum indemnity period.
