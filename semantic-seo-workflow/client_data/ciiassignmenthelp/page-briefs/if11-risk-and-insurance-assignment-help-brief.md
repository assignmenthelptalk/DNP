> **Word count**: 400–450 words

##### H3: Hazard, Peril, and Risk — Precise Definitions for IF11
> **Specific content**:
> - **Risk**: The possibility of an adverse outcome — in insurance, specifically the possibility of financial loss. Risk has two dimensions: frequency (how often a loss event is likely to occur) and severity (how much damage is likely if it does occur).
> - **Peril**: The actual cause of the loss event — the incident that triggers the loss. Examples: fire, flood, theft, collision. Perils are what insurance policies cover (or exclude).
> - **Hazard**: A condition that increases the probability (frequency) or magnitude (severity) of a peril occurring. Three types:
>   - **Physical hazard**: A tangible, material condition — faulty electrical wiring (increases fire peril frequency), oil on a factory floor (increases slip/fall severity), proximity to a flood-prone river.
>   - **Moral hazard**: Deliberate behaviour by the insured that increases the chance of loss — arson to claim insurance proceeds, staging a motor accident.
>   - **Morale hazard** (attitudinal hazard): Careless or indifferent behaviour by the insured because they know they are insured — failing to lock premises because the insurer will pay for theft, leaving tools unattended on a building site.
> - **IF11 exam application**: Scenarios present a fact pattern and ask students to identify whether a condition is a physical hazard, moral hazard, or peril. The distinction between moral and morale hazard is a frequent trap — moral = deliberate intent; morale = careless attitude.

##### H3: Pure Risk vs Speculative Risk — The Insurability Boundary
> **Specific content**:
> - **Pure risk**: The possibility of loss ONLY — there is no possibility of gain. Example: the risk that a factory burns down. The factory either burns (loss) or does not burn (no gain — status quo maintained). Pure risks are insurable because the insurer can calculate expected losses across a large pool of homogeneous risks.
> - **Speculative risk**: The possibility of loss OR gain. Example: a business decision to enter a new market — the business may profit or may lose money. Investment returns, currency exchange rates, and commodity price movements are speculative risks. Speculative risks are NOT insurable.
> - **Why the distinction matters for IF11**: Insurance covers pure risks only. Scenarios that present business decisions, investment losses, or currency losses are testing whether students know these are speculative (uninsurable) risks.
> - **Dynamic vs Static risk** (secondary distinction in IF11): Static risks are pure risks that exist regardless of economic conditions (fire, theft). Dynamic risks relate to change in the economy or business environment — they may be pure or speculative.

##### H3: The Risk Management Process
> **Specific content**:
> - **Step 1 — Risk Identification**: Systematically identify all risks that could affect the organisation. Methods: physical inspection of premises, employee interviews, checklists (risk inventory), hazard and operability studies (HAZOP), SWOT analysis (internal risks), PESTLE analysis (external macro risks), scenario analysis (what-if modelling).
> - **Step 2 — Risk Assessment (Evaluation)**: Assess each identified risk on two dimensions: (1) Frequency — how often is it likely to occur? (2) Severity — how much financial damage if it occurs? Produces a risk matrix (frequency × severity grid) — risks in the high-frequency/high-severity quadrant are highest priority.
> - **Step 3 — Risk Management (Treatment)**: For each assessed risk, select a treatment: Transfer, Avoidance, Reduction, or Retention (TARR mnemonic). Selection is based on cost-effectiveness and risk appetite.
> - **Step 4 — Monitoring**: Continuously monitor residual risks after treatment. Update the risk register. Review controls regularly. Ensure insured sums remain adequate.

#### H2: Risk Financing Options in IF11 — Transfer, Avoidance, Reduction, Retention
> **EAV to cover**: Each financing option with examples, insurance as transfer mechanism, captives, deductibles
> **Word count**: 350–400 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Transfer**: Passing the financial consequence of risk to another party.
>   - **Insurance**: The primary risk transfer mechanism — the insured pays a premium; the insurer absorbs the financial consequence of covered losses. Positions insurance explicitly within the risk financing framework.
>   - **Contractual transfer**: Indemnity clauses and hold harmless agreements in contracts — one party accepts financial liability on behalf of another (e.g., a contractor holds harmless the property owner for accidents caused by the contractor's employees).
> - **Avoidance**: Eliminating the activity that creates the risk entirely. Example: a company discontinues a product line because the product liability exposure is too great. Avoidance eliminates both the risk AND the potential business opportunity — only used when risk is not manageable by other means.
> - **Reduction**: Reducing the frequency or severity of the risk without eliminating it.
>   - **Frequency reduction (prevention)**: Installing sprinkler systems reduces the chance of fire spreading. Employee training reduces workplace accidents.
>   - **Severity reduction (protection)**: Fire suppression systems reduce damage if a fire does start. Business continuity plans reduce downtime from a system failure.
> - **Retention**: The organisation bears the financial consequence of the risk itself.
>   - **Unplanned retention**: Organisation is unaware of the risk and therefore makes no provision — results in unbudgeted losses.
>   - **Planned retention**: Organisation deliberately accepts the risk and sets aside funds to meet losses. Methods: self-insurance (ring-fenced internal fund), deductibles/excesses (organisation pays the first £X of each claim), captive insurer (wholly-owned subsidiary established to write the parent company's risks — provides access to reinsurance markets and potential tax efficiency).
> - **Risk appetite and risk tolerance**: The organisation's stated willingness to accept risk (appetite) and the maximum deviation from risk targets it will tolerate (tolerance) — determines which financing option is appropriate for each risk.

#### H2: Insurable Risk Criteria in IF11
> **EAV to cover**: Six criteria for an insurable risk, fortuitous, homogeneous, catastrophic exclusion
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Specific content**:
> - For a risk to be insurable, it must meet the following criteria:
> 1. **Fortuitous**: The loss must be accidental and unintentional from the insured's perspective — losses caused by deliberate acts of the insured are not insurable (moral hazard prevention).
> 2. **Definite and measurable**: The loss must be capable of being defined (when, where, how) and financially valued (how much).
> 3. **Large number of similar (homogeneous) risks**: The insurer needs enough similar risks in the pool to make statistical law of large numbers predictions reliable. One-of-a-kind risks are difficult to insure or require specialist markets (Lloyd's).
> 4. **Independent risks**: The risks in the pool should not all be correlated — if one loss triggers all others simultaneously (systemic risk), the pool cannot function. This is why catastrophic events (earthquakes, pandemics) challenge standard insurance markets.
> 5. **Not catastrophic**: No single event should be capable of causing total loss across the entire insured pool simultaneously — this would bankrupt the insurer. Reinsurance addresses this at the macro level.
> 6. **Insurable interest**: The insured must have a legally recognised financial interest in the subject matter (Macaura v Northern Assurance 1925 — shareholder cannot insure company property in their personal name).

#### H2: How to Pass the IF11 MCQ Exam — Exam Technique
> **EAV to cover**: Scenario elimination, common IF11 traps, risk classification practice
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Content**:
> - IF11 scenarios typically present a situation and ask: Is this a hazard or peril? Is this a pure or speculative risk? Which risk financing option is most appropriate?
> - **Common IF11 traps**: (1) Confusing moral hazard (deliberate) with morale hazard (careless). (2) Classifying investment risk as a pure risk — investment risk is speculative. > - **Risk matrix**: Practice placing risks on a frequency × severity matrix and recommending the appropriate financing option. High-frequency/low-severity → reduce or retain. Low-frequency/high-severity → transfer (insure).

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is the difference between a hazard and a peril in IF11?**
A peril is the actual cause of the loss — fire, flood, theft. A hazard is a condition that increases the likelihood or severity of the peril occurring. Faulty wiring is a physical hazard that increases the fire peril. The exam tests whether students can correctly classify a given condition as a hazard type or a peril.

**Q: Why is speculative risk not insurable?**
Insurance pools work by distributing the cost of accidental losses across a large group with similar risk profiles. Speculative risks (investment decisions, currency movements) can produce gains or losses — including them in an insurance pool would create moral hazard problems and make loss prediction impossible.

**Q: What is a captive insurer?**
A captive is a wholly-owned subsidiary insurance company established by a parent organisation to write the parent's risks. It enables the parent to retain risks formally, access the reinsurance market directly, and potentially achieve tax efficiency on premium flows.
