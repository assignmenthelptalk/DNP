##### H3: Corporate Governance in Insurance — UK Corporate Governance Code and Board Composition
> **Specific content**:
> The UK Corporate Governance Code (the Code) applies on a comply-or-explain basis to premium-listed UK companies, including listed insurers. For insurers regulated under Solvency II, the PRA also imposes governance requirements through the Governance Conditions — so listed insurers face dual governance obligations: the Code and Solvency II Pillar 2.
>
> **Board composition requirements under the Code**: The Chair and CEO roles must be separated — a single individual cannot hold both. At least one-third of the board (excluding the Chair) must be independent non-executive directors. For FTSE 350 companies, the Code recommends half the board (excluding the Chair) be independent non-executives. The Chair must be independent on appointment.
>
> **Board skills matrix for insurers**: An insurance company board requires specific competencies not required by the Code in generic form. The PRA expects insurance boards to collectively possess expertise in: actuarial and reserving, underwriting (for general and specialist lines), claims management, risk management (including catastrophe modelling and stress testing), investment management, regulatory affairs (SMCR, Solvency II, FCA conduct), technology and cyber risk, and financial management (capital allocation, reinsurance purchasing). 990 exam questions require candidates to evaluate whether a given board composition meets these insurance-specific requirements — generic governance knowledge is insufficient.
>
> **Board committees relevant to insurers**: (1) Audit Committee — must include at least three independent non-executives; reviews financial reporting, internal audit work, and external audit independence; for insurers, the Audit Committee reviews reserving adequacy as a specific insurance item. (2) Risk Committee — for insurers, the Risk Committee is typically separate from the Audit Committee (unlike many non-financial companies where they are combined); the Risk Committee reviews the risk appetite statement, ORSA, and stress and scenario testing; its Chair reports directly to the Board. (3) Remuneration Committee — must ensure remuneration policy does not incentivise excessive risk taking; the PRA imposes specific requirements on remuneration for material risk takers (overlap with SMCR Certified Persons).

##### H3: SMCR — Senior Managers, Certified Persons, and Conduct Rules
> **Specific content**:
> The Senior Managers and Certification Regime (SMCR) was extended to insurers by the FCA and PRA in December 2019. SMCR replaced the Approved Persons Regime and introduced individual accountability for named senior individuals.
>
> **Tier 1 — Senior Managers**: Individuals holding a Prescribed Responsibility (PR). Each Senior Manager must have a Statement of Responsibilities (SoR) — a document mapping their specific area of responsibility. The key Prescribed Responsibilities for insurers include: responsibility for the firm's Solvency II compliance, responsibility for the ORSA, responsibility for the internal audit function, responsibility for the actuarial function, and responsibility for the compliance function. The **duty of responsibility** is the central individual accountability mechanism: if a breach of regulatory requirement occurs in a Senior Manager's area of responsibility, the Senior Manager is personally liable unless they can demonstrate they took reasonable steps to prevent the breach. This reverses the evidential burden — the Senior Manager must prove they acted.
>
> **Tier 2 — Certified Persons**: Individuals performing Significant Harm Functions — typically material risk takers and senior managers who are not FCA/PRA-approved but whose roles could cause significant harm. The firm (not the regulator) must certify their fitness and propriety annually. Criteria: honesty, integrity, reputation, competence, capability, financial soundness. Failure to certify means the individual cannot perform the function. Certified Persons are subject to the Individual Conduct Rules.
>
> **Tier 3 — Conduct Rules**: Apply to all staff, including non-executive directors. The five **Individual Conduct Rules**: (1) Act with integrity. (2) Act with due skill, care, and diligence. (3) Be open and cooperative with the FCA, PRA, and other regulators. (4) Pay due regard to the interests of customers and treat them fairly. (5) Observe proper standards of market conduct. The four additional **Senior Manager Conduct Rules**: (6) Take reasonable steps to ensure the business of the firm for which you are responsible complies with regulatory requirements. (7) Take reasonable steps to ensure effective control over the business. (8) Be responsible for appropriate handover when taking on a new role. (9) Disclose appropriately any information the FCA or PRA would reasonably expect.
>
> **Regulatory investigations under SMCR**: The FCA and PRA can investigate individual Senior Managers and Certified Persons directly — not just the firm. Enforcement actions can result in prohibition from performing regulated activities, financial penalties, and public statements. The firm's obligation to maintain fitness and propriety records (including Conduct Rule breaches, criminal convictions, and regulatory sanctions) creates a compliance infrastructure obligation at Board level.

##### H3: Solvency II Governance — Four Key Functions and ORSA
> **Specific content**:
> Solvency II Pillar 2 mandates four governance Key Functions within every insurer. Each function has a specific role and reporting line.
>
> **(1) Actuarial Function**: Responsible for calculating and certifying the technical provisions (reserves) — the insurer's estimate of its future claims liabilities. For insurers using an internal model for SCR calculation, the Actuarial Function provides an opinion on the model's assumptions and methodology. The Actuarial Function also provides an opinion on the overall underwriting policy (whether the insurer is pricing risk adequately) and on reinsurance adequacy. The Actuarial Function Head is typically a Senior Manager under SMCR with a specific Prescribed Responsibility.
>
> **(2) Risk Management Function**: Implements the risk management system. Owns the risk strategy (the firm's approach to identifying, measuring, monitoring, and mitigating risks). Produces the ORSA — the risk management function is the technical author of the ORSA, though the Board is responsible for owning and challenging it. Monitors the risk profile continuously against risk appetite.
>
> **(3) Compliance Function**: Monitors compliance with all applicable regulatory requirements — Solvency II, SMCR, FCA conduct rules, ICOBS, Consumer Duty, and any other sector-specific requirements. Advises the board and senior management on compliance obligations. Manages regulatory reporting — including RSR (Regular Supervisory Report) and SFCR (Solvency and Financial Condition Report) under Solvency II Pillar 3. Manages conduct risk including complaints handling and Consumer Duty outcomes monitoring.
>
> **(4) Internal Audit**: Provides independent assurance to the board that the risk management system, internal controls, and governance arrangements are effective. Internal Audit must be operationally independent — the Head of Internal Audit cannot hold an executive management role. Typically reports to the Audit Committee and, through it, to the Board. Internal Audit must audit the ORSA process, the Actuarial Function outputs, and the compliance function — providing second- and third-line assurance.
>
> **ORSA at Board level**: The ORSA is the insurer's own assessment of its overall solvency needs, beyond the regulatory minimum SCR. The Board must: approve the ORSA process and methodology, actively challenge the ORSA outputs (not merely receive them), ensure the ORSA reflects the firm's actual risk profile (not just a box-ticking exercise), and submit the ORSA to the PRA at least annually. The ORSA must include: forward-looking scenarios (stress tests covering, for example, a 1-in-200-year catastrophe event, a sustained underwriting loss period, or a major cyber incident), capital planning projections, and any material deviation from the SCR standard formula assumptions. Boards that fail to demonstrate genuine engagement with the ORSA process face PRA supervisory action.

##### H3: M&A Strategy in Insurance — MGAs, Portfolio Acquisition, and Run-Off
> **Specific content**:
> 990 exam questions on M&A strategy require candidates to evaluate the strategic rationale and risks of each acquisition type — not merely describe what each type is.
>
> **MGA (Managing General Agent) acquisition**: An MGA is a delegated authority vehicle that underwrites on behalf of a capacity provider (insurer). Acquiring an MGA provides: distribution reach without proportionate capital consumption (the capacity is provided by the insurer; the MGA earns a management fee plus profit commission); specialist underwriting expertise in a niche class; and technology-driven distribution. Risks include: MGA dependency on continued capacity provision, potential adverse selection if underwriting controls are weak, and conduct risk (the capacity provider retains regulatory liability for the MGA's underwriting decisions).
>
> **Portfolio company acquisition (in-force book)**: Acquiring another insurer's in-force portfolio provides immediate scale, geographic or class diversification, and policyholder relationships. Key risks: reserving adequacy of the acquired book (adverse reserve development post-acquisition), integration of underwriting and claims systems, retention of key staff and broker relationships.
>
> **Run-off acquisition**: A run-off acquirer takes on a legacy portfolio of long-tail liabilities (e.g. employers' liability, product liability, or asbestos-related claims) from an insurer wishing to exit the market. The acquirer specialises in managing the run-off efficiently — using actuarial modelling, commutations (negotiated settlements with reinsurers), and adverse development cover to limit capital exposure. Strategic rationale: significant technical profit opportunity if reserves prove adequate; but requires specialist legacy expertise not present in all insurers.

##### H3: Insurtech Strategy — Partnership, Build, or Buy
> **Specific content**:
> The 990 exam regularly tests strategic decision-making frameworks. For Insurtech strategy, candidates must evaluate the three options against the insurer's capabilities and strategic objectives:
>
> **Partnership**: Co-develop technology solutions with an Insurtech firm. The insurer provides distribution, regulatory authorisation, and capital; the Insurtech provides technology, data science, and speed to market. Lower investment required; the insurer retains optionality. Risk: the Insurtech partner may develop competing distribution capability or exit the partnership.
>
> **Build (internal innovation)**: The insurer develops Insurtech capability through internal innovation labs, hiring data scientists, and building proprietary platforms. Full control and IP ownership. Risk: high cost, slow execution compared to specialist Insurtechs, organisational culture resistance.
>
> **Buy (acquisition)**: Acquires an Insurtech outright. Provides immediate technology access and talent acquisition. Risk: loss of the entrepreneurial culture that made the Insurtech effective; integration complexity; overvaluation (Insurtech valuations often based on growth metrics rather than profitability).

#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions about Unit 990

> FAQ targets representative query variants — not repetition of main content.

**Q1: Is 990 the right unit for a senior insurance manager?**
Answer direction: 990 is specifically designed for professionals operating at or aspiring to Board level, C-suite, or senior risk and governance roles in insurance firms. The syllabus directly maps to the responsibilities of Chief Risk Officers, Chief Compliance Officers, Heads of Internal Audit, and Non-Executive Directors under SMCR. If your role involves governance oversight, ORSA responsibility, rating agency relationships, or ESG strategy, 990 is the most relevant Advanced Diploma optional unit for building exam-assessed capability at Level 6.

**Q2: How long does it take to prepare for the 990 written exam?**
Answer direction: Most candidates with senior insurance experience require 150–180 hours of structured study. The exam requires more than industry knowledge — it tests the ability to apply specific regulatory frameworks (SMCR Prescribed Responsibilities, Solvency II governance function definitions, ORSA requirements) with precision and evaluate strategic choices under exam conditions. Candidates new to governance and regulatory roles typically require 180–220 hours. The 990 exam rewards structured answer technique as much as content knowledge.

**Q3: What is the difference between SMCR and the UK Corporate Governance Code in the 990 exam?**
Answer direction: The UK Corporate Governance Code governs the structure and accountability of the board as a collective body — composition, committees, director independence, board evaluation. SMCR governs individual accountability — it assigns Prescribed Responsibilities to named Senior Managers, imposes a personal duty of responsibility, and creates direct regulatory enforcement exposure for individuals. The 990 exam tests both frameworks and requires candidates to understand how they interact: a Board that satisfies the Code may still have SMCR failures if individual Senior Manager responsibilities are not clearly mapped in Statements of Responsibilities.

**Q4: How often does the ORSA need to be submitted to the PRA?**
Answer direction: At least annually, and additionally after any significant change in risk profile — for example, a major acquisition, entry into a new class of business, or a significant catastrophe loss. The PRA does not prescribe a precise format for the ORSA, but expects it to be a genuine Board-level document: challenged and approved by the Board, not merely produced by the risk management function and rubber-stamped. The 990 exam frequently tests what constitutes a high-quality ORSA versus a compliance exercise — candidates should be able to identify the difference.

**Q5: Does 990 cover Lloyd's of London specifically?**
Answer direction: Yes — 990 includes Lloyd's governance within both the corporate governance and stakeholder management sections. This covers the Lloyd's Franchise Board and its role in oversight of syndicate performance through the Syndicate Business Plan (SBP) process. It also covers the strategic choice facing insurers between operating through the Lloyd's market and operating through the company market — and the specific regulatory and stakeholder considerations that apply in each. Candidates working in the Lloyd's market should expect questions that require them to apply the SMCR and governance frameworks specifically to the Lloyd's operating structure.
