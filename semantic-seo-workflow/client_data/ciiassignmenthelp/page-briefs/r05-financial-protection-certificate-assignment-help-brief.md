#### H2: Term Assurance — Variants and Use Cases in R05
> **EAV to cover**: Level, decreasing, increasing, convertible, renewable term; when each is used
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Level term assurance**: Fixed sum assured payable on death during the policy term — the sum assured does not change throughout the term. Used for: family income replacement where a fixed lump sum is required, key person insurance, business protection.
> - **Decreasing term assurance**: Sum assured reduces over the term — matches the reducing balance of a repayment mortgage. Used for: mortgage protection where the outstanding capital balance decreases with each repayment. Cheaper than level term because the insurer's maximum liability decreases over time.
> - **Increasing term assurance**: Sum assured increases over the term — either by a fixed percentage (e.g., 3% p.a.) or in line with an index (RPI/CPI). Used for: protecting against inflation erosion where the lump sum must maintain real purchasing power over a long term.
> - **Convertible term assurance**: Contains a guaranteed convertibility option — the policyholder can convert the policy to a whole of life or endowment policy at the end of the term (or at defined intervals) without further medical underwriting. The new premium is based on age at conversion, not health status. Used for: clients who may need to extend cover without medical evidence at a future point.
> - **Renewable term assurance**: Contains a guaranteed renewability option — the policyholder can renew the policy for a further term at the end of the original term without further medical evidence. Premium at renewal is based on age and health class at that time (not at inception).
> - **Joint life term**: Covers two lives — pays out on the first death. Cheaper than two single policies but terminates after the first claim.

#### H2: Income Protection Insurance in R05
> **EAV to cover**: Deferred period options, benefit basis, disability definitions, benefit escalation
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Income Protection Insurance (IPI)**: Provides a regular monthly income if the insured is unable to work due to illness or injury. Benefits continue until return to work, death, or the policy cessation age (typically retirement age or end of term).
> - **Deferred period**: The waiting period before IP benefits commence — 4, 8, 13, 26, or 52 weeks. During the deferred period, the insured must fund their own income. Aligning the deferred period with employer sick pay provisions reduces premium cost.
> - **Benefit basis**: IP benefits are typically set at 50–65% of gross pre-incapacity earnings. The benefit is paid free of income tax (HMRC: IP benefits from personally paid premiums are tax-free; employer-paid IP premiums create a taxable benefit in payment).
> - **Disability definitions** (same as GR1, applied at individual level):
>   - **Own occupation**: Unable to perform the specific duties of their own occupation — most generous; most common in professional/specialist occupation policies.
>   - **Any occupation**: Unable to perform any occupation for which reasonably suited — most restrictive; lowest premium.
>   - **Activities of daily living (ADL)**: Unable to perform defined daily tasks — intermediate definition.
> - **Waiver of premium**: A built-in or add-on benefit that waives IP policy premiums during the deferred period (or after a further waiting period) when the insured is making a claim.
> - **Benefit escalation**: IP benefits may be index-linked at claim stage to maintain real value during long periods of payment. Premium index-linking at in-force stage maintains the real value of the maximum benefit.
> - **Short-term IP**: Benefits limited to 1 or 2 years per claim (not full-term) — cheaper but insufficient for long-term disability. Exam question: when is short-term IP appropriate vs long-term IP?

#### H2: Critical Illness Insurance in R05
> **EAV to cover**: ABI model definitions, core conditions, survival period, partial payments, CI vs IP distinction
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Critical Illness Insurance (CI)**: Pays a lump sum (or monthly income in some products) on diagnosis of a defined serious illness. The insured uses the lump sum as they choose — mortgage clearance, medical costs, income replacement.
> - **ABI Model Definitions**: The ABI publishes standardised condition definitions — all CII-compliant CI policies use these definitions, ensuring consistency across insurers for the same named condition.
> - **Core three conditions (by claim volume)**: Cancer (excluding less-advanced cases below defined staging criteria), heart attack (meeting specified ECG/enzyme/angiography criteria), stroke (causing permanent neurological deficit lasting a minimum period).
> - **Additional conditions**: Standard policies typically cover 5–7 conditions; enhanced policies 30–50+. Additional conditions include: multiple sclerosis, Parkinson's disease, major organ transplant, kidney failure, blindness, deafness, total permanent disability, third-degree burns.
> - **Survival period**: Most policies require survival of 14 or 30 days post-diagnosis before the benefit is payable. Death within the survival period: CI does not pay; the life assurance death benefit pays instead (if a combined life+CI policy).
> - **Partial payment conditions**: For less serious forms of named conditions (e.g., carcinoma in situ, less advanced cancer), some policies pay a smaller partial benefit (e.g., 25% of sum assured, capped at a maximum of £25,000).
> - **CI vs IP**: CI pays once on diagnosis; IP pays a monthly income for the duration of incapacity. A client who suffers a heart attack receives a CI lump sum but is able to return to work — IP would not continue paying if they have recovered sufficiently to work.

#### H2: Trust Arrangements and IHT for Life Policies in R05
> **EAV to cover**: Why write in trust, discretionary vs absolute trust, IHT implication, nomination
> **Word count**: 250–300 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Why write a life policy in trust?** Life policy proceeds paid to the estate on death are: (1) subject to probate delay — beneficiaries must wait for grant of probate before receiving funds; (2) subject to Inheritance Tax (IHT) if the estate exceeds the nil-rate band (£325,000 in 2024-25). Placing the policy in trust removes the proceeds from the estate — they pass directly to beneficiaries without probate or IHT.
> - **Discretionary trust**: Trustees have discretion over who among the class of beneficiaries receives the proceeds and in what proportions. No named individual has a fixed entitlement — allows the trustees to respond to changed family circumstances. Most joint life policies are written under a discretionary trust.
> - **Absolute (bare) trust**: A named beneficiary has an irrevocable, fixed entitlement — the policy proceeds must go to them. Once established with a beneficiary over 18, the trust cannot be varied. Used where the beneficiary is known and circumstances are stable.
> - **Expression of wishes**: A letter of wishes (non-binding) that the policyholder gives to discretionary trustees — guides their decision on distribution. Important where family circumstances may change.
> - **Assignment vs trust**: Assigning a life policy transfers legal ownership to another person (e.g., absolute assignment to a mortgage lender as security). Trust separates legal ownership (trustee) from beneficial ownership (beneficiaries) — a more flexible arrangement for family protection.
> - **Nominee vs trust**: A nomination on a pension or life policy directs the insurer on the policyholder's wishes but does not create a trust — relevant for some group schemes; for individual policies, a trust is more protective from an IHT and probate perspective.

#### H2: How to Pass the R05 MCQ Exam — Exam Technique
> **EAV to cover**: Scenario elimination, common R05 traps, trust and CI scenario patterns
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Content**:
> - R05 MCQ scenarios typically present a client fact pattern and ask students to identify the most appropriate protection product or the correct tax/trust outcome.
> - **Common R05 traps**: (1) Recommending decreasing term for an interest-only mortgage — the balance does not decrease; level term is appropriate. (2) Assuming IP benefits are always taxable — personally paid premiums produce tax-free benefits. (3) Confusing survival period (CI pays if insured survives) with deferred period (IP starts paying after the deferred period). (4) Assuming a written nomination avoids IHT and probate — only a formal trust achieves this for life policies.
> - **Product selection logic for MCQ**: Map: repayment mortgage → decreasing term. Interest-only mortgage → level term. Long-term income replacement → IP, own occupation. Mortgage protection with CI component → combined life + CI. Family protection outside estate → level term written in discretionary trust.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Does R05 at Certificate level cover pensions and investments?**
No — R05 at Certificate level (within the CII Insurance pathway) focuses exclusively on protection products: life assurance, income protection, critical illness, and mortgage protection. Pension and investment content appears in separate units at Diploma and Advanced Diploma level.

**Q: Why does writing a life policy in trust matter for the R05 exam?**
Trust-based scenarios are a recurring theme in R05 MCQ questions. The key points to know are: (1) a policy in trust falls outside the estate for IHT purposes; (2) proceeds are paid without probate delay; (3) discretionary trusts give trustees flexibility; (4) absolute trusts fix the beneficiary irrevocably.

**Q: What is the difference between CI and IP for R05 purposes?**
Critical illness pays a single lump sum on diagnosis of a defined serious condition. Income protection pays a monthly benefit during the period of incapacity and continues until return to work or retirement. They address different financial needs and are often combined in a protection portfolio.
