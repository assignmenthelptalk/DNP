#### H2: The Underwriting Risk Assessment Process
> **EAV to cover**: Proposal form, hazard identification, risk factors, underwriting decision outcomes
> **Word count**: 400–450 words

##### H3: Hazard Types in Underwriting Assessment
> **Specific content**:
> - **Physical hazard**: A tangible, material condition that increases the frequency or severity of loss — e.g., old wiring in a property (fire risk), a workshop next to an insured warehouse (ignition risk), a driver's age or vehicle type.
> - **Moral hazard**: Deliberate behaviour that increases the likelihood of a claim — dishonesty, fraud intent, deliberate non-disclosure of material facts. The insurer cannot quantify moral hazard precisely but uses proposal form answers and historical claims data to identify indicators.
> - **Financial hazard**: A financial condition that creates incentive for loss — where the insured stands to gain financially from a claim (overinsurance, sum insured significantly above asset value). A key underwriting concern: if the sum insured greatly exceeds the asset's value, the insured has a financial incentive to allow loss to occur.

##### H3: Rating Factors by Class of Business
> **Specific content**:
> - **Personal motor**: Driver age, occupation, driving record (claims history, convictions), vehicle type (make, model, engine size, value), annual mileage, geographic location (postcode/region), primary use (social/domestic/pleasure vs business use).
> - **Household property**: Property type (construction — masonry, timber, thatch), age of building, occupancy (owner-occupied, let, unoccupied), geographic location (flood zone, subsidence risk, crime rate), claims history, sum insured and any high-value items.
> - **Commercial**: Nature of business, turnover, number of employees, location, construction of premises, security measures, claims experience (frequency and severity over 3–5 years), business continuity arrangements.
> - **Underwriting decision outcomes**: Accept at standard rates → issue quotation. Accept with premium loading → quotation with stated loading and reason. Accept with exclusion → quote with endorsement excluding specific risk element. Refer to senior underwriter → risk outside local authority limit — escalated for specialist assessment. Decline → risk outside the insurer's appetite or assessment criteria — no quotation issued.

##### H3: Duty of Disclosure — Good Faith Principles for International Markets
> **Specific content**:
> - The duty of utmost good faith (uberrimae fidei) is a foundational principle of insurance law across most jurisdictions — requiring both parties to disclose all material facts before contract formation.
> - **Material fact**: Any fact that would influence the judgement of a prudent insurer in deciding whether to accept the risk and on what terms. The insured must disclose all material facts — even those not asked about in the proposal form — unless they relate to matters of common knowledge or the insurer has waived the right to know.
> - **Non-disclosure consequences**: In most jurisdictions, non-disclosure allows the insurer to avoid the policy (treat it as if it never existed), with or without return of premium depending on whether the non-disclosure was innocent or fraudulent.
> - **International variation**: The precise remedy for non-disclosure varies by jurisdiction — the UK Insurance Act 2015 introduced proportionate remedies for business policyholders; common law jurisdictions (Australia, Singapore, Malaysia, UAE) have varying codifications of the duty of disclosure. WUE/WUA exams do not test UK-specific statutory remedies — they test the general principle.

#### H2: Policy Documentation Sequence
> **EAV to cover**: Proposal → quotation → cover note → policy → renewal notice
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Proposal form**: The insured completes a standardised declaration of material facts. Signed declaration confirms truthfulness — misrepresentation voids the policy.
> - **Quotation**: The underwriter reviews the proposal, assesses the risk, and issues a quotation stating the premium and conditions. Not yet a binding contract.
> - **Cover note (temporary cover)**: Interim document providing immediate insurance cover while the full policy is prepared. Common in motor insurance. Typically valid for 30–60 days.
> - **Policy document**: The formal insurance contract — constituting the offer (insurer) and acceptance (insured paying the premium). Contains: schedule (insured details, risk description, sum insured, premium, period), policy wording (terms, conditions, exclusions), any endorsements.
> - **Renewal notice**: Issued before expiry of the policy period — invites the insured to continue cover. The insured's failure to disclose material changes at renewal may affect claims under the renewed policy.
