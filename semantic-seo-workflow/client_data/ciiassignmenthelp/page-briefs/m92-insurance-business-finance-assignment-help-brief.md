#### H2: Financial Performance Metrics in M92
> **EAV to cover**: Loss ratio, expense ratio, combined ratio, IBNR, investment income
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Pure premium**: The estimated cost of claims for a given exposure unit, before loading for expenses, profit, or contingency. Formula: Pure premium = expected claims cost ÷ number of exposure units. The starting point for rate setting.
> - **Loss ratio**: Claims paid and outstanding ÷ earned premiums × 100. Measures the proportion of earned premium consumed by claims. A loss ratio of 70% means 70p of every £1 of premium is used to pay claims.
> - **Expense ratio**: Operating expenses ÷ written (or earned) premiums × 100. Measures the proportion of premium consumed by operating costs (commissions, salaries, IT, claims handling).
> - **Combined ratio**: Loss ratio + expense ratio. The primary measure of underwriting profitability. Combined ratio below 100% = underwriting profit. Combined ratio above 100% = underwriting loss — the insurer relies on investment income to achieve overall profitability. Example: Loss ratio 72% + expense ratio 30% = combined ratio 102% (underwriting loss of 2%).
> - **IBNR (Incurred But Not Reported)**: Claims that have occurred but have not yet been reported to the insurer. Insurers must estimate IBNR reserves to ensure their financial statements are accurate. Significant IBNR is common in liability insurance (latent disease, long-tail claims where a loss event today may generate a claim years later).
> - **Investment income**: Insurers invest the premium float (premiums received before claims are paid) in bonds, equities, and property. Investment income can offset underwriting losses — allowing insurers to write business with combined ratios above 100% in years when investment returns are strong.

#### H2: Reinsurance at Diploma Depth — Types, Structures, and Purpose
> **EAV to cover**: Proportional (quota share, surplus), non-proportional (XL per risk, catastrophe XL, aggregate XL), retrocession
> **Word count**: 350–400 words

##### H3: Proportional Reinsurance
> **Specific content**:
> - **Proportional reinsurance**: The reinsurer shares a fixed proportion of every risk ceded — both premium income and claims are shared in the same proportion.
> - **Quota Share treaty**: The insurer cedes a fixed percentage of EVERY risk in a defined portfolio (e.g., 40% quota share means the reinsurer takes 40% of every premium and pays 40% of every claim). Simple and certain. Used by smaller insurers to reduce exposure and stabilise results.
> - **Surplus treaty**: The insurer retains a fixed amount (the "retention" or "line") on each risk. The reinsurer covers the surplus above the retention, up to a defined maximum (e.g., 9 lines = reinsurer covers up to 9× the retention). The proportion ceded varies by risk size — large risks cede a high proportion; small risks below the retention line are not ceded at all. More complex but more efficient capital use than quota share.
> - **Ceding commission**: Reinsurers pay a ceding commission back to the insurer — compensating the insurer for the acquisition and administrative costs of producing the business. The commission is deducted from the premium ceded.

##### H3: Non-Proportional Reinsurance
> **Specific content**:
> - **Non-proportional reinsurance**: The reinsurer's liability is triggered only when losses exceed a defined threshold (the "retention" or "priority"). Below the threshold, the insurer pays in full; above the threshold, the reinsurer pays the excess.
> - **Excess of Loss per risk (risk XL)**: Triggered by a single large loss on one risk exceeding the insurer's retention. Example: insurer retains first £500,000; reinsurer pays the excess up to a limit of £5 million. Protects against individual large losses.
> - **Excess of Loss per occurrence (catastrophe XL)**: Triggered by the aggregate loss from a single event (e.g., a hurricane or earthquake) exceeding the insurer's retention across all policies affected. Protects against accumulation of losses from one catastrophic event.
> - **Aggregate Excess of Loss**: Triggered when the insurer's total claims for a class of business within a period exceed the aggregate retention. Protects against a bad year with high frequency of small losses.
> - **Retrocession**: Reinsurance of a reinsurer — the reinsurer further cedes part of its risk to another reinsurer (retrocessionaire) to manage its own accumulation risk.

#### H2: Solvency II — Capital Regulation Framework
> **EAV to cover**: Three pillars, SCR, MCR, ORSA, UK post-Brexit position
> **Word count**: 300–350 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Solvency II**: The EU regulatory framework for insurance capital adequacy, transposed into UK law and retained post-Brexit (with modifications). Replaces the previous EU Insurance Directives. Came into force 1 January 2016.
> - **Three-Pillar Structure**:
>   - **Pillar 1 — Quantitative Requirements**: Insurers must hold minimum capital defined by two levels:
>     - **Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR)**: The risk-based capital amount calculated to absorb losses at a 99.5% confidence level over one year (Value at Risk). Calculated using the Standard Formula or an approved Internal Model.
>     - **Minimum Capital Requirement (MCR)**: The absolute minimum capital floor — lower than SCR. Breach of the MCR triggers immediate supervisory action including potential withdrawal of authorisation.
>   - **Pillar 2 — Governance and ORSA**: Insurers must have robust risk management governance and conduct an Own Risk and Solvency Assessment (ORSA) — the insurer's own forward-looking assessment of its specific risk profile and capital needs (which may exceed the minimum SCR). The ORSA is reviewed by the PRA.
>   - **Pillar 3 — Disclosure and Reporting**: Public disclosure of financial condition through the Solvency and Financial Condition Report (SFCR). Regulatory reporting to the PRA. Market transparency requirements.
> - **UK post-Brexit**: The UK retained Solvency II at Brexit but the PRA has been developing UK-specific reforms (Solvency UK) to make the regime more proportionate and competitive for the UK market — particularly around internal model approval and risk margin calculation.

#### H2: Insurance Distribution and Market Structure in M92
> **EAV to cover**: Distribution channels, FCA/PRA roles, Lloyd's vs company market
> **Word count**: 200–250 words

> **Specific content**:
> - **Distribution channels**:
>   - **Direct**: Insurer sells directly to the customer — online, telephone, or branch. No intermediary cost; insurer bears full acquisition cost.
>   - **Broker**: Independent intermediary providing market access and advice. Earns commission or fee. FCA-regulated. Can access multiple insurers for best terms.
>   - **MGA/Coverholder**: Insurer delegates underwriting authority — MGA binds risks on the insurer's behalf. Enables geographic or specialist market access.
>   - **Bancassurance**: Insurance sold through bank branches or bank digital channels. Common in life, protection, and payment protection insurance.
>   - **Affinity schemes**: Group policies sold to members of associations, professional bodies, or loyalty programmes. Premiums may be lower due to risk homogeneity and group negotiating power.
> - **FCA/PRA regulation**: FCA regulates conduct of business (ICOBS, treating customers fairly, product governance, complaints). PRA regulates prudential soundness (capital adequacy, Solvency II, governance). Both regulators can impose requirements simultaneously.
> - **Lloyd's vs company market**: Lloyd's provides specialist, non-standard risk capacity through a syndicate structure; company market (standard insurers — Aviva, AXA, Zurich etc.) provides mainstream risk capacity through corporate balance sheets.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Is M92 assessed by MCQ or written exam?**
M92 is assessed by written examination — not MCQ. Written answer technique including financial calculation workings and structured analysis is required.

**Q: What is the combined ratio and why is it important for M92?**
The combined ratio = loss ratio + expense ratio. It measures whether an insurer is making an underwriting profit or loss. A combined ratio below 100% means the insurer earns more in premium than it pays in claims and expenses. Above 100% means underwriting losses — the insurer relies on investment income for overall profitability.

**Q: What are the three types of proportional reinsurance?**
The two main proportional types are: quota share (fixed percentage of all risks) and surplus (variable percentage based on the insurer's retention line). Ceding commissions are paid by reinsurers to cedants under proportional treaties.

**Q: What is the SCR under Solvency II?**
The Solvency Capital Requirement (SCR) is the risk-based minimum capital an insurer must hold under Solvency II — calibrated at 99.5% Value at Risk over one year. It can be calculated using the Standard Formula or an approved Internal Model.
