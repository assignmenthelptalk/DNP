HND Assignment Resubmission: Rules, Referral Feedback, and How to Improve Your Work

A referral in Pearson HND means the student has not met one or more Pass criteria in a unit assignment. It is not a permanent fail — it is a conditional result that triggers the resubmission process. One resubmission is available per unit within the same academic year. The maximum grade achievable on a resubmission is Pass: regardless of the quality of the resubmitted work, Merit and Distinction cannot be awarded on a resubmission submission. This page covers the resubmission rules, how to interpret the restricted feedback tutors are permitted to give, what must substantively change in the resubmission, and the most common referral reasons by HND subject.

What Does "Referred" Mean in HND? Resubmission vs Fail Explained

A referral result in Pearson HND means the student's submission did not meet one or more of the Pass criteria for the unit. The referral is a conditional outcome — it can be resolved through a single resubmission within the same academic year. At the point the referral is awarded, it is not a permanent fail. The permanent fail is recorded only if the resubmission also fails to meet Pass criteria, or if the student does not submit a resubmission by the college-set deadline.

The resubmission process works as follows. The student receives their marked submission and feedback identifying which criteria have not been met. They use that feedback to diagnose what must change and produce an improved version of the relevant sections. The resubmission is submitted by the college-set deadline — typically within four to eight weeks of receiving the referral result. If the resubmission meets all Pass criteria, the unit is recorded as a Pass and the student progresses. If the resubmission does not meet all Pass criteria, the result becomes a permanent fail for that unit.

There is a critical rule that many students do not know in advance of receiving a referral: the maximum grade achievable on a resubmission is Pass. Regardless of the quality of the resubmitted work — even if the resubmission is of Distinction standard — the grade recorded is a Pass. This rule has a direct consequence for university progression: a unit in which the student was referred and resubmitted cannot contribute to a Merit or Distinction average for the overall qualification. The unit will be recorded as a Pass at best. Students targeting a Merit or Distinction overall qualification grade need to achieve those grades on first submission in every unit, including high-credit units such as the research project.

The academic record implications of a resubmission vary by institution. Some universities reviewing HND transcripts can identify which units were passed on first submission versus resubmission. Students targeting highly competitive degree top-up programmes should check their target university's admissions requirements regarding this distinction, as some admissions processes may assess the transcript at unit level rather than relying solely on the overall qualification grade.

Pearson HND Resubmission Rules: What Is and Is Not Allowed

Pearson HND allows one resubmission opportunity per unit within the same academic year in which the original submission was assessed. There is no second resubmission. If the first resubmission also fails to meet Pass criteria, the unit result becomes a permanent fail. The student may then need to retake the unit in the following academic year, which has both time and cost implications depending on the college's programme structure and fees.

The same academic year restriction means the resubmission must take place within the academic year in which the original submission was assessed. Students who miss the resubmission deadline cannot defer to the following year — the unit result becomes a permanent fail at that point. This restriction makes confirming the exact resubmission deadline with the assessor or programme leader immediately upon receiving the referral result an urgent priority, not an administrative formality.

The Pass grade ceiling applies universally. There are no exceptions to this rule for individual programmes or units. A student who produces distinction-quality work on their resubmission receives a Pass. This is not a marking error — it is Pearson policy for all HND programmes. Students who have received a referral should focus their resubmission effort on meeting Pass criteria fully rather than attempting to produce Merit or Distinction standard work, which will not affect the recorded grade.

The college sets the resubmission deadline — not Pearson directly. Deadlines vary between four and eight weeks after the referral result is issued, depending on the college. Extension requests may be possible in cases of evidenced extenuating circumstances such as documented illness or bereavement; the college's extenuating circumstances policy governs whether extensions are available and what evidence is required. Extensions are not guaranteed and cannot be assumed — they should be applied for formally and as early as possible if circumstances justify them.

Pearson requires the resubmission to demonstrate substantive improvement. A resubmission that makes cosmetic changes — adding a few sentences, adjusting formatting, correcting referencing where referencing was not the source of the referral — will be identified as inadequate and will result in a permanent fail for the unit. Substantive improvement means the specific unmet criteria are now addressed at the required cognitive depth. The assessor will be looking for evidence that the student has independently understood what was required and produced materially different work to address it.

What Tutors Can and Cannot Tell You in HND Referral Feedback

Pearson's academic integrity policy restricts what tutors can communicate in referral feedback. The restriction is intentional and exists to preserve the academic integrity of the resubmission process. If tutors could prescribe the answer content — tell the student exactly what to write, which frameworks to use, or what conclusions to reach — the resubmission would effectively be co-authored by the tutor, which would constitute academic malpractice. The feedback gap is a deliberate feature of the system, not a failure of tutor engagement.

Tutors can tell students which specific criteria have not been met, using the criterion codes from the assignment brief (P1, M2, D1, and so on). They can describe generally where the submission falls short — for example, "the analysis in Task 2 is largely descriptive and does not demonstrate the critical evaluation required by D1." They can identify which sections or tasks are the source of the gap — "Task 3 does not address the synthesis requirement of M3." They can indicate the general type of improvement required — "more critical evaluation of the limitations of the frameworks used is needed in the strategic analysis section."

Tutors cannot tell students exactly what to write in the resubmission. They cannot identify which specific frameworks the student should use, what arguments the student should make, what conclusions the student should reach, or provide any content that would substitute for the student's own independent academic work. This restriction applies even when a student directly asks for more specific guidance — the tutor who complies with such a request may themselves be in breach of Pearson's academic integrity requirements.

Working effectively with restricted feedback requires mapping the feedback language directly to the criteria grid in the assignment brief. Feedback that says "needs more critical analysis" against a Distinction criterion that says "critically evaluate the strategic options available to the organisation" is pointing to a specific cognitive gap: the student responded at analysis level (Merit) against a criterion that required critical evaluation (Distinction). The grading criteria page explains exactly what each command verb requires — using it alongside the referral feedback produces a precise diagnosis of what must change in the resubmission. How to Interpret HND Referral Feedback and Identify What Is Missing

Interpreting referral feedback requires three documents: the original assignment brief with its Pass/Merit/Distinction criteria grid, the marked submission with assessor comments, and the referral summary feedback. Working without all three means diagnosing the gap without the full picture — the criteria grid is the reference standard against which everything else is assessed.

The first step is to locate every criterion code mentioned in the referral feedback in the criteria grid and re-read its full descriptor. The criterion descriptor contains the command verb, the topic, and the expected output — these three elements together define exactly what the response must demonstrate. The command verb is the primary indicator of what is missing. If the descriptor says "critically evaluate the strengths and limitations of the selected management approach" and the feedback says "lacks critical evaluation," the gap is clear: the student evaluated (Merit level) where critical evaluation (Distinction level) was required. The four questions of critical evaluation — what does this show, what does it not show, what does it assume, how reliable is the evidence — provide the framework for addressing this gap in the resubmission.

The second step is to distinguish between two types of referral gap. The first type is missing content — the student omitted something the criterion explicitly required. If the criterion asks for a justified research methodology and the submission does not contain a methodology section at all, the content is absent. The resubmission must add it. The second type is wrong depth — the student addressed the criterion but at the wrong cognitive level. If the criterion asks for critical evaluation and the student produced analysis, the content is present but at an insufficient cognitive depth. The resubmission must replace the inadequate response with a deeper one — not simply add to it.

Common feedback phrases and their diagnoses: "too descriptive" means the response identifies and describes correctly at Pass level but the criterion requires analysis (Merit) or above; "lacks critical evaluation" means the response analyses competently at Merit level but the criterion requires critical evaluation of framework limitations (Distinction); "insufficient synthesis" means frameworks have been applied separately in parallel sections but the criterion requires their insights to be integrated into a single coherent position; "recommendations are too generic" means the conclusions follow reasonably from the analysis but the criterion requires independent professional judgement specific to the context — generic recommendations that could apply to any organisation do not meet this requirement.

Planning the resubmission response criterion by criterion before writing prevents the common mistake of rewriting the entire assignment. For each unmet criterion, a one-sentence plan stating the specific cognitive action the resubmission will take — "For D1, I will synthesise the PESTLE and Porter's findings to show how the macro-environmental cost pressures directly affect the competitive dynamics identified, and critically evaluate which strategic options remain viable given both analyses together" — provides a clear target before writing begins.

What Must Change Between First Submission and HND Resubmission

Pearson's requirement for substantive improvement means the specific unmet criteria must now be met at the required cognitive depth. The assessor reviewing the resubmission will be looking for two things: evidence that the student understood what was required — which means the inadequate response has been materially rewritten or replaced, not just extended — and evidence that the new response actually meets the criterion command verb's cognitive demand.

Where the referral was for missing content, the resubmission must add the content required at the standard the criterion demands. Adding generic or low-depth content will not secure the Pass — the new content must meet the criterion's cognitive demand. A referral for missing methodology justification is not resolved by adding a paragraph defining what a survey is; it is resolved by justifying the choice of survey methodology with reference to the research question and the academic literature on research design.

Where the referral was for wrong depth, the resubmission must replace the shallow response with a deeper one. Simply adding sentences after existing content is insufficient — the assessor will be reading the section as a whole, and a Pass-level response with a few additional sentences does not become Merit or Distinction-level work by virtue of the additions. The student must identify which specific paragraphs represented the insufficient response, remove or substantially rewrite them, and produce content that demonstrates the cognitive operation the criterion requires.

The scope of changes should be limited to the sections that address the unmet criteria. Students who rewrite their entire assignment in a resubmission risk inadvertently reducing the quality of sections that were already adequate, creating new problems in sections the assessor was already satisfied with. The principle is: fix the specific gaps identified in the feedback, leave adequate sections intact.

The most common mistakes in resubmissions are: adding descriptive content to a section cited for lacking critical analysis — this adds volume without changing the cognitive level, and the assessor will award the same result; correcting Harvard referencing when referencing was not the stated reason for the referral; rewriting the entire assignment, which risks reducing the quality of adequate sections; and submitting within hours of receiving the referral result, before the student has had time to properly diagnose what must change and rewrite at the required depth. Substantive improvement requires time — the four to eight week resubmission window exists to allow it.

Common HND Referral Reasons by Subject Area

HND Business — most common referral reasons: Strategic analysis that correctly identifies macro-environmental factors (PESTLE) or competitive forces (Porter's) but describes them rather than analysing their strategic significance is the most frequent source of referral for Merit criteria in HND Business. The fix is to add analysis of significance for each factor — which are most strategically impactful for this organisation, given its competitive position and resource base, and why. For Distinction criteria, the most common gap is frameworks applied separately without synthesis and without critical evaluation of their limitations. The fix is to restructure the analytical sections so that insights from PESTLE directly inform the Porter's analysis and both are evaluated against VRIO resource assessment — and to add a section acknowledging what each framework cannot tell us about the strategic context.

In the research project unit, conclusions that are a general summary of findings rather than specific answers to each research objective is a recurring referral reason. The fix is to restructure the conclusions section with an explicit numbered or labelled response to each original aim and objective, each traceable to specific data from the analysis sections.

HND Computing — most common referral reasons: Technical descriptions of system or network designs that explain what components do without evaluating why this design is more appropriate than available alternatives are the most frequent referral source. The fix is to add a comparative evaluation of design options — identifying two or three realistic alternatives, assessing each against the relevant criteria (performance, security, cost, scalability), and justifying the recommended design on the basis of that comparison. Security analysis that identifies risk categories without assessing likelihood, impact, and countermeasure justification is also a common referral pattern — the fix is to add likelihood/impact assessment for each identified risk and justify each countermeasure against the specific threat scenario it addresses.

HND Engineering — most common referral reasons: Calculation sections that present correct numerical results without interpretive commentary or evaluation of their engineering implications are the most consistent referral source in HND Engineering. A calculation that produces a correct answer to the numerical question does not itself constitute analysis of what that result means for the design — the interpretive commentary must explicitly state what the result implies for material selection, safety margins, compliance with applicable standards, or design optimisation. The fix is to add an analytical commentary after each calculation set that addresses those engineering implications. Missing evaluation against applicable standards — Eurocodes, British Standards, IET Wiring Regulations as appropriate to the engineering pathway — is also a frequent source of Pass criteria referrals.

HND Health and Social Care — most common referral reasons: Policy and legislation sections that describe the legislative framework and note its relevance to the care scenario without evaluating whether the framework is achieving its intended outcomes in practice are the most common referral source for Distinction criteria in HND Health and Social Care. The distinction between "this legislation requires X and therefore applies to the scenario" (Merit) and "this legislation requires X but the outcomes evidence shows that Y — which means the gap between legislative intent and care delivery reality is Z — and the management implication of that gap is..." (Distinction) is the most frequently missed. The fix is to add an outcomes evidence section that draws on research or NHS data to evaluate how well current legislative and policy frameworks are performing, and a management implication that follows from that evaluation. Case study sections that describe the scenario without critically evaluating the practice decisions against theory and evidence are also a common referral pattern — the fix is to explicitly evaluate each practice decision in the case study against the relevant theoretical framework and the legislative requirements.

HND Academic Appeals: When You Can and Cannot Appeal a Referral

A student cannot appeal against a tutor's academic judgement — the assessor's evaluation of whether criteria have been met is a professional academic decision and is not subject to appeal on its merits. Disagreeing with a grade is not grounds for an academic appeal. The only available appeal mechanism at assessment level is against a procedural failure: the assessment was not conducted in accordance with the published assessment plan, the student was not given the opportunity to complete the assessment, or there was an administrative error in recording the result.

The internal appeal process at college level is typically initiated by a written appeal to the programme leader or academic registry within ten working days of the result being issued. The basis of the appeal must be a procedural failing rather than a disagreement with the marking. Pearson's External Standards Verifier process provides quality assurance of the college's assessment practices but does not review individual marking decisions — it assesses whether the college's assessment process as a whole is operating consistently with Pearson's national standards. For most students who have received a referral, the resubmission is the appropriate resolution route. The appeals process is for the rare cases where a procedural failure has affected the result. How to Avoid a Second Referral in HND

Before submitting the resubmission, a structured quality check against the criteria grid prevents the most common causes of a second referral. First, re-read each unmet criterion code in the original brief and confirm that the resubmission specifically addresses it at the required cognitive depth — the command verb in each criterion must be met by the resubmission content. Second, check that every Pass criterion is now fully addressed — not just the ones identified in the feedback, but all Pass criteria, because a resubmission that leaves any Pass criterion incomplete will receive the same referral result regardless of improvements elsewhere. Third, confirm that any previously adequate sections have not been inadvertently reduced in quality by changes made during the resubmission process. Fourth, confirm that Harvard referencing is complete and correctly formatted throughout, including all new material added in the resubmission. Fifth, ask honestly: does the resubmission demonstrate substantive improvement or cosmetic improvement? If the honest answer is cosmetic — the changes are additions of detail rather than changes in cognitive depth — more work is required before submission. The resubmission window of four to eight weeks provides sufficient time for substantive improvement; the pressure to submit quickly should not take priority over the quality of the changes made. Why is my HND referral feedback so vague and what can I do with it?

Pearson's academic integrity policy restricts what tutors can communicate in referral feedback — tutors cannot prescribe answer content or write corrective text because doing so would compromise the integrity of the resubmission as an independent academic submission. To work with vague feedback: map the feedback language to the command verb in the unmet criterion — "lacks critical analysis" against a criterion that says "critically evaluate" means your response was at Merit level when Distinction level was required; use the grading criteria page to understand what the command verb requires; then plan your resubmission specifically to address the criterion at the required cognitive depth.

What does substantive improvement mean for an HND resubmission?

Pearson requires that a resubmission demonstrates substantive improvement — not cosmetic editing. Substantive improvement means the specific unmet criteria are now addressed at the required cognitive depth; the sections identified as insufficient in the feedback have been materially rewritten to meet the criterion command verb; and the resubmission demonstrates that the student independently understood and addressed the identified gaps. Adding a few sentences to an unchanged document, reformatting, or correcting referencing without addressing the identified depth or content gaps does not constitute substantive improvement and will result in the resubmission being failed.
