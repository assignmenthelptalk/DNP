#### H2: What Does "Referred" Mean in HND? Resubmission vs Fail Explained
> **EAV to cover**: referral definition — conditional result, not immediate permanent fail; how referral is recorded; what happens at the end of the resubmission process; difference between referral, Pass on resubmission, and permanent fail; how grade ceiling affects the student's academic record
> **Word count**: 260–340 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Referred in Pearson HND means: the student's submission did not meet one or more of the Pass criteria for the unit; the referral result is a conditional outcome — it can be resolved through a resubmission
> - A referral is NOT a permanent fail at the point it is awarded — it is the mechanism that triggers the resubmission process
> - The resubmission process: the student receives their marked work and feedback, identifies the gaps in their Pass criteria evidence, and submits an improved version by the college-set resubmission deadline (typically within 4–8 weeks)
> - If the resubmission meets all Pass criteria: the unit is recorded as a Pass; the student progresses; the grade is recorded as Pass (regardless of the quality of the resubmission work — Merit and Distinction cannot be awarded on a resubmission submission)
> - If the resubmission also fails to meet Pass criteria: the result becomes a permanent fail for that unit; the student may need to retake the unit in the following academic year, which has time and cost implications
> - The grade ceiling rule: a Pass on resubmission is a Pass — the student cannot achieve Merit or Distinction through resubmission; this means that students who received a referral on a unit cannot count that unit toward a Merit or Distinction average for university progression purposes — they will receive a Pass for that unit at best
> - Academic record implications: some universities that review HND transcripts may be able to see which units were passed on first submission vs resubmission — students should check their target university's admissions requirements if this is relevant to their progression plans

#### H2: Pearson HND Resubmission Rules: What Is and Is Not Allowed
> **EAV to cover**: one resubmission per unit per academic year, same academic year restriction, Pass grade ceiling, college-set deadline, what constitutes adequate resubmission, late resubmission consequences, whether students can request extensions
> **Word count**: 280–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - One resubmission per unit: Pearson HND allows one resubmission opportunity per unit within the same academic year; there is no second resubmission if the first resubmission also fails — the result becomes a permanent fail for that unit
> - Same academic year restriction: the resubmission must take place within the same academic year in which the original submission was assessed; students who miss the resubmission deadline within the academic year cannot defer to the following year — the unit result becomes a permanent fail
> - Pass grade ceiling: the maximum grade achievable on a resubmission submission is Pass — regardless of the quality of the work; a student who produces distinction-quality work on their resubmission will still be awarded a Pass; this rule is a direct incentive to achieve Merit or Distinction on first submission
> - College-set deadline: the resubmission deadline is set by the college (not by Pearson directly) and is typically 4–8 weeks after the referral result is issued; students must confirm their specific resubmission deadline with their assessor or programme leader immediately upon receiving the referral result
> - Late resubmission: submitting after the college-set resubmission deadline typically results in the referral becoming a permanent fail for that unit; late submission policies vary slightly by college but the default position is that a late resubmission is treated as a non-submission
> - Extension requests: some colleges may allow an extension to the resubmission deadline in cases of evidenced extenuating circumstances (illness, bereavement, significant personal crisis); students should check their college's extenuating circumstances policy — extensions are not guaranteed and cannot be assumed
> - What Pearson considers an adequate resubmission: the resubmission must demonstrate substantive improvement — it must address the specific criteria that were not met with sufficiently improved evidence; cosmetic edits (adding a few sentences, reformatting) that do not substantively address the identified gaps will result in the resubmission being graded as a second referral (permanent fail)

#### H2: What Tutors Can and Cannot Tell You in HND Referral Feedback
> **EAV to cover**: Pearson's academic integrity restrictions on referral feedback, what tutors are permitted to tell students, what they are prohibited from saying, why the restriction exists, how to interpret feedback that feels vague
> **Word count**: 280–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Pearson's academic integrity policy restricts what tutors can communicate in referral feedback to preserve the academic integrity of the resubmission process
> - What tutors CAN tell you:
>   - Which specific criteria have not been met (e.g., "D1 criterion has not been met")
>   - A general description of where the work falls short (e.g., "the analysis is largely descriptive and does not demonstrate critical evaluation of the frameworks used")
>   - Which sections or tasks within the assignment are the source of the gap (e.g., "task 3 does not address the evaluation requirements of the D1 criterion")
>   - Guidance on the general type of improvement required (e.g., "more critical evaluation and synthesis is needed")
> - What tutors CANNOT tell you:
>   - Exactly what to write in the resubmission
>   - Which specific frameworks to use or arguments to make
>   - Corrections to the student's analysis or conclusions
>   - Content that would substitute for the student's own independent academic work
> - Why this restriction exists: if a tutor could prescribe the answer content in feedback, the resubmission would effectively be co-written by the tutor — this would constitute malpractice and undermine the academic integrity of the assessment; the restriction protects both the student and the institution
> - How to interpret feedback that feels vague: referral feedback that says "needs more critical analysis" or "lacks synthesis" is not unhelpful — it is pointing to a specific gap in the cognitive standard of the response; map the feedback language directly to the criteria grid in the assignment brief; "lacks critical analysis" in feedback against a Distinction criterion means the criterion requires "critically evaluate" and the student responded at "analyse" level; the grading criteria page (linked below) explains exactly what each command verb requires

#### H2: How to Interpret HND Referral Feedback and Identify What Is Missing
> **EAV to cover**: practical process for interpreting referral feedback — mapping feedback to criteria grid, identifying which criteria were not met, diagnosing the depth gap between what was submitted and what was required, planning the resubmission response
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Step 1 — Obtain your marked assignment and the criteria grid: the starting point is the original assignment brief with the Pass/Merit/Distinction criteria grid, your marked submission (with assessor comments), and the referral summary feedback; you need all three documents before you can begin diagnosing the gap
> - Step 2 — Map each referral note to the specific criteria: assessors typically note which criteria have not been met (P1, P2, M1, D1, etc.) — locate each noted criterion in the criteria grid and re-read the descriptor carefully; identify the command verb in each unmet criterion
> - Step 3 — Compare command verb to response depth: for each unmet criterion, ask whether your original response met the cognitive demand of the command verb; if the criterion says "critically evaluate" (Distinction) and your response described and applied (Pass level), the diagnosis is a depth gap — your response was at the wrong cognitive level for that criterion
> - Step 4 — Identify whether the issue is missing content or wrong depth: there are two types of referral gap: (a) the student omitted content required by the criterion entirely — in which case the resubmission must add that content; (b) the student addressed the criterion but at the wrong cognitive depth — in which case the resubmission must replace the shallow response with a deeper one, not just add to it
> - Step 5 — Plan the resubmission response criterion by criterion: for each unmet criterion, write a one-sentence plan: "For [criterion], I will [specific cognitive action — e.g., synthesise the PESTLE and Porter's findings, critically evaluate the limitations of the VRIO framework, produce specific strategic recommendations with identified assumptions]"
> - Feedback phrases and their diagnoses:
>   - "Too descriptive" → the response identifies and describes but does not analyse significance; the criterion requires analysis or above
>   - "Lacks critical evaluation" → the response analyses using frameworks but does not evaluate their limitations or assumptions; the criterion requires evaluation or critical evaluation
>   - "Insufficient synthesis" → frameworks are applied separately but not connected into a coherent position; the criterion requires synthesis
>   - "Recommendations are too generic" → the recommendations follow from the analysis but do not reflect independent judgement specific to the context; the criterion requires independent professional recommendations

#### H2: What Must Change Between First Submission and HND Resubmission
> **EAV to cover**: substantive vs cosmetic improvement definition, what Pearson considers adequate resubmission, specific types of change required for different referral reasons, common resubmission mistakes
> **Word count**: 300–380 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Pearson requires substantive improvement: a resubmission that makes cosmetic changes to an essentially unchanged document — adding a few sentences, reformatting, correcting referencing — will be assessed as an inadequate resubmission if the identified gaps in Pass criteria have not been addressed with substantively different and improved evidence
> - Substantive improvement means: the specific unmet criteria are now met at the required cognitive depth; the sections identified as insufficient in the feedback now demonstrate the analytical standard required by the criterion command verb; the overall quality of the analytical argument supporting the criteria in question has improved materially
> - If the referral was for missing content: the resubmission must add the content required — but adding generic or low-depth content will not secure the Pass; the new content must meet the criterion's cognitive demand
> - If the referral was for wrong depth: the resubmission must replace the shallow response with a deeper one; simply adding sentences after the existing content is not sufficient; the assessor will be looking for evidence that the student understands what was required — which means the original insufficient response should be substantially rewritten or replaced
> - The resubmission is not a new draft of the whole assignment: students should only change the sections that address the unmet criteria — modifying sections that were already adequate risks inadvertently reducing their quality; the principle is: fix the specific gaps identified, do not rewrite everything
> - Common resubmission mistakes:
>   - Adding descriptive content to a section cited for lacking critical analysis — this adds volume but does not change the cognitive level
>   - Correcting referencing when referencing was not the reason for the referral
>   - Rewriting the entire assignment, reducing the quality of sections that were already adequate
>   - Submitting within hours of receiving the referral result — substantive improvement requires time for re-reading the criteria, re-planning the response, and rewriting at the appropriate depth

#### H2: Common HND Referral Reasons by Subject Area
> **EAV to cover**: the most frequent reasons for referral in HND Business, HND Computing, HND Engineering, and HND Health and Social Care — with specific examples of what the insufficient response looks like vs what the resubmission must provide
> **Word count**: 340–420 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **HND Business — most common referral reasons**:
>   - Strategic analysis is too descriptive: PESTLE analysis that lists and describes macro-environmental factors without analysing their significance or strategic implications (criterion requires "analyse" or above); fix: add analysis of significance for each factor — which are most strategically impactful and why
>   - Lacks critical evaluation: frameworks applied as if definitively correct without acknowledging limitations; fix: add a section evaluating what each framework cannot tell you and where the evidence base is limited
>   - Research project conclusions not linked to aims and objectives: conclusions are a general summary of findings rather than a specific answer to each research aim; fix: re-structure the conclusion section with a numbered or labelled response to each original aim and objective
> - **HND Computing — most common referral reasons**:
>   - Technical description without evaluation: system or network design describes what the solution does without evaluating why this design is more appropriate than alternatives; fix: add a comparative evaluation of design options with criteria-based justification
>   - Security analysis without risk quantification: identifies security risks but does not assess their likelihood and impact or justify countermeasures; fix: add likelihood/impact assessment and justify selected countermeasures against specific threat scenarios
> - **HND Engineering — most common referral reasons**:
>   - Calculation without interpretation: presents correct calculations but does not interpret what the results mean for the engineering decision, safety compliance, or design optimisation; fix: add analytical commentary after each calculation set explaining the engineering implications
>   - Missing safety and compliance evaluation: design proposal addresses performance requirements but omits evaluation against relevant standards (Eurocodes, British Standards, Health and Safety regulations); fix: add a dedicated section evaluating the design against applicable standards
> - **HND Health and Social Care — most common referral reasons**:
>   - Policy description without outcomes evaluation: legislation and policy is described accurately but not evaluated against evidence of how well it achieves its intended outcomes in practice; fix: add outcomes evidence (research and data) and evaluate where the policy achieves and where it falls short
>   - Case study description without critical analysis: case study scenario is described but the analysis does not evaluate the professional practice decisions against theory and evidence; fix: rewrite the case study analysis to explicitly evaluate each practice decision against the relevant theoretical and legislative framework

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How many times can I resubmit an HND assignment?**
Pearson HND allows one resubmission per unit within the same academic year in which the original submission was assessed. There is no second resubmission — if the resubmission also fails to meet Pass criteria, the result becomes a permanent fail for that unit. The student may then need to retake the unit in the following academic year, depending on the programme structure and the college's regulations.

**Q: What is the maximum grade I can get on an HND resubmission?**
The maximum grade achievable on a resubmission is Pass. Regardless of the quality of the resubmission, Merit and Distinction cannot be awarded on a resubmission submission. This is a fixed Pearson policy for all HND programmes. This rule means that a unit in which the student was referred cannot contribute to a Merit or Distinction average for the overall qualification — the unit will be recorded as a Pass at best. This is why achieving Merit or Distinction on first submission is strategically important for students targeting a Merit or Distinction overall qualification grade.

**Q: Why is my HND referral feedback so vague and what can I do with it?**
Pearson's academic integrity policy restricts what tutors can communicate in referral feedback — tutors cannot prescribe answer content or write corrective text because doing so would compromise the integrity of the resubmission as an independent academic submission. Tutors can tell you which criteria have not been met and describe generally where the work falls short. To work with vague feedback: map the feedback language to the command verb in the unmet criterion (e.g., "lacks critical analysis" against a criterion that says "critically evaluate" means your response was at the wrong cognitive level — you analysed where critical evaluation was required); use the grading criteria page to understand exactly what the command verb requires; plan your resubmission specifically to address the criterion at the required cognitive depth.

**Q: What does "substantive improvement" mean for an HND resubmission?**
Pearson requires that a resubmission demonstrates substantive improvement — not cosmetic editing. Substantive improvement means the specific unmet criteria are now addressed at the required cognitive depth; the sections identified as insufficient in the feedback have been materially rewritten to meet the criterion command verb; and the resubmission demonstrates that the student independently understood and addressed the identified gaps. Adding a few sentences to an unchanged document, reformatting, or correcting referencing without addressing the identified depth or content gaps does not constitute substantive improvement and will result in the resubmission being failed.
