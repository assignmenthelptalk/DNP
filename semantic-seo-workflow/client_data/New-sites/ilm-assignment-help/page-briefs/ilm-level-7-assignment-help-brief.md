> **EAV to cover**: McKinsey 7S framework (Strategy, Structure, Systems, Shared values, Style, Staff, Skills — hard elements vs soft elements, interconnection), Burke-Litwin causal model (twelve factors: transformational — external environment, mission, leadership, organisational culture; transactional — management practices, work unit climate, structure, systems), Stacey's complexity theory (simple, complicated, complex, chaotic domains — Cynefin framework), Distinction criteria for strategic change synthesis
> **Word count**: 340–420 words
> **Specific content**:

> - Assessment criteria require: analysis of a strategic change initiative, application of a strategic change framework, critical evaluation of change effectiveness, strategic change recommendations

> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate the strategic change approach using both McKinsey 7S and Burke-Litwin; assess the degree of alignment between the seven S elements after the change; evaluate whether the change addressed root transformational causes (Burke-Litwin transformational factors) or only surface transactional factors; synthesise the analysis into a strategic recommendation for change acceleration or consolidation
> - McKinsey 7S: the seven elements are interconnected — changing one affects all others; hard elements (Strategy, Structure, Systems) are easier to change; soft elements (Shared Values, Style, Staff, Skills) are harder to change and often determine whether hard changes are sustainable; Distinction requires evaluation of the alignment between hard and soft elements after the strategic change
> - Burke-Litwin causal model: distinguishes between transformational factors (leadership, mission, culture — drive fundamental change) and transactional factors (management practices, work unit climate, structure — drive incremental change); Distinction requires evaluation of whether the strategic change was driven at the transformational level or only at the transactional level — and what the difference implies for sustainability

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is the difference between ILM Level 5 and ILM Level 7 assignment standards?**
ILM Level 5 requires work-based written assignments demonstrating management competence in middle management roles, with Harvard referencing of management theory at a standard equivalent to a professional development programme. ILM Level 7 requires postgraduate-equivalent strategic analysis — the scope covers the whole organisation rather than a specific team, the analytical frameworks are more sophisticated (Mintzberg's schools, Balanced Scorecard, McKinsey 7S, Burke-Litwin), and the referencing standard requires peer-reviewed academic management journals rather than management textbooks alone. The written analytical quality expected at Level 7 is equivalent to Master's level management study.

**Q: Which strategic frameworks are used in ILM Level 7 assignments?**
ILM Level 7 assignments use strategic-level management frameworks: Developing Strategic Leadership uses Mintzberg's schools of strategy, Kaplan and Norton's Balanced Scorecard (four perspectives and strategy maps), and Heifetz's adaptive leadership model. Understanding Strategic Change uses McKinsey's 7S framework (seven interconnected elements), Burke-Litwin's causal model (transformational vs transactional change factors), and Stacey's complexity theory (Cynefin domains). Equality and Diversity units use the Equality Act 2010 framework, Edmondson's psychological safety model, and the Deloitte Inclusive Leadership model. All frameworks must be critically evaluated — not just applied.

**Q: Does ILM Level 7 require peer-reviewed academic sources or are management books sufficient?**
Peer-reviewed academic journals are expected as primary sources at ILM Level 7. Key journals include: Strategic Management Journal, Leadership Quarterly, Journal of Applied Psychology, and Journal of Organizational Behavior. Academic management textbooks (Mintzberg, Kaplan and Norton, Heifetz, Porter) are appropriate core references. The Harvard Business Review is a practitioner journal — not peer-reviewed — and can be used for current business examples but should not form the academic foundation of Level 7 arguments. This distinction between peer-reviewed and practitioner sources is assessed at Level 7 in a way that it is not at Level 5.

**Q: What coaching models are assessed in the ILM Level 7 Executive Coaching assignment?**
The ILM Level 7 Executive Coaching assignment typically assesses: GROW (Whitmore — Goal, Reality, Options, Will), OSCAR (McLeod — Outcome, Situation, Choices, Actions, Review), and CLEAR (Hawkins — Contracting, Listening, Exploring, Action, Review). Distinction requires critical evaluation of the limitations of GROW for complex executive coaching contexts and comparison of at least two models for the specific coaching scenarios conducted. Professional coaching competency frameworks — EMCC (European Mentoring and Coaching Council) and ICF (International Coaching Federation) — are used to evaluate own coaching practice. Evidence of coaching supervision is also required at Level 7.
