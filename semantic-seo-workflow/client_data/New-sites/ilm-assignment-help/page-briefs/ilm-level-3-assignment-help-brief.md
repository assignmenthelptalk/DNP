#### H2: ILM Understanding Leadership Assignment: Hersey and Blanchard, Adair, and Theory Application
> **EAV to cover**: Hersey and Blanchard situational leadership (S1 Directing — high task low relationship, S2 Coaching — high task high relationship, S3 Supporting — low task high relationship, S4 Delegating — low task low relationship; follower readiness levels R1-R4), Adair's action-centred leadership (overlapping circles: task, team, individual needs), Blake and Mouton managerial grid (concern for people vs concern for production — five styles: impoverished 1,1; country club 1,9; produce or perish 9,1; middle of the road 5,5; team leader 9,9), McGregor's Theory X/Y, assessment criteria, reflective evaluation
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Understanding Leadership is the foundational ILM Level 3 unit — it requires the candidate to describe a leadership theory and reflect on how their own team leader experience relates to it
> - Assessment criteria typically require: description of one or more leadership theories, identification of how the candidate's own leadership style compares to the theory, reflection on leadership effectiveness
> - Pass criteria: describe at least one leadership theory accurately; identify own leadership style using the theory framework; describe a real leadership situation from own team leader role
> - Distinction criteria: critically reflect on which theory most accurately describes own leadership approach; evaluate the limitations of the theory for the specific team context; plan specific leadership development actions based on the reflection
> - Hersey and Blanchard situational leadership: the key insight is that effective team leaders adjust their style to match each team member's readiness level — not a fixed management style for all; Distinction requires reflection on whether the candidate is genuinely adapting their style per team member or defaulting to one style regardless of readiness
> - Adair's action-centred leadership: three overlapping circles represent the needs the team leader must balance — Task (achieving the objective), Team (maintaining team cohesion), Individual (meeting individual team member needs); Distinction requires evaluation of whether the candidate is over-focusing on one circle at the expense of others (e.g., all task, no individual development)
> - Blake and Mouton managerial grid: the 9,9 "Team Leader" style (high concern for both people and production) is presented as the ideal but requires specific conditions to be effective; Distinction requires critical reflection on whether 9,9 is achievable in the candidate's specific team and organisational context
> - Written format: typically a structured report — describe the theory, apply it to own leadership, reflect on effectiveness, identify development actions

> **Word count**: 270–340 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Managing Workplace Projects at Level 3 focuses on managing or contributing to a project at team level — not strategic organisational projects, but real improvement initiatives, process changes, team events, or operational projects within the candidate's area of responsibility
> - Assessment criteria typically require: project plan with timeline and milestones, stakeholder identification, risk assessment, evidence of monitoring progress, evaluation of project outcomes
> - Pass criteria: produce a project plan for a real workplace project; identify key stakeholders; identify risks and initial mitigation actions; describe how the project was monitored
> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate the project management approach — what worked well and what caused delays or problems? Evaluate stakeholder communication effectiveness — did all stakeholders receive the right information at the right time? Synthesise the project evaluation into specific lessons learned with recommendations for managing similar projects in future
> - Gantt chart at Level 3: a simple timeline showing tasks, dependencies, and milestones — it does not need to be a complex project management tool; hand-drawn or Excel Gantt charts are sufficient as supporting evidence
> - Risk register at Level 3: for each identified risk — description, likelihood (high/medium/low), impact (high/medium/low), what will be done to reduce the risk — a simple table format is acceptable; Distinction requires evaluation of the effectiveness of risk mitigation actions for any risks that materialised
> - Real project evidence: the project must be real — it can be a small project (organising a team training event, implementing a new rota system, managing a stock-take) as long as it had identifiable objectives, stakeholders, and risks

#### H2: ILM Understanding Conflict Management Assignment: Thomas-Kilmann and Real Conflict Evidence
> **EAV to cover**: conflict sources at team level (personality clashes, communication breakdown, resource competition, unclear roles, workload imbalance), Thomas-Kilmann conflict mode instrument (five conflict management styles: Competing — high assertiveness low cooperativeness; Collaborating — high assertiveness high cooperativeness; Compromising — medium assertiveness medium cooperativeness; Avoiding — low assertiveness low cooperativeness; Accommodating — low assertiveness high cooperativeness), mediation approach, work-based evidence of managing a real conflict
> **Word count**: 290–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Understanding Conflict Management in the Workplace at Level 3 requires candidates to evidence handling a real conflict situation in their team leader role — not describing what they would do, but what they did
> - Assessment criteria typically require: description of conflict sources, application of a conflict management approach, evidence of managing a real conflict situation, evaluation of the outcome
> - Pass criteria: describe sources of workplace conflict relevant to own team; describe the Thomas-Kilmann conflict management styles; describe a real conflict situation managed in the team leader role
> - Distinction criteria: critically reflect on the conflict management style used — was it appropriate for the specific conflict? Would a different style (e.g., collaborating rather than avoiding) have produced a better outcome? Plan what would be done differently in a similar conflict situation
> - Thomas-Kilmann five styles: Competing (I win, you lose — appropriate for emergencies or when a decision must be made quickly); Collaborating (I win, you win — time-intensive but produces the most durable resolution); Compromising (we both get some of what we want — faster but neither party fully satisfied); Avoiding (neither addresses the conflict — appropriate for low-stakes disputes); Accommodating (I yield to you — appropriate when the relationship matters more than the outcome)
> - Distinction requires evaluation: not all conflicts require the same management style — a team member dispute over shift allocation may call for compromising; a customer-facing service failure caused by team behaviour may require competing (decisive management action); Distinction requires evaluation of whether the style chosen matched the conflict type

#### H2: ILM Motivating People in the Workplace Assignment: Maslow, Herzberg, and Team Evidence
> **EAV to cover**: Maslow's hierarchy of needs (five levels: physiological, safety, belonging/love, esteem, self-actualisation; deficit motivation vs growth motivation), Herzberg's two-factor theory (hygiene factors — working conditions, salary, job security, company policy, supervision — prevent dissatisfaction but do not motivate; motivators — achievement, recognition, responsibility, advancement, the work itself — create genuine motivation), McGregor Theory X/Y (assumptions about human nature: Theory X — people are lazy and need controlling; Theory Y — people are motivated and seek responsibility), real evidence of team motivation
> **Word count**: 300–380 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Motivating People in the Workplace at Level 3 requires candidates to demonstrate understanding of motivation theory and evidence applying it in their own team leader role
> - Assessment criteria typically require: description of at least one motivation theory, evidence of motivating team members, evaluation of the effectiveness of the motivation approach used
> - Pass criteria: describe Maslow's hierarchy or Herzberg's two-factor theory; identify motivators relevant to own team; describe a real situation where the candidate motivated a team member or the team
> - Distinction criteria: critically evaluate the motivation approach used — was it successful? What theory explains why it worked or did not work? Compare at least two motivation theories for the specific team context; plan specific motivation improvement actions for the team
> - Maslow's hierarchy at team leader level: the key practical application is identifying where team members are on the hierarchy — a team member preoccupied with job insecurity (safety needs) cannot be motivated by responsibility or achievement (esteem needs) until the safety need is addressed; Distinction requires evidence of recognising different team members' motivational levels and adapting the approach
> - Herzberg's two-factor distinction: hygiene factors (salary, working conditions) eliminate dissatisfaction but do not create motivation — a common team leader error is focusing entirely on hygiene factors (better rota, pay increase) when the team needs motivators (recognition, more responsibility, interesting work); Distinction requires critical evaluation of whether the team leader's motivation approach addressed motivators or only hygiene factors
> - McGregor Theory X/Y: if a manager assumes Theory X (people are lazy, need supervising closely), they will use a directive, controlling leadership style; if they assume Theory Y (people seek responsibility), they will delegate and develop — the assumption drives the leadership behaviour; Distinction requires self-reflection on which assumption drives own team leader behaviour

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What ILM Level 3 units does this service cover?**
All major ILM Level 3 units are covered, including Understanding Leadership (Hersey and Blanchard, Adair, Blake and Mouton), Managing Workplace Projects at team level (project planning, stakeholder communication, risk register), Understanding Conflict Management in the Workplace (Thomas-Kilmann five conflict styles, real conflict evidence), and Motivating People in the Workplace (Maslow, Herzberg two-factor theory, team motivation evidence). Guidance is unit-specific and addresses the exact assessment criteria for each unit from the ILM unit specification.

**Q: How long are ILM Level 3 assignments and what format do they need?**
ILM Level 3 unit assignments are typically 1,000–1,500 words per unit — shorter than Level 5 assignments but requiring the same criterion-by-criterion evidence coverage. Each unit has its own assessment criteria that must all be individually addressed. The format is typically a structured written report (not an essay) — organised around the assessment criteria, with each section providing evidence of specific management or leadership activity from the candidate's real team leader role. Supporting evidence (meeting records, project plans, witness statements) can be included as appendices.

**Q: Do ILM Level 3 assignments require Harvard referencing?**
Harvard referencing is not formally required at ILM Level 3, but using academic references to support claims about leadership and management theory strengthens the assignment. At Level 3, citing the theorists whose models are applied is good practice — for example, referencing Hersey and Blanchard (1969) for situational leadership or Herzberg (1959) for two-factor motivation theory — even without full Harvard format. If the candidate's programme specifies referencing requirements, those should be followed. At Level 5 and Level 7, Harvard referencing is mandatory.

**Q: How do I use the Thomas-Kilmann conflict styles in an ILM Level 3 assignment?**
The Thomas-Kilmann model provides five conflict management styles: Competing (win-lose), Collaborating (win-win), Compromising (partial satisfaction for both), Avoiding (neither addresses the conflict), and Accommodating (yielding to the other party). In an ILM Level 3 assignment, the candidate describes a real conflict situation from their team leader role, identifies which style they used (or which combination), and reflects on whether it was appropriate for the situation. Distinction criteria require evaluation — was the style chosen the most effective option for this conflict type? Would a different style have produced a better outcome? What would be done differently?
