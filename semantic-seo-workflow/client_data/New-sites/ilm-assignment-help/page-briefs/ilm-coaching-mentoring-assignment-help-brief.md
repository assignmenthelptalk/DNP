## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: ILM assignment (Coaching and Mentoring pathway)

**Main Attributes**: coaching models (GROW, OSCAR, CLEAR, TGROW, CIGAR), mentoring definition and distinction from coaching, coaching session evidence types, reflective account requirement, coaching philosophy statement, level-by-level assessment standard, professional coaching bodies (EMCC, ICF)

**Derived Attributes**:
- **GROW Model (Whitmore, 2002)**: Goal (SMART, stretch, motivating), Reality (current state, obstacles, resources, what has been tried), Options (brainstormed options, pros and cons, feasibility), Will (commitment to action, specific next steps, accountability, follow-up timing); most widely used coaching model; appropriate for performance coaching and skills development; limitations: linearity may be too structured for complex coaching conversations; can create premature focus on solutions before sufficiently exploring the coachee's systemic Reality
- **OSCAR Model (McLeod, 2003)**: Outcome (desired end state and why it matters), Situation (current state and context), Choices (range of options available), Actions (specific committed next steps), Review (how progress will be measured and when); places Context before Solution — less linear than GROW; appropriate for systemic and solution-focused coaching
- **CLEAR Model (Hawkins and Smith, 2006)**: Contracting (establishing the coaching relationship — purpose, boundaries, confidentiality, what success looks like), Listening (deep coaching presence, reflective listening), Exploring (widening perspective, challenging assumptions), Action (committed next steps), Review (reflection on the coaching process and learning); the most relational of the three models — appropriate for transformational coaching and executive coaching where the coaching relationship itself is part of the developmental process
- **TGROW Model**: extension of GROW — Topic (what the coachee wants to explore broadly) prepended before Goal; useful when the coachee brings an unfocused presenting issue that needs to be clarified before a specific goal can be set
- **CIGAR Model**: Current (current state), Ideal (desired state), Gaps (between current and ideal), Actions (to close the gaps), Review (of progress) — a simplified coaching model used at Level 3 and as an alternative to GROW for line manager coaching contexts
- **Coaching vs Mentoring Distinction**: coaching = non-directive, raises self-awareness and responsibility, coach does not provide advice or solutions, coachee-generated solutions; mentoring = advisory, experienced mentor shares wisdom, provides guidance and role modelling, mentor may give advice directly; the distinction matters for ILM assessment — assignments that conflate coaching and mentoring demonstrate a fundamental misunderstanding of the coaching approach
- **Coaching Philosophy Statement**: a reflective piece articulating the candidate's approach to coaching — their beliefs about how people change, their coaching values, how they apply their chosen coaching models, their understanding of the coaching relationship; typically 800–1,200 words; assessed at Level 5 and Level 7; Distinction requires critical reflection on how the philosophy has developed through coaching practice
- **Coaching Session Logs**: evidence of real coaching conversations — structured records including date, duration, coaching objective, summary of coaching process (which model stage was reached), coachee commitments and next steps, candidate's reflective notes; typically 3–6 sessions of evidence per unit assignment
- **Coaching Supervision**: at Level 7, professional coaching supervision is required — engaging with an experienced coaching supervisor to reflect on coaching practice; evidence of supervision sessions must be included; the supervision process itself is assessed as part of professional coaching practice

#### H2: Coaching vs Mentoring: The Foundational Distinction in Every ILM Coaching Assignment
> **EAV to cover**: coaching definition (non-directive, raises self-awareness and accountability, coach facilitates the coachee's own thinking, coachee generates solutions), mentoring definition (advisory, experienced mentor shares wisdom and guidance, mentor may give advice and direction), the coaching approach (questioning, listening, reflecting back, challenging assumptions — not advising), why this distinction is assessed in ILM assignments, common assessment failure from conflating the two
> **Word count**: 280–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - ILM Coaching and Mentoring assessments require candidates to demonstrate a clear understanding of the distinction between coaching and mentoring — and to evidence applying a genuinely coaching (non-directive) approach in their sessions
> - Coaching: the coach uses questioning, active listening, reflecting, summarising, and challenging to raise the coachee's self-awareness and enable them to generate their own solutions; the coach does not provide advice, share opinions, or tell the coachee what to do; the coachee's own thinking drives the session
> - Mentoring: the mentor shares their own experience, knowledge, and wisdom; the mentor may give direct advice, make suggestions, and provide role modelling; the relationship involves the mentor's expertise being shared with a less experienced mentee
> - The coaching approach does not mean the coach is passive — effective coaching requires highly skilled questioning (open questions that widen perspective: "What else might be possible?", "What's stopping you?", "What would you do if you knew you couldn't fail?") and the courage to challenge the coachee's assumptions
> - Assessment failure: candidates who write coaching session logs that describe giving advice to the coachee, solving the coachee's problem, or directing the conversation toward a predetermined outcome are demonstrating mentoring (at best) or management consultation — not coaching; this results in a referral because the fundamental coaching criteria are not met
> - ILM allows assignments covering both coaching and mentoring as distinct activities — but the candidate must demonstrate clear understanding of which approach is being used at any point in the session and why

#### H2: GROW Model in ILM Coaching Assignments: Application, Evaluation, and Distinction
> **EAV to cover**: GROW model four stages with component detail (Goal — SMART and motivating; Reality — current state, obstacles, resources; Options — brainstormed options; Will — committed next steps, accountability, review timing), how to apply GROW in a real coaching session, coaching questions at each stage, GROW model limitations (linearity, premature solution focus), when GROW is not the best choice, Distinction criteria for critical evaluation
> **Word count**: 340–420 words
> **Specific content**:
> - GROW (Whitmore, 2002) is the most widely used coaching model and is the first model most ILM candidates learn — making it the most common starting point for ILM coaching assignments
> - Goal stage: identifying a SMART goal for the coaching session (specific to this session, not just a life goal); two types of goal — the end goal (what the coachee ultimately wants) and the performance goal (what is achievable in this session); powerful goal-setting questions: "What do you want to achieve from this session today?", "How will you know you've been successful?"
> - Reality stage: exploring the current situation — what has been tried, what is working, what obstacles exist, what resources are available; the Reality stage is where skilled coaches spend most time; questions: "What's happening right now?", "What have you already tried?", "What is the impact of the current situation on you?"
> - Options stage: generating a wide range of potential approaches without evaluation — brainstorming before filtering; questions: "What options do you have?", "What else could you do?", "What would you do if there were no constraints?"
> - Will stage: committing to specific actions with accountability — "What will you do?", "When will you do it?", "What might get in the way?", "On a scale of 1–10, how committed are you to taking this action?"
> - GROW limitations at Level 5/Level 7 Distinction: GROW was designed for performance coaching — it is most effective when the coachee has a clear goal and needs support moving from Reality to action; it is less effective when the coachee's presenting issue is systemic (organisational culture, leadership identity, values conflict) because the linear structure may create premature closure on solutions before the deeper systemic Reality is fully explored; CLEAR or OSCAR may be more appropriate in these contexts
> - How to evidence GROW in coaching session logs: structure the session log around the four stages — describe what happened in each stage, what questions were used, how the coachee responded, what the session achieved; Distinction requires critical reflection on whether GROW was the most appropriate model for this coachee and this presenting issue

#### H2: OSCAR and CLEAR Models: When and How to Apply Alternative Coaching Frameworks
> **EAV to cover**: OSCAR model (McLeod) — Outcome, Situation, Choices, Actions, Review — systemic and solution-focused approach, when OSCAR is more appropriate than GROW; CLEAR model (Hawkins and Smith) — Contracting, Listening, Exploring, Action, Review — relational coaching, coaching presence, coaching relationship as developmental tool; TGROW and CIGAR as simpler variants; comparative evaluation of models for Distinction criteria
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - OSCAR (McLeod, 2003): Outcome (the desired end state — why it matters to the coachee), Situation (current state including context and systemic factors), Choices (range of options), Actions (specific next steps), Review (how progress will be measured); OSCAR places Situation before Outcome — this means the coachee's current context is fully explored before goal-setting; more appropriate than GROW when the presenting issue is complex or systemic and the coachee is not yet clear on their desired outcome
> - CLEAR (Hawkins and Smith, 2006): Contracting (establishing the coaching relationship — purpose, boundaries, confidentiality, success criteria for the coaching engagement, not just the session), Listening (deep coaching presence — the coach's quality of attention), Exploring (widening the coachee's perspective, challenging assumptions, using systemic and creative questioning), Action (committed next steps), Review (reflection on both the coachee's learning and the coaching process itself); CLEAR is the most relational model — it explicitly includes the coaching relationship as a development tool; appropriate for transformational coaching and executive coaching
> - Comparing GROW and CLEAR at Level 5 Distinction: GROW is task-focused and solution-oriented; CLEAR is relationship-focused and systemic; for a coachee working on a skills goal (presenting better), GROW may be more efficient; for a coachee working on leadership identity or values alignment, CLEAR's Exploring stage may provide more developmental depth; Distinction requires critical evaluation of this choice for specific coaching scenarios
> - TGROW: Topic prepended before Goal — the Topic stage allows the coachee to describe the broad presenting issue before a specific Goal is identified; useful when coachees arrive with a vague sense of what they want to work on; reduces premature goal-narrowing
> - CIGAR: Current, Ideal, Gaps, Actions, Review — a simplified model suited to Level 3 line manager coaching contexts and brief coaching conversations; less theoretically developed than GROW but more accessible for first-time coaches

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What coaching models do ILM Coaching and Mentoring assignments require?**
ILM Coaching and Mentoring assignments use several coaching models depending on the level and unit. GROW (Whitmore, 2002 — Goal, Reality, Options, Will) is the most widely used at all levels. OSCAR (McLeod, 2003 — Outcome, Situation, Choices, Actions, Review) is preferred for systemic coaching contexts. CLEAR (Hawkins and Smith, 2006 — Contracting, Listening, Exploring, Action, Review) is used for transformational and executive coaching at Level 5 and Level 7. TGROW (GROW with Topic added) and CIGAR (Current, Ideal, Gaps, Actions, Review) are simpler variants used at Level 3 and in line manager coaching contexts. Distinction criteria at Level 5 and Level 7 require critical evaluation of the models — not just application.

**Q: What is the difference between coaching and mentoring in ILM assignments?**
Coaching is a non-directive process where the coach uses questioning and listening to raise the coachee's self-awareness and enable them to generate their own solutions — the coach does not provide advice or share opinions. Mentoring is an advisory relationship where an experienced mentor shares their knowledge, wisdom, and guidance with a less experienced mentee — the mentor may give direct advice. In ILM Coaching and Mentoring assignments, demonstrating that coaching sessions were genuinely non-directive (the coachee generated their own solutions) is a fundamental assessment criterion. Assignments that describe giving advice to the coachee are evidencing mentoring or management, not coaching.

**Q: How many coaching sessions do I need for an ILM coaching assignment?**
The number of coaching sessions required varies by level. ILM Level 3 typically requires 2–3 coaching session logs. ILM Level 5 typically requires 4–6 session logs across 2–3 different coachees. ILM Level 7 typically requires 6 or more sessions including at least one executive coaching engagement with a senior leader coachee. The exact requirement is specified in the unit assessment criteria for the specific unit. All sessions must be with real coachees and all coachee details must be anonymised in the submitted evidence.

**Q: What is a coaching philosophy statement and is it required at ILM Level 3?**
A coaching philosophy statement is a 800–1,200 word reflective piece articulating the candidate's approach to coaching — their beliefs about how people change, their coaching values, the models they apply, and the coaching relationship they seek to create. It is typically required at ILM Level 5 and Level 7, not Level 3. Distinction-level coaching philosophy statements demonstrate critical reflection on how the philosophy has been tested and refined through real coaching practice — not just a description of ideals or a summary of the coaching models covered in the unit. The philosophy must be connected to specific coaching session evidence and critical reflection on coaching effectiveness.
