## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: NVQ portfolio evidence

**Main Attributes**: evidence validity (VACS criteria), evidence hierarchy, portfolio organisation, cross-referencing method, sufficiency standards, evidence types

**Derived Attributes**:
- **VACS Criteria**:
  - Valid: the evidence must relate directly to the NVQ unit standard — evidence from an unrelated job role or qualification is not valid for a current unit
  - Authentic: the evidence must be the candidate's own work — assessors check that product evidence was produced by the candidate, and written evidence is in the candidate's own voice and workplace context; third-party evidence (witness testimony, expert witness) is authenticated by the third party's signature and job title
  - Current: evidence must demonstrate current (or recent enough) competence — what "current" means varies by subject; in health and social care, evidence older than two years is generally considered outdated because legislation, care standards, and practice change
  - Sufficient: evidence must cover ALL performance criteria for the element; if even one PC is not evidenced, the element cannot be signed off; "sufficient" does not mean "lots of" — it means complete coverage of all PCs
- **Evidence Hierarchy**: Direct Observation (most valid — assessor witnessed the competence directly); Expert Witness (specialist verification — high validity); Professional Discussion (assessor-led — assessor can probe and clarify); Witness Testimony (third-party observation — supports but rarely stands alone); Written Evidence / Product Evidence (candidate-generated — valid but requires VACS confirmation); Q&A / Questions and Answers (primarily used for K&U items)
- **Portfolio Organisation**: physical portfolio uses unit dividers/tabs labelled by unit number; evidence index (contents page) lists every evidence item with its reference number, type, and the unit/element/PCs it addresses; each evidence item is given a unique reference number (e.g., "PS-4" for Personal Statement 4, "WT-2" for Witness Testimony 2); cross-reference sheets sit at the front of each unit tab
- **Cross-Reference Sheet**: maps each evidence item to the specific unit number, element number, and individual PC codes it addresses; example format: Evidence Ref PS-4 → Unit 4, Element 4.1 → PCs a, b, c; the same evidence item may appear on multiple cross-reference sheets if it addresses PCs across more than one element
- **Sufficiency Standard**: assessors need to see ALL PCs evidenced; for PCs that require consistent performance (not just a one-off demonstration), the assessor may require more than one evidence item for the same PC — for example, if a PC requires the candidate to "consistently support service users to maintain independence," a single personal statement may not be sufficient without corroborating witness testimony or a second observation
- **Digital Portfolios**: e-portfolio platforms increasingly used by awarding bodies and training providers — OneFile, Bud, ProMonitor, Aptem; these platforms allow candidates to upload evidence, tag it to unit/element/PC codes, and submit for assessor review digitally; the VACS and cross-referencing requirements are identical to paper portfolios

**Values per Attribute**:
- VACS — Valid: unit standard relevance confirmed

- VACS — Current: typically within 2 years, subject-dependent (H&SC more stringent)

- Evidence reference naming conventions: PS (Personal Statement), RA (Reflective Account), WT (Witness Testimony), PE (Product Evidence), OB (Observation), PD (Professional Discussion)

#### H1: NVQ Portfolio Evidence Help: What Counts as Valid Evidence, How Much You Need, and How to Organise It
> **Macro-context**: NVQ portfolio evidence as the primary assessment mechanism — validity criteria, organisation, cross-referencing, and sufficiency for assessor sign-off
> **First paragraph must**: Define NVQ portfolio evidence — state that the portfolio is the sole assessment mechanism for NVQs (no exams), that it is a structured collection of evidence items each cross-referenced to specific unit, element, and performance criteria codes, and that evidence is assessed against four validity criteria (VACS). First word should be "Portfolio" or "NVQ." No rhetorical opener.

#### H2: What NVQ Portfolio Evidence Is — Definition, Purpose, and How It Proves Competence
> **EAV to cover**: Portfolio definition; competence-based assessment rationale; relationship between evidence and PCs; assessor's role in reviewing the portfolio
> **Word count**: 280–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - NVQ portfolio evidence is the collection of materials that demonstrates a candidate's competence against the performance criteria (PCs) of each unit element in their NVQ qualification
> - The portfolio is the primary — and in most cases the only — assessment instrument; there are no examinations or academic coursework in an NVQ
> - A qualified assessor (holding TAQA, CAVA, or legacy D32/D33/A1/A2 qualifications and occupationally competent in the subject area) reviews every piece of evidence and makes a judgment: does this evidence demonstrate the specific PC it is cross-referenced to?
> - Evidence does not prove general competence in the job role — it must prove specific competence against specific, named performance criteria. A care assistant who is excellent at their job will still have evidence returned if it does not address the individual PC codes listed under each element
> - The portfolio is built incrementally — candidates add evidence items as they complete activities in their workplace, building toward full PC coverage across all mandatory units

#### H2: The VACS Criteria — How Assessors Judge Whether Evidence Is Valid
> **EAV to cover**: All four VACS criteria with specific definitions and examples; what fails each criterion; how to ensure evidence meets each
> **Word count**: 350–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Valid**: The evidence must relate directly to the NVQ unit standard and the specific element and PCs it is cross-referenced to. Evidence from a different job role, a different qualification, or a different context is not valid. Example: a personal statement written for a Level 2 Health and Social Care unit cannot be reused as evidence for a Level 3 Leadership and Management unit even if the candidate worked in the same setting
> - **Authentic**: The evidence must be the candidate's own work and genuinely reflect the candidate's performance. Written evidence must be in the candidate's own voice and workplace context. Product evidence must have been produced by the candidate. Assessors may conduct a professional discussion to test the authenticity of written evidence if they have concerns. Witness testimonies and expert witness statements authenticate the candidate's performance from a third-party perspective
> - **Current**: Evidence must demonstrate current (or sufficiently recent) competence — not historical practice. Most awarding bodies apply a two-year currency rule, though this varies by sector. In Health and Social Care, evidence must reflect current legislation (e.g., Care Act 2014, Mental Capacity Act 2005) and current practice standards; evidence written before a significant legislative change may be considered out of date
> - **Sufficient**: Evidence must cover ALL performance criteria for the element — there are no partial passes. If an element has six PCs (a through f) and the evidence addresses only PCs a, b, c, and d, the element is not complete. "Sufficient" is a completeness standard, not a quantity standard — a single well-written personal statement covering all six PCs is more useful than three documents that collectively miss PC f

#### H2: The NVQ Evidence Hierarchy — Which Types Carry the Most Weight
> **EAV to cover**: Evidence validity hierarchy; why Direct Observation is most valid; role of each evidence type in the hierarchy; how assessors use hierarchy when making sign-off decisions
> **Word count**: 300–340 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Evidence types are not equal in evidential weight. Assessors and awarding bodies recognise a hierarchy based on the directness of the competence demonstration
> - **Direct Observation** sits at the top — the assessor attended the workplace and watched the candidate perform the competence in real conditions; this is the most unambiguous evidence of competence; many awarding bodies require at least one direct observation per unit, and some require it for specific high-risk PCs (e.g., manual handling, medication administration, fire safety)
> - **Expert Witness** carries high weight because a specialist has verified the competence — commonly used in clinical or technical contexts where the assessor may not have specialist expertise (e.g., a registered nurse verifying medication management competence)
> - **Professional Discussion** is assessor-led and allows real-time probing — the assessor can ask follow-up questions to confirm understanding; particularly effective for Knowledge and Understanding (K&U) items
> - **Witness Testimony** from a qualified observer supports but rarely replaces direct observation — it is third-party confirmation that the candidate performed the activity, but the witness may not hold TAQA/CAVA and cannot make a formal competence judgment
> - **Written Evidence** (personal statements, reflective accounts) and **Product Evidence** (completed forms, reports, care plans) are candidate-generated and require VACS authentication; they are valid and frequently used but should be supported by observation or witness evidence where possible
> - **Q&A responses** primarily address Knowledge and Understanding requirements rather than performance competence

#### H2: How to Organise an NVQ Portfolio — Structure, Dividers, and Evidence Index
> **EAV to cover**: Physical portfolio structure; evidence index format; unit tab labelling; evidence reference numbering; digital portfolio structure
> **Word count**: 280–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - A well-organised portfolio enables assessors to locate and verify evidence quickly — a disorganised portfolio is not a reason for return, but it slows assessment and creates the impression that cross-referencing has not been completed
> - **Unit dividers/tabs**: the portfolio is divided by unit, with clearly labelled tabs for each unit the candidate is completing; each tab contains a cross-reference sheet at the front followed by the evidence items for that unit
> - **Evidence index (contents page)**: a master list at the front of the portfolio listing every evidence item, its unique reference code, its type (e.g., PS = Personal Statement, RA = Reflective Account, WT = Witness Testimony, PE = Product Evidence, OB = Observation Record, PD = Professional Discussion Record), and the unit/element/PCs it addresses
> - **Evidence reference codes**: assigning a unique code to every evidence item (e.g., PS-4, WT-2, RA-7) enables cross-referencing without physically duplicating documents — the same evidence item can appear on multiple cross-reference sheets by reference code
> - **Digital portfolios (e-portfolios)**: platforms including OneFile, Bud, ProMonitor, and Aptem replicate this structure digitally; candidates upload evidence documents, tag them to specific unit/element/PC codes within the platform, and submit for assessor review; the VACS requirements and cross-referencing logic are identical to paper portfolios

#### H2: Evidence Cross-Referencing — How to Map Every Piece of Evidence to Performance Criteria
> **EAV to cover**: Cross-reference sheet format; unit number + element + PC code mapping; example cross-reference entry; what happens when cross-referencing is missing or incomplete
> **Word count**: 300–350 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Cross-referencing is the process of formally recording which performance criteria each piece of evidence addresses — it is not optional; without cross-referencing, assessors cannot efficiently verify PC coverage, and portfolios are returned incomplete
> - **Cross-reference sheet format**: for each unit, a cross-reference sheet (sometimes called a "tracking sheet" or "assessment record") lists each element and each PC within it, and for each PC records the reference codes of the evidence items that address it
> - **Example entry**: Unit 4, Element 4.1, PC a → evidenced by PS-4, WT-2 | PC b → evidenced by RA-3 | PC c → evidenced by PS-4, OB-1 | PC d → evidenced by WT-2
> - **One evidence item can address multiple PCs** across one or more elements — this is encouraged; a reflective account about a safeguarding situation may address PCs across Element 4.1 (recognise signs of abuse), Element 4.2 (respond to suspected abuse), and Element 5.1 (duty of care obligations) simultaneously
> - **Missing cross-references** are one of the most common reasons assessors return portfolios — the evidence itself may be sufficient, but if the candidate has not recorded which PCs it addresses, the assessor cannot confirm coverage
> - Awarding body specifications include a standard cross-reference or tracking sheet format — candidates should use their awarding body's template rather than creating their own unless their training provider has an approved alternative

#### H2: How Much Evidence Is Enough — Sufficiency, Consistency, and Assessor Judgment
> **EAV to cover**: Sufficiency standard (all PCs must be covered); difference between sufficiency and quantity; when assessors require multiple evidence items for a single PC; consistency requirement
> **Word count**: 250–290 words
> **Specific content**:
> - There is no minimum number of pages, documents, or word count specified for NVQ portfolio evidence — "sufficient" means that all PCs for every element are addressed, not that the portfolio contains a certain volume of material
> - A single, well-constructed personal statement that explicitly addresses PCs a, b, c, d, and e for a unit element is sufficient if the assessor is satisfied that it demonstrates the required competence
> - However, assessors may require more than one evidence item for a specific PC if the PC implies consistent performance rather than a one-off demonstration — for example, a PC stating "consistently ensures service users are treated with dignity and respect" may require corroboration across more than one evidence type (e.g., personal statement + witness testimony) to demonstrate that the behaviour is habitual
> - Assessors apply professional judgment — if they have doubt about the authenticity or consistency of evidence for a specific PC, they may request a professional discussion to probe further before signing off
> - The most reliable approach: ensure every PC is addressed by at least one primary evidence item (personal statement or reflective account) cross-referenced explicitly to that PC, and supplement with product evidence or witness testimony where the PC implies observable performance

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What are the VACS criteria for NVQ portfolio evidence?**
VACS stands for Valid, Authentic, Current, and Sufficient — the four criteria every piece of NVQ portfolio evidence must meet. Valid means the evidence relates to the specific unit standard and performance criteria. Authentic means it is the candidate's own work. Current means it demonstrates present or recent competence (typically within two years, though this varies by sector). Sufficient means it covers all performance criteria for the element — not just some.

**Q: How do I cross-reference NVQ portfolio evidence to performance criteria?**
Cross-referencing is recorded on a tracking sheet (provided by the awarding body or training provider) for each unit. Each performance criteria (PC) code is listed, and the reference codes of the evidence items that address it are recorded beside it — for example, "PC a → evidenced by PS-4, WT-2." The same evidence item can be cross-referenced to multiple PCs across different elements using its reference code, without physically copying the document into each unit section.

**Q: Can one piece of NVQ evidence cover multiple performance criteria?**
A single evidence item — for example, a reflective account about a safeguarding incident — can address performance criteria across multiple elements and even multiple units if the content is relevant to each. The evidence item is assigned a unique reference code and recorded on every cross-reference sheet for every PC it addresses. This is efficient and encouraged — it reduces the total volume of evidence the candidate needs to produce.

**Q: How do I know if my NVQ portfolio has enough evidence?**
Sufficient evidence means every performance criteria (PC) for every mandatory element in each unit is covered by at least one evidence item. There is no minimum page count or document count. Complete your cross-reference sheet for each unit, and if every PC has at least one evidence reference beside it, the portfolio is structurally complete. Assessors may still request additional evidence if they have concerns about authenticity or consistent performance, but PC coverage is the baseline measure of sufficiency.
