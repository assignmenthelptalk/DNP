## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: NVQ reflective account (analytical evidence type)
**Main Attributes**: definition and purpose, reflective model application (Gibbs 1988), structure, criteria mapping, difference from personal statement, sufficient vs insufficient reflective evidence
**Derived Attributes**:
- **What an NVQ reflective account is**: A written account that goes beyond describing what the candidate did (personal statement territory) and analyses a specific incident or period of practice — what happened, how the candidate felt, what was good/bad about it, what conclusions they draw, and what they will do differently in future. Used to evidence performance criteria that require evaluation of own practice, professional judgement, and development awareness — particularly common in Level 3 and Level 4 NVQ units in health and social care, management, education support, and early years.
- **Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988)**: The most commonly used reflective model in NVQ contexts. Six stages: (1) Description — what happened (what, where, who, when — factual account); (2) Feelings — what was the candidate thinking and feeling at the time; (3) Evaluation — what was good and bad about the experience; (4) Analysis — what sense can be made of the situation (applying knowledge, legislation, policy); (5) Conclusion — what else could have been done; (6) Action Plan — what will be done differently next time if the same situation arises. Each stage must be addressed — a reflective account that stops at Description is not reflective.
- **Other reflective models used in NVQ context**: Schon (1983) — Reflection-in-Action (thinking during the event and adjusting in the moment) and Reflection-on-Action (thinking about the event after it has happened). Kolb (1984) — Concrete Experience → Reflective Observation → Abstract Conceptualisation → Active Experimentation. Most NVQ assessors accept any recognised model — Gibbs is most widely understood and recommended for NVQ candidates because its six stages are explicit and easier to follow as a structure guide.
- **What differentiates reflective account from personal statement**: Personal statement = describes competence in practice. Reflective account = analyses a specific event or decision, evaluates outcomes, and identifies learning or development arising from the experience. Both can evidence different PCs within the same unit. Reflective accounts are required when PCs use language like: "evaluate own practice," "reflect on...," "identify areas for development," "demonstrate ability to learn from..."
- **Criteria mapping in reflective accounts**: Same as personal statements — the reflective account must identify which performance criteria it evidences. However, reflective accounts often cover fewer PCs per account because they focus on depth of analysis of one or two specific events rather than breadth of competence demonstration.
- **What makes reflective evidence insufficient — NVQ-specific failures**: (1) Describing only what happened without analysis ("I gave Mrs J her medication and she was pleased") — Description stage only, not reflective; (2) Reflection without any PC linkage — "I felt this went well" is not evidence unless it is mapped to a specific criterion; (3) Analysis without connecting to practice context — citing Gibbs stages as headings without putting real practice content beneath them; (4) Action plan that is vague ("I will improve my communication") vs specific ("I will attend the manual handling refresher training scheduled for 15 June and will monitor my technique against the safe system of work in my next three shifts")
- **Length and scope**: Typically 400–800 words per reflective account. Depth over breadth — one well-analysed incident with all six Gibbs stages addressed is stronger than three incidents described superficially.

#### H2: Gibbs' Reflective Cycle Applied to an NVQ Practice Incident
> **EAV to cover**: All six Gibbs stages (1988) with a complete NVQ health and social care worked example
> **Word count**: 360–420 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Gibbs (1988) Reflective Cycle: Description → Feelings → Evaluation → Analysis → Conclusion → Action Plan
> - Worked example — a care assistant dealing with a distressed service user:
>   - **Description**: "During my afternoon shift on Monday I was supporting Mr T, an 84-year-old resident, during personal care. Mr T became very distressed and asked me to stop the activity. He was crying and saying he felt humiliated."
>   - **Feelings**: "I felt shocked and concerned. I was unsure whether to stop immediately or continue as he still needed personal care to be completed. I felt anxious about doing the wrong thing."
>   - **Evaluation**: "What went well: I stopped the care activity immediately when Mr T asked me to and gave him time to compose himself. What did not go well: I had not noticed signs of distress building during the early stages of the care activity, which suggests I was not monitoring his non-verbal cues effectively."
>   - **Analysis**: "Mr T's distress may have been connected to his previous experience of care — his care plan notes that he has found personal care difficult since arriving at the home. The person-centred values of dignity and choice (Care Act 2014) require that I respond to the individual's needs in the moment, not follow a fixed routine. I had followed the routine rather than reading his cues."
>   - **Conclusion**: "I could have done more to prepare Mr T before beginning — explaining each step, asking his preference, and observing his responses before proceeding. I could also have read his care plan more carefully before the shift."
>   - **Action Plan**: "I will re-read Mr T's care plan before every shift, specifically the section on personal care preferences. I will also attend the dignity and respect training session scheduled for 20 May and discuss this incident with my supervisor at my next supervision."
> - Every Gibbs stage must contain substantive content — stage headings with one-line answers are not sufficient

#### H2: Why NVQ Reflective Accounts Get Returned as Insufficient
> **EAV to cover**: Four most common failures with examples from each Gibbs stage area
> **Word count**: 240–280 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Stopping at Description**: Writing 400 words of what happened without progressing to Feelings, Evaluation, or Analysis. This is the single most common failure — the candidate provides a personal statement and calls it a reflective account.
> - **No Analysis stage**: Moving from Evaluation straight to Action Plan without explaining WHY things happened — what theory, policy, or legislation contextualises the incident. Analysis requires connecting the experience to professional knowledge.
> - **Vague Action Plan**: "I will improve my communication skills in future" fails the Action Plan stage. Specific action plans name the exact action, when it will happen, and how progress will be measured: "I will complete the communication training module by 30 June and ask my supervisor to observe one interaction per week to give me feedback on my non-verbal communication."
> - **No PC linkage**: A well-written reflection that doesn't state which performance criteria it addresses. The assessor cannot cross-reference it in the portfolio without this linkage.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Do I have to use Gibbs' Reflective Cycle for my NVQ reflective account?**
Gibbs (1988) is the most widely recognised reflective model in NVQ contexts and is accepted by all major awarding organisations. Other models — Schon's (1983) reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action framework, or Kolb's (1984) Experiential Learning Cycle — are also accepted. Candidates should check whether their assessor or awarding organisation has a preference. Gibbs is recommended for NVQ candidates because its six explicit stages (Description, Feelings, Evaluation, Analysis, Conclusion, Action Plan) provide a clear structure that is easy to follow and easy for assessors to verify.

**Q: How is a reflective account different from a personal statement in an NVQ portfolio?**
A personal statement describes what the candidate did — it demonstrates competence in a specific practice activity by narrating the action taken and referencing the relevant performance criteria. A reflective account analyses a specific incident — it goes beyond what happened to examine why, what was learned, and what will change in future practice. Both types of evidence co-exist in a portfolio and address different categories of performance criteria. Performance criteria using words like "evaluate," "reflect," or "identify development" require reflective accounts; criteria using words like "carry out," "ensure," or "support" are typically evidenced by personal statements or observation records.

**Q: How long should an NVQ reflective account be?**
Most NVQ reflective accounts are 400–800 words. The appropriate length depends on how many Gibbs stages are addressed and the complexity of the incident being reflected on. Length should be determined by content — ensuring all six Gibbs stages are substantively addressed — not by hitting a word target. A 500-word account with genuine Analysis and Action Plan is stronger than an 800-word account that repeats the Description stage in different words.

**Q: Can I use the same incident in a reflective account and a personal statement?**
An incident can be described in a personal statement (demonstrating competence in the actions taken) and separately analysed in a reflective account (evaluating what happened, what was learned, and what will change) — these are different types of evidence and address different performance criteria. Both documents are filed in the portfolio and cross-referenced where relevant. This approach is efficient and is accepted practice — the same real work event can generate evidence across multiple evidence types if the accounts address different aspects of the experience.
