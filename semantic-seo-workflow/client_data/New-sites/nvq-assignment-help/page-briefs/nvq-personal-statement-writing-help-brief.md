## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: NVQ personal statement (written evidence type)
**Main Attributes**: definition of a personal statement in NVQ context, what it must demonstrate, how to structure it, criteria mapping methodology, sufficient vs insufficient evidence, difference from reflective account
**Derived Attributes**:
- **What an NVQ personal statement is**: A written first-person account in which the candidate describes their occupational practice — what they do, how they do it, and why — in sufficient detail to demonstrate competence against specific performance criteria (PCs) within a unit. Also called a "written personal account" or "statement of competence" in some awarding organisation frameworks. Used when direct observation by the assessor is not possible, impractical, or has not been arranged for the specific activity being evidenced.
- **What it must demonstrate**: Competence — not just knowledge. The distinction is critical. A personal statement that explains what the candidate knows about a topic ("I understand that person-centred care means treating each individual according to their preferences") does NOT demonstrate competence. A statement that describes what the candidate did in practice ("When Mrs J expressed a preference for a bath rather than a shower, I discussed this with the senior and adjusted her care plan to reflect her preference, ensuring the updated plan was signed and dated") demonstrates competence.
- **Performance criteria (PCs) and unit elements**: Every NVQ unit is broken into elements (sub-sections), and each element has PCs that define exactly what the candidate must demonstrate. A sufficient personal statement addresses specific PCs by number — not the unit title generically. Example: "This statement provides evidence for Unit 24, Element 24.1, Performance Criteria 24.1a, 24.1b, and 24.1c."
- **Structure of an NVQ personal statement**: (1) Opening header: unit number, element, performance criteria being addressed; (2) Context: brief description of the workplace and role (1–2 sentences); (3) Main account: detailed first-person description of specific work activities that demonstrate each PC in turn — referenced explicitly ("This demonstrates PC 24.1a because..."); (4) Closing cross-reference: list any other units this statement partially evidences (for cross-referencing in the portfolio index)
- **What makes evidence sufficient**: VACS criteria — Valid (directly relevant to the unit PC being claimed), Authentic (genuinely the candidate's own work), Current (demonstrates current competence — within the last 2–3 years for most awarding organisations), Sufficient (covers all required PCs — partial coverage is insufficient for the element). Assessors check all four before accepting evidence.
- **What makes evidence insufficient — common failures**: (1) Generic descriptions that could apply to any worker ("I always treat service users with dignity and respect") — no specific incident, no observable practice; (2) Describing knowledge instead of practice ("I know that confidentiality is important") — knowledge is assessed separately, usually by professional discussion; (3) Failing to reference which PCs the statement covers — assessors cannot cross-reference unlinked evidence in the portfolio; (4) Using one personal statement to claim an entire unit without addressing every PC within the element
- **Difference from reflective account**: A personal statement describes what the candidate did — factual, practice-focused, competence-demonstrating. A reflective account additionally analyses why they made the decisions they did, what the outcome was, what they would do differently, and what they learned. Some NVQ assessors accept personal statements for descriptive PCs and require reflective accounts for analytical PCs. Both types are distinct evidence types and should not be conflated.
- **Cross-referencing**: One personal statement can be cross-referenced to multiple units if it provides evidence for PCs in more than one unit. The portfolio index must show these cross-references — a personal statement used for three units is noted in the index under all three. This maximises evidence efficiency and is good practice.

- Currency: evidence typically must be within 2–3 years of assessment (check awarding organisation guidance)

#### H2: What an NVQ Personal Statement Is — and What It Must Prove
> **EAV to cover**: Definition, purpose, when it is used vs direct observation, what competence means in this context
> **Word count**: 230–270 words
> **Specific content**:
> - An NVQ personal statement is a written first-person account of specific work activities — describing in detail what the candidate did in their workplace role to demonstrate the competence required by a specific unit element and its performance criteria
> - Also called "written personal account," "statement of competence," or "written evidence" — the terminology varies by awarding organisation (City & Guilds, Pearson, NCFE, Highfield) but the purpose is the same
> - Used when direct assessor observation is not practical for a specific activity — e.g., a one-off event, a confidential situation, a task that occurs infrequently. Not a replacement for observation — both types of evidence are typically required across the portfolio
> - What it must prove: competence — that the candidate actually performs the task to the required standard in their real work context. Not what they know. Not what they think. What they do.

#### H2: Competence vs Knowledge — The Most Important NVQ Distinction
> **EAV to cover**: Contrast between competence evidence and knowledge statements, with parallel examples
> **Word count**: 280–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Competence evidence describes observable practice — what the candidate did, with whom, in what context, and to what outcome
> - Knowledge statement describes understanding — what the candidate knows, believes, or understands about a topic
> - **Insufficient (knowledge)**: "I understand that confidentiality is important in health and social care. Service users have the right to have their personal information kept private. I always ensure I do not share information unnecessarily."
> - **Sufficient (competence)**: "In my role as a care assistant at Hillview Care Home, I received a phone call from a person claiming to be a family member asking about Mr K's condition. Following the home's confidentiality policy and the Data Protection Act 2018, I did not share any information and advised the caller to contact the registered manager. I documented the call in the communication log and reported it to the senior on duty."
> - The difference: the sufficient version names a specific incident, describes the specific action taken, references the policy and legislation applied, names the outcome, and demonstrates the practice in context — giving the assessor observable evidence of competence
> - Writers must ask themselves: "If my assessor watched a video of my workplace, would they see what I am describing?" If yes — competence evidence. If no — knowledge statement.

#### H2: VACS Criteria — What Makes NVQ Evidence Sufficient
> **EAV to cover**: Valid, Authentic, Current, Sufficient — each with definition and NVQ application
> **Word count**: 240–280 words
> **Specific content**:
> - All NVQ evidence is assessed against VACS criteria — four standards that every piece of evidence must meet before an assessor can accept it
> - **Valid**: The evidence directly relates to the performance criteria it claims to demonstrate. A personal statement about medication administration does not demonstrate competence in manual handling.
> - **Authentic**: The evidence is genuinely the candidate's own work. Assessors verify authenticity through professional discussion — they ask candidates to explain their personal statements in detail. Evidence that the candidate cannot discuss or explain is rejected as non-authentic.
> - **Current**: The evidence reflects current competence — typically within the last 2–3 years of assessment (check the awarding organisation's guidance for the specific qualification). Evidence of practice from 5 years ago does not demonstrate current competence.
> - **Sufficient**: The evidence covers all required performance criteria within the element — not just some of them. A personal statement that evidences PCs a and b but not c is insufficient for the element. Gaps must be filled with additional evidence.

#### H2: How to Structure an NVQ Personal Statement
> **EAV to cover**: Opening header, context statement, main account with PC referencing, cross-reference note
> **Word count**: 260–300 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Opening header**: Unit number, element, and the specific performance criteria this statement addresses. Example: "Unit 24 — Support Individuals in Meeting Personal Care Needs / Element 24.1 — Support individuals to maintain their personal hygiene / Evidence for PCs: 24.1a, 24.1b, 24.1c"
> - **Context** (1–2 sentences): Candidate's role, workplace type, relevant setting. "I work as a care assistant at Hillview Residential Care Home providing personal care support to adults aged 65+ with varying levels of dependency."
> - **Main account**: Detailed first-person description of specific activities that demonstrate each PC. After describing the relevant activity, the candidate explicitly states the PC it evidences: "This demonstrates PC 24.1a — I prepared the environment to meet the individual's personal hygiene needs." One to two specific incidents with enough detail to demonstrate the standard.
> - **Closing cross-reference**: If the statement also evidences PCs in other units, note them. "This statement also provides partial evidence for Unit 35.2b and Unit 41.1a." The portfolio index records these cross-references.
> - Length: 300–600 words is typical. Short is acceptable if all PCs are addressed. Lengthy generic statements are worse than focused shorter ones.

#### H2: Common NVQ Personal Statement Failures — Why Evidence Gets Returned
> **EAV to cover**: Four most common failures with examples
> **Word count**: 240–280 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Generic statements**: "I always treat service users with dignity and respect" — no incident, no context, no observable practice. Assessors cannot accept this as evidence of a specific PC.
> - **Knowledge statements**: Describing understanding of policies rather than application. "I know the Data Protection Act 2018 requires confidentiality" vs describing a specific situation where the Act was applied.
> - **No PC reference**: Writing a detailed account that doesn't state which PCs it addresses. Assessors cannot cross-reference evidence they cannot link to specific criteria. Always label the PCs at the top of the statement.
> - **Partial element coverage**: A personal statement that addresses only 3 of 5 PCs in an element. The assessor cannot pass the element without all PCs evidenced — additional evidence must be provided for the missing PCs.

### SUPPLEMENTARY CONTENT

#### H2: Personal Statement vs Reflective Account in Your NVQ Portfolio
> **Content**: A personal statement describes what the candidate did — factual, practice-focused, competence-demonstrating. A reflective account goes further — it analyses why decisions were made, what the outcome was, what the candidate learned, and what they would do differently next time. Some units require reflective accounts specifically for PCs involving decision-making, professional judgement, or adapting practice. Candidates should use personal statements for descriptive practice PCs and reflective accounts for analytical or evaluative PCs — or as instructed by their assessor.

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How long should an NVQ personal statement be?**
Most NVQ personal statements are 300–600 words — long enough to describe the specific work activity in sufficient detail for the assessor to verify competence against each performance criterion, but focused enough to avoid generic content that dilutes the evidence. Some complex elements with many PCs may require longer statements; straightforward elements with 2–3 PCs may be adequately evidenced in 250 words. Always check that every PC listed in the header is addressed somewhere in the statement before submitting.

**Q: Can one personal statement cover multiple units?**
A single personal statement can provide evidence for performance criteria in more than one unit if the activity it describes is relevant to multiple criteria across the portfolio. This is called cross-referencing. The statement is filed once (typically in the unit where it is most directly relevant) and the portfolio index notes the other units it partially evidences. Cross-referencing is efficient — it reduces the total volume of evidence required — and is accepted by all major awarding organisations.

**Q: What is the difference between a personal statement and a professional discussion?**
A personal statement is written evidence — a narrative account the candidate writes independently. A professional discussion is an observed conversation between the candidate and assessor, where the assessor asks questions about the candidate's practice to verify competence. Both generate evidence for the portfolio. Professional discussions are often used to follow up on personal statements — the assessor asks the candidate to expand on or explain something they described in writing, confirming that the account is authentic and that the candidate genuinely performed the activities described.

**Q: What happens if my personal statement is returned as insufficient?**
If an assessor returns a personal statement as insufficient, they will provide feedback identifying which performance criteria were not adequately evidenced. The candidate must provide supplementary evidence — either a revised or extended personal statement addressing the gaps, additional witness testimony, or an agreed professional discussion. Insufficient evidence does not mean failure — it means the evidence gap must be addressed before the element can be signed off. Our service reviews assessor feedback and provides specific guidance on how to strengthen the evidence for resubmission.
