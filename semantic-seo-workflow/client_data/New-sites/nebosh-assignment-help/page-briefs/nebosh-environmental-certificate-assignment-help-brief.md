#### H2: NEBOSH Environmental Practical Assessment: Site Inspection Requirements
> **Context vector**: EC practical assessment methodology — what a complete aspects and impacts register requires, how significance is evaluated, grading criteria
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Environmental aspects and impacts assessment (the core practical output):
>   - Aspect = what the organisation or site activity does (fuel storage, vehicle movements, effluent discharge, waste generation)
>   - Impact = the environmental consequence of that aspect (soil contamination, air quality reduction, greenhouse gas emissions, watercourse pollution)
>   - Significance evaluation: each impact assessed by combination of likelihood, severity, scale (local/regional/global), duration, and whether a legal compliance obligation applies
> - Source-pathway-receptor (SPR) model: source (where the aspect arises) → pathway (how the impact travels — via air, water, ground) → receptor (who or what is affected — watercourse, populated area, protected species habitat); breaking any link in SPR prevents the impact
> - ISO 14001:2015 Clause 6.1.2 requirements for aspects identification: organisation must determine aspects considering normal operations, abnormal conditions, and emergency situations
> - Output document: aspects and impacts register with significance rating, existing controls, recommended additional controls, and prioritised action plan
> - Grading: assessed on aspects identification breadth (range of activity types covered), significance evaluation accuracy (criteria applied consistently), control recommendations appropriateness, and improvement plan quality
> - Common practical failures: identifying only operational aspects and missing waste, maintenance, and emergency scenario aspects; applying generic controls without reference to EPR permit conditions or EPA duty of care requirements

#### H2: FAQ — NEBOSH Environmental Certificate Representative Query Extensions

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. **What legislation is most important for the NEBOSH Environmental Certificate?** → Answer: The Environmental Protection Act 1990 (duty of care, statutory nuisances), Environmental Permitting Regulations 2016 (environmental permits for pollution-risk activities), Water Resources Act 1991 (s.85 water pollution offences), Waste (England and Wales) Regulations 2011 (waste hierarchy as legal requirement), and the Environment Act 2021 (overarching UK environmental targets and enforcement framework). ISO 14001:2015 is the management system standard that structures how these legislative requirements are applied in an organisation.
2. **What is the difference between an environmental aspect and an environmental impact in NEBOSH EC?** → Answer: An environmental aspect is an element of an organisation's activities, products, or services that can interact with the environment — for example, fuel combustion or chemical storage. An environmental impact is the change to the environment that results from that aspect — for example, air pollution or soil contamination. NEBOSH EC assignments require students to identify both for each site activity and to evaluate the significance of each impact using a likelihood × severity matrix.
3. **Does the NEBOSH EC practical assessment require ISO 14001 certification?** → Answer: No. ISO 14001 certification is not required for the EC practical assessment. ISO 14001:2015 provides the management system framework (aspects identification, significance evaluation, monitoring, improvement) that structures the assessment methodology. Students apply the ISO 14001 approach to evaluate the site's environmental performance — they do not need to have undergone formal certification.
4. **How does the NEBOSH Environmental Certificate differ from the NEBOSH IGC?** → Answer: Both qualifications use the same OBE plus practical assessment format and the same grading bands. The EC is focused specifically on environmental management — aspects and impacts, environmental legislation, ISO 14001, and pollution control. The IGC covers occupational health and safety — workplace hazards, HSWA 1974, ISO 45001, and risk assessment. The EC is intended for professionals with specific environmental management responsibilities; the IGC is the general entry-level health and safety qualification.
