## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: CACHE observation record

**Main Attributes**: observation method selection, objective description writing, child development theory application, EYFS area of learning identification, next steps for learning, assessment criteria compliance, portfolio evidence function

**Derived Attributes**:
- **Observation Methods**: Narrative/running record (continuous written description of a child's activity in real time — captures full sequence of behaviour, language, movement); time sampling (observation recorded at pre-set timed intervals — e.g., every 5 minutes for 30 minutes — captures pattern of behaviour across time); event sampling (record made each time a target behaviour occurs — captures frequency and context of specific behaviour); anecdotal record (brief written account of a significant event noted after it occurs — less structured, used for incidental observations); sociogram (maps social relationships and interactions — who child plays with, initiates contact with, avoids); child tracker (maps physical movement around the setting — used to analyse use of learning environment)
- **Objective Description**: The observation section must describe only what is seen and heard — no interpretation, no inference about feeling or motivation; interpretation occurs in the analysis section only; language must be specific — "picked up the red block and placed it on top of the blue block" not "played with blocks"
- **Child Development Theorists**: Piaget (cognitive development — four stages: Sensorimotor 0–2, Preoperational 2–7, Concrete Operational 7–11, Formal Operational 11+; schema theory — assimilation, accommodation, equilibration; object permanence); Vygotsky (Zone of Proximal Development — the gap between what a child can do independently and what they can do with support; scaffolding — temporary support withdrawn as competence grows; More Knowledgeable Other — peer or adult who provides support; social constructivism — learning occurs through social interaction); Bowlby (attachment theory — four attachment types: secure attachment, anxious-avoidant attachment, anxious-resistant attachment, disorganised attachment; internal working model; separation anxiety; stranger anxiety); Bruner (scaffolding — parallel to Vygotsky but focused on language and representation modes: enactive, iconic, symbolic; discovery learning; spiral curriculum — revisiting concepts at increasing complexity); Bronfenbrenner (ecological systems model — five nested systems: microsystem, mesosystem, exosystem, macrosystem, chronosystem — observation is situated within the microsystem of the setting); Bandura (social learning theory — observational learning, modelling, self-efficacy; four conditions for observational learning: attention, retention, reproduction, motivation); Erikson (eight psychosocial stages — Stage 1: Trust vs Mistrust 0–18 months; Stage 2: Autonomy vs Shame 18m–3 years; Stage 3: Initiative vs Guilt 3–5 years; Stage 4: Industry vs Inferiority 6–12 years)
- **EYFS Areas of Learning**: Prime areas: Communication and Language (listening, attention and understanding, speaking); Physical Development (gross motor skills, fine motor skills, health and self-care); Personal Social and Emotional Development (self-regulation, managing self, building relationships). Specific areas: Literacy (comprehension, word reading, writing); Mathematics (number, numerical patterns); Understanding the World (past and present, people culture and communities, the natural world); Expressive Arts and Design (creating with materials, being imaginative and expressive)
- **Next Steps**: Must be specific and actionable — state what the practitioner will do, what resources will be provided, what adult interaction will be planned, and what EYFS development statement is being targeted next; cannot be generic ("continue to support the child")
- **Harvard Referencing in Observations**: theorists cited in the analysis section must follow Harvard format — in-text: (Vygotsky, 1978: 86) for direct quotes, (Vygotsky, 1978) for paraphrase; reference list entry for a book: Vygotsky, L.S. (1978) *Mind in Society: The Development of Higher Psychological Processes*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press.

**Values per Attribute**:
- Piaget schema theory: schema = a mental framework for understanding the world; assimilation = applying existing schema to new experience; accommodation = modifying schema when new experience does not fit; equilibration = the drive to balance assimilation and accommodation
- Vygotsky ZPD: the ZPD is not a learning style — it is a measurable gap between independent performance and supported performance; scaffolding must be withdrawn progressively
- Bowlby attachment types: secure (child explores from a secure base — caregiver is responsive); anxious-avoidant (child suppresses attachment needs — caregiver is consistently unresponsive); anxious-resistant (child shows heightened distress — caregiver is inconsistently responsive); disorganised (child shows no consistent strategy — caregiver is source of fear)

#### H2: What a CACHE Observation Record Is and Why It Is Required
> **EAV to cover**: Observation record entity — attributes: purpose in CACHE portfolio, required components, when it is assessed, difference from reflective account and personal statement, portfolio mapping to assessment criteria
> **Word count**: 280–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Purpose: to demonstrate the candidate's ability to observe a child's activity, record it objectively, analyse the developmental significance using theory, connect it to the EYFS statutory framework, and plan appropriate next steps
> - Required in: NCFE CACHE Level 3 Diploma for the Early Years Workforce (Early Years Educator); CACHE Level 2 Early Years Practice; may also be required in other CACHE qualifications involving direct childcare practice
> - Components: (1) Observation details — child's age (not name — use initials or pseudonym for data protection), date, setting, context; (2) Objective observation — what was seen and heard; (3) Analysis — development interpretation using named theory; (4) EYFS link — area and development statement; (5) Next steps — planned adult response
> - How it differs from a reflective account: an observation record is about the child's learning; a reflective account is about the practitioner's own practice — they require different structures and different evidence
> - Assessment criteria: observation records are mapped to units covering child development, observation and assessment, and planning and implementation — specific AC codes vary by NCFE CACHE unit

#### H2: CACHE Observation Methods: Which Type to Use and When
> **EAV to cover**: Observation method entity — attributes: method name, description, purpose, appropriate use context, advantages, limitations for CACHE portfolios
> **Word count**: 400–480 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Narrative (running record): continuous written description in real time; captures the full sequence of a child's activity; best used when you want to observe the full context of a behaviour or interaction; most commonly required for CACHE portfolio observations; requires the practitioner to write continuously without pausing — record what is seen and heard moment by moment
> - Time sampling: observation recorded at pre-set timed intervals (e.g., every 5 minutes for 30 minutes); captures the pattern of behaviour across a time period rather than a single episode; best used to assess concentration, social engagement, or activity type across a session; produces quantifiable data (e.g., "in 6 out of 10 intervals, the child was engaged in solitary play")
> - Event sampling: a record is made each time a target behaviour occurs — regardless of time; captures frequency and contextual triggers; best used when a specific behaviour is being monitored (e.g., instances of aggression, instances of sustained shared thinking); antecedent-behaviour-consequence (ABC) format commonly used
> - Anecdotal record: brief written account of a significant event noticed after it occurs; less structured than a running record; used to capture incidental but meaningful behaviour that was not planned for observation; written up as soon as possible after the event
> - Sociogram: maps the social relationships and interaction patterns of a child or group — who initiates contact with whom, who is isolated, who is socially central; drawn as a diagram with directional arrows; useful for identifying peer relationships and social development stage
> - Child tracker: maps physical movement around the learning environment using a floor plan; tracks where the child spends time and which areas are avoided or preferred; used to analyse the child's use of the learning environment and to inform planning

#### H2: How to Write an Objective CACHE Observation: Language, Structure, and What to Avoid
> **EAV to cover**: Objective description entity — attributes: language standards (observable language), description structure, prohibited interpretations in observation section, tense, person, level of detail required
> **Word count**: 320–380 words
> **Specific content**:
> - The observation section must describe only what can be directly seen or heard — no inference about internal state, intention, or emotion in the observation section (these belong in the analysis section)
> - Correct: "Amara (2 years 4 months) picked up the red block using a palmer grasp and placed it on top of the tower. She then placed another block. When the tower fell, she said 'uh oh' and began again."
> - Incorrect: "Amara enjoyed playing with the blocks and felt frustrated when they fell down. She showed perseverance by starting again." — "enjoyed," "felt frustrated," and "showed perseverance" are interpretations, not observations
> - Tense: narrative observations are written in present tense during recording ("Amara picks up the block..."); some centres accept past tense in final written-up versions — follow centre guidance
> - Level of detail: include precise descriptions of grip type (palmer, pincer, tripod), body position, language used (exact words in quotation marks if possible), other children or adults present and their response, materials used and how they are used
> - Data protection: use initials, pseudonym, or code (e.g., "Child A") — never full name; do not include identifying photographs unless centre has explicit consent process
> - Common errors: writing the analysis into the observation section; using evaluative language in the description; failing to note the child's exact age (months matter at early years stage — "2 years 4 months" not "around 2")

#### H2: Linking CACHE Observations to Child Development Theory
> **EAV to cover**: Child development theory entity — attributes per theorist: name, year of key work, specific concept applied to observation, how to cite, how to connect to observed behaviour
> **Word count**: 420–500 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Piaget (1936, 1952): schema theory — when a child uses a new object in an existing way (e.g., banging a spoon like a hammer), this demonstrates assimilation; when the child modifies behaviour for a new object, this demonstrates accommodation; four stages determine which cognitive capabilities can be expected — a child at 2 years 4 months is in the Preoperational stage (2–7 years), so symbolic play and egocentric thinking are developmentally expected
> - Vygotsky (1978): Zone of Proximal Development — if a child can build a 4-block tower independently but builds a 7-block tower when a practitioner demonstrates, the ZPD for tower building spans from 4 to 7 blocks; scaffolding is the practitioner support that enables performance within the ZPD; reference: Vygotsky, L.S. (1978) *Mind in Society*. Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press
> - Bowlby (1969, 1973): attachment type informs interpretation of separation behaviour — a child who explores freely from a base and returns periodically demonstrates secure attachment (Ainsworth Strange Situation, 1970); a child who shows no distress on separation and ignores return may demonstrate anxious-avoidant attachment; reference: Bowlby, J. (1969) *Attachment and Loss, Volume 1: Attachment*. London: Hogarth Press
> - Bruner (1966): enactive, iconic, symbolic representation modes — a child using physical manipulation of objects is in the enactive mode; a child using images or mental images is in the iconic mode; a child using language or symbols is in the symbolic mode; the practitioner's scaffolding (verbal prompts, modelling, questioning) facilitates movement between modes
> - Bandura (1977): social learning and modelling — when a child reproduces behaviour observed in another child or adult, this demonstrates observational learning; the four conditions — attention (was the child watching?), retention (can the child remember?), reproduction (does the child have the motor skill?), motivation (is there reinforcement?) — should each be briefly addressed when applying Bandura
> - Citation instruction: always cite the specific theorist and year in-text in the analysis section; always include the full reference in the reference list at the end of the portfolio unit; do not write "theorists suggest that..." without naming the theorist

#### H2: Identifying EYFS Areas of Learning in a CACHE Observation
> **EAV to cover**: EYFS entity — attributes: seven areas (three prime, four specific), development statements by age, how to match observed behaviour to the correct area and statement, statutory framework reference
> **Word count**: 360–420 words
> **Specific content**:
> - EYFS statutory framework 2021 (updated from 2012): seven areas of learning — three prime areas (Communication and Language; Physical Development; Personal Social and Emotional Development) and four specific areas (Literacy; Mathematics; Understanding the World; Expressive Arts and Design)
> - Prime areas are fundamental: learning in the specific areas depends on strong foundation in the three prime areas — candidates must understand this hierarchy when linking observations to development
> - Communication and Language: covers listening, attention and understanding, and speaking — observed language, comprehension of instructions, narrative speech, questioning; development statements include: "Uses language to imagine and recreate roles and experiences in play situations" (typical 3–4 years)
> - Physical Development: covers gross motor skills (running, jumping, climbing, balance), fine motor skills (grip types — palmer, pincer, tripod; cutting, mark-making), and health and self-care; development statements include: "Uses a comfortable grip with good control when holding pens and pencils" (typical 3–4 years)
> - Personal Social and Emotional Development: covers self-regulation (managing impulses and emotions), managing self (independence in self-care, following rules), and building relationships (friendships, empathy, cooperative play)
> - Literacy: comprehension (listening to stories, understanding what has been read) and word reading (phonological awareness, blending) and writing (mark-making, letter formation, purposeful writing)
> - Mathematics: number (counting, number recognition, simple operations) and numerical patterns (patterns, shape, space, measure)
> - Candidate instruction: identify the primary EYFS area — the one most directly demonstrated by the observed behaviour — then note any secondary areas evident; cite the specific development statement from the EYFS framework (2021 version) that matches the child's observed behaviour; state whether the child is meeting, exceeding, or emerging toward the expected development for their age

#### H2: How to Write Next Steps for Learning in a CACHE Observation Record
> **EAV to cover**: Next steps entity — attributes: specificity requirement, practitioner action, resource planning, EYFS targeting, connection to planning cycle (observation — assessment — planning)
> **Word count**: 280–340 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Next steps are not a summary of what the child has achieved — they are a forward-looking plan for what the practitioner will do to extend or support development
> - Structure of an effective next steps section: (1) what development area needs to be supported or extended (cite the EYFS area and development statement); (2) what the practitioner will do (specific adult action — e.g., "introduce counting songs during group time, model one-to-one correspondence during snack time"); (3) what resources or environments will be prepared (e.g., "set up a number line in the mark-making area, add numeral tiles to the sand tray"); (4) who else is involved (key person, parent partnership, SENCO if relevant)
> - Connection to planning cycle: observation feeds into assessment (what is the child's current level?), which feeds into planning (what does the child need next?), which informs the next observation — this cycle is central to EYFS practice and should be referenced in the next steps section
> - Parent partnership: EYFS 2021 requires practitioners to engage parents in their child's learning — next steps may include sharing the observation finding with parents and co-planning home-based activities
> - SENCO referral: if observation reveals development significantly below expected level for age, next steps must include flagging to the Setting SENCO and considering Early Help Assessment — candidates should be aware of this pathway
> - What to avoid: "continue to support and encourage the child" is not a next step — it is a non-specific statement that demonstrates no planning ability and will not meet assessment criteria

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is the difference between a narrative observation and a time sampling observation in CACHE?**
A narrative observation (also called a running record) is a continuous written description of a child's activity in real time, capturing the full sequence of behaviour. A time sampling observation records a snapshot of behaviour at pre-set intervals — for example, every five minutes — to capture patterns across a session. Narrative observations are most commonly required for CACHE portfolio evidence; time sampling is used when the pattern of behaviour over time is more informative than a single episode.

**Q: How do I link my CACHE observation to child development theory?**
Identify the specific developmental behaviour observed — for example, a child using trial and error to fit shapes into a shape sorter. Name the theorist whose framework explains this behaviour — in this case, Piaget's schema theory, specifically the process of accommodation (Piaget, 1952), in which the child modifies an existing schema when a new experience does not fit. State the concept precisely, apply it to the observed behaviour, and cite the theorist using Harvard referencing in the analysis section.

**Q: How do I identify the EYFS area of learning from a CACHE observation?**
Read the observation and identify the primary skill or behaviour demonstrated — for example, the child counting five objects correctly. Match this to the EYFS framework (2021): Mathematics — specifically the Number sub-area. Then locate the development statement that best describes the observed behaviour within the expected age range. Cite the EYFS Statutory Framework 2021 (Department for Education) in the reference list.

**Q: What should next steps include in a CACHE observation record?**
Next steps must state: (1) the EYFS area and development statement being targeted; (2) the specific adult action that will be taken to support or extend the child's development; (3) the resources or environment changes that will be prepared; and (4) any additional professionals or parents to be involved. A statement such as "continue to encourage the child" does not meet assessment criteria — next steps must be specific, planned, and linked to the EYFS framework.
