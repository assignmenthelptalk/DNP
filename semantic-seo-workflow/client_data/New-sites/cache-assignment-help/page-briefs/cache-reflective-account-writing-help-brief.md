**Derived Attributes**:
- **Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988)**: Six stages — (1) Description: factual account of what happened — who, what, when, where; no evaluation or interpretation; (2) Feelings: what were you thinking and feeling before, during, and after the event?; (3) Evaluation: what was good and bad about the experience?; (4) Analysis: what sense can you make of the situation? — this is where theory and legislation must be applied; (5) Conclusion: what else could you have done? What would you do differently?; (6) Action Plan: if the situation arose again, what would you do? What CPD action will you take? Reference: Gibbs, G. (1988) *Learning by Doing: A Guide to Teaching and Learning Methods*. Oxford: Further Education Unit, Oxford Polytechnic.
- **Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (1984)**: Four stages — (1) Concrete Experience: the specific event or activity that is reflected upon; (2) Reflective Observation: what did you notice? What stood out? What went well and what did not?; (3) Abstract Conceptualisation: what theory or principle explains what happened? — theory and legislation applied here; (4) Active Experimentation: how will you apply this learning in practice? What will you do differently? Reference: Kolb, D.A. (1984) *Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development*. Englewood Cliffs, NJ: Prentice Hall.
- **Schon's Reflection (1983)**: Two concepts — Reflection-in-Action: the practitioner adjusts their approach in the moment, during the experience, without stopping to consciously analyse — the "thinking on your feet" process; Reflection-on-Action: the practitioner reviews and analyses what happened after the experience — this is the type of reflection produced in written reflective accounts; Reference: Schon, D.A. (1983) *The Reflective Practitioner: How Professionals Think in Action*. New York: Basic Books.
- **Theory Integration in Analysis Stage**: child development theories applied — Piaget (cognitive stage matching expected behaviour), Vygotsky (ZPD and scaffolding applied to interaction with a child), Bowlby (attachment behaviour observed), Bandura (modelling and self-efficacy); legislation applied — EYFS 2021 (statutory framework), Children Act 1989 and 2004, Care Act 2014, Mental Capacity Act 2005, Equality Act 2010 — selected based on which unit the reflective account is submitted for

- **Harvard Referencing in Reflective Accounts**: all theorists and legislation cited in the analysis stage require Harvard in-text citations and a full reference list at the end of the portfolio unit; format for book: Author Surname, Initial. (Year) *Title*. Place: Publisher; format for statutory document: Department for Education (2021) *Statutory Framework for the Early Years Foundation Stage*. London: DfE.

#### H2: What a Reflective Account Is in CACHE Assessments
> **EAV to cover**: Reflective account entity — attributes: definition, purpose in CACHE portfolio, structural requirements, how it differs from personal statement and observation record, assessment criteria function, level of analytical demand
> **Word count**: 280–340 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Definition: a structured written account in which the candidate reflects on a specific professional situation or experience using a named reflective model, evaluates what happened, connects it to theory and/or legislation, and identifies future actions and CPD
> - Purpose: to demonstrate that the candidate can learn from experience, apply theoretical knowledge to practice, and plan improvements — not to describe what happened
> - How it differs from a personal statement: a personal statement describes what the candidate knows, understands, or can do — it is descriptive; a reflective account uses a specific structure to analyse a real experience — it is evaluative and analytical
> - How it differs from an observation record: an observation record focuses on the child's learning and development; a reflective account focuses on the practitioner's own practice, responses, and professional development
> - Assessment criteria: reflective accounts are typically mapped to units requiring the candidate to demonstrate reflective practice, professional development awareness, or application of values and legislation to practice — specific AC codes vary by NCFE CACHE unit and level
> - Level of analytical demand: increases with qualification level — at Level 2, basic reflection with guided prompts is acceptable; at Level 3, a named model must be applied and theory/legislation must be referenced; at Level 4 and 5, critical evaluation and engagement with research evidence is expected

#### H2: Gibbs' Reflective Cycle Applied to CACHE Assessments
> **EAV to cover**: Gibbs 1988 entity — attributes: all six stages (name, description, function in CACHE context, example content for a childcare practitioner scenario)
> **Word count**: 480–560 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Reference: Gibbs, G. (1988) *Learning by Doing: A Guide to Teaching and Learning Methods*. Oxford: Further Education Unit, Oxford Polytechnic.
> - Stage 1 — Description: factual account of what happened; include setting, date, who was present, what occurred; do not evaluate or interpret; example: "During a free play session in the nursery room, a 3-year-old child (Child A) became distressed when another child took the toy she was using. I intervened by..."
> - Stage 2 — Feelings: what was the candidate thinking and feeling before, during, and after the situation? Acknowledge uncertainty, anxiety, or confidence — assessors value honesty; example: "I felt uncertain about how to respond without escalating the situation. I was concerned that my intervention might make Child A feel unsupported."
> - Stage 3 — Evaluation: what went well? What did not go well? Both must be addressed — a purely positive evaluation signals lack of critical thinking; example: "What went well: Child A was reassured and the interaction ended positively. What did not go well: I did not have a strategy prepared for this type of conflict and my initial response was hesitant."
> - Stage 4 — Analysis: what sense can you make of the situation? This is the most important stage for CACHE assessment — theory and legislation must be applied here; example: "Vygotsky (1978) identified the Zone of Proximal Development as the gap between what a child can achieve independently and with support. My intervention provided scaffolding — but Bandura (1977) suggests that self-efficacy in emotional regulation develops through modelled behaviour; I could have modelled calm conflict resolution more explicitly."
> - Stage 5 — Conclusion: what else could you have done? What would have been a more effective approach? This stage requires the candidate to identify alternatives — not just validate what they did
> - Stage 6 — Action Plan: what will you do differently if this situation arises again? What CPD will you undertake? Example: "I will read Chapter 7 of 'Understanding Children's Behaviour' (MacLeod-Brudenell, 2007) on conflict resolution strategies. I will discuss conflict management approaches with my mentor at our next supervision session by [specific date]."
> - Instruction: write each stage as a separate, clearly labelled paragraph or section; do not merge stages; assessors check for all six stages individually

#### H2: Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle in CACHE Practice
> **EAV to cover**: Kolb 1984 entity — attributes: all four stages (name, description, function in CACHE context, childcare application example), comparison to Gibbs structure
> **Word count**: 360–420 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Reference: Kolb, D.A. (1984) *Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development*. Englewood Cliffs, NJ: Prentice Hall.
> - Stage 1 — Concrete Experience: the specific event being reflected upon — parallel to Gibbs' Description stage; the candidate describes the experience they are learning from; example: "During a planning session, I led a small group story activity for four children aged 4–5 years."
> - Stage 2 — Reflective Observation: what did the candidate notice? What stood out? What went well and what did not? — parallel to Gibbs' Evaluation stage; "Three children were engaged throughout. One child became distracted after approximately 5 minutes. I noticed that my pace may have been too slow for the group's attention span."
> - Stage 3 — Abstract Conceptualisation: what theory or principle explains what happened? — this is the equivalent of Gibbs' Analysis stage and is where theory and legislation must appear; "Piaget (1952) identified that children in the Preoperational stage (2–7 years) have limited attention spans and learn best through active, concrete engagement rather than passive listening. The Specific Areas of the EYFS (Communication and Language — Listening, Attention and Understanding) require practitioners to adapt interactions to maintain engagement."
> - Stage 4 — Active Experimentation: what will the candidate do differently? How will the learning be tested in practice? — parallel to Gibbs' Action Plan; "In the next story session, I will use props and interactive questioning to sustain engagement. I will observe whether this reduces distraction and will use event sampling to record attention behaviour."
> - Key difference from Gibbs: Kolb's model does not include an explicit "Feelings" stage — it is more cognitive and action-focused; Gibbs is more commonly preferred for CACHE because the Feelings stage aligns with CACHE's emphasis on personal and professional development
> - When to use Kolb: appropriate when the candidate wants to focus on planning and testing a change in practice rather than on the emotional and personal dimension of an experience

#### H2: Schon's Reflection-in-Action and Reflection-on-Action for Childcare Practitioners
> **EAV to cover**: Schon 1983 entity — attributes: Reflection-in-Action definition and childcare application, Reflection-on-Action definition and how it produces written reflective accounts, distinction between the two concepts, how Schon is cited in CACHE portfolios
> **Word count**: 300–360 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Reference: Schon, D.A. (1983) *The Reflective Practitioner: How Professionals Think in Action*. New York: Basic Books.
> - Reflection-in-Action: the practitioner adjusts their approach in the moment, during the experience, without stopping to consciously analyse — described by Schon as "thinking on your feet"; a practitioner who notices a child is losing interest mid-activity and immediately changes their approach (changes tone, introduces a prop, asks a direct question) is demonstrating Reflection-in-Action; this cannot be written in real time but can be retrospectively described in a reflective account
> - Reflection-on-Action: the practitioner reviews, analyses, and evaluates what happened after the experience has ended — this is the type of reflection that produces written reflective accounts; Schon argues that practitioners become more effective when they systematically reflect on past actions to inform future practice
> - How to use Schon in a CACHE reflective account: in the analysis stage of a Gibbs or Kolb-structured account, candidates can reference Schon (1983) to explain the value of structured reflection itself — "Schon (1983) argued that professional effectiveness depends on the practitioner's capacity to reflect on action, allowing experience to become a source of learning rather than repetition"
> - When to use Schon: most appropriate for Level 3 and above; candidates can acknowledge that they reflected in the moment (Reflection-in-Action) and are now reflecting after the event (Reflection-on-Action) — this demonstrates awareness of both types and is valued by assessors
> - Schon is commonly used alongside Gibbs or Kolb — not instead of them; a reflective account structured around Schon alone would lack the stage-by-stage framework that assessors expect

#### H2: How to Link Theory and Legislation to Your CACHE Reflective Account
> **EAV to cover**: Theory-legislation integration entity — attributes: which stage of the model to apply theory (analysis/abstract conceptualisation), how to connect theory to the practitioner's specific experience (not generic), which legislation applies to which CACHE route, citation format, depth requirement
> **Word count**: 360–420 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Theory and legislation must be applied in the Analysis stage (Gibbs) or Abstract Conceptualisation stage (Kolb) — not scattered throughout the account
> - Generic statements are not sufficient: "According to Vygotsky, children learn through interaction" is not coverage — "Vygotsky (1978) identified that a child's Zone of Proximal Development represents the gap between independent performance and supported performance. In the interaction I described, Child A was unable to resolve the conflict independently but responded to my verbal scaffold — 'What could we do to make sure everyone gets a turn?' — to reach a resolution she could not have achieved alone. This confirms that my intervention was appropriately positioned within her ZPD." — this is coverage
> - Theory selection by CACHE route: Early Years route — Piaget (cognitive development stages), Vygotsky (ZPD and scaffolding), Bowlby (attachment), Bruner (scaffolding and representation modes), Bandura (modelling and self-efficacy); Health and Social Care route — Maslow (hierarchy of needs), Erikson (psychosocial stages), Kübler-Ross (stages of grief, relevant to bereavement support), Bandura (self-efficacy in health behaviour change)
> - Legislation selection by unit: safeguarding units — Children Act 1989 and 2004, Working Together 2018 (EY route), Care Act 2014 (HSC Adults route); equality and diversity units — Equality Act 2010 (nine protected characteristics); duty of care units — Health and Social Care Act 2008 Regulations 2014; communication units — GDPR and Data Protection Act 2018 for information-sharing context; EYFS units — EYFS Statutory Framework 2021
> - Citation format reminder: in-text — (Vygotsky, 1978: 86) for direct quote; (Vygotsky, 1978) for paraphrase; legislation in-text — (Children Act 2004) or "Under Section 47 of the Children Act 1989..."; full reference list required at the end of the portfolio unit
> - Depth minimum: at least two distinct theoretical or legislative references per reflective account at Level 3; at Level 4 and 5, engagement with secondary academic sources and research studies is expected alongside primary legislation

#### H2: Common Mistakes in CACHE Reflective Accounts and How to Avoid Them
> **EAV to cover**: Failure mode entity — attributes: description without analysis (most common), no theory or legislation in analysis stage, no CPD in action plan, first-person narrative without critical evaluation, vague action plan, all positive evaluation (no identification of what went wrong or what could be improved)
> **Word count**: 320–380 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Mistake 1 — Description without analysis (most common referral reason): writing what happened in narrative form without applying a reflective model, without evaluating, and without connecting to theory — the account reads as a story, not a reflection; fix: use Gibbs' six stages as explicit section headers and ensure the Analysis stage contains named theory with citation
> - Mistake 2 — Theory mentioned without application: writing "Vygotsky believed in the importance of social interaction" and moving on — the theory is named but not applied to the specific situation being reflected upon; fix: connect the specific theoretical concept (ZPD, schema, attachment type) to the specific moment in the described experience
> - Mistake 3 — Legislation listed without application: writing "I followed the Children Act 2004" without explaining which provision was relevant and how it guided the practitioner's response; fix: name the specific section or principle of the legislation and state how it informed the decision made in the experience being reflected upon
> - Mistake 4 — No CPD in the Action Plan: the action plan stage simply states "I will improve my practice" without naming what the candidate will do, read, attend, or observe; fix: name a specific CPD action with a timeframe — a book chapter, a training session, a supervision discussion, a period of observation of a more experienced practitioner
> - Mistake 5 — Entirely positive evaluation: the Evaluation stage states only what went well; assessors expect candidates to identify what did not go well and why — a reflective account with no self-criticism signals a lack of analytical depth
> - Mistake 6 — First-person narrative throughout without stepping back: writing in first person is correct, but the account must include moments of stepping back to evaluate from an external perspective — "On reflection, I can see that..." or "In hindsight, my response was..." signals the shift from description to reflection

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is the difference between a reflective account and a personal statement in a CACHE portfolio?**
A reflective account uses a structured model — such as Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988) — to analyse a specific professional experience, linking it to theory, legislation, and CPD planning. A personal statement describes what the candidate knows, understands, or can do without requiring the evaluative and analytical structure of a reflective model. Both are used as portfolio evidence but are mapped to different assessment criteria and require different writing approaches.

**Q: Which reflective model should I use for my CACHE assignment?**
Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988) is the most commonly used model in CACHE portfolios because its six stages — Description, Feelings, Evaluation, Analysis, Conclusion, and Action Plan — provide a structured framework that maps clearly to NCFE CACHE assessment criteria. Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (1984) is an alternative when the focus is on learning from experience and planning action. Schon (1983) is most effectively used alongside Gibbs or Kolb to explain the value of reflective practice itself. Check with your assessor which model is preferred or whether you have a free choice.

**Q: What goes in the Analysis stage of a Gibbs reflective account for CACHE?**
The Analysis stage requires the candidate to apply named child development theory or health and social care legislation to the specific situation described in the account. Name the theorist, cite the year of their key work, identify the specific concept (not just the general theory), and connect it to a precise moment or decision in the experience being reflected upon. At Level 3, a minimum of two distinct theoretical or legislative references per account is expected. Generic statements without specific application will not meet the assessment criteria.

**Q: Why do I keep getting referred for my CACHE reflective accounts?**
The most common reason for referral is that the account describes what happened without analysing it — the candidate writes a detailed narrative of the experience but does not apply a reflective model, does not evaluate what went well and what did not, and does not include named theory or legislation in an analysis stage. Assessors are specifically checking for the Analysis and Action Plan stages of Gibbs or the equivalent in Kolb. A referral citing "lacks critical reflection" or "needs to link theory to practice" indicates that the Description stage has been completed but the Analysis stage is absent or insufficient.
