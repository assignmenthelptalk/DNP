CACHE Reflective Account Writing: How to Reflect on Practice Using Gibbs, Kolb, and Schon

A CACHE reflective account is a structured piece of portfolio evidence in which a student analyses a specific professional practice episode using a recognised reflective model, applies child development theory or health and social care legislation to the analysis, and produces an evidence-based action plan for changed future practice. It is not a description of what happened. The most common cause of referral in CACHE reflective accounts is producing detailed descriptive narrative — an accurate, clearly written account of an event — without performing the analysis that the reflective model requires. Length and descriptive accuracy do not compensate for missing theoretical analysis. This page explains what a CACHE reflective account requires structurally and academically, how to apply Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988), Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (1984), and Schon's reflection framework (1983), how to link theory and legislation to the Analysis stage, and the most common mistakes that result in referral.

What a CACHE Reflective Account Is and How It Differs from Other Portfolio Evidence

A reflective account in a CACHE Level 3 portfolio documents a student's capacity for professional self-evaluation — the ability to review their own practice, identify what went well and why, identify what could be improved and how, and commit to specific changes in future practice grounded in theory and evidence. It is a fundamentally different evidence format from an observation record (which focuses on a child's behaviour) and from a written assignment (which develops a theoretical argument in response to a set question). The reflective account focuses on the student's own professional actions and their theoretical and legislative basis.

The practice episode that forms the basis of a reflective account must be specific — a real, bounded event from placement or employment with a defined beginning and end. "Reflecting on my safeguarding practice generally" is not a reflective account in the CACHE sense; "reflecting on my response when a child disclosed that their parent hit them during my placement on 14 March" is. The specificity of the incident determines the depth of analysis possible — a vague, composite account of general practice cannot generate the theoretically grounded, criterion-specific analysis that Level 3 reflective accounts require.

At Level 3, reflective accounts must include Harvard references. Every theorist, framework, or piece of legislation applied in the Analysis stage requires an in-text citation in the format (Author, Year) or, for legislation, the Act name and year. A reflective account about a communication challenge with a non-verbal child must cite Vygotsky (1978) when discussing scaffolded interaction, Bowlby (1969) when discussing attachment security as a prerequisite for communication, and relevant EYFS (2021) statutory guidance on Communication and Language. Missing citations in the Analysis stage will result in referral on the referencing criterion regardless of the quality of the analysis itself.

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle 1988: All Six Stages Applied to Early Years and Health and Social Care Practice

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (1988) is the most widely used reflective model in CACHE Level 3 portfolios. It was developed by Graham Gibbs in his 1988 publication "Learning by Doing: A Guide to Teaching and Learning Methods" as a framework for structured reflection on experience. The model has six stages: Description, Feelings, Evaluation, Analysis, Conclusion, and Action Plan. All six stages must be identifiable in a reflective account that uses this model, either through explicit subheadings or through clearly stage-by-stage progression. Accounts that merge stages — combining Evaluation and Analysis into a single section, or omitting Feelings because they feel "unprofessional" — will be referred on the structural criterion for Gibbs' application.

The Description stage records what happened — the specific practice episode, its context, the people involved, and the sequence of events. This stage should be concise: three to five sentences for most accounts. Its purpose is to orient the reader, not to provide a detailed narrative. Common error: writing the entire account at the description stage and treating subsequent stages as brief add-ons. The Description stage is the foundation, not the main content.

The Feelings stage documents the student's emotional response during and immediately after the event. This stage is often undervalued by students who feel that emotions are not relevant to professional assessment. But Gibbs (1988) includes Feelings as a distinct stage because unexplored emotional responses bias the evaluation that follows — a student who felt anxious during a safeguarding disclosure may evaluate the episode differently from a student who felt confident, and understanding that emotional response is part of reflective awareness. The Feelings stage should be authentic and specific: "I felt uncertain about whether I was following the correct procedure" is more reflective than "I felt nervous." In childcare accounts, the Feelings stage might also note the child's observable emotional response as context, though this remains a description rather than a psychological interpretation.

The Evaluation stage assesses what went well and what did not go well in the episode. It requires the student to make explicit judgements rather than neutral descriptions. This stage should identify specific strengths in the practice response — "the verbal reassurance I provided aligned with the attachment theory principle of providing a secure base" — and specific areas for development — "I did not immediately inform the Designated Safeguarding Lead and instead attempted to gather more information from the child, which is contrary to the safeguarding procedure under Working Together to Safeguard Children (2018)." Evaluation must be balanced and honest; assessors at Distinction level expect students to identify genuine areas for improvement, not to write entirely self-congratulatory accounts.

The Analysis stage is the most academically demanding stage and should be the longest section of the reflective account. It requires the student to make sense of the episode by drawing on theory, legislation, and professional standards to explain why things happened as they did and what the correct professional response was or should have been. In an Early Years account, the Analysis stage might apply Bowlby's (1969) Attachment Theory to explain why a child responded to a transition as they did, cite the EYFS (2021) statutory requirement for key person relationships, and reference the setting's transition policy. In a Health and Social Care account, the Analysis stage might apply the Mental Capacity Act 2005 five statutory principles to a decision-making situation, cite the Care Act 2014 wellbeing principle, and reference the duty of care and person-centred values framework. The Analysis stage must cite every theoretical and legislative reference in Harvard format.

The Conclusion stage identifies what else could have been done differently and what has been learned from the episode. It is a synthesis stage that draws on the Analysis to identify the gap between practice as it occurred and practice as it should have been according to theory and legislation. The Conclusion should not introduce new theoretical content — all theory and legislation should have been applied in the Analysis. Common error at this stage: repeating the Evaluation or using generic language such as "I learned a lot from this experience." The Conclusion must be specific about what changed in understanding as a result of the analytical process.

The Action Plan stage specifies what the student will do differently in future practice, when, and how they will know they have achieved it. Action Plan statements must be specific and measurable, not generic commitments to improvement. Example for an Early Years account: "Within the next two weeks, I will review the setting's safeguarding policy and the Working Together to Safeguard Children (2018) statutory guidance on the role of the Designated Safeguarding Lead. I will arrange a meeting with the DSL to clarify the correct disclosure procedure and will complete the setting's internal safeguarding refresher training before 31 May. I will assess my competence by practising the disclosure response procedure in the next supervision session." Generic action plans — "I will improve my communication skills" or "I will continue to develop professionally" — do not meet the Distinction criterion for the Action Plan stage.

Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle 1984: Four Stages in Childcare and Health and Social Care Reflection

Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (Kolb, 1984) is an alternative or complementary reflective framework to Gibbs (1988). It was developed by David Kolb in "Experiential Learning: Experience as the Source of Learning and Development" (1984) and proposes that effective learning from experience follows four stages: Concrete Experience, Reflective Observation, Abstract Conceptualisation, and Active Experimentation. The cycle is iterative — the outcome of Active Experimentation becomes the next Concrete Experience, generating continuous professional development.

The Concrete Experience stage corresponds to the practice episode — what happened, the specific event that is being reflected upon. In CACHE assignments, this is equivalent to the Description stage in Gibbs. The Reflective Observation stage involves stepping back from the experience and observing it from multiple perspectives — considering how the situation appeared to the service user or child, to a more experienced colleague, and to the student. This multi-perspective observation enriches the subsequent analytical stage and distinguishes Kolb's framework from purely self-focused reflection. The Abstract Conceptualisation stage applies theoretical frameworks and conceptual understanding to the experience to make sense of it — this is the equivalent of Gibbs' Analysis stage and requires the same level of theoretical referencing. The Active Experimentation stage plans how the learning derived from the conceptual analysis will be applied in future practice — equivalent to Gibbs' Action Plan.

Kolb's (1984) framework is often used in CACHE units that focus on professional development and CPD because the iterative cycle model maps more naturally to the continuous learning process of professional practice. When using Kolb in a reflective account, the student should cite (Kolb, 1984) at the point where the framework is first introduced and should structure the account visibly around the four stages, either with subheadings or with explicit stage-labelling in the text.

Schon's Reflection-in-Action and Reflection-on-Action 1983: Application to CACHE Assignments

Donald Schon's (1983) framework from "The Reflective Practitioner: How Professionals Think in Action" introduces two concepts of professional reflection: Reflection-in-Action and Reflection-on-Action. These are not a cyclical process model in the same way as Gibbs or Kolb — they describe two types of reflective thinking that occur at different points in relation to a practice episode.

Reflection-in-Action (Schon, 1983) is thinking that occurs during the practice episode itself — adjusting approach in response to what is happening in real time, without stopping to follow a formal reflective process. A practitioner who modifies their communication approach mid-interaction because a service user is becoming distressed, without leaving the room to consult a model, is engaging in Reflection-in-Action. This type of reflection is characteristic of experienced practitioners and is associated with what Schon calls the "swampy lowland" of complex, real-world professional situations that do not fit theoretical models cleanly.

Reflection-on-Action (Schon, 1983) is the formal reflective process that occurs after the practice episode — reviewing what happened, why, and what could be done differently. This is the type of reflection that structured models like Gibbs (1988) and Kolb (1984) formalise. All structured CACHE reflective accounts are, by definition, examples of Reflection-on-Action. However, a student can demonstrate evidence of Reflection-in-Action in the Description stage of a reflective account by describing adjustments they made to their approach during the episode — and then reflect on those in-the-moment decisions using the analytical framework.

For CACHE assignments that cover professional practice and CPD, Schon's (1983) framework is particularly useful for the Analysis stage because it provides a vocabulary for distinguishing between responsive in-the-moment professional judgement (Reflection-in-Action) and structured post-hoc analysis (Reflection-on-Action). The correct citation is (Schon, 1983) — note that some sources spell the name Schön with an umlaut; both are acceptable in academic referencing as long as the spelling is consistent throughout the assignment.

Linking Theory and Legislation to the Analysis Stage of a Reflective Account

The Analysis stage of a CACHE reflective account must connect the specific practice episode to named theoretical frameworks and relevant legislation, with Harvard citations. The connection must be specific — not "Vygotsky's theory is relevant here" but "Vygotsky's (1978) concept of the Zone of Proximal Development explains why C.'s inability to complete the puzzle independently does not indicate cognitive limitation — the scaffolded interaction I provided moved the task into her ZPD, enabling completion with support." This specificity demonstrates that the student understands the theory and can apply it to a concrete situation, not merely that they know the theory exists.

For Early Years reflective accounts, the primary theoretical sources for the Analysis stage are the eight child development theorists at Level 3 (Piaget 1952, Vygotsky 1978, Bowlby 1969, Bruner 1966, Bronfenbrenner 1979, Erikson 1950, Skinner 1938, Bandura 1977) and the EYFS 2021 statutory framework. The theorist selected must be genuinely applicable to the episode — do not apply Bronfenbrenner's (1979) ecological systems model to a single indoor play interaction unless the analysis explicitly considers the wider family and cultural context that the observation of the interaction reveals. Applying theory inappropriately — using a framework because it was covered in class rather than because it applies — is detectable by assessors and will not meet the analytical criterion.

For Health and Social Care reflective accounts, the primary legislative sources are the Care Act 2014, Mental Capacity Act 2005, Equality Act 2010, and Human Rights Act 1998, alongside professional practice standards such as the Care Certificate (for care workers) and the Code of Conduct for Healthcare Support Workers and Adult Social Care Workers in England. The analysis must apply the legislative provision to the specific decision or action described in the account. If the episode involved a conflict between a service user's choice and a perceived risk of harm, the Analysis stage must apply both the MCA 2005 best interests framework and the duty of care principle, explaining how they interact in the specific scenario. Citing one without acknowledging the tension with the other is an incomplete analysis.

At Distinction level, the Analysis stage must also demonstrate critical thinking — acknowledging limitations or contradictions within the theoretical or legislative framework applied. For example: "While Bowlby's (1969) secure base theory explains C.'s separation response, Bronfenbrenner's (1979) ecological systems model adds the additional analytical dimension that C.'s home microsystem context — including the recent family change identified in the key person meeting — may have increased the intensity of the attachment behaviour observed, in a way that Bowlby's dyadic framework alone does not account for." This kind of multi-framework analysis, where two theories are placed in dialogue to provide a richer explanation, characterises Distinction-quality analytical writing across both CACHE routes.

Common Mistakes in CACHE Reflective Accounts and How to Avoid Them

The most common mistake in CACHE reflective accounts is writing the account as a narrative of events — a detailed, well-written description of what happened — without performing the Analysis stage adequately. Students who are experienced practitioners often produce the most detailed descriptive accounts because they have rich professional experience to draw on. But assessment criteria at Level 3 are not rewarding the richness of the experience; they are rewarding the depth of the analysis. A shorter, less detailed description of a simpler episode, analysed with precision using correctly cited theory and legislation, will achieve Distinction more reliably than a vivid account of a complex incident with superficial or absent theoretical analysis.

The second most common mistake is treating all six Gibbs stages as roughly equal in length. The Feelings stage should be concise — two to four sentences; the Description stage similarly so. The Analysis stage should be the longest, most detailed, and most academically referenced section. A reflective account in which the Description runs to five paragraphs and the Analysis runs to one paragraph has inverted the required proportion and will not meet the analytical criteria at Merit or Distinction level.

The third common mistake is using generic Action Plans. Statements such as "I will try to be more patient with children" or "I will improve my communication skills" are not action plans in the assessment sense — they are aspirations without specificity, measurability, or evidence basis. An action plan must specify what will be done, by when, how, and how success will be recognised. It must derive from the Analysis stage — the specific gap between current practice and the legislatively or theoretically defined standard must be explicit, and the action plan must address that specific gap.

The fourth common mistake is writing in the third person or in a detached, impersonal academic register. Reflective accounts must be written in the first person because they are accounts of the student's own experience and professional response. Using the third person — "the practitioner observed" instead of "I observed" — removes the personal accountability that reflective practice requires and will be identified by assessors as inappropriate for the reflective account format. The analytical sections should use the academic conventions of the third person where citing theory: "Vygotsky (1978) proposed..." but the description, feelings, evaluation, conclusion, and action plan sections must use first person throughout.

How Reflective Accounts Fit Within the Portfolio Evidence Map and Support Continuous Professional Development

Reflective accounts serve a dual purpose in the CACHE Level 3 portfolio: they are assessed evidence that meets specific criteria across multiple units, and they are the practical vehicle through which the student develops as a reflective practitioner — a skill that the DfE and Ofsted both identify as a quality indicator in Early Years practice, and that the Care Quality Commission identifies as a professional standard in health and social care. A portfolio that contains strong reflective accounts demonstrates not only academic competence but also the professional disposition to learn from experience systematically rather than reactively.

Cross-referencing reflective accounts to observation records within the portfolio mapping document creates a richer evidence base. An observation identifies a child's developmental need; a related reflective account analyses the student's professional response to that need; the portfolio mapping document shows the assessor how the two pieces of evidence together build a complete picture of the student's professional practice cycle — observe, analyse, reflect, plan, act. Students who approach their portfolios as an integrated evidence system rather than as a collection of separate tasks produce stronger portfolios with fewer referrals and faster sign-off.

Observation Records, CPD Planning, and the Assessment Criteria for Reflective Practice Units

Reflective accounts in CACHE portfolios must be connected to practice observations where the evidence mapping requires it. A reflective account that references an observation should cite the observation by the child's pseudonym and the observation date to enable the assessor to cross-reference within the portfolio. The chain of evidence — observation (what was seen), reflective account (how the student responded professionally), action plan (what will change in practice) — must be traceable through the portfolio mapping document.

Reflective accounts can also evidence CPD activities rather than direct practice episodes. A reflection on attending a safeguarding training session, completing the autism awareness e-learning module, or receiving feedback in supervision are all valid subjects for reflective accounts in CPD units. The same structural requirements apply — Gibbs' (1988) or Kolb's (1984) framework, theoretical and legislative references in the Analysis stage, and a specific, measurable Action Plan. The evidence gathered from a CPD reflection should show how the training has changed, or will change, specific aspects of practice — not simply that the training was interesting or informative.

For students who find the Analysis stage challenging, a useful technique is to identify the theoretical framework most relevant to the episode before beginning to write. If the episode involves a child's response to transition, identify Bowlby (1969) and the EYFS key person guidance before writing. If the episode involves a vulnerable adult's choice, identify the MCA 2005 five principles before writing. Having the framework and citation ready before drafting the Analysis stage ensures that the analysis is theory-driven rather than being retrofitted with theory after the description has already been written.

Which reflective model is most commonly used in CACHE assignments?

Gibbs' Reflective Cycle (Gibbs, 1988) is the most commonly specified reflective model in CACHE Level 3 assignments. It has six clearly defined stages (Description, Feelings, Evaluation, Analysis, Conclusion, Action Plan) that map directly onto the assessment criteria for reflective accounts across both Early Years and Health and Social Care routes. Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (Kolb, 1984) is used in some CPD and professional development units. Schon's (1983) Reflection-in-Action and Reflection-on-Action framework is applied in units focused on professional practice and complex decision-making. Students should check their specific unit brief for the model specified — if no model is specified, Gibbs (1988) is the safest default.

Do I need to reference Gibbs in my reflective account?

Yes — if you state that your account follows Gibbs' Reflective Cycle, you must cite (Gibbs, 1988) in the text where the model is first introduced, and include the full Harvard reference in your reference list. The full reference is: Gibbs, G. (1988) Learning by Doing: A Guide to Teaching and Learning Methods. Oxford: Further Education Unit, Oxford Polytechnic. Failing to cite the model you are using is inconsistent referencing and may be flagged during internal verification.

What is the difference between reflection-in-action and reflection-on-action?

Reflection-in-Action (Schon, 1983) is thinking and adjusting that occurs during the practice episode itself — in real time, without pausing to consult a formal model. Reflection-on-Action (Schon, 1983) is the structured retrospective review of a practice episode after it has occurred — this is the type of reflection formalised by Gibbs (1988) and Kolb (1984). All structured CACHE reflective accounts are examples of Reflection-on-Action. However, students can demonstrate Reflection-in-Action within the Description stage by describing mid-episode adjustments to their approach and then analysing those adjustments in the Analysis stage.

Can one reflective account satisfy multiple assessment criteria?

Yes — a single well-constructed reflective account can be mapped to assessment criteria across multiple units in the CACHE Level 3 portfolio. A reflective account on a safeguarding disclosure might simultaneously evidence criteria in the safeguarding unit, the professional values unit, and the reflective practice unit. This cross-referencing must be recorded in the portfolio mapping document — the assessor needs to be able to see which evidence is claimed against which criteria. Overclaiming (mapping evidence to criteria it does not meet) will be identified during internal verification; but correctly mapping rich evidence to all relevant criteria it genuinely meets is both permitted and recommended.
