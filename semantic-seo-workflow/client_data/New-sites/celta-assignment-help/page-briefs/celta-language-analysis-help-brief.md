## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: CELTA language analysis — FMU+P framework
**Main Attributes**: form analysis, meaning analysis, use analysis, phonology analysis, CCQs, anticipated problems, language item selection
**Derived Attributes**:
- **Form analysis**: How the language item is constructed grammatically. For grammar: positive/negative/interrogative sentence structures, auxiliary verbs, morphological changes (verb endings, irregular forms), contracted forms. For lexis: word class (noun/verb/adjective/adverb), morphology (prefix/suffix/root), collocations, compound and phrasal forms. Example for "used to + infinitive": [USED TO] + [BASE INFINITIVE]. Positive: "She used to swim every day." Negative: "She didn't use to swim every day." / "She used not to swim every day." (less common). Interrogative: "Did she use to swim every day?" — note: "use to" NOT "used to" in negative and interrogative with auxiliary "did."
- **Meaning analysis**: What the language item communicates. Denotation: core literal meaning. Connotation: associated meanings, emotional tone, register implications. Appropriacy: when is this item appropriate to use? Temporal/aspectual meaning for grammar (e.g., "used to" = habitual past action that no longer happens — contrast with "would" for repeated past actions and simple past for one-off events). Semantic prosody: does this item carry positive or negative associations in typical usage?
- **Use analysis**: When and why speakers use this item. Register (formal/informal/neutral). Frequency (is it common in everyday speech? written more than spoken?). Typical contexts (what situations prompt this language). Contrastive analysis (how does it differ from a similar structure the learner might confuse it with — e.g., "used to" vs "be used to + gerund" vs "get used to + gerund"). Collocations and typical co-text.
- **Phonology analysis**: Word stress (primary ˈ and secondary ˌ stress marked), connected speech features (elision — sounds dropped in natural speech, e.g., "want to" → /wɒnə/; assimilation — sounds change to match adjacent sounds, e.g., "ten boys" → /tem bɔɪz/; linking — final consonant links to initial vowel of next word, e.g., "turn off" → /tɜːnɒf/; weak forms — unstressed function words reduce, e.g., "to" → /tə/ in "want to"), IPA transcription of full item or target sounds.
- **CCQs (Concept Checking Questions)**: Simple questions to check learners understand the MEANING — without using the target language in the question. Rules: use simple, short questions; avoid yes/no questions where possible (binary answers don't reveal understanding depth — use short answer or this/that alternatives); don't use the target language in the CCQ itself; typically 3–4 CCQs per language item. For "used to": "Do I still do this?" (No) → "Did I do this in the past?" (Yes) → "Did I do it regularly, or just once?" (Regularly).
- **Anticipated problems**: Prediction of difficulties learners will have with this item — divided into form problems, meaning problems, and phonology problems. Solutions for each. Form problem example for "used to": learners from languages without an equivalent structure may produce "I was used to go" or "I use to go" — solution: highlight the fixed "used to + base infinitive" form and contrast with "be used to + gerund."

#### H2: Meaning Analysis — What the Language Item Communicates
> **EAV to cover**: Denotation, connotation, temporal/aspectual meaning, contrast with similar structures, appropriacy
> **Word count**: 270–310 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Denotation: the core meaning. "Used to + infinitive" = a habitual or repeated action or state in the past that is no longer true in the present.
> - Temporal/aspectual meaning: past habit or state, no longer occurring. Emphasis on the contrast between past and present — implies a change.
> - Contrast with similar structures: "used to" vs "would" (both for past habits — "would" requires an established past time frame; "used to" can open a new topic); "used to" vs simple past (simple past = one-off event or specific time; "used to" = habit or ongoing state)
> - Connotation: often used with a sense of nostalgia or contrast between past and present — writers use it to signal change.
> - Contrast with false friends: "be used to + gerund" (= be accustomed to, present habit) and "get used to + gerund" (= become accustomed to) — completely different meaning, same spelling base. This is a major source of learner confusion.

---

#### H2: Use Analysis — When and Why Speakers Use This Item
> **EAV to cover**: Register, frequency, typical contexts, collocations, when this form is preferred over alternatives
> **Word count**: 250–290 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Register: neutral to slightly informal. More common in spoken English than formal written English (formal writing prefers "formerly," "in the past," or simple past).
> - Frequency: moderately frequent in spoken English, especially in personal narrative and biographical contexts.
> - Typical contexts: personal stories ("I used to live in Paris"), comparisons of past and present lifestyles, nostalgic reminiscence, describing lapsed habits.
> - When preferred over simple past: when the speaker wants to emphasise that the action was habitual or repeated (not a one-off event) AND that it is no longer true.
> - Collocations: "I used to + everyday verbs" — go, live, work, play, eat, drink. Less common with stative verbs that don't naturally express habit (though possible: "He used to know everyone in the village").

#### H2: Phonology Analysis — Stress, Connected Speech, and IPA
> **EAV to cover**: Word stress, connected speech (elision, assimilation, linking, weak forms), IPA transcription
> **Word count**: 290–330 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Word stress**: In "used to" — primary stress on USED: /ˈjuːst tə/ in natural speech (weak form of "to" = /tə/)
> - **Connected speech — weak form**: "to" reduces to /tə/ in natural speech: "I used to go" → /aɪ ˈjuːstə ɡəʊ/
> - **Connected speech — elision**: The /d/ in "used" is frequently elided in natural speech before "to": "used to" → /juːstə/ (sounds like "yoosta") — this is the most important phonological feature for teaching, as learners often fail to recognise "used to" in listening because the /d/ disappears
> - **Connected speech — linking**: Final consonant of "used" links to "to" — no pause between the words in natural speech
> - **IPA transcription**: Full citation form: /juːzd tuː/. Natural speech: /ˈjuːstə/
> - **Teaching implication**: Learners who learn only the citation form /juːzd tuː/ will struggle to recognise "used to" in authentic listening (films, podcasts, conversations) because native speakers almost never produce the full citation form
> - **Phonological problem to anticipate**: Learners who pronounce each word separately with full stress — "USED TO" — rather than the reduced connected speech form will sound unnatural

#### H2: Writing Concept Checking Questions (CCQs)
> **EAV to cover**: What CCQs are, rules for writing them, worked example with 3–4 CCQs, common CCQ errors
> **Word count**: 280–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - CCQs = Concept Checking Questions. Simple questions to verify learners understand the MEANING of the language item — without using the target language in the question.
> - **Rules**: (1) Do not use the target language in the CCQ — "Does 'used to' mean a habit?" uses the target language; (2) Use simple vocabulary — simpler than the target language itself; (3) Prefer short-answer or this/that alternative questions over pure yes/no — binary answers don't reveal depth of understanding; (4) Write 3–4 CCQs per language item; (5) Each CCQ should check a different aspect of meaning.
> - **CCQ example for "used to + infinitive"** (sentence: "He used to play football every weekend"):
>   - "Does he play football now?" → No.
>   - "Did he play football in the past?" → Yes.
>   - "Did he play football once, or many times?" → Many times / regularly.
>   - "Did something change — is he different now?" → Yes.
> - **Common CCQ errors**: Asking "Do you understand?" (not a CCQ — no information), asking "What does 'used to' mean?" (asks learners to define, not demonstrate understanding), using the target language in the question, writing only one CCQ (insufficient to verify understanding)

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Do I need to cover all four components (Form, Meaning, Use, Phonology) in Assignment 2?**
All four components — Form, Meaning, Use, and Phonology — must be covered in CELTA Assignment 2. Omitting any one component results in an incomplete language analysis and will prevent a Pass grade on that assignment. The depth of each component may vary depending on the language item chosen, but all four must be present.

**Q: How many CCQs should I write for my language item?**
Three to four CCQs per language item is the standard at CELTA level. Each CCQ should check a different aspect of meaning — not four questions asking the same thing in different words. Ensure no CCQ uses the target language in the question itself.

**Q: How do I transcribe phonology if I don't know the IPA?**
The IPA (International Phonetic Alphabet) can be learned systematically using any standard CELTA phonemic chart — the same chart used on the Cambridge ESOL phonemic typewriter or in Scrivener's "Learning Teaching." Candidates need to be able to mark stress (using ˈ for primary stress) and transcribe the most important sounds — particularly connected speech features like elision (sounds that disappear) and weak forms. Full transcription of every word is less important than showing awareness of how the item sounds in natural connected speech.

**Q: What language items are best for CELTA Assignment 2?**
Grammar structures at A2–B2 level work best — items that are teachable, have clear form/meaning/use distinctions, and present identifiable phonological features. Strong choices include: present perfect (have + past participle), second conditional (if + past simple + would + infinitive), modal verbs (can/could/will/would/must/might), used to + infinitive, passive voice. Avoid items that are too simple (present simple — too much to cover), too complex (subjunctive — not core EFL), or too phonologically similar to the target class's L1.
