IOSH Managing Safely Project Help: How to Structure, Write, and Pass the Workplace Risk Assessment Report

The IOSH Managing Safely project is a workplace risk assessment report — not an academic essay. It is completed after the Managing Safely training course and submitted to the candidate's IOSH-approved course provider for marking. It is not submitted to IOSH directly. The project is scored out of 100 against IOSH marking criteria by the provider, with a pass threshold of approximately 60 out of 100. This page covers the complete structure of the project: what each section requires, how the risk matrix works, how to write a compliant action plan, and what assessors look for in a confident pass submission.

What Is the IOSH Managing Safely Project?

The IOSH Managing Safely project is the workplace risk assessment report component of the IOSH Managing Safely qualification — a Level 3 Award accredited by Ofqual. Unlike the multiple-choice examination that tests knowledge, the project tests the candidate's ability to apply that knowledge to their own real workplace. It is a practical demonstration of competence, not a theoretical exercise.

The project must be based on a real workplace the candidate has access to and can physically observe. Candidates cannot base the project on a hypothetical scenario, a fictional organisation, or a workplace they cannot visit. The purpose is to demonstrate that the candidate can identify genuine hazards, assess their real risk levels, select appropriate control measures from the Hierarchy of Control, and propose actionable improvement objectives in a SMART action plan.

The project is scored out of 100 by the IOSH-approved course provider against IOSH marking criteria. The pass threshold is approximately 60 out of 100. Course providers may categorise results as a strong pass, a borderline pass, or a weak pass — there is no formal IOSH distinction grade for the project component. Candidates who do not achieve 60 will typically be given an opportunity to revise and resubmit the project, depending on the provider's policy.

The project is independently weighted — a strong examination score does not compensate for a project that falls below the pass threshold. Both components must be passed to achieve the IOSH Managing Safely certificate.

Choosing the Right Workplace for Your IOSH Project

Workplace selection has a direct effect on the quality of every section of the project. A poorly chosen workplace produces weak hazard descriptions, which produce weak risk ratings, which produce weak control measures and a weak action plan. Selecting the right workplace and the right scope within it is the first structural decision in the project.

Candidates should choose a workplace they know well and can physically access during the project period. Their current employment setting is the natural starting point — the familiarity with operations, processes, and existing controls provides the contextual depth that assessors reward. Candidates who choose an unfamiliar workplace produce generic hazard descriptions that fail the specificity requirement of Section 1.

Scope is as important as setting. Candidates should define a specific operational area rather than attempting to cover an entire organisation. A warehouse loading bay, a clinical treatment room, a production line section, or a school workshop provides a manageable scope with sufficient hazard variety for a well-evidenced submission. Attempting to cover an entire hospital, a full manufacturing site, or a multi-storey office building produces superficial coverage — too many hazards described in too little depth, which fails the specificity requirements throughout.

Candidates in primarily desk-based roles can still produce a valid project. A desk-based setting presents ergonomic hazards (workstation design, display screen equipment, seating posture), psychosocial hazards (workload, lone working, shift patterns), fire hazards (electrical equipment, blocked escape routes), and manual handling hazards (document storage, equipment movement). These are legitimate hazard categories covered by UK health and safety legislation and fully acceptable in the IOSH project — provided they are described with the same specificity required of industrial hazards.

IOSH Project Section 1 — Hazard Identification: What You Must Include

Section 1 requires candidates to identify, describe, and categorise the workplace hazards that will be carried through the rest of the project. The quality of this section determines the quality of every section that follows. Vague hazard descriptions in Section 1 produce unjustifiable risk ratings in Section 2 and inadequate control measures in Section 3.

Most IOSH-approved providers require a minimum of 4 workplace hazards — many specify 6 to 8 for a well-evidenced submission. Candidates should confirm the minimum hazard requirement with their course provider before beginning the project, as requirements vary. A submission at the minimum threshold with weak descriptions is far more likely to produce a borderline pass than a submission with 6 to 8 precisely described hazards.

Hazards must be categorised by type. The five hazard categories used in IOSH Managing Safely are:

  • Physical hazards: machinery, noise, vibration, radiation, slip and trip surfaces, manual handling loads, work at height, temperature extremes, electrical equipment.
  • Chemical hazards: substances, cleaning agents, solvents, fumes, dust, biological products that trigger chemical reactions.
  • Biological hazards: pathogens, legionella, blood-borne viruses, animal-borne disease, mould, organic dusts.
  • Ergonomic hazards: workstation design, repetitive strain, display screen equipment, awkward postures, tool design.
  • Psychosocial hazards: work-related stress, lone working, shift patterns, workplace violence and aggression, excessive workload, poor job control.

Each hazard description must achieve four things: it must state what the hazard is, where it exists in the workplace, who is at risk, and under what conditions the risk is realised. Describing a "trip hazard" is not sufficient. The following is the level of specificity required: "Uneven concrete floor surface in the delivery bay entrance, approximately 2cm raised edge at the loading door threshold, present throughout the 06:00–22:00 operating shift. HGV drivers and warehouse operatives carrying loads cross this threshold approximately 40 times per shift, often under time pressure and in reduced lighting conditions during the winter months."

That level of description is what enables a justified likelihood rating, a credible severity rating, and a meaningful control measure. For full guidance on hazard categorisation techniques, description standards, and worked examples across all five categories, see the IOSH Managing Safely hazard identification guidance page.

IOSH Project Section 2 — Risk Assessment: Likelihood, Severity, and the Risk Matrix

Section 2 requires candidates to assess the level of risk for each hazard identified in Section 1 using the IOSH Likelihood × Severity risk matrix. The risk rating must be calculated twice for each hazard: before control measures are applied (inherent risk) and after control measures are applied (residual risk). Both ratings are mandatory — a risk assessment that records only one is incomplete and will lose marks.

The likelihood scale runs from 1 (Rare) to 5 (Almost Certain). Likelihood describes the probability of harm occurring given the current conditions and any existing controls already in place. The severity scale runs from 1 (Negligible) to 5 (Catastrophic). Severity describes the realistic worst-case outcome from the hazard as described — not the most extreme theoretical outcome and not a minimised view. The risk rating is calculated by multiplying likelihood by severity: Risk Rating = Likelihood × Severity, producing a score from 1 to 25.

The four risk level bands are:

  • Low (1–6): Acceptable risk. Monitor and review periodically. No immediate additional controls required.
  • Medium (7–14): Action required within a planned timescale. Controls must be implemented; work may continue in the interim if existing controls are in place.
  • High (15–19): Urgent action required. Implement additional controls as a priority.
  • Very High (20–25): Work should not proceed without immediate control measures in place.

Every risk rating must be justified with a written explanation. Assigning a likelihood of 4 (Likely) without explaining why it is likely — and not merely possible — is an incomplete assessment that loses marks. The justification must reference the specific conditions described in the hazard identification: frequency of exposure, number of people at risk, existing control failures, and operational context. Unjustified risk ratings are consistently identified as one of the most common causes of a borderline rather than confident pass score.

The residual risk rating after control measures must always be lower than the inherent risk rating before controls. If the before and after ratings are identical, it signals that the proposed control measures have no effect on the risk — which is either factually incorrect or indicates that the controls were poorly chosen. Assessors reject this pattern. For full guidance on risk matrix completion, inherent and residual risk calculation, and before-and-after rating techniques, see the IOSH Managing Safely risk assessment guidance page.

IOSH Project Section 3 — Control Measures: Applying the Hierarchy of Control

Section 3 requires candidates to identify the control measures that will reduce each hazard to the lowest reasonably practicable level. Control measures must be selected and presented in Hierarchy of Control order — from most effective to least effective. The five levels of the hierarchy are:

  1. Elimination: Remove the hazard entirely. Example: discontinue a chemical process that generates toxic fumes; redesign a task to remove the need for manual handling altogether.
  2. Substitution: Replace the hazard with a less dangerous alternative. Example: replace a solvent-based cleaning agent with a water-based equivalent; substitute a heavy manual load with a lighter pre-packaged alternative.
  3. Engineering Controls: Physical changes that reduce or contain the hazard. Example: install machine guards or interlocks; fit non-slip flooring; install local exhaust ventilation; erect barriers around pedestrian and vehicle interaction zones.
  4. Administrative Controls: Procedural changes that reduce exposure to the hazard. Example: introduce safe systems of work; provide health and safety training; implement job rotation to limit exposure duration; introduce a permit-to-work system; install warning signage.
  5. Personal Protective Equipment (PPE): Last resort only. Example: provide hearing protection where engineering controls cannot reduce noise below the Lower Exposure Action Value; provide respiratory protective equipment where ventilation alone is insufficient to reduce dust below the Workplace Exposure Limit.

The most common and most penalised error in Section 3 is proposing PPE as the primary or sole control measure for a medium, high, or very high risk. PPE does not eliminate or reduce the hazard — it reduces the individual's exposure to it, and only when worn correctly and consistently. Assessors treat PPE-first submissions as evidence that the candidate does not understand the Hierarchy of Control, which is a core Managing Safely learning outcome. For every hazard with a Medium risk rating or above, at least one higher-order control from Levels 1 to 4 of the hierarchy must be proposed before PPE is introduced.

Candidates should apply at least 3 levels of the hierarchy to each significant hazard. A comprehensive control measure entry references the ISO 45001 international standard for occupational health and safety management systems or HSE guidance — doing so demonstrates that the candidate understands that the hierarchy is not an IOSH-only concept but a legally and internationally recognised framework. For full guidance on applying all five hierarchy levels with technically specified examples, see the IOSH Hierarchy of Control guidance page.

IOSH Project Section 4 — Action Plan: Writing SMART Objectives

Section 4 converts the control measures identified in Section 3 into a formal improvement action plan. Each entry in the action plan must correspond directly to one or more of the control measures proposed in Section 3 — the action plan is the implementation roadmap for those controls, not a separate list of generic health and safety improvements.

Every action plan entry must satisfy the SMART framework: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound. Entries that fail any one of these five criteria are not SMART objectives and will lose marks in this section.

Specific — the action must state exactly what will be done, not what outcome is intended. Not "improve manual handling procedures" but "install a powered pallet truck at loading Bay 3 to eliminate manual carry of loads exceeding 20kg from vehicle to racking."

Measurable — the entry must state how successful completion will be verified. Example: "Installation confirmed by engineer sign-off and operational acceptance test. Manual handling incident reports for Bay 3 reviewed at 3-month post-installation audit to confirm zero load-carrying incidents."

Achievable — the action must be realistic given the organisation's resources, budget, and operational constraints. An action plan that requires capital expenditure the organisation cannot realistically fund within the stated timeframe will be questioned by assessors. Candidates should select controls that are proportionate to the risk and feasible for the organisation type described.

Relevant — the action must directly address the risk identified in the risk assessment. An action that does not reduce the likelihood or severity of the specific hazard assessed in Section 2, or that addresses a different hazard not identified in Section 1, is not relevant to the action plan entry it is attached to.

Time-bound — the action must state a specific deadline date, not "ASAP," "as soon as possible," or "in the near future." Urgent actions addressing Very High and High risk ratings must have the earliest target dates — typically within days to weeks. Medium risk actions should have a planned timescale of weeks to months. Low risk actions may have longer review timescales but must still have a specific review date, not an open-ended commitment.

Each action plan entry should also identify the person responsible by job title (not by name, which may change), the resource required (financial cost, equipment, personnel time), and the success measure by which the organisation will know the action has been completed and is effective. For complete action plan guidance with worked examples across all hazard categories, see the IOSH Managing Safely action plan guidance page.

What separates a borderline IOSH project submission from one that earns a confident pass is not the number of hazards identified or the length of the document — it is the consistency and specificity of reasoning across all four sections. The same errors appear in almost every borderline submission. Knowing what they are, and knowing what the corrected version looks like, is often the difference between a pass and a resubmission.

Common IOSH Project Errors That Cause Borderline or Fail Scores

Five errors appear consistently in IOSH Managing Safely projects that achieve a borderline pass or fail to meet the 60-mark threshold. Each error is identifiable, correctable, and avoidable with the right preparation.

Error 1 — Vague hazard descriptions. The error: "Slip hazard in the warehouse." The correction: "Contaminated concrete floor surface at the entrance to Warehouse Bay 2, caused by water tracking from the loading dock seal during rainfall events. The surface becomes slippery when wet. Warehouse operatives and HGV drivers cross this area approximately 30 times per shift while carrying or moving loads, often at pace. No wet floor signage or drainage channel is present." Vague descriptions cannot support justified risk ratings and produce a cascade of weakness through Sections 2, 3, and 4.

Error 2 — Unjustified risk matrix scores. The error: assigning a Likelihood of 4 without explaining why it is Likely rather than Possible, or assigning identical ratings to all hazards across all rows. The correction: every likelihood and severity score must be accompanied by a written justification that references the specific conditions described in Section 1 — frequency of exposure, number of people at risk, operational context, and any existing control failures. Risk ratings that vary logically across the hazard list signal professional judgement; identical ratings across all rows signal that the assessment was not conducted thoughtfully.

Error 3 — PPE as the primary or sole control measure. The error: recommending safety boots and high-visibility vests as the only controls for a Very High manual handling risk. The correction: the Hierarchy of Control must be applied in order. PPE is the last resort — it is proposed only after higher-order controls have been exhausted or found impractical. For a Very High risk, the first controls proposed must be Elimination, Substitution, or Engineering Controls. PPE may then be added as a supplementary measure for residual exposure that cannot be eliminated by the higher-order controls.

Error 4 — Non-SMART action plan objectives. The error: "Improve manual handling training for all staff." The correction: "Deliver a Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992-compliant practical training session (maximum group size 8) for all 24 warehouse operatives by [specific date], delivered by the qualified Health and Safety Manager. Attendance records signed and filed by HR. Training effectiveness reviewed at 3-month post-delivery audit against manual handling incident frequency in Bay 2." The corrected version states what, to whom, by when, by whom, how completion is verified, and how effectiveness is measured.

Error 5 — Failure to reference legislation. The error: completing the entire project without citing any UK health and safety legislation. The correction: reference the HSWA 1974 in the introduction to establish the general duty of care framework; reference MHSWR 1999 Regulation 3 in the risk assessment section to establish the legal basis for the risk assessment duty; reference hazard-specific legislation where applicable — COSHH Regulations 2002 for chemical hazards, Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992 for manual handling hazards, Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005 for noise hazards, and DSE Regulations 1992 for display screen equipment. Legislation references demonstrate that the candidate understands the legal framework underpinning the Managing Safely qualification.

Legislation to Reference in Your IOSH Managing Safely Project

Two pieces of legislation provide the foundational legal framework for the IOSH Managing Safely project and should be referenced in every submission, regardless of the hazards identified.

Health and Safety at Work etc. Act 1974 (HSWA 1974) establishes the general duty of care that underpins all UK occupational health and safety law. Section 2 places a duty on every employer to ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health, safety, and welfare of all employees. Section 3 extends this duty to persons who are not employees but who may be affected by the employer's work activities — covering contractors, visitors, and members of the public. The HSWA 1974 is the appropriate legislation to reference when introducing the project and explaining why the hazard identification and risk assessment exercise is being conducted.

Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (MHSWR 1999), Regulation 3 places a specific legal duty on every employer to carry out a suitable and sufficient assessment of the risks to the health and safety of their employees and of any other persons who may be affected by their work activities. Employers with five or more employees must record the significant findings of the risk assessment in writing — making the written project document itself a legal requirement in most workplace contexts. Reference to MHSWR 1999 Regulation 3 belongs in the risk assessment section of the project, where it directly supports the candidate's explanation of why the assessment is being conducted and why written records are maintained.

For hazard-specific legislation — COSHH Regulations 2002, Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992, Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005, Provision and Use of Work Equipment Regulations 1998, Work at Height Regulations 2005 — reference the specific regulation when it is directly relevant to a hazard identified in Section 1 and assessed in Section 2. Referencing legislation that does not apply to any hazard in the submission demonstrates knowledge but adds no analytical value; referencing legislation that is directly applicable and explaining how it applies to the specific hazard described is what earns marks.

Frequently Asked Questions

How is the IOSH Managing Safely project scored?

The IOSH Managing Safely project is marked out of 100 by the IOSH-approved course provider against IOSH marking criteria. The pass threshold is approximately 60 out of 100. Providers may describe the outcome as a strong pass, borderline pass, or weak pass — IOSH does not award a formal distinction grade for the project.

How many hazards do I need in the IOSH Managing Safely project?

Most course providers require a minimum of 4 hazards, with many specifying 6 to 8 for a well-evidenced submission. Each hazard must be precisely described, categorised by hazard type (physical, chemical, biological, ergonomic, psychosocial), and carried through the full risk assessment and control measures sections.

What does the IOSH risk matrix look like?

The IOSH Managing Safely risk matrix multiplies Likelihood (1=Rare to 5=Almost Certain) by Severity (1=Negligible to 5=Catastrophic) to produce a Risk Rating from 1 to 25. Risk levels are: Low (1–6), Medium (7–14), High (15–19), Very High (20–25). The assessment must be completed before and after control measures to show risk reduction.

What legislation should I reference in the IOSH Managing Safely project?

The primary legislation to reference is the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, specifically Regulation 3, which places a legal duty on employers to conduct suitable and sufficient risk assessments. Employers with five or more employees must record the significant findings in writing.
