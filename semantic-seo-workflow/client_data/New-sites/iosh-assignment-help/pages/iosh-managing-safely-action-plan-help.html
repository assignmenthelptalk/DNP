IOSH Managing Safely Project Action Plan Help: How to Write SMART Objectives That Score High

The IOSH Managing Safely project action plan is the final scored section of the project report. It requires candidates to identify the most important improvement actions revealed by the risk assessment and write each as a SMART objective — Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, and Time-bound. Every action plan entry must trace back to a specific hazard identified and rated in the risk assessment. Vague objectives such as "improve safety awareness" or "train all staff" score zero marks for that entry. This page covers every component of the action plan: SMART criteria applied to IOSH hazards, action plan entry structure, risk prioritisation, and the failure patterns that most commonly cost marks.

What the IOSH Action Plan Section Requires

The action plan is the final scored section of the IOSH Managing Safely project report. Its purpose is to identify the most important improvements revealed by the risk assessment and commit to implementing them with specific actions, responsible persons, resources, dates, and success measures.

Every action plan entry must trace back to a specific hazard identified and rated in the risk assessment section. Assessors check this linkage directly — disconnected actions that do not correspond to any identified hazard in the risk assessment cannot be verified against the evidence in the project and will score poorly. The action plan is not a general workplace safety improvement wish list — it is a structured response to the specific risks identified and rated in the candidate's own risk assessment.

The typical number of action plan entries required is 3 to 5. Candidates should check their provider's specific project guide — some require a minimum of 4 entries, others specify no fixed number but expect all high and very high risk rated hazards to be addressed. Each entry must be a separate SMART objective, addressing a specific hazard by its reference number from the risk assessment table.

Assessors look for: SMART format (applied, not just stated), a clearly named responsible person, realistic resource specification, a specific target date, and a measurable success criterion. Evidence of risk prioritisation — Very High and High risk entries appearing first with the earliest target dates — demonstrates understanding of risk management principles that is rewarded in the marking scheme.

Writing SMART Objectives for Your IOSH Project

SMART is the objective-writing framework the IOSH Managing Safely project requires for every action plan entry. Each letter applies a specific quality criterion that, if not met, reduces the marks available for that entry. Stating that an objective is SMART without it actually meeting all five criteria scores nothing — assessors apply the criteria to the content, not to the label.

Specific — names the hazard, the action, and the location. The objective must identify precisely what will be done. Vague: "Improve housekeeping." Specific: "Remove all oil and fluid spillages from the factory floor in Zone B within 24 hours of occurrence, using the absorbent granule kit stored in cabinet A3, and document each spillage and clean-up in the housekeeping log."

Measurable — has an observable or quantifiable outcome that confirms the action is complete and effective. Vague: "Reduce slipping accidents." Measurable: "Zero slip incidents recorded in Zone B for three consecutive months following implementation, as measured by the monthly accident and near-miss log."

Achievable — realistic given the organisation's resources, authority, and budget. An objective that requires capital expenditure the organisation has not approved, or that depends on regulatory change outside the candidate's control, fails the Achievable criterion. The objective must be within the candidate's or their organisation's realistic power to deliver.

Relevant — directly addresses a specific risk identified and rated in the risk assessment section. If the action plan addresses a hazard that does not appear in the risk assessment, the assessor cannot verify it against the project evidence. Every action plan entry must reference the hazard number and risk rating from the risk assessment table.

Time-bound — states a specific calendar date by which the action will be completed. "As soon as possible" fails the Time-bound criterion immediately. "Within a reasonable timeframe" fails the Time-bound criterion immediately. A specific date — "by 30 May 2026" or "by 15 June 2026" — is required. The target date should be proportionate to the urgency indicated by the risk rating: a Very High risk action should have an earlier target date than a Medium risk action.

A complete worked SMART objective for a slip hazard in a food processing area (Hazard 2 from the risk assessment, Risk Rating 15 = High):

"Install 5m × 2m anti-slip flooring tiles (minimum R11 rating to DIN 51130) in the food preparation area between the preparation benches and the cooking station by 15 June 2026. Responsible: Facilities Manager (J. Smith). Resources: anti-slip tile supply and installation — estimated £800 from the maintenance capital budget. Success measure: risk rating for Hazard 2 reduced from High (15) to Low (4) on post-installation re-assessment, confirmed by supervisor sign-off."

This objective names the specific action (tile installation), the specific location (food preparation area between two fixed reference points), the specification (R11 rating, DIN 51130), the target date (15 June 2026), the responsible person (named role and name), the resource requirement (£800, source identified), and the measurable success criterion (risk rating reduction from 15 to 4, confirmed by re-assessment).

The Complete Action Plan Entry — What Every Row Must Include

The standard action plan table in the IOSH Managing Safely project contains five columns. Every column must be completed for every action plan entry — a missing column reduces the score for that row.

Action: The SMART objective — the full, specific description of what will be done. This is the most heavily marked column. Vague entries ("improve housekeeping," "train staff," "review risk assessment") score zero regardless of the other columns.

Person Responsible: The named role — or named individual — who is accountable for ensuring the action is completed by the target date. "Management" is not acceptable — it does not identify who specifically is accountable. "Everyone" is not acceptable — diffuse accountability means no accountability. The responsible person must be a single identifiable role: "Health and Safety Manager," "Site Supervisor," "Facilities Manager," or a named individual where appropriate.

Resources Required: What is needed to implement the action — in specific terms. This column commonly includes: materials with cost estimates (e.g., "anti-slip matting, approximately £800 from maintenance budget"), training costs (e.g., "manual handling refresher training for 12 warehouse operatives — estimated cost £450 via approved training provider"), time (e.g., "2 hours installation time during planned Sunday maintenance shutdown"), or equipment (e.g., "one mechanical pallet truck, procurement cost approximately £1,200"). Generic entries ("resources as required" or "budget to be allocated") score minimal marks.

Target Completion Date: A specific date in DD/MM/YYYY or Month Year format. The date must be future-dated from the project submission date. The date must be proportionate to the risk level — Very High risk actions should have the earliest completion dates in the action plan. A Very High risk action with a target date 12 months in the future while a Low risk action is addressed in 2 weeks shows poor risk prioritisation understanding and will lose marks.

Success Measure: How the action's completion and effectiveness will be verified — not just that something has been done, but that it has worked. This is the most commonly omitted component and its absence reduces the score significantly. Examples: "risk rating for Hazard 3 reduced from High (15) to Low (4) on post-implementation re-assessment carried out by Health and Safety Manager within 30 days of installation"; "no manual handling incidents involving loads above 15kg recorded in the goods-in area for 3 consecutive months, as measured by the accident and near-miss log"; "maintenance inspection checklist for forklift trucks signed off as satisfactory by Site Supervisor every Monday morning for 12 consecutive weeks."

Prioritising Actions From Your Risk Assessment

Actions in the IOSH project action plan must appear in descending risk rating order: Very High (Risk Rating 20–25) first, then High (15–19), then Medium (7–14), then Low (1–6). This sequence demonstrates that the candidate understands risk prioritisation — that higher-rated risks demand earlier and more urgent responses.

Assessors check the prioritisation of target dates against the risk rating order. If the Very High risk action has a target date 9 months away while the Medium risk action is addressed in 2 weeks, this signals a failure to apply risk-based prioritisation to the action plan. The target completion date must reflect the urgency of the risk level — not the convenience of the implementation.

Each action plan entry should cross-reference the risk rating from the risk assessment section. Example: "Action addresses Hazard 2 — Manual Handling (Inherent Risk Rating 16 — High; Residual Risk Rating 4 — Low after controls)." This cross-reference demonstrates that the action plan flows coherently from the risk assessment and allows the assessor to verify the linkage without having to search through the project.

Not all hazards need to appear in the action plan. Focus on the hazards with the highest residual risk ratings — those where the risk remains at Medium, High, or Very High after the control measures in Section 3 have been applied. Hazards that have been reduced to Low residual risk through comprehensive control measures may not require an action plan entry if no further improvement actions are needed. If the project identifies 6 or more hazards, select the 4 to 5 with the highest residual risk ratings for the action plan.

Common IOSH Action Plan Mistakes That Lose Marks

Vague objectives are the most common and most costly action plan failure. "Improve safety awareness" — no Specific action, no Measurable outcome, no Time-bound date, and no link to any identified hazard — scores zero for that entry. "Train all staff" — who exactly, in what, by when, measured how — also scores zero without specifics. Every element of the SMART criteria must be present in the objective text itself, not implied.

Missing or non-specific responsible person entries lose marks in the Person Responsible column. "Management" or "the HSE team" identifies no one accountable. If there is no single identifiable person who is responsible for the action, the action will not be implemented — and assessors know this. Name a role or a person.

No target date — or vague date language — fails the Time-bound criterion immediately. "ASAP," "when possible," "within a reasonable timeframe," and "to be confirmed" are not dates. A specific calendar date is required.

Repetition of control measures from Section 3 is a failure pattern that many candidates are unaware of until they receive assessor feedback. The action plan is for new improvement actions — steps to be taken in addition to the controls already listed in the control measures section. If the control measures section already states "provide EN 374-compliant nitrile gloves," the action plan cannot list "provide PPE" as a new action. The action plan must go further — for example, "Conduct a COSHH assessment for all chemical substances used in the cleaning department and update the COSHH register by 30 May 2026 — responsible: Health and Safety Manager — success measure: COSHH register updated and signed off, copy filed in safety management system."

Actions not linked to risk assessment hazards cannot be scored against the project evidence. If an action plan entry addresses a hazard that does not appear in the risk assessment section, the assessor has no basis for awarding marks for relevance or for verifying the risk-action linkage. Every action plan entry must reference a specific hazard number from the risk assessment table.

Unrealistic objectives — "eliminate all workplace hazards by next week" or "achieve zero accidents" — fail the Achievable criterion. Zero accidents is a goal, not an objective. Eliminating all workplace hazards is not achievable within any finite timescale. Achievable objectives are specific, bounded improvements that the candidate's organisation can realistically implement within the stated resources and authority.

Action Plan Format — Table vs Narrative

Most IOSH Managing Safely providers supply a structured action plan template in table format. The table is the most common and most assessor-friendly format — it makes all five SMART components visible in labelled columns, allowing the assessor to check each criterion without searching through prose.

Where a provider does not supply a template, or where the candidate is using a self-designed format, table format is strongly recommended over narrative prose. The column headings should be clearly labelled: Action (SMART description), Responsible Person, Resources Required, Target Completion Date, Success Measure. Each row is one action plan entry for one identified hazard.

Where a narrative format is required or preferred by the provider, each action plan entry should still cover all five column equivalents — the SMART objective text, the responsible person, the resources, the date, and the success measure — in sequential sentences within the narrative paragraph for that entry. Omitting any component in narrative format loses the same marks as omitting a table column.

How the Action Plan Connects to the Full Project

The action plan is the final link in the chain that runs through the entire IOSH Managing Safely project. Hazard identification (Section 1) identifies the hazards. Risk assessment (Section 2) rates them and determines which are highest priority. Control measures (Section 3) recommends what to do. The action plan (Section 4) commits to doing the most important things — with accountability, resources, a date, and a way to measure success.

Assessors read the project as a coherent document, not as four independent sections. A strong action plan that directly references the highest-rated risk assessment hazards, recommends improvement actions that go beyond the control measures already listed in Section 3, and structures each entry with all five action plan components demonstrates that the candidate has understood the project as an integrated risk management exercise — which is the competence the IOSH Managing Safely qualification is designed to assess.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many action plan entries does the IOSH Managing Safely project require?

Most IOSH Managing Safely providers require 3–5 action plan entries. Check your specific provider's project guide — some require a minimum of 4, others give no fixed number but expect the highest-priority risks to be addressed. Each entry must be a separate SMART objective addressing a specific hazard from your risk assessment.

Can I include PPE as an action plan entry?

PPE can appear in the action plan only if it represents a new improvement — for example, if your risk assessment identified that no PPE was in use for a specific hazard. If you already listed PPE as a control measure in the control measures section, you cannot repeat it as an action plan entry. The action plan must be for additional improvements beyond existing controls.

What is the difference between control measures and the action plan?

Control measures describe what is currently being done or what you are recommending to reduce risk during the assessment. The action plan describes additional improvement actions — new steps to be taken after the project is submitted, with a responsible person, resources, date, and success measure. The two sections must not repeat each other.

What happens if my SMART objective is not measurable?

An action plan entry that cannot be measured fails the Measurable criterion of SMART and will score reduced or zero marks for that entry. Assessors expect to see how you will verify the action is complete — not just that something will be "improved." Replace vague outcomes like "improve safety" with quantifiable ones: "risk rating reduced from High to Low on re-assessment" or "zero incidents recorded in this area in the 3 months following implementation."
