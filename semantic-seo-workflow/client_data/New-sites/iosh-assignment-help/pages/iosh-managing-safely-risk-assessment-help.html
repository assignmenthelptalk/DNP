IOSH Managing Safely Risk Assessment Help: Likelihood and Severity Matrix, Risk Ratings, and Project Guidance

The risk assessment section of the IOSH Managing Safely project requires candidates to rate each identified hazard using a Likelihood × Severity matrix, producing a numerical risk rating and a risk level classification — both before and after control measures are applied. This section is the numerical core of the project. Every risk rating must be justified by the hazard description that precedes it, and every risk rating drives the control measures and action plan that follow it. This page covers the full risk assessment section: the likelihood scale, the severity scale, risk rating calculation, inherent and residual risk, and how to identify who is at risk.

What the IOSH Managing Safely Risk Assessment Section Requires

The risk assessment section of the IOSH Managing Safely project is structured as a table — one row per hazard identified in the hazard identification section. Each row in the risk assessment table must contain: the hazard reference number (carried from Section 1), the hazard description, the people at risk, any existing controls already in place, the likelihood score before controls, the severity score before controls, the risk rating before controls, the control measures recommended (or a cross-reference to Section 3), the likelihood score after controls, the severity score after controls, the risk rating after controls, and the risk level classification after controls.

Candidates must assess both the inherent risk (the risk with no controls in place, or with only the existing inadequate controls in place) and the residual risk (the risk remaining after all recommended control measures from Section 3 are applied). Both ratings are mandatory — a risk assessment that only records ratings before controls, or only after controls, is incomplete and will lose marks.

The legal basis for the risk assessment duty is the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (MHSWR), Regulation 3. Regulation 3 requires every employer to carry out a suitable and sufficient assessment of the risks to the health and safety of their employees and of any other persons who may be affected by their work activities. The phrase "suitable and sufficient" is the legal standard — it means the assessment must identify the significant risks, be proportionate to the nature of the hazard, and be appropriate to the workplace. It does not require every conceivable risk to be assessed to an infinite level of detail.

The risk assessment section is heavily weighted in the IOSH project marking scheme. Inadequate ratings, unsupported scores, or identical ratings applied to all hazards will directly reduce the marks available in this section. Assessors look for risk ratings that are justified by the hazard descriptions and that vary logically across the hazards identified.

The Likelihood Scale in the IOSH Risk Matrix

The likelihood scale runs from 1 (Rare) to 5 (Almost Certain). Each level has a descriptor and a specific meaning. Selecting a likelihood score requires an understanding of what each level represents in terms of realistic exposure conditions — not just the label.

1 — Rare: The hazard could theoretically cause harm, but the conditions required are extremely unlikely given the current environment and controls. Example: electrocution from a tested and certified fixed electrical installation with annual Portable Appliance Testing (PAT), a Periodic Inspection and Test report completed within the last 5 years, and no reported defects or near-misses. Justification language: "There are no foreseeable circumstances under normal operating conditions that would result in contact with a live conductor given the certified installation condition and regular inspection regime."

2 — Unlikely: Harm could occur but would be unusual given current conditions. Example: chemical splash to the eyes in an area where splash guards are installed, operatives wear EN 166-compliant safety glasses as standard, and the chemical is dispensed in small volumes — a splash incident would require multiple simultaneous control failures to occur.

3 — Possible: Harm might occur — not expected routinely, but a realistic outcome given the exposure scenario described in the hazard identification section. Example: a slip on oil-contaminated flooring in a production area where mopping is performed reactively after a reported spillage rather than on a preventive schedule — a slip could realistically occur between the time of contamination and the time of cleaning.

4 — Likely: Harm is expected to occur at some point without additional controls. Example: musculoskeletal injury among warehouse operatives who manually lift 30 to 40kg loads repeatedly over an 8-hour shift without mechanical handling aids and without a team lift procedure — given the exposure frequency and load weight, a manual handling injury is statistically expected within a reasonable operating period.

5 — Almost Certain: Harm is expected to occur repeatedly or inevitably if the current conditions continue. Example: progressive noise-induced hearing loss among workers exposed to noise levels exceeding 85dB(A) daily without hearing protection — over months of uncontrolled exposure, hearing damage is a near-certain outcome. The Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005 sets the Upper Exposure Action Value at 85dB(A) LEP,d precisely because harm becomes near-certain above this threshold without controls.

The Severity Scale in the IOSH Risk Matrix

The severity scale runs from 1 (Negligible) to 5 (Catastrophic). Each level describes the nature of the injury or harm that could result from the hazard. The severity score must reflect the realistic worst-case outcome from the hazard as described — not the most extreme theoretical outcome possible, and not a minimised view designed to reduce the risk rating.

1 — Negligible: No injury or trivial discomfort — no first aid required, no lost time, no lasting effect. Example: momentary eye irritation from airborne paper dust in a well-ventilated office environment, resolved without intervention within seconds of moving away from the source.

2 — Minor: Injury requiring first aid only — no medical referral or GP visit required, no lost time beyond the immediate first aid treatment, full recovery expected rapidly. Example: a small laceration from a sharp edge on packaging material, treated with a plaster from the first aid kit, with full function restored immediately.

3 — Moderate/Significant: Injury requiring medical treatment (GP referral or Accident and Emergency attendance), possible short-term lost time of days to a few weeks, full or near-full recovery expected. Example: a sprained wrist resulting from a manual handling incident, leading to 5 days of absence from work and a physiotherapy course, with full recovery expected within 4 to 6 weeks.

4 — Major: Serious injury requiring hospitalisation, possible long-term or permanent health effect, possibly reportable under RIDDOR 2013. Example: a fractured lumbar vertebra from a fall from height resulting in hospitalisation and a 6-month recovery period, or industrial dermatitis from repeated skin contact with a strong acid cleaner resulting in permanent chemical sensitisation. Under RIDDOR 2013 (Regulation 4), specified injuries — including fractures other than to fingers, thumbs, or toes — must be reported to the HSE.

5 — Catastrophic: Fatality or permanent disability. Example: death from electrocution at 240V; permanent total deafness from acute acoustic trauma; occupational lung cancer from long-term uncontrolled exposure to respirable crystalline silica dust; fatal fall from a height of 4 metres. All workplace fatalities must be reported to the HSE under RIDDOR 2013, Regulation 6.

Calculating Risk Ratings and Determining Risk Levels

The risk rating is calculated by multiplying the likelihood score by the severity score: Risk Rating = Likelihood × Severity. The result is a number between 1 and 25, which is then classified into one of four risk level bands that determine the required response.

The four risk level bands are:

  • Low — 1 to 6: Acceptable risk. Monitor and review periodically. No immediate additional controls required, but the risk should not be ignored.
  • Medium — 7 to 14: Action required within a planned timescale. Controls must be implemented, but the work may continue in the interim if existing controls are in place.
  • High — 15 to 19: Urgent action required. Implement additional controls as a priority before the risk level is reviewed.
  • Very High — 20 to 25: Work should not proceed without immediate control measures. The hazard poses an unacceptable level of risk in its current state.

Three worked examples of risk rating calculation:

Worked example 1: Slip hazard from oil-contaminated production floor. Likelihood 3 (Possible — contamination occurs regularly and cleaning is reactive) × Severity 3 (Moderate — a slip could result in a sprained wrist or ankle requiring several days off work) = Risk Rating 9 = Medium. Action required within a planned timescale.

Worked example 2: Unguarded rotating machinery with no interlock. Likelihood 3 (Possible — operatives reach past the unguarded area periodically during operation) × Severity 5 (Catastrophic — contact with rotating machinery could result in a degloving injury, amputation, or fatality) = Risk Rating 15 = High. Urgent action required.

Worked example 3: Noise exposure above 85dB(A) without hearing protection. Likelihood 5 (Almost Certain — operatives are exposed for the full shift duration daily and no hearing protection is provided) × Severity 4 (Major — progressive noise-induced hearing loss is a permanent, irreversible occupational disease) = Risk Rating 20 = Very High. Work should not proceed without immediate control measures.

Candidates must state the risk level band classification — Low, Medium, High, or Very High — not just the numerical score. The classification is what drives the action plan prioritisation: Very High and High risk ratings must appear as the first entries in the action plan, with the earliest target completion dates.

Before and After Risk Ratings — Inherent Risk and Residual Risk

The IOSH risk assessment section requires candidates to calculate two risk ratings for every hazard: the rating before control measures are applied (inherent risk) and the rating after control measures are applied (residual risk). Both are mandatory. A risk assessment that records only the before rating, or that records identical before and after ratings without explanation, will lose marks.

Inherent risk (before rating) is the risk level when no controls are in place, or when only the existing inadequate controls described in the hazard identification section are in place. It reflects the genuine danger of the hazard if left unmanaged. For a hazard described as having "no controls in place," the before rating should reflect that exposed state honestly — it should not be reduced by controls that do not yet exist.

Residual risk (after rating) is the risk level remaining after all of the control measures recommended in Section 3 (Control Measures) have been fully implemented. Residual risk must always be lower than inherent risk. If the before and after ratings are identical, it means the recommended control measures have no effect on the risk — which is either incorrect or indicates that the control measures were poorly selected. Assessors will reject this pattern.

Control measures reduce the risk rating by reducing likelihood, severity, or both. Examples:

  • Installing non-slip flooring in Machinery Bay 3 reduces the likelihood of a slip from 3 (Possible) to 1 (Rare) — the physical hazard mechanism is controlled at source. If severity remains at 3 (Moderate), the after rating = 1 × 3 = 3 (Low).
  • Providing a fall arrest harness for roof maintenance work reduces the severity of a fall from 5 (Catastrophic) to 3 (Moderate, because the harness limits the fall distance) — the likelihood of a slip may not change. If likelihood remains at 3 (Possible), the after rating = 3 × 3 = 9 (Medium).
  • Introducing a mechanical pallet truck for warehouse lifting, combined with a team lift procedure and manual handling training, reduces both likelihood and severity: likelihood from 4 (Likely) to 2 (Unlikely), severity from 3 (Moderate) to 2 (Minor). After rating = 2 × 2 = 4 (Low).

Two common errors to avoid: leaving before and after ratings identical (signals that the control measures are ineffective), and reducing all residual risk ratings to 1 (the minimum possible score). Assessors expect residual risk ratings to reflect realistic post-control conditions, not a theoretical zero risk state. A residual risk rating of 1 × 1 = 1 for every hazard is not credible and signals that the assessment has not been conducted with professional judgement.

Identifying People at Risk in the IOSH Project

The "People at Risk" column in the risk assessment table must not simply state "employees." It must identify which employees, approximately how many, and any vulnerability factors that affect the risk rating for that specific hazard.

People at risk categories to consider for each hazard:

  • Direct employees: Name the specific role and approximate number. Example: "warehouse operatives (approximately 15 on day shift, 8 on night shift)." The number affects the scale of harm if the risk is realised.
  • Contractors and sub-contractors: Specify the trade and the frequency of access. Example: "electrical maintenance contractors accessing the plant room for monthly PPM visits — approximately 2 persons per visit." MHSWR 1999 Regulation 3 extends the risk assessment duty to persons other than direct employees who may be affected by the work.
  • Visitors: Specify the type of visitor and approximate frequency. Example: "client visitors to the showroom — approximately 20 per week, none of whom receive a health and safety induction."
  • Members of the public: Specify proximity and access. Example: "pedestrians using the pavement adjacent to the construction site hoarding boundary — continuous throughout the working day."
  • Vulnerable groups: New and expectant mothers (MHSWR 1999, Regulations 16–17 require a specific risk assessment for this group), young workers under 18 (higher risk due to inexperience — Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, Regulation 19), lone workers without monitoring procedures, and workers with pre-existing health conditions that increase susceptibility to a specific hazard.

Vulnerability affects the risk rating. A chemical hazard that poses a Moderate severity risk to a healthy adult of working age may warrant a Major severity rating if the people exposed include new or expectant mothers, because MHSWR 1999 Regulations 16–17 require the employer to conduct a specific risk assessment for this group and to take additional protective action where necessary. Noting the presence of vulnerable groups and their effect on the severity rating demonstrates additional legal knowledge that is rewarded in the marking scheme.

The risk rating — and particularly the risk level band — determines the urgency and type of control measures that must be recommended in Section 3. Very High and High risk ratings demand higher-order controls from the Hierarchy of Control. How risk ratings drive control measure selection, and why PPE cannot be the primary response to a high-rated risk, is covered in the hierarchy of control guidance.

How Risk Ratings Drive Control Measure Selection

The risk level band classification — Low, Medium, High, or Very High — determines the type of control measures that are appropriate and the urgency with which they must be implemented. Very High and High risk ratings require higher-order controls from the Hierarchy of Control: Elimination, Substitution, or Engineering Controls. Recommending PPE as the primary or sole control measure for a Very High or High risk is not acceptable in the IOSH project — it does not reflect the Hierarchy of Control principle and will lose marks in the control measures section.

Medium risk ratings (7–14) may be addressed through a combination of Engineering Controls and Administrative Controls, with PPE as a complementary measure for residual exposure. Low risk ratings (1–6) require monitoring and periodic review — they do not typically require new capital expenditure but should appear in a review schedule.

The risk rating before controls also determines which hazards appear as the first entries in the action plan — the highest-rated risks must be assigned the earliest target completion dates. A project where the Very High risk hazard has an action plan target date 12 months in the future, while a Low risk hazard is addressed immediately, signals a failure to understand risk prioritisation.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between inherent risk and residual risk in the IOSH project?

Inherent risk is the risk level before any control measures are applied — it represents the full danger of the hazard in an uncontrolled state. Residual risk is the risk level remaining after all recommended control measures have been implemented. Candidates must calculate and record both in the risk assessment section, and the residual risk rating must always be lower than the inherent risk rating to demonstrate that the proposed controls are effective.

Can I give the same likelihood and severity score to every hazard?

Giving identical ratings to all hazards indicates that each hazard poses exactly the same level of risk — which is almost never true in a real workplace. Assessors expect a range of risk ratings reflecting genuine differences in the likelihood of harm and the severity of potential injury for each hazard. Identical ratings across all rows suggest the risk assessment has not been conducted thoughtfully and will lose marks.

What risk level requires immediate action in the IOSH project?

A Very High risk level (Risk Rating 20–25, calculated as Likelihood × Severity) requires immediate control measures before work proceeds. A High risk level (Risk Rating 15–19) requires urgent action as a priority. Medium risks (7–14) require action within a planned timescale. Low risks (1–6) are acceptable but require monitoring and periodic review. These thresholds determine the priority order of the action plan.

Which legislation requires employers to carry out a risk assessment?

The Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, Regulation 3, places a legal duty on every employer to carry out a suitable and sufficient risk assessment of risks to the health and safety of employees and others affected by their work. Additional regulations require specific risk assessments for particular hazards: COSHH Regulations 2002 (chemical hazards), Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992 (manual handling), and DSE Regulations 1992 (display screen equipment).
