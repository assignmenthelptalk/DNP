IOSH Hierarchy of Control: How to Apply Control Measures Correctly in Your Managing Safely Project

The hierarchy of control is a five-level framework — endorsed by the HSE, ISO 45001:2018, and underpinned by the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, Schedule 1 — that ranks control measures from most to least effective for managing workplace hazards. The IOSH Managing Safely project requires candidates to apply this hierarchy in the control measures section: working from the top level (Elimination) downward, applying the highest practicable control before moving to the next. The most common project failure in this section is leading with PPE when higher-order controls are practicable.

What the Hierarchy of Control Is and Why the IOSH Project Requires It

The hierarchy of control exists because not all control measures are equally effective. PPE depends entirely on the individual wearing it correctly, consistently, and keeping it in good condition — if the glove tears, the ear muff is not worn for 30 seconds during a task, or the respirator does not fit correctly, the protection is completely lost. Engineering controls — machine guards, local exhaust ventilation systems, interlocked safety devices — function regardless of individual behaviour. They protect all workers in the area continuously, without relying on human compliance at any moment.

The legal basis for the hierarchy is the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (MHSWR), Schedule 1 — General Principles of Prevention. Schedule 1 lists the order in which risk must be managed: avoid risks; evaluate unavoidable risks; combat risks at source; adapt to technical progress; replace the dangerous with the non-dangerous or less dangerous; develop a coherent prevention policy; give priority to collective protective measures over individual protective measures (engineering controls over PPE); give appropriate instructions to workers.

ISO 45001:2018, Clause 8.1.2 — Hierarchy of Controls — requires organisations to implement controls in the order: eliminate, substitute, use engineering controls, use administrative controls, use PPE. This international standard for occupational health and safety management systems codifies the same hierarchy in a management system framework, reinforcing its status as the globally accepted approach to risk control.

The IOSH project requires candidates to list control measures for each identified hazard in hierarchy order, working from the highest practicable control level downward. Assessors look specifically for evidence that candidates have considered and ruled out higher-level controls before recommending PPE. A project that lists PPE as the first or only control measure for a hazard rated High or Very High, without explaining why elimination, substitution, or engineering controls are not practicable, will lose significant marks in the control measures section.

The pyramid representation of the hierarchy — Elimination at the top, widest protection, fewest people involved in its implementation; PPE at the bottom, narrowest protection, reliant entirely on individual compliance — is the visual model candidates should use to check that their control measure recommendations are working from the most to least effective level.

Level 1 — Elimination: Removing the Hazard Completely

Elimination removes the hazard from the workplace entirely. It does not reduce or manage the hazard — it makes it non-existent. Elimination is the most effective control level because it produces zero residual risk from the eliminated hazard. No training, no PPE, no procedure is required because there is no longer a hazard to control.

Specific elimination examples at the technical depth the IOSH project requires:

  • Manual handling elimination: Automating a repetitive manual lifting task with a robotic palletiser — the manual handling hazard is eliminated, not reduced. No manual handling training, no mechanical aid, no team lift procedure is required because operatives no longer lift the load.
  • Chemical hazard elimination: Switching from a solvent-based degreasing bath (containing trichloroethylene, a suspected carcinogen under COSHH 2002) to an ultrasonic aqueous cleaning system — the chemical hazard is eliminated. The ultrasonic system achieves the same surface cleanliness without hazardous solvent vapour.
  • Trip hazard elimination: Permanently relocating a piece of equipment from a pedestrian walkway rather than marking the hazard with tape or installing a ramp over it — elimination removes the obstruction, while marking or ramping only manages it.
  • Working at height elimination: Redesigning a maintenance procedure so that it can be performed from ground level using an extended-reach tool, removing the need to work at height entirely. The Working at Height Regulations 2005, Regulation 6(1) requires employers to avoid work at height where it is reasonably practicable to do so — elimination is the legislative first preference.

Practicability statement: candidates must state whether elimination is practicable for each hazard. If it is not practicable — because the hazard is inherent to the process — the candidate must state the specific reason and move to the next level. Example: "Elimination is not practicable because the casting process requires molten metal at temperatures exceeding 1,400°C — the heat hazard cannot be removed without ceasing production entirely." This statement demonstrates that the hierarchy has been applied deliberately, not skipped.

Level 2 — Substitution: Replacing the Hazardous with the Less Hazardous

Substitution replaces a hazardous substance, material, piece of equipment, or process with an alternative that poses a lower risk — without eliminating the underlying task or activity. The substituted alternative must be technically capable of performing the same function as the original — substitution is not valid if the replacement cannot do the job to the required standard.

Specific substitution examples with measurable hazard reduction:

  • Chemical substitution: Replacing a solvent-based contact adhesive containing n-hexane (a neurotoxin classified under COSHH 2002) with a water-based equivalent of equal bonding strength — confirmed by the supplier to meet the bond strength specification for the application. The hazard category changes from COSHH-notifiable chemical to low-hazard aqueous product.
  • Chemical concentration substitution: Replacing a bulk concentrated acid descaler with a pre-diluted pH 2–3 formulation dispensed via a closed dosing system — reduces the concentration hazard and the splash risk, while achieving the same descaling performance.
  • Noise substitution: Replacing a pneumatic impact wrench (noise level 102dB(A)) with an electric torque wrench (noise level 76dB(A)) for the same fastening task — the electric tool's noise level is below the Lower Exposure Action Value of 80dB(A) LEP,d under the Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005, potentially eliminating the need for hearing protection during this task.
  • Vibration substitution: Replacing a petrol-driven plate compactor (Hand-Arm Vibration daily exposure exceeding the Exposure Action Value of 2.5 m/s² A(8)) with a high-frequency electric roller for surface compaction — the electric roller's vibration magnitude may reduce daily HAV exposure below the EAV under the Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005.

Practicability statement for substitution: candidates must address whether the substitute meets the technical performance requirement. Example: "The water-based adhesive was evaluated and confirmed by the supplier to achieve the required bond strength for this application — substitution is both practicable and technically viable."

Level 3 — Engineering Controls: Physical Barriers and Mechanical Solutions

Engineering controls are physical or mechanical measures that remove or reduce the hazard at source. They do not rely on individual compliance to function — a machine guard that cuts power to the motor automatically when opened protects every worker in the area regardless of whether they remember to press a button or wear a piece of PPE.

Specific engineering control examples with the technical specification required in the IOSH project:

Machine guarding: Interlocked fixed guards on a conveyor drive that cut power to the motor automatically when the guard is opened — protects against entrapment and entanglement at the conveyor nip point; guarding must comply with BS EN ISO 14119 (interlocking devices associated with guards). Two-hand control device requiring simultaneous depression of both controls to activate a press — prevents access to the danger zone during the machine cycle by physically requiring both hands to be on the controls.

Local exhaust ventilation (LEV): Capture hood positioned at the extraction point of a woodworking lathe, designed to achieve a capture velocity of 0.5m/s at the source, to remove wood dust — a Group 1 carcinogen (hardwoods) under COSHH 2002 — before it enters the operator's breathing zone. Under COSHH Regulation 9, LEV systems must be examined and tested by a competent person at least every 14 months, and the test records must be kept for at least 5 years.

Noise control at source: Acoustic enclosure built around a diesel compressor, reducing noise at the nearest operator position from 98dB(A) to 82dB(A) — reducing the daily noise dose below the Upper Exposure Action Value of 85dB(A) LEP,d under the Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005.

Slip prevention: Anti-slip flooring tiles with a minimum R11 rating to DIN 51130 installed in the food preparation area to prevent slips from oil and water contamination — a physical measure that functions continuously and does not require any worker action to be effective.

Fall prevention: Permanent edge protection system — top rail at 1,000mm, intermediate rail at 500mm, and toe board at 150mm — compliant with BS EN 13374 Class A, installed at the 3-metre mezzanine loading bay. Scissor lift platform with a working platform height of up to 4 metres as an alternative to unsecured ladder access for maintenance tasks above 2 metres.

Segregation: Physical Armco crash barrier system in the warehouse separating the forklift truck operating lanes from the pedestrian routes — removes the proximity hazard between vehicles and pedestrians, functioning at all times regardless of driver or pedestrian behaviour.

Critical presentation point: in the IOSH project, "install LEV" is insufficient as a control measure description and will score minimal marks. "Install capture-hood LEV at the woodworking lathe extraction point, designed to achieve a capture velocity of 0.5m/s at the source, examined and tested every 14 months by a competent person in accordance with COSHH Regulation 9" scores marks. The technical specification demonstrates that the control has been considered at the level of implementation, not just named as a concept.

Level 4 — Administrative Controls: Procedures, Training, and Work Organisation

Administrative controls manage how work is done — they reduce exposure through rules, training, supervision, and work organisation. They are less effective than engineering controls because they depend on consistent human compliance. A written procedure does not protect a worker who does not follow it. Training does not protect a worker who applies it incorrectly. Administrative controls must be actively maintained — procedures reviewed, training refreshed, permits monitored — or they deteriorate under production pressure.

Specific administrative control examples with the documentation and frequency details required in the IOSH project:

Safe systems of work and procedures: Written manual handling procedure specifying the team lift requirement for loads exceeding 25kg, the maximum single-person lift weight of 15kg, and the mandatory use of a mechanical pallet truck for loads exceeding 25kg — reviewed annually and re-signed by all relevant workers to confirm receipt and understanding.

Permit to work systems: Confined space entry permit under the Confined Spaces Regulations 1997 — written authorisation required before any entry; atmospheric monitoring for oxygen depletion (below 19.5%) and toxic gas presence; a trained rescue team on standby with rescue equipment; a standby person at the entry point throughout. Working at height permit for tasks above 2 metres using non-fixed access equipment (ladders, mobile elevated work platforms).

Training: COSHH awareness training for all workers who use or may be exposed to hazardous substances — delivered at induction and refreshed every 2 years; records kept detailing the trainer's name, training date, content summary, and attendee signature. Manual handling training for all warehouse operatives — documented, with content covering safe lifting technique, team lift procedures, and mechanical aid operation.

Job rotation and exposure limitation: Rotating noise-exposed workers between high-noise tasks (above 85dB(A)) and quiet tasks after a maximum 2-hour exposure period — reducing daily noise dose below the Upper Exposure Action Value of 85dB(A) LEP,d under the Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005. Scheduling high-noise tasks away from adjacent areas where non-noise-exposed workers are present.

Supervision: Weekly supervisor walk-round inspection of forklift truck operating areas to verify that Armco segregation barriers are in place, pedestrian routes are unobstructed, and forklift speed limit signage is visible — findings recorded and signed off by the supervisor, with any deficiencies raised as maintenance requests within 24 hours.

Signage: Mandatory PPE signage at entry points to noise zones, chemical storage areas, and mandatory hard hat areas — compliant with the Health and Safety (Safety Signs and Signals) Regulations 1996, which require pictogram-format signs (blue and white for mandatory actions, yellow and black for warnings) rather than text-only notices.

Administrative controls must not be presented as if they are permanent solutions to high-rated risks. Their limitation — that they depend on human compliance — must be acknowledged, and higher-order controls should be recommended wherever practicable before administrative controls are listed.

Level 5 — PPE: The Last Resort and How to Specify It Correctly

PPE is the last line of defence — it does not remove or reduce the hazard; it only protects the individual if worn correctly, consistently, and in good condition. If PPE fails — a torn glove, a respirator not worn during a 30-second task, an ear muff pushed off one ear — the protection is completely lost for that person at that moment. Engineering controls function at all times regardless of individual behaviour; PPE does not.

PPE should appear in the IOSH project only as a complementary control alongside higher-level controls — for residual exposure that remains after engineering and administrative controls have been implemented. Recommending PPE as the primary or sole control for a hazard rated Medium, High, or Very High is incorrect and will lose marks in both the control measures section and in the hierarchy of control justification.

The Personal Protective Equipment at Work Regulations 1992 (as amended 2022) place a duty on employers to provide suitable PPE free of charge to employees where risk cannot be adequately controlled by other means. Employers must also ensure that PPE is suitable for the risk, correctly maintained, stored appropriately, and that employees are trained in its correct use, fitting, and limitations.

Specific PPE types with the full specification required in the IOSH project — "wear gloves" scores minimal marks; the specifications below score marks:

Hearing protection: EN 352-1 compliant ear muffs with an SNR (Single Number Rating) value sufficient to reduce the exposure at the ear below 80dB(A). For a noise environment of 98dB(A), an SNR of at least 33dB is required. Inspected before each use for cushion condition (cracked, hardened, or flattened cushions reduce attenuation significantly); replaced annually or when cushion condition deteriorates; stored in a protective case when not in use to prevent contamination of the cushion seals.

Eye and face protection: EN 166-compliant safety spectacles with side shields for chemical splash risk — minimum optical class 1 (clear, undistorted vision). Replaced if the lens is scratched (a scratched lens distorts vision and reduces impact protection). Stored in a rigid carry case between uses to prevent lens damage. For full-face splash protection, EN 166-compliant face shields with adjustable headgear.

Hand protection: EN 374-compliant nitrile examination gloves, minimum 0.1mm thickness, for contact with a pH 2–3 acid descaler. Nitrile is specified (not latex) to avoid latex sensitisation in the workforce; EN 374 compliance is required (not general-purpose household gloves). Inspected visually before each use for pin holes, cuts, or contamination — replaced immediately if any defect is found. Latex-free procurement is mandatory where any worker has a known or suspected latex sensitivity.

Respiratory protective equipment (RPE): Half-face respirator fitted with a P3 particulate filter (EN 143) for silica dust exposure during concrete cutting. The P3 filter removes at least 99.95% of airborne particles. Fit testing is required before first use — quantitative fit testing (e.g., Portacount) or qualitative fit testing (e.g., saccharin or Bitrex) per HSE guidance. A negative-pressure face-seal check must be performed by the wearer before each use. The filter must be replaced when breathing resistance increases perceptibly or according to the manufacturer's scheduled replacement interval.

Head protection: EN 397 industrial safety helmet for overhead hazard exposure on construction sites. Inspected for cracks, dents, and UV degradation (fading, chalking) before each use. Replaced immediately after any significant impact — even if no visible damage is present, the internal shock-absorbing structure may have been compromised. Maximum service life is 3 to 5 years from the manufacture date marked inside the shell — not from the date of purchase or first use.

How to Apply the Hierarchy of Control in Your IOSH Project

For each hazard in the control measures section, list controls in hierarchy order — starting with the highest level that is practicable and working downward. Each control must include the type (elimination/substitution/engineering/administrative/PPE), the specific action, and the relevant technical specification or standard. Where a higher-level control is not practicable, state the reason explicitly — this demonstrates that the hierarchy has been applied deliberately.

A worked example for a noise hazard (grinding operations at 98dB(A)):

  • Elimination: Not practicable — the grinding process is essential to the manufacturing specification and cannot be redesigned to remove the need for grinding.
  • Substitution: Evaluate diamond blade cutting technology as an alternative to angle grinding for compatible applications — diamond blade cutting achieves the same cut quality at a lower noise level. Implement substitution where technically compatible; specify the noise level of the diamond blade cutter to confirm it is below the Lower Exposure Action Value of 80dB(A).
  • Engineering controls: Install an acoustic enclosure around the fixed grinding station, designed and constructed to achieve a 16dB(A) noise reduction at the operator position, reducing the exposure from 98dB(A) to 82dB(A). Install acoustic screens between workstations to reduce noise propagation to adjacent work areas.
  • Administrative controls: Limit each worker's exposure on grinding tasks to a maximum of 2 hours per shift before rotation to a non-noise-exposed task — calculated to reduce daily noise dose below the Upper Exposure Action Value of 85dB(A) LEP,d. Schedule annual audiometric surveillance for all workers regularly exposed above 85dB(A).
  • PPE: EN 352-1 compliant ear muffs, minimum SNR 33dB, as a last resort for residual noise exposure that remains after engineering and administrative controls — inspected before each use, replaced annually or when cushion condition deteriorates.

The before and after risk ratings in the risk assessment section must reflect the combined effect of these controls. Engineering controls reduce the likelihood of harm at source; administrative controls reduce the duration and frequency of exposure. The after risk rating should show a realistic reduction — not an identical rating to the before rating (which would indicate the controls are ineffective), and not a reduction to 1 (which is rarely realistic after controls short of complete elimination).

The legislation underpinning the hierarchy of control extends beyond MHSWR 1999. Specific statutory hierarchies exist for individual hazard types. Legislation Underpinning the Hierarchy of Control and Its Connection to the Action Plan

The Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (MHSWR), Schedule 1 — General Principles of Prevention — is the overarching legislative basis for the hierarchy of control in the IOSH project. Schedule 1 mandates the order of priority: avoid risks first, then evaluate unavoidable risks, then combat risks at source, then give priority to collective protective measures over individual protective measures.

Specific statutory hierarchies exist for individual hazard types and are the most precise legislation to cite in the control measures section:

  • COSHH Regulations 2002, Regulation 7 — hierarchy for controlling exposure to hazardous substances: eliminate, substitute with a less hazardous substance or form, control exposure using engineering controls (enclosure, LEV), apply administrative controls (safe systems of work, reducing exposure time), use PPE as a last resort. This specific hierarchy for chemical hazards parallels the general hierarchy but is the legally correct citation for COSHH hazards.
  • Working at Height Regulations 2005, Regulation 6 — requires employers to avoid work at height where reasonably practicable, prevent falls where work at height cannot be avoided (using collective fall prevention such as guardrails and working platforms), and mitigate the consequences of falls where prevention is not reasonably practicable (using fall arrest systems such as harnesses).
  • Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005, Regulation 6 — hierarchy for noise control: eliminate noise at source, reduce noise at source by engineering means, reduce exposure duration, use PPE as a last resort.

The control measures recommended in the hierarchy — particularly those that require capital expenditure, procurement, installation work, or the development of new procedures — become the entries in the action plan section of the project. High-priority controls (those recommended for Very High and High risk hazards) generate the first action plan entries and must be assigned the earliest target completion dates. This connection between Sections 3 and 4 is what assessors look for when evaluating whether the project has been completed as a coherent workplace risk management exercise.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why is PPE considered the least effective control measure in the IOSH hierarchy?

PPE is the least effective control because it does not remove or reduce the hazard itself — it only provides a barrier between the hazard and the individual. Protection depends entirely on the PPE being worn correctly every time, fitting properly, being in good condition, and not failing. Engineering controls — such as machine guards or local exhaust ventilation — function regardless of individual behaviour and protect everyone in the area continuously. This is why the IOSH project and the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 require PPE to be treated as a last resort after higher-level controls have been considered.

Do I need to show that I considered elimination before recommending other controls?

Demonstrating that you have considered and assessed elimination (and substitution) before moving to engineering or administrative controls is what differentiates a high-scoring project from an average one. Assessors look for evidence that the hierarchy has been worked through deliberately — not just applied as a checklist. Where elimination is not practicable, state the specific reason why. "Elimination was considered but is not practicable because the task requires the use of this hazardous substance for the process to function" scores marks; skipping elimination without comment does not.

Can I recommend both engineering controls and PPE for the same hazard?

Combining controls from multiple hierarchy levels is correct practice and is expected in the IOSH project. For example, a noise hazard may be addressed with engineering controls (acoustic enclosure) to reduce noise at source, administrative controls (job rotation) to limit exposure duration, and PPE (ear muffs) for residual exposure that remains after engineering and administrative controls. PPE in this context is complementary — it addresses the residual risk remaining after higher controls. What scores poorly is recommending PPE as the only control where engineering or administrative controls are clearly practicable.

What is the difference between engineering controls and administrative controls?

Engineering controls are physical or mechanical measures that reduce or remove the hazard regardless of what any individual does — machine guards, ventilation systems, anti-slip flooring. Administrative controls change how work is done through rules, training, procedures, and organisation — they are less effective because they depend on human compliance. Both are above PPE in the hierarchy. For most hazards in an IOSH project, the correct approach is to implement at least one engineering control and at least one administrative control before specifying PPE.
