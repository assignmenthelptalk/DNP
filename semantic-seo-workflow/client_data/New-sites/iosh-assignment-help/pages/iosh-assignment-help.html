What Is IOSH Managing Safely and Who Takes It?

IOSH Managing Safely is a qualification awarded by the Institution of Occupational Safety and Health — the world's largest professional body for health and safety, a chartered body headquartered in Wigston, Leicestershire. The qualification sits at Level 3 on the Regulated Qualifications Framework (RQF), which places it at the level of A-level study. It is designed for workplace training — not academic study — and is structured to equip managers and supervisors with the practical competence to manage health and safety within their own teams and work environments.

Over 200,000 candidates take IOSH Managing Safely globally each year, making it the single most widely taken health and safety qualification in the world. The target candidates are managers, supervisors, and team leaders — people who have day-to-day responsibility for the safety of a team but who are not specialist health and safety professionals. The qualification is designed to give them the knowledge and tools to fulfil that responsibility legally and effectively.

IOSH Managing Safely is delivered in approximately 3 to 4 days of classroom training, or the equivalent in structured e-learning, followed by time for project completion and submission. The qualification is widely recognised by UK employers across construction, healthcare, manufacturing, logistics, facilities management, hospitality, and the public sector. In many organisations, completing IOSH Managing Safely is a mandatory requirement for promotion to a supervisory or management role. The course is almost always employer-funded.

IOSH Managing Safely Assessment: Project and Test Explained

The IOSH Managing Safely assessment has two mandatory components — the Project and the Test. Both must be passed to achieve the qualification. Failing either component requires a resit of that component only; a candidate who passes one and fails the other is not required to repeat the component they have already passed.

The Project is a workplace risk assessment report submitted to the IOSH-approved course provider and marked by IOSH. The project is scored out of 100. The pass threshold is approximately 60 out of 100, though course providers have historically used slightly different internal thresholds. Providers may describe results as a strong pass, borderline pass, or weak pass — IOSH does not award a formal distinction or merit grade for the project. The project is completed by the candidate in their own workplace using real hazards, real colleagues, and real control measures. It is not a theoretical exercise.

The Test consists of 45 multiple-choice questions completed in 45 minutes. Each question offers four answer options. The pass mark is 36 correct answers out of 45, which is equivalent to 80% — a high threshold that requires solid knowledge of all Managing Safely module content. The test is typically completed on the final day of the classroom course. Candidates completing the qualification via e-learning complete the test online under invigilation conditions specified by their provider. Results are returned by the course provider on the day of the test in most cases. Candidates who fail the test may resit — resit arrangements are provider-dependent and may involve a waiting period and a resit fee.

The project is typically the more challenging component for candidates. Many candidates underestimate the level of specificity required in the hazard descriptions, risk matrix justification, and control measure selection. IOSH Managing Safely Project Sections: What You Must Complete

The IOSH Managing Safely project is a workplace risk assessment report structured into four or five sections, depending on the course provider's template. Each section contributes to the total score out of 100. The sections must be completed in sequence — the quality of each section depends directly on the quality of the section before it.

Section 1 — Hazard Identification: Candidates must identify a minimum of 4 workplace hazards, though most providers require 6 to 8 for the higher mark bands. Each hazard must be assigned to a recognised hazard category — Physical, Chemical, Biological, Ergonomic, or Psychosocial — and described with sufficient specificity to justify a risk rating in the next section. Vague descriptions such as "slipping hazard" score minimal marks. A scoring description names the hazard agent, the location or task, the people exposed, and any existing control failure.

Section 2 — Risk Assessment: For each identified hazard, the candidate completes a risk matrix using a Likelihood scale (1 = Rare to 5 = Almost Certain) and a Severity scale (1 = Negligible to 5 = Catastrophic). The Risk Rating is calculated as Likelihood × Severity, producing a score between 1 and 25. Risk ratings must be completed both before controls are applied (inherent risk) and after controls are applied (residual risk). The residual risk must always be lower than the inherent risk.

Section 3 — Control Measures (Hierarchy of Control): Candidates must identify control measures for each hazard following the Hierarchy of Control — working from Elimination (most effective) down through Substitution, Engineering Controls, and Administrative Controls, to PPE (least effective). A project that recommends PPE as the primary or sole control measure for a high-rated hazard will lose significant marks. PPE is the last resort, not the first response.

Section 4 — Action Plan: Candidates write SMART objectives (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) for the most important improvement actions identified through the risk assessment. Each action plan entry must name a responsible person, specify resources required, state a target completion date, and define a measurable success criterion. Vague action plan entries such as "improve safety awareness" score zero.

Section 5 — Reflective Review (provider-dependent): Some providers require a short reflective statement on what the candidate learned from completing the project. This section is not universal — check the provider's template.

Weak performance in the Hazard Identification section typically undermines all subsequent sections, because the risk ratings, control measures, and action plan entries all flow from the quality of the hazards identified.

Why Candidates Struggle With the IOSH Managing Safely Project

The most common reason IOSH Managing Safely projects are referred (returned for resubmission) or score poorly is not lack of health and safety knowledge — it is failure to meet the specificity and depth requirements of each section. The project is assessed on the quality of workplace analysis and practical judgement, not on essay structure or writing style.

The most common failure is hazards described too vaguely. A description such as "slipping hazard" gives the assessor no basis for a risk rating. A description such as "wet floor surface in the main warehouse entrance during shift changeover in winter months — the entrance is unroofed and surface water pools from 06:00 to 07:30 during the night shift changeover — 18 operatives use this entrance during this period and no non-slip matting or drainage gradient is in place" gives the assessor everything needed to justify a risk rating.

The second most common failure is risk matrix scores applied without justification. A likelihood score of 4 (Likely) requires a plausible explanation for why harm is expected to occur under current conditions — not just the number in the cell.

The third most common failure is control measures that go directly to PPE — for example, recommending "wear safety boots" as the primary control for a manual handling hazard — without considering or addressing higher-order controls such as mechanical handling aids or a team lift procedure. The Hierarchy of Control requires candidates to work from the top of the hierarchy downward and to state why higher-level controls are not practicable before recommending PPE.

The fourth most common failure is action plan objectives that are not SMART. Objectives that state "train all staff" or "improve housekeeping" fail the Specific and Measurable criteria immediately. Each action plan entry must name a specific action, a specific responsible person, specific resources, a specific completion date, and a measurable success criterion.

A fifth failure pattern is absence of legislative references. Candidates who do not reference the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 (HSWA) or the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (MHSWR), specifically Regulation 3, in the risk assessment section miss marks that are available for demonstrating awareness of the legal framework underpinning the risk assessment duty.

IOSH Managing Safely Test: Topics, Format, and How to Prepare

The IOSH Managing Safely test is 45 multiple-choice questions completed in 45 minutes. Each question presents four answer options. The pass mark is 36 correct answers out of 45, equivalent to 80% — which is a high threshold compared to many other qualification tests at this level. Candidates must demonstrate solid knowledge of all Managing Safely module content, not just the project-related topics.

The test covers: hazard identification and categorisation; risk assessment principles including the Likelihood × Severity matrix; the Hierarchy of Control and risk control principles; the legal framework (Health and Safety at Work Act 1974, Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999); incident investigation methods and root cause analysis; measuring and reviewing health and safety performance; managing contractors safely; and understanding occupational health issues.

The test is taken on the final day of classroom training in most cases. E-learning candidates complete it online under invigilation conditions set by their provider — these vary and should be confirmed with the provider before the test date.

Candidates who fail the test may resit. Resit arrangements are provider-dependent and may involve a waiting period, an additional fee, and in some cases a requirement to reattend part of the course. Candidates should confirm the resit process with their provider at the start of the course, not after a failure.

Preparation for the test should focus on the legislation modules, the incident investigation module, and the risk control module — these are the areas where candidates most commonly lose marks. Reviewing all module handouts, completing practice multiple-choice questions, and understanding the precise definitions of terms used in the IOSH Managing Safely syllabus (such as the distinction between a hazard and a risk, or the difference between qualitative and quantitative risk assessment) is the most effective preparation strategy.

Legislation Behind the IOSH Managing Safely Project

The IOSH Managing Safely project is grounded in UK health and safety law. Understanding the legislation that underpins the risk assessment duty is not optional — referencing it correctly in the project report earns marks that candidates who omit it do not receive.

The Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 (HSWA) is the primary piece of UK health and safety legislation. It places a general duty on every employer to ensure, so far as is reasonably practicable, the health, safety, and welfare of all employees. It also places duties on employers regarding non-employees — contractors, visitors, and members of the public — who may be affected by the employer's work activities. The HSWA is the legislative foundation on which all subsequent UK health and safety regulations are built.

The Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (MHSWR), Regulation 3 is the specific legal provision that creates the duty to conduct risk assessments. Regulation 3 requires every employer to carry out a suitable and sufficient assessment of the risks to the health and safety of their employees and of any other persons who may be affected by their work activities. This is the legal basis for the IOSH Managing Safely project itself — the project is designed to demonstrate that a candidate can fulfil this legal duty in their own workplace.

Regulation 3 also requires employers with five or more employees to record the significant findings of their risk assessment. This makes documented risk assessment a legal requirement for any organisation of meaningful size — not merely good practice or voluntary best practice.

Referencing the HSWA 1974 and MHSWR 1999 Regulation 3 within the project report — by their full name and year, not vague references to "health and safety law" — demonstrates legislative awareness and is rewarded in the marking scheme. Additional regulations applicable to specific hazards (such as COSHH Regulations 2002 for chemical hazards, Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992, or Working at Height Regulations 2005) should also be referenced in the hazard identification section where applicable.

How IOSH Managing Safely Connects to IOSH Membership and Further Qualifications

IOSH Managing Safely is the entry point for many health and safety professionals. Successfully completing it and gaining practical workplace experience can lead to progression through the IOSH membership grades: Technician Safety Practitioner (TechIOSH), Graduate Member (GradIOSH), and Chartered Member (CMIOSH) — with Chartered Fellow (CFIOSH) representing the highest grade. Each grade has specific experience and qualification requirements beyond the Managing Safely level.

The IOSH Managing Safely qualification is not the same as the NEBOSH General Certificate in Occupational Health and Safety, which is a Level 3 qualification but at a higher level of academic and practical demand than IOSH Managing Safely. NEBOSH is typically the next step for candidates who wish to pursue a career in health and safety rather than simply meet an employer's management training requirement. Many candidates complete IOSH Managing Safely first as a grounding qualification before moving to NEBOSH.
