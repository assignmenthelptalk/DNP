IOSH Managing Safely Hazard Identification Help: How to Choose, Categorise, and Describe Workplace Hazards in Your Project

The hazard identification section of the IOSH Managing Safely project requires candidates to identify a minimum of 4 to 8 workplace hazards, assign each to a recognised hazard category, and describe each hazard with sufficient specificity to justify a meaningful risk rating in the next section. Without correctly identified and described hazards, every subsequent section — risk assessment, control measures, action plan — cannot be completed accurately. The quality of the hazard identification section determines the quality of the entire project report.

What the IOSH Managing Safely Hazard Identification Section Requires

The hazard identification section of the IOSH Managing Safely project requires candidates to identify real workplace hazards, assign each to one of five recognised hazard categories, and describe each with sufficient detail to allow a risk rating to be calculated and justified in the risk assessment section that follows.

The minimum hazard count required for a pass is 4 hazards with most IOSH-approved providers. Identifying 6 to 8 hazards is recommended for candidates aiming for higher mark bands — more hazards provide more substantive content for the risk assessment and control measures sections, both of which are scored on the quality and completeness of their coverage of the hazards identified.

Each hazard must be assigned to a category. The five recognised hazard categories are Physical, Chemical, Biological, Ergonomic, and Psychosocial. Assessors look for category variety — a project that identifies 6 hazards all from the Physical category signals a narrow understanding of workplace risk. Identifying hazards from at least 3 different categories demonstrates the range of awareness the qualification requires.

Hazards must be genuine to the candidate's actual workplace. The project assessor is an experienced health and safety professional who will assess whether the identified hazards are plausible for the candidate's industry and role. Fabricated hazards, or hazards borrowed from a workplace the candidate does not work in, are detectable and risk project failure.

Each hazard description must enable the risk assessment that follows. The description must identify the hazard agent (what causes the harm), the task or location where exposure occurs, and the people at risk. Vague hazards produce unjustifiable risk ratings. The legal basis for the duty to identify and assess risk is the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 (MHSWR), Regulation 3, which requires every employer to carry out a suitable and sufficient assessment of risks to employees and others affected by the work.

The Five Hazard Categories in IOSH Managing Safely — With Workplace Examples

The five hazard categories used in the IOSH Managing Safely project correspond to the five recognised types of occupational hazard. Each category has specific regulations that govern the duty to manage it, and referencing those regulations in the hazard identification section earns marks.

Physical hazards include any source of physical energy or force that can cause harm. Examples at the specificity required by the project:

  • Noise exceeding 85dB(A) TWA from continuous grinding operations in a metal fabrication workshop — operatives work within 2 metres of the grinding station for up to 6 hours per shift without hearing protection (Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005)
  • Whole-body vibration from daily forklift truck operation on uneven warehouse flooring — drivers operate for 4 to 6 hours per shift (Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005)
  • Manual handling of incoming stock items weighing 30 to 40kg from ground level to shelf height at 1.8 metres — no mechanical handling aid provided and no team lift procedure in place (Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992)
  • Slips and trips from oil contamination on the production floor between Machinery Bay 3 and the emergency exit — contamination occurs from hydraulic system leaks and is addressed reactively rather than preventively
  • Working at height on roof-mounted air conditioning units at 4 metres above ground level — no edge protection and access via an unsecured ladder (Working at Height Regulations 2005)
  • Extreme cold in refrigerated storage areas maintained at -18°C — workers access the area for up to 20 minutes at a time without thermal protective clothing

Chemical hazards include substances that can cause harm through inhalation, skin contact, ingestion, or injection. Relevant legislation: Control of Substances Hazardous to Health Regulations 2002 (COSHH). Examples:

  • Isocyanate exposure during two-pack polyurethane spray painting in the vehicle repair bay — isocyanates are a leading cause of occupational asthma and are classified as sensitisers under COSHH 2002; no respiratory protective equipment in use during spraying
  • Respirable crystalline silica dust generated during concrete cutting with an angle grinder — silica dust is a Group 1 carcinogen; cutting is performed without LEV or RPE in an unventilated maintenance yard
  • Solvent vapour from adhesive application in a confined gluing station — the adhesive contains n-hexane, a neurotoxic solvent; no LEV installed and the area is inadequately ventilated
  • Wood dust in the joinery workshop — wood dust from hardwoods and softwoods is classified as a Group 1 carcinogen (hardwood) under COSHH 2002; dust extraction is not fitted to all bench tools

Biological hazards include microorganisms and biological agents that can cause infection, allergy, or toxin exposure. Examples:

  • Legionella bacteria in a poorly maintained water cooling tower — the cooling tower has not been inspected or cleaned within the last 12 months; the site employs maintenance engineers who work adjacent to the tower (Approved Code of Practice L8 — Legionellosis)
  • Bloodborne pathogen exposure from needlestick injury risk — healthcare support workers disposing of sharps without a sharps box immediately available at the point of use
  • Mould in a damp maintenance access corridor — black mould growth on walls in a maintenance corridor accessed weekly by maintenance engineers; no respiratory protection in use
  • Zoonotic infection risk on an agricultural site — livestock handlers working with cattle without gloves or face protection during calving, with risk of Cryptosporidium and Campylobacter exposure

Ergonomic hazards include workplace conditions that cause musculoskeletal harm through biomechanical stress. Relevant legislation: Display Screen Equipment Regulations 1992 (DSE). Examples:

  • Repetitive upper limb movement from production line assembly — operatives perform more than 2,000 repetitive upper limb movements per hour for the full 8-hour shift without rotation; no ergonomic assessment has been conducted
  • DSE posture hazard — office-based staff use a laptop as their primary workstation for 7 to 8 hours per day without a separate keyboard, mouse, or monitor stand; no DSE assessment has been completed (Display Screen Equipment Regulations 1992)
  • Contact stress from vibrating hand tools — construction operatives use hand-arm vibrating tools including disc grinders and hammer drills for periods exceeding the Exposure Action Value of 2.5 m/s² A(8) per day (Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005)
  • Sustained awkward posture during under-floor maintenance — engineers work in a prone or crouched position in a 0.8-metre clearance void for up to 2 hours without task rotation

Psychosocial hazards include workplace conditions that cause psychological harm. Relevant framework: HSE Management Standards for work-related stress. Examples:

  • Work-related stress arising from excessive workload following a 30% staff reduction — remaining employees report completing the work of 1.3 full-time equivalents each; no workload review or occupational health referral process is in place (HSE Management Standards: Demands)
  • Violence and aggression risk for lone retail staff working after 9pm — staff close the store alone with no panic alarm, no CCTV visible to staff, and no check-in procedure with a manager
  • Lone working without a monitoring procedure — field engineers visit client premises alone, sometimes in isolated locations, with no check-in schedule and no escalation process if contact is not made
  • Shift work disrupting circadian rhythm — workers on a rotating continental shift pattern (2 days, 2 nights, 4 off) have not received occupational health guidance on managing fatigue and sleep disruption

How to Write a Hazard Description That Scores Marks

A hazard description that scores marks answers five specific questions: What is the hazard? Where does it occur? During which task or activity? Who is exposed? What existing control is absent or failing? A description that omits any of these components cannot support a justified risk rating and will score poorly in the hazard identification section and in the risk assessment section that depends on it.

The contrast between a weak and a strong hazard description is the most important practical distinction in the entire project. Consider two descriptions of an electrical hazard:

WEAK (will not score): "Electrical hazard in the workplace."

STRONG (will score): "Exposed 240V single-phase wiring on the south wall of maintenance Room 12, accessed daily by non-electrical maintenance staff during equipment checks — no lockout/tagout isolation procedure is in place and there is no warning signage at the room entrance."

The strong version identifies the hazard agent (240V single-phase wiring), the precise location (south wall of maintenance Room 12), the exposure mechanism (daily access during equipment checks), the people at risk (non-electrical maintenance staff — people who are not qualified to work near live conductors), and the existing control failure (no isolation procedure, no warning signage). This level of specificity is what allows the risk matrix rating that follows to be justified. A likelihood score of 4 (Likely) is defensible when access is daily and there is no isolation procedure. It is not defensible when the description says only "electrical hazard."

A second before-and-after example for a manual handling hazard:

WEAK: "Manual handling hazard."

STRONG: "Warehouse operatives manually lifting incoming stock pallets weighing 30 to 40kg from the goods-in bay floor to shelf height at 1.8 metres — no mechanical pallet handling aid is provided, no team lift procedure is in place, and no manual handling training has been delivered to the current workforce in the past 3 years."

The strong description names the specific weight range, the exact task (floor to shelf height at 1.8m), the workforce affected (warehouse operatives), and the three specific control failures — each of which maps to a control measure that must be recommended in the control measures section. A risk rating of Likelihood 4 × Severity 3 = 12 (Medium, requiring action) is now fully justifiable from this description. The same rating applied to "manual handling hazard" is not.

How to Choose the Right Hazards for Your Workplace

Hazard selection determines the quality of every section that follows. Selecting hazards that are real, varied, and high-rated produces a project with substantive content in the risk assessment, control measures, and action plan sections. Selecting hazards that are trivial, low-rated, or implausible for the candidate's industry undermines the project from the first page.

Select hazards that are genuine to the candidate's actual work environment. Assessors are experienced health and safety professionals who assess plausibility against the candidate's stated industry and role. A warehouse candidate who lists Legionella as a primary hazard without any water systems in their workplace will be questioned. A hospital ward administrator who lists heavy machinery entrapment as a hazard will not be credible. The project must reflect what the candidate actually encounters at work.

Select hazards from at least 3 different categories. A project containing only physical hazards signals limited awareness of the full spectrum of workplace risk. Identifying hazards from Physical, Chemical, and Psychosocial categories, for example, demonstrates that the candidate understands that harm can arise from multiple directions — not just from machinery and slips.

Prioritise hazards that have a high inherent risk rating before controls are applied. A hazard rated Very High (20–25) or High (15–19) generates more meaningful content for the control measures section and the action plan. A hazard already at Low risk (1–6) with comprehensive controls in place leaves little for the subsequent sections to address and may produce an action plan with nothing significant to improve.

Avoid selecting hazards that are already comprehensively controlled. If a thorough engineering control is already in place and working — for example, a fully guarded machine with an interlocked guard in good working order — the before-controls risk rating may already be Low and the control measures section will have no meaningful recommendations to make.

Consider which hazards connect to specific legislation. Selecting a chemical hazard allows reference to COSHH 2002. Selecting a manual handling hazard allows reference to the Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992. Selecting a DSE hazard allows reference to the Display Screen Equipment Regulations 1992. Legislation references in the hazard identification section earn marks and demonstrate legal competence.

Common Hazard Identification Mistakes That Cost Marks

Vague hazard descriptions are the primary cause of low marks in the hazard identification section — and in every section that depends on it. Each failure pattern below has a direct consequence for the project score.

Vague descriptions used as hazard entries: "Poor housekeeping" is not a hazard — it is a cause or a condition. "Floor contaminated with hydraulic oil in the area between Machinery Bay 3 and the emergency exit, creating a slip hazard for all production staff during shift changes — the contamination occurs from an unrepaired hydraulic cylinder seal on Press 4 and is cleaned reactively with paper towels, leaving a residual film on the floor surface" is a hazard. Vague descriptions cannot be rated on the risk matrix — the assessor has no basis for the likelihood or severity score.

Insufficient number of hazards: Submitting only 4 hazards when the provider awards marks for 6 to 8 results in lost marks both in this section and in the risk assessment section that follows. Fewer hazards mean fewer rows in the risk matrix, fewer control measure entries, and fewer action plan objectives — each of which represents a scoring opportunity not taken.

All hazards from one category: Selecting 6 physical hazards only — slips, trips, manual handling, noise, vibration, working at height — demonstrates a one-dimensional understanding of workplace risk. The marking scheme rewards category range. Selecting from Physical, Chemical, and Psychosocial categories, for example, shows breadth of hazard awareness.

Fabricated or industry-inappropriate hazards: Hazards that do not plausibly exist in the candidate's stated workplace undermine the project's credibility with the assessor. If the project template asks for the candidate's workplace and role, the hazards listed must be consistent with that workplace and role.

Using generic category labels as descriptions: "Chemical hazard," "fire hazard," "biological hazard" — these are category names, not hazard descriptions. They tell the assessor nothing about the agent, the location, the task, or the people exposed.

Failing to reference applicable legislation: Where specific regulations apply to a hazard category — COSHH 2002 for chemical hazards, Working at Height Regulations 2005 for height hazards, Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992 for manual handling — naming the regulation in the hazard identification section demonstrates legal competence that is rewarded in the marking scheme.

How to Present Hazard Identification in the IOSH Project Report

Most IOSH-approved providers supply a structured project template. The hazard identification section is typically a table with the following columns: Hazard Number, Hazard Description, Hazard Category, People at Risk, and Applicable Legislation. The candidate completes one row per identified hazard.

Where a provider does not supply a template, or where the candidate is using a self-designed format, a table is strongly preferred over narrative prose. A table allows the assessor to cross-reference each hazard directly against the corresponding row in the risk assessment table — the numbering must carry through from Section 1 to Section 2 (Hazard 1 in the identification table becomes Row 1 in the risk matrix).

Each hazard row must be numbered sequentially. These numbers are the reference system for the entire project — the risk assessment table, the control measures section, and the action plan all refer back to these hazard numbers. A project without numbered hazards creates ambiguity for the assessor and loses the cross-referencing marks available for demonstrating that the project sections are coherently linked.

The "People at Risk" column must not simply say "employees." It must identify which employees, approximately how many, and any vulnerability factors relevant to the risk rating. For example: "warehouse operatives (approximately 12 on day shift) and agency staff during the goods-in window (08:00–10:00 Monday to Friday, approximately 4 additional persons)." The distinction between direct employees and contractors or agency staff is relevant — MHSWR 1999 Regulation 3 extends the duty to assess risk to others affected by the work, not just direct employees.

The "Applicable Legislation" column should cite the most specific regulation that applies — not just the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974. For a manual handling hazard, cite the Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992. For a chemical hazard, cite COSHH Regulations 2002. For a DSE hazard, cite the Display Screen Equipment Regulations 1992. The HSWA 1974 can be referenced as the overarching legislative framework but should not be the only legislation cited where more specific regulations exist.

The hazard identification section does not end with the description — it feeds directly into the risk assessment section, where each identified hazard becomes a row in the risk matrix. How hazard identification connects to risk rating, and why the specificity of your descriptions determines the defensibility of your risk scores, is covered in the risk assessment section guidance below.

How Hazard Identification Connects to the Risk Assessment Section

Each hazard identified and described in Section 1 becomes a row in the risk matrix in Section 2. The specificity of the hazard description directly determines whether the risk rating in that row can be justified. A vague hazard produces an arbitrary risk rating — the assessor cannot verify that a likelihood score of 4 (Likely) is appropriate because no exposure scenario has been described. A specific hazard allows the candidate to justify why a particular likelihood and severity score was chosen — because the description provides the exact conditions, frequency, and failure points that determine the risk level.

The risk assessment section requires the candidate to rate each hazard before and after control measures are applied. The "before" rating reflects the inherent risk — the risk that exists given current conditions and any existing inadequate controls. The "after" rating reflects the residual risk — the risk remaining after the control measures recommended in Section 3 are fully implemented. The relationship between hazard description, risk rating, and control measures is the evaluative chain the assessor uses to assess the project as a whole. A strong hazard description supports a justified risk rating, which supports a proportionate set of control measures, which supports a meaningful action plan.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many hazards do I need to identify in the IOSH Managing Safely project?

Most IOSH Managing Safely providers require a minimum of 4 hazards for a pass. Identifying 6 to 8 hazards from varied categories (Physical, Chemical, Biological, Ergonomic, Psychosocial) is recommended to achieve higher mark bands, as more hazards give the risk assessment and control measures sections more substantive content to assess.

Can I use hazards from a different workplace if mine does not have many hazards?

Hazards must be genuine to your own workplace — assessors are experienced health and safety professionals who verify that identified hazards are plausible for your industry and role. Fabricating or borrowing hazards from an unrelated environment risks project failure. If your workplace has limited hazards, broaden your scope to include all five categories and examine tasks, not just fixed locations.

What is the difference between a hazard and a risk in the IOSH project?

A hazard is the source of potential harm — for example, exposed machinery guarding. A risk is the likelihood and severity of harm occurring from that hazard given current controls. The IOSH project requires both: hazard identification in Section 1, risk quantification (using the Likelihood × Severity matrix) in Section 2. Conflating the two terms in the project report loses marks.

Does each hazard need to reference a specific piece of legislation?

Referencing applicable legislation in the hazard identification section demonstrates legal awareness and is rewarded in most IOSH Managing Safely marking schemes. Each hazard category has specific regulations: Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992 for manual handling hazards, COSHH Regulations 2002 for chemical hazards, Display Screen Equipment Regulations 1992 for ergonomic DSE hazards, and Working at Height Regulations 2005 for height-related physical hazards.
