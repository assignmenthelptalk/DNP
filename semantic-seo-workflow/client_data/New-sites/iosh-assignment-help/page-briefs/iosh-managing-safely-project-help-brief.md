#### H2: Choosing the Right Workplace for Your IOSH Project
> **EAV to cover**: Workplace selection criteria, what counts as a valid workplace, scope definition (too broad vs appropriate), why workplace choice affects hazard quality
> **Word count**: 200–300 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Candidates should choose a workplace they know well and have access to — their current job role or a workplace they can physically observe
> - Scope must be manageable: choose a specific area (a warehouse loading bay, a clinical room, a production line section) rather than an entire organisation
> - Too broad a scope leads to superficial hazard descriptions that fail the specificity requirements of Section 1
> - The workplace must present identifiable hazards — a purely administrative desk environment with no physical hazards will produce weak Section 1 evidence. Choose a workplace with operational activity.
> - Candidates in desk-based roles can include ergonomic hazards, psychosocial hazards (workload, lone working), and fire/electrical hazards for a valid project

#### H2: IOSH Project Section 1 — Hazard Identification: What You Must Include
> **EAV to cover**: Minimum hazard count (4+, many providers 6–8), hazard categories (physical, chemical, biological, ergonomic, psychosocial), specificity requirement, what vague vs precise descriptions look like
> **Word count**: 300–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Most IOSH-approved providers require a minimum of 4 workplace hazards — many specify 6 to 8 for a well-evidenced submission
> - Hazards must be categorised by type: Physical (machinery, noise, slip/trip surfaces, manual handling), Chemical (substances, cleaning agents, fumes), Biological (pathogens, legionella, animal-borne), Ergonomic (workstation design, repetitive strain, display screen equipment), Psychosocial (work-related stress, lone working, shift patterns)
> - Each hazard must be described precisely: not "trip hazard" but "uneven concrete floor surface in the delivery bay entrance, approximately 2cm raised edge at the loading door threshold, present throughout the shift"
> - The hazard description must identify: what the hazard is, where it exists in the workplace, who is at risk, and under what conditions the risk is realised
> - Vague descriptions fail the specificity requirement and reduce the quality of all subsequent sections — the risk assessment and control measures cannot be specific if the hazard was not specific
> - See the dedicated Hazard Identification guidance page for full depth on categorisation, description techniques, and worked examples

#### H2: IOSH Project Section 2 — Risk Assessment: Likelihood, Severity, and the Risk Matrix
> **EAV to cover**: Risk matrix mechanics (L×S = RR), likelihood scale (1–5: Rare to Almost Certain), severity scale (1–5: Negligible to Catastrophic), risk rating bands (Low 1–6, Medium 7–14, High 15–19, Very High 20–25), before-and-after assessment requirement, justification of ratings
> **Word count**: 300–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - For each hazard identified, candidates complete a risk matrix: Likelihood × Severity = Risk Rating
> - Likelihood scale: 1 = Rare, 2 = Unlikely, 3 = Possible, 4 = Likely, 5 = Almost Certain
> - Severity scale: 1 = Negligible, 2 = Minor, 3 = Moderate, 4 = Major, 5 = Catastrophic
> - Risk Rating range: 1 (minimum) to 25 (maximum)
> - Risk level bands: Low = 1–6, Medium = 7–14, High = 15–19, Very High = 20–25
> - The risk assessment must be completed TWICE for each hazard: before control measures are applied (current state) and after control measures are applied (residual risk)
> - The difference between the two ratings demonstrates the effectiveness of the controls — a reduction from High (16) to Low (4) shows meaningful risk reduction
> - Ratings must be justified — do not simply assign numbers. Explain why the likelihood is assessed as "Likely" (e.g., "The loading bay floor is used by 8 HGV drivers daily under time pressure, making contact with the raised threshold probable without intervention")
> - Unjustified risk ratings are one of the most common reasons for a borderline rather than confident pass score

---

#### H2: IOSH Project Section 3 — Control Measures: Applying the Hierarchy of Control
> **EAV to cover**: Hierarchy of Control levels (Eliminate, Substitute, Engineering, Administrative, PPE), application order (most effective first), PPE as last resort, what counts as each level, common error (PPE as primary control)
> **Word count**: 250–350 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Control measures must be proposed in hierarchy order: 1. Elimination (remove the hazard entirely), 2. Substitution (replace with less hazardous alternative), 3. Engineering Controls (physical barriers, guards, ventilation, non-slip surfaces), 4. Administrative Controls (procedures, training, signage, job rotation, supervision), 5. PPE (last resort — gloves, hard hats, safety boots, hearing protection)
> - Candidates must apply controls starting at the top of the hierarchy and work down — proposing PPE as the first or only control is the most common error in this section and is specifically penalised
> - Example: for a manual handling hazard — Elimination (redesign task to remove manual handling), Substitution (use mechanical lifting aid instead of manual carry), Engineering (provide trolleys, adjustable work surfaces), Administrative (manual handling training, reduced load limits, two-person lifting procedure), PPE (back support belt — last resort)
> - The hierarchy is defined in ISO 45001 and the UK HSE guidance — referencing these strengthens the project
> - For the project to score well, candidates should apply at least 3 levels of the hierarchy to each significant hazard

---

#### H2: IOSH Project Section 4 — Action Plan: Writing SMART Objectives
> **EAV to cover**: SMART criteria (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound), action plan entry components (action, person responsible, resource required, target date, success measure), common non-SMART errors
> **Word count**: 250–350 words
> **Specific content**:
> - The action plan converts the control measures into formal improvement objectives using the SMART framework
> - Each action plan entry must include: the action to be taken (what), the person responsible (who), the resource required (budget, equipment, personnel), the target date (when — a specific date, not "ASAP"), the success measure (how you will know the action has been completed and is effective)
> - SMART breakdown: Specific = states exactly what will be done (not "improve manual handling" but "install a powered pallet truck at Bay 3 to eliminate manual carry of loads over 20kg"); Measurable = states how completion will be verified; Achievable = realistic given the organisation's resources; Relevant = directly addresses the risk identified in the risk assessment; Time-bound = states a specific deadline date
> - Most common action plan failure: objectives that state what will be improved without specifying how, who, by when, or how success will be measured — these are not SMART objectives
> - Each action plan entry should directly correspond to a control measure identified in Section 3 — the action plan is the implementation roadmap for those controls

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: How is the IOSH Managing Safely project scored?**
The IOSH Managing Safely project is marked out of 100 by the IOSH-approved course provider against IOSH marking criteria. The pass threshold is approximately 60 out of 100. Providers may describe the outcome as a strong pass, borderline pass, or weak pass — IOSH does not award a formal distinction grade for the project.

**Q: How many hazards do I need in the IOSH Managing Safely project?**
Most course providers require a minimum of 4 hazards, with many specifying 6 to 8 for a well-evidenced submission. Each hazard must be precisely described, categorised by hazard type (physical, chemical, biological, ergonomic, psychosocial), and carried through the full risk assessment and control measures sections.

**Q: What does the IOSH risk matrix look like?**
The IOSH Managing Safely risk matrix multiplies Likelihood (1=Rare to 5=Almost Certain) by Severity (1=Negligible to 5=Catastrophic) to produce a Risk Rating from 1 to 25. Risk levels are: Low (1–6), Medium (7–14), High (15–19), Very High (20–25). The assessment must be completed before and after control measures to show risk reduction.

**Q: What legislation should I reference in the IOSH Managing Safely project?**
The primary legislation to reference is the Health and Safety at Work Act 1974 and the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, specifically Regulation 3, which places a legal duty on employers to conduct suitable and sufficient risk assessments. Employers with five or more employees must record the significant findings in writing.
