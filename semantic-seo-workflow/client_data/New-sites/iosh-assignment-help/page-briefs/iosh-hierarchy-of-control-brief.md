**Derived Attributes**:
- **Hierarchy Framework Source**: HSE guidance and ISO 45001:2018 (Occupational Health and Safety Management Systems standard) — both endorse the same five-level hierarchy. Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, Schedule 1 (General Principles of Prevention) is the legislative basis — Principle (d): adapting to technical progress; Principle (f): replacing dangerous with non-dangerous or less dangerous; Principle (g): developing a coherent prevention policy
- **Elimination examples**: redesigning a manufacturing process to remove the need for a hazardous chemical entirely (e.g., switching from a solvent-based degreasing bath to an ultrasonic aqueous cleaning system); automating a manual handling task using a robotic palletiser so no human lifting is required; removing a tripping hazard by permanently relocating equipment (not just taping off the hazard)
- **Substitution examples**: replacing a solvent-based paint (containing toluene) with a water-based equivalent of equal technical performance; replacing a bulk concentrated acid cleaner with a pre-diluted, lower-concentration formulation dispensed by a closed dosing system; switching from a powered angle grinder (high vibration, high noise) to a quieter, lower-vibration diamond blade tool for the same cutting task
- **Engineering Controls examples**: interlocked machine guarding that stops the machine automatically when the guard is opened; local exhaust ventilation (LEV) for a woodworking lathe to capture wood dust at source before it enters the breathing zone; noise enclosure around a compressor to reduce noise at source; non-slip flooring installed in Machinery Bay 3 to eliminate the slip hazard from oil contamination; edge protection (guardrails and toe boards) at a 3-metre-high mezzanine floor; two-hand control device requiring simultaneous hand operation to prevent access to a machine's danger zone during operation
- **Administrative Controls examples**: permit to work system for confined space entry — written authorisation, atmospheric testing, rescue plan, standby person required before entry; manual handling training delivered to all warehouse operatives every 12 months (documented); job rotation schedule limiting noise-exposed workers to 2-hour maximum exposure periods before rotation to a quieter task; pre-shift inspection checklist for forklift trucks; safe system of work document for working at height tasks below the threshold requiring a Working at Height Regulations permit; lone worker check-in procedure — worker checks in by phone every 2 hours, supervisor escalates if no contact
- **PPE examples**: EN 352-compliant ear defenders (rated SNR 33dB) for residual noise exposure after engineering controls — must specify the standard, not just "ear defenders"; EN 374-compliant nitrile gloves (0.1mm minimum thickness) for skin contact with cleaning chemicals — must specify the type (nitrile, not generic "gloves"), the EN standard, and the inspection requirement (visual check before each use, replace if punctured or degraded); EN 166-compliant safety glasses for chemical splash risk — specify the impact and chemical splash rating; RPE (respiratory protective equipment) — half-face respirator with P3 filter for silica dust — specify the filter type, fit testing requirement, and face-seal check procedure
- **PPE specification depth**: IOSH project marks are awarded for specifying PPE correctly — "wear gloves" scores minimal marks; "EN 374-compliant nitrile examination gloves, minimum 0.1mm thickness, inspected visually before each use, replaced immediately if punctured or contaminated" scores fully

**Values per Attribute**:

### MAIN CONTENT

#### H1: IOSH Hierarchy of Control: How to Apply Control Measures Correctly in Your Managing Safely Project
> **Macro-context**: The hierarchy of control is the conceptual framework that determines how control measures must be selected and presented in the IOSH Managing Safely project. It is a five-level framework — from most to least effective — that requires candidates to work from the top (elimination) downward, applying the highest practicable control level before moving to the next. The most common project failure in this section is leading with PPE when higher-order controls are practicable.
> **First paragraph must**: Define the hierarchy of control immediately — do not open with a general statement about workplace safety. Open with: "The hierarchy of control is a five-level framework — endorsed by the HSE, ISO 45001:2018, and underpinned by the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, Schedule 1 — that ranks control measures from most to least effective for managing workplace hazards."

#### H2: What the Hierarchy of Control Is and Why the IOSH Project Requires It
> **EAV to cover**: Hierarchy of control (entity) → purpose, legal basis, five-level structure, most-to-least effectiveness ranking (attributes) → specific values per attribute (values)
> **Word count**: 280–350 words
> **Specific content**:
> - The hierarchy exists because not all control measures are equally effective — PPE depends on the individual wearing it correctly every time; engineering controls function regardless of individual behaviour
> - Legal basis: MHSWR 1999, Schedule 1 — General Principles of Prevention — lists the order in which risk must be managed: avoid risks; evaluate unavoidable risks; combat risks at source; adapt to technical progress; replace dangerous with less dangerous; develop a coherent prevention policy; give priority to collective protective measures over individual protective measures (i.e., engineering over PPE); give appropriate instructions to workers
> - ISO 45001:2018, Clause 8.1.2 — Hierarchy of Controls — requires organisations to implement controls in the order: eliminate, substitute, use engineering controls, use administrative controls, use PPE
> - The IOSH project requires candidates to list controls for each identified hazard in hierarchy order — leading with the highest practicable control level
> - Assessors look specifically for whether candidates have considered and rejected higher-level controls before recommending PPE — simply listing PPE without this justification loses marks
> - The pyramid representation: Elimination (top, widest protection, fewest people involved) → Substitution → Engineering Controls → Administrative Controls → PPE (bottom, narrowest protection, reliant on individual compliance)

#### H2: Level 1 — Elimination: Removing the Hazard Completely
> **EAV to cover**: Elimination (entity) → definition, mechanism, practicability assessment, workplace examples (attributes) → specific elimination actions with industry context (values)
> **Word count**: 250–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Elimination means the hazard no longer exists — it has been removed from the workplace entirely, not just reduced or managed
> - Most effective control level because it produces zero residual risk from the eliminated hazard
> - Practical examples: automating a repetitive manual lifting task with a robotic palletiser — the manual handling hazard is eliminated, not reduced; switching from an aqueous degreasing system requiring a hazardous solvent bath to an ultrasonic cleaner — the chemical hazard is eliminated; permanently relocating a piece of trip-hazard equipment from a walkway rather than marking or guarding it
> - Working at height: elimination example — redesigning a maintenance procedure so it can be performed from ground level using a long-handled tool, removing the need to work at height entirely (Working at Height Regulations 2005, Regulation 6(1) — avoid work at height where reasonably practicable)
> - Practicability statement: candidates must state whether elimination is practicable. If it is not — because the hazard is inherent to the process (e.g., a foundry cannot eliminate heat) — the candidate must state this and move to the next control level. "Elimination is not practicable because the casting process requires molten metal at temperatures exceeding 1,400°C — the hazard cannot be removed without ceasing production."

#### H2: Level 2 — Substitution: Replacing the Hazardous with the Less Hazardous
> **EAV to cover**: Substitution (entity) → definition, mechanism, technical feasibility requirement, examples per hazard type (attributes) → specific substitution actions with measurable hazard reduction (values)
> **Word count**: 250–320 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Substitution replaces a hazardous substance, material, piece of equipment, or process with one that poses lower risk — without eliminating the underlying task
> - The substituted alternative must be technically capable of performing the same function — substitution is not valid if the replacement cannot do the job
> - Chemical examples: replacing a solvent-based contact adhesive (containing n-hexane, a neurotoxin) with a water-based equivalent of equal bonding strength — hazard category changes from Chemical/COSHH-notifiable to low-hazard; replacing a bulk concentrated acid descaler with a pre-diluted, pH 2–3 formulation dispensed via a closed dosing system — reduces concentration hazard and splash risk
> - Noise examples: replacing a pneumatic impact wrench (noise level 102dB(A)) with an electric torque wrench (noise level 76dB(A)) for the same fastening task — noise exposure is reduced below the lower exposure action value (80dB(A) LEP,d under Control of Noise at Work Regulations 2005), potentially eliminating the need for hearing protection
> - Vibration examples: replacing a petrol-driven plate compactor with a high-frequency electric roller for surface compaction — HAV (hand-arm vibration) daily exposure may be reduced below the Exposure Action Value (2.5 m/s² A(8)) under Control of Vibration at Work Regulations 2005
> - Practicability statement: candidates must address whether the substitute meets the technical performance requirement. "The water-based adhesive was evaluated and confirmed by the supplier to meet the bond strength specification for this application."

#### H2: Level 3 — Engineering Controls: Physical Barriers and Mechanical Solutions
> **EAV to cover**: Engineering controls (entity) → definition, subcategories (guarding, ventilation, segregation, noise control, fall prevention), how they work independently of individual behaviour (attributes) → specific engineering control examples with technical specification (values)
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Engineering controls are physical or mechanical measures that remove or reduce the hazard at source — they do not rely on individual compliance to function
> - Subcategories and examples:
>   - **Machine guarding**: interlocked fixed guards on a conveyor drive that cut power to the motor automatically when the guard is opened — protects against entrapment and entanglement; two-hand control device requiring simultaneous depression to activate a press — prevents access to the danger zone during the machine cycle
>   - **Local exhaust ventilation (LEV)**: capture hood positioned 150mm above a woodworking lathe extraction point to remove wood dust (Group 1 carcinogen under COSHH 2002) before it enters the operator's breathing zone; LEV systems must be examined and tested every 14 months under COSHH Regulation 9
>   - **Noise control at source**: acoustic enclosure around a diesel generator reducing noise at the operator position from 98dB(A) to 82dB(A); acoustic screens between workstations in a call centre environment reducing speech interference
>   - **Slip prevention**: anti-slip flooring tiles (R11 or R12 rating to DIN 51130) installed in the food production area to prevent slips from oil and water contamination — a physical measure that functions continuously without worker action
>   - **Fall prevention**: edge protection system (top rail, intermediate rail, toe board to BS EN 13374 Class A) at a 3-metre mezzanine loading bay; scissor lift platform as an alternative to ladder access for maintenance tasks above 2 metres
>   - **Segregation**: pedestrian/vehicle segregation using physical barriers (Armco crash barriers) in a warehouse — removes the proximity hazard between forklift trucks and pedestrians
> - Engineering controls in the IOSH project: list each engineering control with a description of how it reduces the hazard — not just the name of the control. "Install LEV" is insufficient; "Install capture-hood LEV at woodworking lathe extraction point, designed to achieve capture velocity of 0.5m/s at source, examined and tested every 14 months per COSHH Regulation 9" scores marks.

#### H2: Level 4 — Administrative Controls: Procedures, Training, and Work Organisation
> **EAV to cover**: Administrative controls (entity) → definition, subcategories (procedures, training, supervision, permits to work, job rotation, signage), limitations (rely on human compliance), examples (attributes) → specific administrative controls with required documentation or frequency (values)
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - Administrative controls manage how work is done — they reduce exposure through rules, training, and organisation, but do not physically alter the hazard. They are less effective than engineering controls because they depend on consistent human compliance.
> - Subcategories and examples:
>   - **Safe systems of work / procedures**: written manual handling procedure specifying team lift requirement for loads above 25kg, maximum single-person lift of 15kg, use of mechanical aids for loads above 25kg — reviewed annually and signed off by workers as evidence of instruction
>   - **Permit to work systems**: confined space entry permit — written authorisation required before entry, atmospheric monitoring for oxygen depletion and toxic gas, rescue team on standby, no entry if conditions not met. Working at height permit for tasks above 2 metres using non-fixed access equipment.
>   - **Training**: COSHH awareness training for all chemical users, delivered at induction and refreshed every 2 years; manual handling training for all warehouse operatives — documented with trainer name, date, content summary, and attendee signature
>   - **Job rotation / exposure limitation**: rotating noise-exposed workers between high-noise tasks (>85dB(A)) and quiet tasks after a maximum 2-hour exposure period to reduce daily noise dose below the Upper Exposure Action Value (85dB(A) LEP,d)
>   - **Supervision**: weekly supervisor walk-round inspection of forklift truck operating areas to verify segregation barriers are in place and pedestrian routes are unobstructed — findings recorded and signed
>   - **Signage**: mandatory PPE signage at entry points to noise zones, chemical storage areas, and mandatory hard hat areas — must comply with Health and Safety (Safety Signs and Signals) Regulations 1996 (pictogram format, yellow/black for warning, blue/white for mandatory)
> - Limitation to note: administrative controls must be actively maintained — training becomes outdated, permits get bypassed, procedures are ignored under production pressure. For this reason they sit below engineering controls in effectiveness.

#### H2: Level 5 — PPE: The Last Resort and How to Specify It Correctly
> **EAV to cover**: PPE (entity) → definition, why it is last resort, specification requirements per PPE type, EN standards, maintenance obligations, IOSH project marking criteria for PPE (attributes) → specific PPE types with full specification values (values)
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - PPE is the last line of defence — it does not reduce the hazard; it only protects the individual if worn correctly, consistently, and in good condition. If PPE fails (torn glove, incorrect fit, not worn for 30 seconds of a task), the protection is lost entirely.
> - PPE should only appear in the IOSH project as a COMPLEMENTARY control alongside higher-level controls for residual risk — not as the primary or sole control for a hazard rated Medium, High, or Very High
> - Specification requirements for the IOSH project — "wear gloves" loses marks; a full specification scores marks:
>   - **Hearing protection**: EN 352-1 compliant ear muffs, SNR value sufficient to reduce exposure below 80dB(A) at the ear — for 98dB(A) environment, SNR 33dB minimum; inspected before each use; replaced if cushions are cracked or seal is degraded; stored in protective case when not in use
>   - **Eye / face protection**: EN 166-compliant safety glasses with side shields for chemical splash risk — minimum optical class 1; replaced if lens is scratched (scratched lens distorts vision); stored in carry case
>   - **Hand protection**: EN 374-compliant nitrile examination gloves, minimum 0.1mm thickness, for contact with pH 2–3 acid descaler — inspected visually before each use, replaced immediately if punctured or contaminated; latex-free to avoid latex sensitisation
>   - **Respiratory protective equipment (RPE)**: half-face respirator with P3 filter (EN 143) for silica dust in concrete cutting operations — P3 filters 99.95% of particles; fit test required before first use (quantitative or qualitative per HSE guidance); face-seal check (negative pressure check) before each use; filter replaced when breathing resistance increases
>   - **Head protection**: EN 397 industrial safety helmet for overhead hazard exposure on construction sites — inspected for cracks and UV degradation before each use, replaced after any significant impact, maximum service life 3–5 years from manufacture date (manufacturer's guidance)
> - Personal Protective Equipment at Work Regulations 1992 (as amended 2022): employer duty to provide PPE free of charge where risk cannot be adequately controlled by other means; duty to ensure it is suitable, maintained, and that employees are trained in its correct use

#### H2: How to Apply the Hierarchy of Control in Your IOSH Project
> **EAV to cover**: Application in IOSH project (entity) → hierarchy ordering requirement, practicability justification, presentation format, before/after risk rating connection (attributes) → specific guidance on what assessors look for per column in the project template (values)
> **Word count**: 280–350 words
> **Specific content**:
> - For each hazard, the control measures column must list controls in hierarchy order — start with the highest level that is practicable and work down
> - Each control must include: the type (elimination/substitution/engineering/administrative/PPE), the specific action, and where relevant the technical specification or standard
> - Where a higher-level control is not practicable, state this explicitly with a brief reason — this demonstrates that the hierarchy has been considered, not skipped
> - Example presentation for a noise hazard (98dB(A) grinding operation):
>   - Elimination: not practicable — the grinding process is essential to the manufacturing specification
>   - Substitution: evaluate quieter grinding technology — replace with diamond blade cutter (76dB(A)) where technically compatible; implement where compatible
>   - Engineering: acoustic enclosure around grinding station where fixed process allows; acoustic screens between workstations
>   - Administrative: limit exposure duration — rotate workers after maximum 2 hours on grinding tasks; schedule high-noise tasks away from adjacent non-noise-exposed areas; annual audiometric surveillance for all workers regularly exposed above 85dB(A)
>   - PPE: EN 352-1 ear muffs, SNR 33dB minimum, as last resort for residual exposure — inspected before each use, replaced annually or when degraded
> - The before/after risk rating in the risk assessment section must reflect the combined effect of these controls — each control reduces likelihood, severity, or both, and the after rating should show this reduction realistically

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: Why is PPE considered the least effective control measure in the IOSH hierarchy?**
PPE is the least effective control because it does not remove or reduce the hazard itself — it only provides a barrier between the hazard and the individual. Protection depends entirely on the PPE being worn correctly every time, fitting properly, being in good condition, and not failing. Engineering controls — such as machine guards or local exhaust ventilation — function regardless of individual behaviour and protect everyone in the area continuously. This is why the IOSH project and the Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999 require PPE to be treated as a last resort after higher-level controls have been considered.

**Q: Do I need to show that I considered elimination before recommending other controls?**
Demonstrating that you have considered and assessed elimination (and substitution) before moving to engineering or administrative controls is what differentiates a high-scoring project from an average one. Assessors look for evidence that the hierarchy has been worked through deliberately — not just applied as a checklist. Where elimination is not practicable, state the specific reason why. "Elimination was considered but is not practicable because the task requires the use of this hazardous substance for the process to function" scores marks; skipping elimination without comment does not.

**Q: Can I recommend both engineering controls and PPE for the same hazard?**
Combining controls from multiple hierarchy levels is correct practice and is expected in the IOSH project. For example, a noise hazard may be addressed with engineering controls (acoustic enclosure) to reduce noise at source, administrative controls (job rotation) to limit exposure duration, and PPE (ear muffs) for residual exposure that remains after engineering and administrative controls. PPE in this context is complementary — it addresses the residual risk remaining after higher controls. What scores poorly is recommending PPE as the only control where engineering or administrative controls are clearly practicable.

**Q: What is the difference between engineering controls and administrative controls?**
Engineering controls are physical or mechanical measures that reduce or remove the hazard regardless of what any individual does — machine guards, ventilation systems, anti-slip flooring. Administrative controls change how work is done through rules, training, procedures, and organisation — they are less effective because they depend on human compliance. Both are above PPE in the hierarchy. For most hazards in an IOSH project, the correct approach is to implement at least one engineering control and at least one administrative control before specifying PPE.
