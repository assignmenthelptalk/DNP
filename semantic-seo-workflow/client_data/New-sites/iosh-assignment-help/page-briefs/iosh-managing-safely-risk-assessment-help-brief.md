#### H2: The Likelihood Scale in the IOSH Risk Matrix
> **EAV to cover**: Likelihood scale (entity) → five levels (attribute) → specific score, descriptor, and workplace scenario justifying each level (values)
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **1 — Rare**: The hazard could theoretically cause harm but the conditions required are extremely unlikely. Example: electrocution from a tested and certified fixed electrical installation with annual PAT testing and no reported defects. Justification language: "There are no foreseeable circumstances under normal operating conditions that would result in contact with a live conductor."
> - **2 — Unlikely**: Harm could occur but would be unusual given current conditions. Example: chemical splash to eyes where splash guards are installed and operatives wear safety glasses — an incident would require multiple simultaneous control failures.
> - **3 — Possible**: Harm might occur — not expected routinely but a realistic outcome given the exposure scenario. Example: slip on oil-contaminated flooring where mopping is done reactively rather than preventively — a slip could occur between contamination and cleaning.
> - **4 — Likely**: Harm is expected to occur at some point without additional controls. Example: manual handling injury among warehouse operatives lifting 30–40kg loads repeatedly over an 8-hour shift without mechanical aids — musculoskeletal injury is statistically expected given this exposure.
> - **5 — Almost Certain**: Harm is expected to occur repeatedly or inevitably. Example: progressive noise-induced hearing loss among workers exposed to >85dB(A) daily without hearing protection — hearing damage is a near-certain outcome over months of uncontrolled exposure.

#### H2: Before and After Risk Ratings — Inherent Risk and Residual Risk
> **EAV to cover**: Before/after risk rating requirement (entity) → inherent risk definition, residual risk definition, rating reduction logic, common errors (attributes) → specific values showing how control measures reduce likelihood, severity, or both (values)
> **Word count**: 320–400 words
> **Specific content**:
> - **Inherent risk** (before rating): the risk level when NO controls are in place, or when only existing inadequate controls are present. This reflects the true severity of the hazard if left unmanaged.
> - **Residual risk** (after rating): the risk remaining after all recommended control measures from Section 3 are implemented. Residual risk must always be lower than inherent risk — if it is not, the control measures are insufficient or incorrectly specified.
> - How control measures reduce the rating: controls can reduce likelihood (e.g., installing non-slip flooring reduces the likelihood of a slip from 3/Possible to 1/Rare), reduce severity (e.g., providing fall arrest equipment reduces the severity of a fall from 5/Catastrophic to 3/Moderate), or both
> - Worked example: Manual handling hazard. Before: Likelihood 4 × Severity 3 = 12 (Medium). Control measures: introduce mechanical pallet truck, implement team lift procedure, deliver manual handling training. After: Likelihood 2 × Severity 2 = 4 (Low).
> - Common error: leaving the before and after ratings identical — this signals that the recommended control measures do not reduce the risk and will lose marks
> - Common error: reducing risk to 1 (Negligible/Rare) in all cases — assessors expect residual risk to reflect realistic post-control conditions, not theoretical zero risk

### FAQ SECTION

**Q: What is the difference between inherent risk and residual risk in the IOSH project?**
Inherent risk is the risk level before any control measures are applied — it represents the full danger of the hazard in an uncontrolled state. Residual risk is the risk level remaining after all recommended control measures have been implemented. Candidates must calculate and record both in the risk assessment section, and the residual risk rating must always be lower than the inherent risk rating to demonstrate that the proposed controls are effective.

**Q: Can I give the same likelihood and severity score to every hazard?**
Giving identical ratings to all hazards indicates that each hazard poses exactly the same level of risk — which is almost never true in a real workplace. Assessors expect a range of risk ratings reflecting genuine differences in the likelihood of harm and the severity of potential injury for each hazard. Identical ratings across all rows suggest the risk assessment has not been conducted thoughtfully and will lose marks.

**Q: What risk level requires immediate action in the IOSH project?**
A Very High risk level (Risk Rating 20–25, calculated as Likelihood × Severity) requires immediate control measures before work proceeds. A High risk level (Risk Rating 15–19) requires urgent action as a priority. Medium risks (7–14) require action within a planned timescale. Low risks (1–6) are acceptable but require monitoring and periodic review. These thresholds determine the priority order of the action plan.

**Q: Which legislation requires employers to carry out a risk assessment?**
The Management of Health and Safety at Work Regulations 1999, Regulation 3, places a legal duty on every employer to carry out a suitable and sufficient risk assessment of risks to the health and safety of employees and others affected by their work. Additional regulations require specific risk assessments for particular hazards: COSHH Regulations 2002 (chemical hazards), Manual Handling Operations Regulations 1992 (manual handling), and DSE Regulations 1992 (display screen equipment).
