**Values per Attribute**:
- Medical model → definition → disability is understood as a problem residing in the individual — a deficit, disorder, or impairment requiring medical intervention, cure, or normalisation
- Medical model → critique in OT → locates the problem in the person, not the environment; ignores social, cultural, and structural barriers; historically informed charity model and institutional segregation
- Social model → definition → disability is not the impairment itself but the barriers created by society — physical, attitudinal, and structural — that restrict participation; the person has an impairment; society disables
- Social model → origin → UPIAS (Union of the Physically Impaired Against Segregation) 1976 — first formal articulation; separated "impairment" from "disability"
- Social model → foundational text → Oliver (1990) The Politics of Disablement — conceptualised social model as academic framework
- ICF → structure → WHO International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (2001); components: body functions (b codes), body structures (s codes), activities and participation (d codes), environmental factors (e codes), personal factors (not coded); health condition sits outside the model as context
- ICF → activity limitation → difficulty executing tasks; ICF d codes (e.g., d4 mobility, d5 self-care) directly map to OT assessment domains
- ICF → participation restriction → problems in involvement in life situations; distinct from activity limitation — a person may be able to perform an activity (activity capacity) but be restricted from participation due to environmental/social barriers (performance)
- ICF → environmental factors → e codes: products and technology (e1), natural environment (e2), support and relationships (e3), attitudes (e4), services/systems/policies (e5) — these are barriers or facilitators
- UN CRPD → Article 19 → right to live independently and be included in the community; OT relevance: community OT support for independent living, assistive technology, personal assistance
- UN CRPD → Article 24 → right to inclusive education; OT relevance: school-based OT, EHCP (Education, Health and Care Plan) contributions, educational inclusion support
- UN CRPD → Article 27 → right to work and employment; OT relevance: vocational rehabilitation, workplace modification, supported employment
- Ableism → definition → discrimination and social prejudice against people with disabilities based on the belief that typical abilities are superior
- Ableism → types → individual ableism (interpersonal discrimination and micro-aggressions); institutional ableism (policies and practices that exclude disabled people — e.g., inaccessible physical environments, discriminatory hiring); cultural ableism (representations of disability as tragedy, burden, or inspiration that reinforce negative stereotypes)
- Occupational justice → OT-specific social model link → Townsend and Wilcock's occupational justice framework applies social model principles to occupation — structural barriers create occupational deprivation, apartheid, and marginalisation; OT has a role in advocacy and enablement beyond individual-level intervention
- Neurodiversity paradigm → definition → conceptualises autism, ADHD, dyslexia, and other neurological conditions as natural variations in human neurology — differences, not deficits; associated with disability studies and activism
- Neurodiversity → OT implications → strengths-based assessment language; avoiding diagnostic deficit framing in goal-setting; collaborative goal-setting; NICE autism guidelines (NG142) align with neurodiversity-affirming practice
- Language → identity-first vs person-first → identity-first: "autistic person" (preferred by many autistic self-advocates: disability is part of identity, not separate); person-first: "person with autism" (preferred in some clinical and family contexts: separates person from condition); OT students must know both and justify their choice in assignments
- Language → deficit language → language to avoid in OT academic writing: "suffers from," "afflicted by," "wheelchair-bound," "confined to," "normal" (vs "non-disabled" or "neurotypical") — these reflect medical model assumptions

#### H2: Biopsychosocial Model — WHO ICF Framework for OT Assignments — *Major Query Need: biopsychosocial model ICF occupational therapy*
> **Context vector**: ICF as a biopsychosocial framework that bridges medical and social model perspectives and is widely used in OT academic work
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - ICF → full name → International Classification of Functioning, Disability and Health (WHO, 2001)
> - ICF → philosophy → biopsychosocial synthesis: neither purely medical nor purely social; recognises interaction between health condition, body functions/structures, activities, participation, and contextual factors
> - Body functions → definition → physiological functions including psychological; coded as b codes; examples: b710 (joint mobility), b730 (muscle power), b760 (control of voluntary movement), b140 (attention), b144 (memory)
> - Body structures → definition → anatomical parts; coded as s codes; examples: s730 (upper extremity), s110 (brain structure)

> - Participation → definition → involvement in life situations; restricted by environmental barriers even when activity capacity exists
> - Environmental factors → definition → physical and social environment outside the person; e codes; facilitators (positive factors) or barriers (negative factors); examples: e1 products and technology, e3 social support, e4 attitudes, e5 services and policies
> - Personal factors → definition → internal factors (age, gender, education, coping styles) — not coded in ICF but acknowledged
> - ICF and OT models → connection → ICF's participation and environmental factors components align with MOHO's environment construct and CMOP-E's environment component; ICF provides a neutral classification framework that complements OT models

##### H3: Activity Limitation vs Participation Restriction — The ICF Distinction for OT
> **Attribute + Value**: Activity limitation → difficulty in executing tasks (individual level — measured in standard environment). Participation restriction → problems in involvement in life situations (social/contextual level — affected by environmental and social factors). An OT assignment example: a person with hemiplegia may have an activity limitation in reaching (b730 muscle power limitation) but their participation restriction in work participation (d850) results from inaccessible workplace design (e1 products/technology barrier) — not only from the activity limitation.
> **Sequence modeling note**: "Activity limitation" vs "participation restriction" — these terms must always appear in contrast to maintain the ICF distinction that is tested in assignments.

#### H2: FAQ — Disability Models in OT Assignments
> **Critical rule**: New representative query variants only — no repetition of Main Content.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. **What is the difference between the social model and the biopsychosocial model of disability?** → Answer direction: The social model argues that disability is entirely socially produced by barriers; the biopsychosocial model (WHO ICF) recognises the interaction between health condition, body functions/structures, activities, participation, AND environmental factors. The ICF is not purely social — it acknowledges body function limitations — but it incorporates environmental and personal factors that the medical model ignores. For OT assignments, the ICF is the most widely applicable framework because it accounts for both individual function and contextual barriers.
2. **Is "person-first" or "identity-first" language correct in OT assignments?** → Answer direction: There is no universally correct answer. Acknowledge the debate in assignments, note that autistic self-advocates often prefer identity-first language while some clinical guidelines recommend person-first. Demonstrate awareness of the student's reasoning. The key is to be consistent, to justify the choice briefly, and to avoid deficit language regardless of which convention is used.
3. **What is ableism and how does it appear in OT practice?** → Answer direction: Ableism is discrimination and prejudice against disabled people based on the assumption that typical abilities are superior. In OT, it can appear in assessment tools normed against non-disabled populations, in goal-setting language that frames "normal" function as the only acceptable outcome, in paternalistic communication, or in environmental designs that assume ambulatory, non-disabled users.
4. **How does the UN CRPD apply to occupational therapy practice?** → Answer direction: The CRPD establishes disability as a human rights issue. Articles 19 (independent living), 24 (inclusive education), and 27 (employment) are directly relevant to OT scope. OTs support CRPD compliance by enabling independent living through community-based practice, supporting educational inclusion through school-based OT, and contributing to vocational rehabilitation.
5. **What is the neurodiversity paradigm and why does it matter for OT assignments?** → Answer direction: The neurodiversity paradigm conceptualises autism, ADHD, and related conditions as natural neurological variations rather than deficits. For OT assignments, it requires a shift from normalisation goals to strengths-based, autonomy-affirming, client-centred goals that respect the person's neurological identity. Language matters: "sensory differences" rather than "sensory dysfunction"; "regulation challenges" rather than "behaviour problems."
