##### H3: COPM Administration — Problem Identification Interview
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Semi-structured interview format: OT facilitates; client leads
> - Areas explored: self-care (personal care, functional mobility, community management), productivity, leisure
> - Client identifies problems in their OWN words — paraphrasing by OT to confirm; problems must be occupational in nature
> - Up to 5 problems prioritised by the client; importance rated immediately after identification
> - Total time: typically 30–45 minutes for initial COPM
> - Can be adapted for clients with cognitive impairment, communication difficulties, or literacy challenges — proxy versions exist for paediatric populations
> **Writer note**: Emphasise that the COPM is NOT a deficit-identification tool driven by the OT's observations — this is a common student misunderstanding. The client's voice drives problem identification. 120–150 words.

##### H3: Scoring and Interpreting COPM Results in Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Performance score formula: sum of all performance ratings ÷ total number of identified problems
> - Satisfaction score formula: sum of all satisfaction ratings ÷ total number of identified problems
> - Score interpretation: 1–10 continuous scale — no normative cut-offs for comparison against a population; COPM is an individually referenced measure (change from baseline is what matters, not absolute score)
> - Clinically significant change: ≥2.0 point change on performance OR satisfaction scale is considered clinically meaningful (Law et al., original validation studies; replicated across multiple populations)
> - Re-assessment: same interview format; same problems re-rated; new mean scores calculated; change scores = re-assessment score minus baseline score
> **Writer note**: Give a worked example with 3 problems showing all ratings, score calculation, and change score — this is exactly what students need to replicate in their assignments. 180–220 words.

#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions About CMOP-E Assignments — *Supplementary Content — Micro-contexts and Minor Query Needs*
> **Critical rule**: Answers must NOT repeat Main Content — they extend toward new representative query variants.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **What is spirituality in CMOP-E, and how do I write about it in an OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: Spirituality in CMOP-E is the essence of self — the source of personal meaning, purpose, and will. It is NOT necessarily religious, though it may be expressed through religion for some clients. In an assignment, spirituality is inferred from what the client says about what matters most to them, what gives their life meaning, and which occupations they feel most strongly connected to. Write about it through client statements: "The client described painting as the activity through which she feels most like herself, suggesting strong occupational meaning aligned with the spirituality construct in CMOP-E." Do not write "the client is not religious therefore spirituality is not relevant" — this misapplies the construct entirely and will be penalised in marking.

2. **How do I calculate COPM scores correctly in an OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: COPM produces two separate mean scores — performance and satisfaction. Calculate each by summing all ratings for that scale and dividing by the number of identified problems. For example: if a client rates three occupational problems on performance as 2, 3, and 4, the mean performance score is (2+3+4) ÷ 3 = 3.0. At re-assessment, if scores are 5, 6, and 6, the mean becomes 5.67 and the change score is 2.67 — which exceeds the minimum clinically important difference of 2.0 points. Always show the formula, the raw numbers, and the change score in your assignment. Reporting only a final mean without showing the calculation process is insufficient at Level 5 and above.

3. **What are the 8 action points of the CPPF, and do I need to include them all in a case study?**
   → Answer direction: The 8 CPPF action points are: (1) Enter/Initiate, (2) Set the Stage, (3) Assess/Evaluate, (4) Agree on Objectives and Plan, (5) Implement Plan, (6) Monitor and Modify, (7) Evaluate Outcome, (8) Conclude/Exit. In a case study assignment, you do not need to write a separate paragraph for each action point — but you should demonstrate that your case study follows this sequence. Action points 3, 4, and 5 typically receive the most detail: assessment (using COPM), goal negotiation, and intervention planning. Mention CPPF explicitly in your theoretical framework section and reference specific action points when describing your clinical decisions. This demonstrates model integration rather than surface-level application.

4. **Can I use CMOP-E in a physical rehabilitation assignment, or is it mainly for mental health?**
   → Answer direction: CMOP-E is applicable across ALL practice areas. Its occupation categories (self-care, productivity, leisure) and its emphasis on client-identified priorities through the COPM are relevant in physical rehabilitation, neurological OT, geriatric care, and paediatrics — not only mental health. In fact, COPM was originally validated in adult physical rehabilitation contexts. For a physical rehabilitation case study, use CMOP-E to show how a client's physical performance component interacts with their affective component (adjustment to disability) and their institutional environment (access to rehabilitation services), while centring the client's self-identified occupational priorities through the COPM.

5. **What is the difference between CMOP-E and CMOP?**
   → Answer direction: CMOP (Canadian Model of Occupational Performance) was published in the 1997 edition of Enabling Occupation (CAOT). CMOP-E added the Engagement dimension — recognising that occupational therapy involves engagement in occupation, not only performance. Engagement encompasses subjective experience, participation, and meaning — not just task completion. In academic writing, use CMOP-E (not CMOP) unless you are specifically discussing pre-2007 literature. The addition of Engagement is particularly relevant in mental health OT and paediatric OT, where engagement with occupation is often an intervention goal before performance is achievable.
