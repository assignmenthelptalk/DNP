##### H3: Gibbs Reflective Cycle — Six Stages for OT Placement Reflection
> **Attribute + Value**: Gibbs Reflective Cycle → stage 1 (Description) → factual account of the specific practice scenario: who was present, what happened, what was your role; stage 2 (Feelings) → honest account of emotional response during and after the event; stage 3 (Evaluation) → what went well, what did not go well in the interaction or intervention; stage 4 (Analysis) → the academic core of the essay — which theoretical framework or OT model explains what occurred; which HCPC standard is relevant to this scenario; what research evidence supports a different approach; this is where citations must appear; stage 5 (Conclusion) → what could have been done differently; stage 6 (Action Plan) → specific professional development commitment for future similar scenarios; in academic writing, each stage corresponds to a paragraph section with a stage-identifying topic sentence; total essay word allocation: Description + Feelings 15%, Evaluation 15%, Analysis 45–50%, Conclusion + Action Plan 20–25%

##### H3: Schon's Reflection-in-Action and Reflection-on-Action for OT Practice
> **Attribute + Value**: Schon → Reflection-in-Action → the cognitive process occurring during the clinical encounter: recognising an unexpected outcome, adjusting approach in real time, testing a new hypothesis within the session; documented in OT academic writing as retrospective first-person narrative ("at the moment the client refused the task, I recognised that my initial assumption about motivation was incorrect; I adjusted by..."); → Reflection-on-Action → the retrospective analysis conducted after the session: identifying which knowledge framed the initial approach, why the in-action adjustment was made, what theoretical framework explains the decision; documented as systematic self-inquiry ("reflecting on the interaction, I identify that my clinical reasoning drew on procedural reasoning regarding the physical task demands without adequately applying narrative reasoning to the client's occupational history"); Schon is most applicable at Level 5–7 when students have sufficient clinical experience to document genuine in-action adjustments

##### H3: Johns Model of Structured Reflection — Five Cues for Ethically Complex OT Scenarios
> **Attribute + Value**: Johns Model → cue 1 (Description) → describe the experience including the significant factors involved; cue 2 (Reflection) → what was I trying to achieve, why did I respond as I did, what were the consequences for the client, for myself, for people I work with; cue 3 (Influencing Factors) → what internal factors influenced my decision (prior knowledge, values, assumptions, emotional state), what knowledge was available that should have informed me; this is the most academically demanding cue — HCPC standard references belong here; cue 4 (Could I have dealt better) → what other choices existed, what would have been the consequences of alternative actions; cue 5 (Learning) → how does this experience change my ways of knowing, what future practice will be different; Johns is particularly appropriate for scenarios involving professional dilemmas (conflicting client autonomy with duty of care), ethical decisions (mandatory reporting, safeguarding), or complex MDT interactions; Johns authored the model in 1994, revised in Becoming a Reflective Practitioner (5th edition, 2017)
> **Writer note**: The academic rigour of Johns comes from cue 3 (Influencing Factors) — this is where the student must name the theoretical or evidential knowledge that should have informed the decision. In OT, this means citing OT models (MOHO, CMOP-E), HCPC standards by code, and OT evidence. Students who skip this cue produce a personally insightful journal that fails academically. #### H2: FAQ — Supplementary Content — Micro-contexts and Minor Query Needs
> **Critical rule**: Questions must NOT repeat Main Content. They target new representative query variants for passage indexing and PAA.
> Each answer: 80–120 words, self-contained.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **Which reflective model should I use for my OT placement journal — Gibbs, Schon, or Johns?** → Answer direction: Check your assignment brief first — most programmes specify the required model. If unspecified: use Gibbs if you are at Level 4 or 5 and new to reflective writing, as its 6-stage structure provides clear scaffolding; use Schon if you want to capture real-time clinical decision-making during a complex intervention (this model suits Level 5–6 students with meaningful placement experience); use Johns for ethically complex scenarios involving professional dilemmas, client autonomy challenges, or conflicting values — Johns' cue 3 (Influencing Factors) is particularly effective at exploring underlying assumptions. Multiple models can be used across a portfolio — one model per entry is not required if the programme brief does not specify it.

2. **How many HCPC standards do I need to reference in a reflective essay?** → Answer direction: Most OT programme marking rubrics require a minimum of one HCPC Standard of Proficiency per reflective entry. For distinction-level work, two or more standards should be referenced with explicit connection to the scenario — not simply listed. The most commonly applicable standards for placement reflective journals are 1.1 (best interests), 2.1 (scope of practice), 9.1 (working with others), 10.1 (record-keeping), and 14.1 (reflective practice). Each standard must be cited accurately — "(HCPC, 2013: Standard 1.1)" — and the specific relevance to the scenario must be explained. Vague references ("I followed HCPC standards") receive no marks.

3. **What is the difference between a reflective essay and a practice placement journal in OT?** → Answer direction: A reflective essay is typically a standalone academic assignment that reflects on one significant practice experience in depth, usually 1,500–3,000 words, submitted as a single document. A practice placement journal is a portfolio of multiple shorter reflective entries (typically 5–10 entries of 500–1,000 words each) written throughout a placement block, demonstrating professional development over time. The essay format allows deeper analysis of a single scenario; the journal format requires showing growth and change across entries. Both require the same components — reflective model, HCPC standards, theory linkage — but at different depths per entry.

4. **Can I reflect on a challenging or difficult placement experience in my OT reflective essay?** → Answer direction: Yes — challenging experiences often produce the strongest reflective essays because they generate genuine analysis. Ethical dilemmas, difficult client interactions, moments of uncertainty, or situations where you recognised the limits of your competence are all appropriate for academic reflection. The key academic requirement is that the analysis (Gibbs stage 4, Johns cue 3) connects the challenge to specific HCPC standards and OT theory, not just to personal feelings. A client refusing treatment, a safeguarding concern, or a disagreement within an MDT are all legitimate and high-value scenarios for reflective assignments. The service can help structure any scenario using the appropriate reflective model framework.

5. **Does my OT reflective essay need to include citations and a reference list?** → Answer direction: Yes — citations are required in the Analysis section (Gibbs stage 4) and the Influencing Factors or Theory sections of other models. Citations in OT reflective essays should reference: (1) HCPC Standards of Proficiency (cited as HCPC, 2013); (2) RCOT professional standards or code of ethics where relevant; (3) OT theoretical model sources (e.g., Kielhofner, 2008 for MOHO; Townsend and Polatajko, 2013 for CMOP-E); (4) peer-reviewed evidence that supports or challenges the approach taken in practice (AJOT, BJOT, OTseeker sources). A reference list in Harvard format is required. The reflective style of writing does not exempt the assignment from academic citation requirements.
