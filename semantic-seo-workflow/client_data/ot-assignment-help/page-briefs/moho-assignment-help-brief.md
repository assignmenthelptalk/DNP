##### H3: Habituation — Roles and Habits
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Roles: internalised sets of behaviours associated with a social position (e.g., parent, worker, student) — role scripts guide what occupations are expected
> - Habits: acquired tendencies to respond in familiar ways in familiar contexts — habits operate largely without conscious attention

##### H3: MOHOST — The Model of Human Occupation Screening Tool
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - 6 sections: Motivation for Occupation, Pattern of Occupation, Communication and Interaction Skills, Process Skills, Motor Skills, Environment
> - Rating scale: F (Facilitates) = occupation enabled and supported; A (Allows) = occupation possible without significant barriers; I (Inhibits) = occupation limited or disrupted; R (Restricts) = occupation severely compromised
> - Total scale: 4 × 6 sections = 24 items rated; each section produces a composite rating
> - Administration: structured observation + interview; can be completed by OT from observation alone in inpatient settings
> - Population: broad — originally designed for mental health but applied across settings
> - Assignment implication: each section maps to MOHO constructs — Motivation maps to volition, Pattern maps to habituation, Skills sections map to performance capacity, Environment section maps to environment construct
> **Writer note**: Writers must include the rating scale definitions (F/A/I/R) explicitly, not assume the reader knows them. Give an example: a client with depression who stays in bed until midday would receive an Inhibits (I) on Pattern of Occupation. 150–200 words.

##### H3: OPHI-II — Occupational Performance History Interview
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Three scales: Occupational Identity Scale (11 items, score range 11–44), Occupational Competence Scale (9 items, score range 9–36), Occupational Behaviour Settings Scale (9 items, score range 9–36)
> - Each item rated 1–4: 4 = exceptionally competent/highly positive, 3 = appropriate, 2 = some difficulty/partial occupational function, 1 = extreme difficulty/dysfunction
> - Narrative component: Life History Narrative Form — captures key life events, daily routine changes, and their impact on occupational identity over time
> - Semi-structured interview: 3 domains — occupational roles (present and past), daily routine, occupational behaviour settings
> - Assignment relevance: OPHI-II is particularly suited for reflective essays and occupational formulation — the narrative component provides the "story" that MOHO's occupational identity construct requires
> **Writer note**: Clarify when OPHI-II is chosen over MOHOST in an assignment — OPHI-II is appropriate for longer-term occupational histories and where understanding change over time matters (e.g., chronic mental health, progressive neurological conditions). 120–160 words.

##### H3: OCAIRS, WRI, and OSA — Additional MOHO Assessment Tools
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - OCAIRS (Occupational Circumstances Assessment Interview and Rating Scale): 12 domains rated 1–4; covers personal causation, values, interests, roles, habits, skills, short-term goals, long-term goals, interpretation of past experience, physical environment, social environment, readiness for change; interview-based; widely used in community mental health and forensic OT
> - WRI (Worker Role Interview): 17 items rated 4-point scale (Strongly Supports/Supports/Interferes/Strongly Interferes); specifically designed for vocational rehabilitation contexts; assesses MOHO constructs in the context of return to work
> - OSA (Occupational Self Assessment): client self-report; 2 columns per item — competence (how well I do this) and importance (how important this is to me); 4-point scale each; used to guide goal-setting in client-centred practice; assignment implication: OSA results directly reflect client's self-identified occupational priorities for the intervention plan
> **Writer note**: Many students are unaware of WRI and OSA. Including these demonstrates graduate-level MOHO knowledge. Connect WRI to work-rehabilitation case studies and OSA to goal-setting sections of case study assignments. 180–220 words.

#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions About MOHO Assignments — *Supplementary Content — Micro-contexts and Minor Query Needs*
> **Critical rule**: These questions must NOT repeat Main Content. They expand toward NEW query variants.
> **Writer note**: Each answer must be self-contained enough to rank as a passage independently. Write 80–120 words per answer. Optimised for People Also Ask, passage indexing, and voice search.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **What is the difference between volition and habituation in MOHO?**
   → Answer direction: Volition is the MOTIVATIONAL dimension — it explains WHY someone chooses to engage in (or avoids) occupation through personal causation, values, and interests. Habituation is the STRUCTURAL dimension — it explains how occupation is organised into daily life through internalised roles and habits. A useful assignment distinction: volition is about want and meaning; habituation is about pattern and routine. Disruption to volition shows as loss of interest or confidence; disruption to habituation shows as loss of routine structure or role identity. Do not repeat construct definitions verbatim from main content — frame this as a comparison answer.

2. **How do I score the MOHOST correctly in an assignment?**
   → Answer direction: The MOHOST uses a 4-point qualitative rating per section — not a numerical total. F (Facilitates): the factor actively supports occupational engagement. A (Allows): the factor is neutral and does not create barriers. I (Inhibits): the factor limits occupational engagement. R (Restricts): the factor severely compromises occupational engagement. In an assignment, cite the rating and the section name, then justify the rating with specific observation evidence. Example: "The client received an Inhibits (I) rating on the Pattern of Occupation section, reflecting disrupted morning routines and absence of productive role occupations since job loss six months prior."

3. **Can I use MOHO for a physical rehabilitation case study, or is it only for mental health?**
   → Answer direction: MOHO is applicable across ALL practice areas — not only mental health. The model addresses motivation, roles, habits, and the subjective experience of doing, which are relevant in physical rehabilitation, neurological conditions, paediatrics, and geriatrics. In a stroke case study, MOHO explains why a patient resists ADL practice (low personal causation), why losing the worker role creates distress beyond physical disability, and why the lived body experience must be addressed alongside physical goals. The common misconception that MOHO is only for mental health often results in weaker assignments for physical rehab settings.

4. **What is occupational adaptation in MOHO, and how do I use it in a reflective essay?**
   → Answer direction: Occupational adaptation is the dynamic process by which a person constructs a positive occupational identity and achieves occupational competence over time. In a reflective essay, occupational adaptation is the outcome frame — it describes whether the client is moving toward a meaningful occupational life despite impairment, life change, or environmental barriers. A good reflective essay will use occupational adaptation to evaluate whether OT intervention has created the conditions for the client to adapt — not whether specific skills have been regained. This shifts the reflective framing from cure-focused to identity-focused, which examiners expect at Level 6 and Level 7.

5. **How does the OPHI-II differ from the MOHOST, and when should I choose each?**
   → Answer direction: The OPHI-II is a semi-structured interview-based assessment that generates a narrative life history alongside three quantitative scales (Occupational Identity, Occupational Competence, Occupational Behaviour Settings — each scored 1–4 per item). It is best used when the occupational history over time is clinically important — chronic conditions, major life transitions, long-term mental health. The MOHOST is an observational screening tool rated across 6 sections using F/A/I/R — it is best used for screening occupational functioning in a current context, particularly in inpatient settings where time is limited. In assignments, choose OPHI-II when you need to demonstrate understanding of occupational identity over time; choose MOHOST when the case focuses on current functional occupational status.
