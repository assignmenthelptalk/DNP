## EAV Depth Map
**Primary Entity**: Occupational Therapy Assignment Mistakes (by type)

**Main Attributes** (mistakes by assignment type):

**Case Study Mistakes**:
1. Describing assessment scores without interpreting them — stating "COPM performance score = 4" without explaining functional implications or clinically significant change (MCID ≥2 points)
2. Selecting a model without justifying the choice — "MOHO was applied" without explaining why MOHO over CMOP-E or OTPF for this specific client and context
3. SMART goals missing measurability — "improve independence with dressing" fails; "achieve independent dressing with adaptive hook and button aid within 4 weeks as rated by COPM performance score ≥6" passes
4. Intervention plans without evidence citations — every OT intervention must be supported by a cited source; "I chose CIMT because it works" is not evidence; "CIMT was selected based on Level 1A evidence (Cochrane review, Corbetta et al. 2015)" is evidence
5. Confusing reasoning types — describing what was done (procedural) without explaining how it connects to the whole client (conditional) or the client's narrative (narrative reasoning)
6. Informal language in occupational profile — "the client has always been a busy person" vs "the client reports a strong occupational identity centred on productive roles including employment and caregiving"

**Reflective Essay Mistakes**:
1. Summarising events instead of reflecting — "I attended an MDT meeting" is a summary; "The MDT meeting challenged my assumption that OT goals are set independently, requiring me to reconsider the role of collaborative goal-setting" is reflection
2. Using Gibbs' cycle as headings without genuine depth — listing Description/Feelings/Evaluation/Analysis/Conclusion/Action Plan as sections without substantive critical reflection in each
3. Not linking theory to practice — must name the OT theoretical framework (MOHO, CMOP-E) or HCPC Standard of Proficiency code (e.g., Standard 10: be able to maintain records) that the experience illuminates
4. Missing HCPC Standard references — Level 5+ reflective essays are expected to reference specific HCPC SoP standards; generic reference to "professional standards" is insufficient
5. Writing in third person — reflective writing requires first-person voice; "the therapist observed" undermines the reflective process required for the assignment

**Literature Review Mistakes**:
1. PICO question too broad — "Is OT effective?" cannot be reviewed systematically; "In adults post-stroke (P), does constraint-induced movement therapy (I) compared with conventional therapy (C) improve upper limb function (O) within 3 months (T)?" is a reviewable PICO
2. Searching only Google Scholar — must use OTseeker (OT-specific RCT/systematic review database), CINAHL, and PubMed/MEDLINE as minimum; Google Scholar has no quality filters
3. Including studies without noting their evidence level — every included study must be appraised (CASP or PEDro); the hierarchy must be stated (Level 1A systematic review > Level 2 RCT > Level 3 cohort)
4. Describing studies instead of synthesising — "Study A found X. Study B found Y. Study C found Z." is description; synthesis identifies patterns, contradictions, and themes across studies
5. CASP completed but not integrated into argument — critical appraisal is only useful if the quality score influences which studies are given more weight in the argument

**Activity Analysis Mistakes**:
1. Describing the activity generically instead of using OT frameworks — "cooking involves standing, reaching, and stirring" vs OTPF demand categories: "objects and properties (utensils, stove controls), space demands (counter height 90cm, workspace 1m²), sequencing (multi-step process with 12+ steps), required body functions (b710 mobility of joint functions, b730 muscle power functions)"
2. Missing body function ICF codes — OT activity analysis must reference ICF body function codes (b-codes) and body structure codes (s-codes)
3. Grading without therapeutic purpose — "grading: start seated, progress to standing" without linking to a specific client goal or impairment rationale
4. Confusing grading with adapting — grading = changing the demand of the activity itself (e.g., simpler recipe, shorter duration); adapting = changing the environment or tools (e.g., non-slip mat, long-handled spoon)

**Dissertation/Research Proposal Mistakes**:
1. Methodology misaligned with research question — phenomenological IPA for a question asking "how many OT students fail placements?" (that is a prevalence question requiring quantitative design); IPA for "what is the lived experience of OT students on first placement?" is correct
2. Confusing method with methodology — method = what you do (interview, survey, systematic search); methodology = the philosophical framework justifying why that method is appropriate (interpretivist, positivist, pragmatist)
3. Incomplete ethical considerations — must address: informed consent process; participant capacity; right to withdraw without consequence; data storage and anonymisation; researcher role and reflexivity; risk of harm; NHS REC vs university ethics committee distinction
4. Discussion section describing results instead of discussing them — results section reports; discussion section interprets, contextualises, compares with existing literature, and draws implications for OT practice

**Cross-Assignment Errors**:
1. Over-quoting — more than 10% direct quotation undermines academic voice; paraphrase with citation is preferred at Level 5+
2. Descriptive not analytical writing — describe = what happened; analyse = what + why it happened + what it means for OT practice
3. Weak conclusions not linked back to question — conclusions must directly address the assignment question; new information or evidence must not appear in conclusions
4. Word count distribution imbalances — introduction and conclusion should not each exceed 10-15% of total word count; over-long introductions delay main content; over-long conclusions repeat main body rather than synthesising

**Values per Attribute**:
- SMART goal → measurable component → COPM performance scale 1-10 used as quantified outcome metric; clinically significant change = ≥2 points
- Evidence citation → CIMT → Level 1A Cochrane systematic review (Corbetta et al. 2015) vs Level 5 expert opinion in a textbook
- PICO → "too broad" threshold → question that would return > 500 results with no inclusion criteria is too broad
- Reflective writing → HCPC Standard reference → specific: "This experience evidenced Standard 2.3: recognise and respond appropriately to situations where it is necessary to share information"; generic: "This met professional standards" (insufficient)

#### H2: FAQ — Occupational Therapy Assignment Questions
> **Critical rule**: Questions must NOT repeat Main Content. Expand to new representative query variants.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. How do I know if my OT case study has enough clinical reasoning? → Answer: Clinical reasoning is present when you can explain not just what you did but why — specifically: why this assessment over alternatives, why this model fits this client, why this intervention at this point in the OT process. If the word "because" followed by a theoretical or evidence-based justification does not appear frequently in your case study, clinical reasoning is likely insufficient.
2. What do OT markers mean by "not applied to the case"? → Answer: This feedback indicates that theoretical content (an OT model, a principle, a piece of evidence) was described in isolation rather than connected to the specific client in the case. Every theoretical statement must be followed by application: "MOHO's concept of habituation explains why this client's loss of work role disrupted daily routines, specifically..."
3. Can I use first person in an OT assignment? → Answer: Reflective essays always require first person. Case studies at most UK universities use third person ("the client", "the therapist") but some allow first person — check your module handbook. Literature reviews and dissertations use third person throughout. Activity analysis assignments use neutral/impersonal framing focused on the activity not the analyst.
4. Why does my literature review keep getting described as "descriptive not analytical"? → Answer: Description reports what studies found. Analysis compares, contrasts, and evaluates: which studies had stronger methodology (and why that matters for the conclusions you can draw), where studies agree and disagree, what the pattern of evidence suggests for OT practice. CASP appraisal integrated into your argument (not just completed as an appendix) is the mechanism for shifting from descriptive to analytical.
5. How much of my OT assignment should be my own analysis vs quotation? → Answer: At Level 5+, direct quotation should not exceed 10% of the assignment. Level 6-7 markers want your analytical voice supported by paraphrased evidence. A high proportion of direct quotes signals that you are reporting sources rather than engaging with them.
