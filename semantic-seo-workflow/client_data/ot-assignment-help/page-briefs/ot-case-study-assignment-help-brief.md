##### H3: Using CMOP-E's Person-Environment-Occupation Framework in Case Studies
> **Attribute + Value**: CMOP-E → person domain → affective component (emotional response to condition), cognitive component (attention, memory, executive function — mapped to MMSE or MoCA if administered), physical component (sensorimotor, motor — mapped to AMPS or FIM motor scores); environment domain → physical environment (home accessibility, assistive technology access), social environment (family involvement, social isolation), cultural environment (occupational norms and expectations in the client's cultural context), institutional environment (care setting, available services); occupation domain → self-care (COPM self-care priorities), productivity (work or homemaking roles), leisure (identified leisure interests disrupted by condition); spirituality → the client's personal meaning drawn from their occupations — must be addressed in the occupational profile using the client's own language about what matters to them

##### H3: Narrative, Interactive, and Pragmatic Reasoning in Case Study Writing
> **Attribute + Value**: Narrative reasoning → the client's occupational story — written in the occupational profile, contextualises why certain occupations hold priority; marker in academic writing: student explicitly states "narrative reasoning is employed to understand the significance of cooking for this client, whose occupational history identifies it as the primary means of social expression within the family role"; interactive reasoning → understanding established through COPM interview and demonstrated in the student's reflection on how priorities were elicited collaboratively; pragmatic reasoning → constraints named explicitly (hospital length of stay 14 days, home assessment arranged for week 3, family training session available once); goal timelines justified by these constraints in the intervention plan
> **Writer note**: Narrative and pragmatic reasoning are the two types students most commonly omit. Include both in case study writing. #### H2: SMART Goals and Evidence-Based Intervention Planning in OT Case Studies — *Major Query Need: How to write SMART occupational goals and plan interventions correctly*
> **Context vector**: Goal setting and intervention planning — SMART format for OT goals, intervention approach selection, evidence base requirements
> **EAV to cover**: SMART goal in OT → Specific (names the exact occupation and level of performance, e.g., "will independently prepare a light meal"); Measurable (quantified by assessment score criterion or functional observation criterion, e.g., "achieving a COPM performance score of ≥6/10" or "completing the task in <20 minutes without verbal prompting"); Achievable (realistic given client's AMPS motor score of 1.8 logits and 6-week timeline); Relevant (aligned to top-priority COPM issues rated ≥8/10 for importance); Time-bound (e.g., "within 6 weeks of weekly 45-minute OT sessions"); OT goal type → occupational performance goal (targets the occupation itself, not the underlying impairment — "will dress independently" not "will improve shoulder ROM to 90°"); intervention approach selection → evidence-based rationale required; occupation-based approach (client engages in the actual occupation) vs compensatory approach (adaptation of task or environment) vs remediation approach (address underlying impairment); each approach must be cited with peer-reviewed evidence from AJOT, BJOT, OTseeker, or Cochrane OT
> **Predicate**: "specifies" — a SMART goal specifies the occupation, specifies the performance criterion, specifies the timeframe
> **First word of first paragraph**: "SMART" — "SMART goals in occupational therapy case study assignments specify not just what the client will achieve..."
> **Word count target**: 320–380 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: Writing Occupational Performance Goals — Not Impairment Goals
> **Attribute + Value**: Occupational performance goal → definition → targets the occupation or activity the client wants or needs to perform, not the underlying body function; examples: correct → "Mrs J will independently dress her upper body daily within 10 minutes by week 4 of intervention"; incorrect → "Mrs J will improve shoulder flexion to 90° within 4 weeks"; the second example is an impairment goal (physiotherapy domain) not an occupational performance goal; OT case studies that set only impairment-level goals typically receive low marks on theoretical model application criteria because impairment goals do not reflect occupational therapy's occupational focus
> **Writer note**: This is a frequent marking failure point — distinguish occupational performance goals from impairment goals with concrete examples. The "incorrect" example is physiotherapy-style, which will lose marks in an OT case study. Provide one correct SMART occupational goal example that the writer can adapt. Word count: 160–200 words.

##### H3: Evidence-Based Intervention Planning — What to Cite and How
> **Attribute + Value**: Intervention plan evidence requirements → each named intervention approach must be cited with a minimum of one peer-reviewed study demonstrating efficacy for the client population; OT-specific evidence sources → OTseeker (OT evidence database, RCT and systematic review summaries), AJOT (American Journal of Occupational Therapy), BJOT (British Journal of Occupational Therapy), Cochrane Occupational Therapy Field, CINAHL OT subject heading searches; evidence hierarchy for OT → systematic reviews and meta-analyses (highest), RCTs, cohort studies, case series, expert opinion (lowest); CASP checklist used to appraise study quality before citing; example intervention approaches with evidence → constraint-induced movement therapy (CIMT) for upper limb stroke rehabilitation (Cochrane review evidence, 2015+), sensory integration therapy for ASD (AJOT systematic review evidence), cognitive rehabilitation for TBI (Cochrane review evidence), task-specific training for ADL retraining (BJOT RCT evidence)
> **Writer note**: Name the intervention approaches AND cite the evidence base type (Cochrane review, AJOT systematic review, etc.) — this models the level of specificity required in the student's own assignment. Word count: 220–270 words.

1. **How many standardised assessments do I need in an OT case study assignment?** → Answer direction: Most OT programme marking rubrics require at least one standardised assessment fully documented with score interpretation. Higher marks (distinction/first class) typically require two or more assessments, with the selection justified by the client's presentation. For example, a stroke case study might use FIM (overall ADL function) and COPM (occupational priorities from the client's perspective). The key requirement is not the number of tools but the quality of documentation — scores must be stated, interpreted, and connected to the intervention rationale. Check the assignment brief for specific requirements.

2. **Can I use a fictional client vignette or does the OT case study need to be a real client?** → Answer direction: Most OT assignment briefs at BSc Level 5 and Level 6 accept either a fictional vignette or a real anonymised client. If using a real client, anonymisation is mandatory (NHS and university ethical guidelines require removal of all identifying information). If using a fictional vignette, the clinical presentation must be realistic and internally consistent — invented impairments must align with the named diagnosis and assessment scores must fall within realistic ranges for that condition. Dissertations at Level 7 involving real data require formal ethics approval.

3. **What is the difference between an occupational performance goal and an impairment goal in OT?** → Answer direction: An occupational performance goal targets the occupation the client wants to perform — "will dress upper body independently within 10 minutes" — and is the correct format for OT case study assignments. An impairment goal targets underlying body functions — "will improve shoulder flexion to 90°" — and is more appropriate to physiotherapy. OT marking rubrics typically deduct marks when students set only impairment goals because they do not reflect occupational therapy's occupational focus. SMART occupational performance goals should be aligned to the client's top-priority COPM items.

4. **How do I link MOHO to my OT case study assessment findings?** → Answer direction: MOHO constructs must be explicitly connected to specific case data, not just named. Volition (personal causation) links to any evidence that the client doubts their ability to perform occupations — a COPM performance score of 3/10 for a previously mastered task is evidence of personal causation deficit. Habituation (roles/habits) links to the occupational history section — a disrupted daily routine documents habit breakdown. Performance capacity links to standardised assessment scores (AMPS logit score, FIM item scores). Environment links to the home assessment or observation findings. Each construct must be named and connected to a specific piece of case evidence.

5. **Do OT case study assignments need to follow a specific referencing style?** → Answer direction: Yes — most UK OT programmes require Harvard referencing for case study assignments. Australian and North American OT programmes typically use APA 7th edition. The assignment brief will specify the required format. In-text citations are required for every evidence-based claim in the intervention plan section — each named intervention approach must be supported by at least one peer-reviewed citation from OT-specific sources (AJOT, BJOT, OTseeker, CINAHL). The reference list must include DOIs or accessed dates for online sources. Referencing errors are a common mark-loss area that the service corrects in every case study it produces.
