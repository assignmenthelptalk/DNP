**Values per Attribute**:
- Ayres → biography → A. Jean Ayres, PhD, OTR; USC (University of Southern California); developed SI theory in 1960s–70s; key publications: Sensory Integration and Learning Disorders (1972); Sensory Integration and the Child (1979)
- Tactile system → SI function → discriminative touch (localisation, object identification) and protective touch (pain, temperature); OT relevance: tactile defensiveness, hypo/hyper-sensitivity; assessed via Sensory Profile 2 tactile subscale
- Proprioception → SI function → body awareness and position sense; muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs; OT relevance: body awareness difficulties in DCD, autism; contributes to motor planning and postural control
- Vestibular system → SI function → gravity detection (utricle, saccule), movement detection (semicircular canals); bilateral integration; OT relevance: gravitational insecurity, poor bilateral coordination; contributes to visual-motor integration
- Visual system → SI function → visual discrimination, visual-spatial processing, visual-motor integration; OT relevance: VMI assessments (Beery VMI), visual perception after TBI or stroke
- Auditory system → SI function → auditory discrimination and processing; OT relevance: auditory processing disorder and its impact on learning and attention
- Gustatory system → SI function → taste discrimination; OT relevance: feeding difficulties in paediatric OT
- Olfactory system → SI function → smell; OT relevance: olfactory sensitivities in autism and feeding disorders
- Dunn's Model → 4 quadrants → model based on two dimensions: neurological threshold (high vs low) × behavioural response (passive vs active self-regulation)
- Quadrant 1 → Bystander → high threshold + passive regulation → under-responsive, misses sensory information; appears inattentive; requires intense stimulation to register
- Quadrant 2 → Seeker → high threshold + active regulation → actively seeks intense sensory experiences; high activity level; sensory-seeking behaviour
- Quadrant 3 → Sensor → low threshold + passive regulation → highly reactive to ordinary sensory input; easily overwhelmed; hypersensitive
- Quadrant 4 → Avoider → low threshold + active regulation → actively avoids sensory input; withdrawal, rigid routines; negative reactions to unexpected sensory experiences
- Miller SPD nosology → 3 main categories → (1) Sensory Modulation Disorder (SMD): subtype over-responsivity, under-responsivity, sensory seeking; (2) Sensory Discrimination Disorder (SDD): difficulty interpreting sensory information quality; (3) Sensory-Based Motor Disorder (SBMD): subtypes dyspraxia (motor planning difficulty) and postural disorder (poor postural stability and endurance)
- ASI fidelity criteria → Parham et al (2011) → 10 elements: (1) evaluation including SIPT-informed assessment; (2) active participation of child; (3) enhanced sensory opportunities; (4) just-right challenge; (5) collaborative goal-setting with family; (6) therapeutic playfulness; (7) therapist clinical reasoning; (8) therapeutic alliance; (9) family involvement in planning and intervention; (10) outcome monitoring
- ASI vs sensory-based activities → distinction → ASI requires all 10 fidelity elements (Parham 2011) including child-directed activity, just-right challenge, and therapeutic playfulness in a specialised sensory gym environment; sensory-based activities provide sensory input (e.g., weighted blankets, bouncing, brushing) WITHOUT meeting ASI fidelity — they are not equivalent in evidence or theoretical basis; in academic writing students must distinguish the two
- SIPT → full name → Sensory Integration and Praxis Tests; author: Ayres (1989); 17 subtests measuring vestibular, proprioceptive, tactile, visual, and praxis functions; standardised normative data; scores as standard deviations from norm
- SIPT → 17 subtests → include: Space Visualisation, Figure-Ground Perception, Standing-Walking Balance, Design Copying, Postural Praxis, Bilateral Motor Coordination, Praxis on Verbal Command, Constructional Praxis, Postrotary Nystagmus, Motor Accuracy, Sequencing Praxis, Oral Praxis, Manual Form Perception, Kinaesthesia, Finger Identification, Graphaesthesia, Localisation of Tactile Stimuli
- Evidence controversy → Bodison and Parham (2018) → systematic review in AJOT found moderate evidence for ASI (fidelity) for autism and SPD; evidence for non-fidelity sensory-based activities is weaker; ongoing debate about whether sensory processing disorder (SPD) is a distinct clinical diagnosis or symptom cluster
- Evidence base → current status → ASI with fidelity has moderate evidence for paediatric populations, particularly autism; AOTA has formal position on sensory integration (2019 position paper); controversy remains about whether SPD should be a separate DSM/ICD diagnosis

#### H2: FAQ — Sensory Integration Theory Questions from OT Students
> **Critical rule**: New representative query variants only.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. **What is the difference between ASI and sensory-based activities in OT?** → Answer direction: ASI (Ayres Sensory Integration with fidelity) meets all 10 criteria defined by Parham et al (2011): child-directed, just-right challenge, therapeutic playfulness, enhanced sensation, SIPT-informed assessment, etc. Sensory-based activities (weighted blankets, sensory diets, brushing) provide sensory input but do not meet ASI fidelity. They are not equivalent. In evidence-based OT assignments, students must clearly distinguish between the two and cite the appropriate evidence for each.
2. **What are the 4 quadrants of Dunn's Sensory Processing Model?** → Answer direction: Bystander (high threshold, passive response — under-registers sensory input), Seeker (high threshold, active response — seeks intense sensory experiences), Sensor (low threshold, passive response — hypersensitive, easily overwhelmed), Avoider (low threshold, active response — actively avoids sensory experiences). The Sensory Profile 2 maps to these four quadrants.
3. **Is sensory processing disorder (SPD) a recognised diagnosis?** → Answer direction: SPD is not currently included in DSM-5 or ICD-11 as a standalone diagnosis. It is used clinically by OTs and some paediatricians but remains diagnostically contested. For assignments, students should note the diagnostic controversy and distinguish between SPD as described by Miller's nosology (a clinical OT framework) and formal psychiatric/medical diagnosis.
4. **How many subtests does the SIPT have?** → Answer direction: 17 subtests. The SIPT assesses vestibular, proprioceptive, tactile, visual, and praxis functions. It requires certified administration training (post-qualifying specialist course) and is standardised for ages 4–8 years 11 months.
5. **What evidence is there for sensory integration therapy in OT?** → Answer direction: Bodison and Parham's (2018) systematic review in AJOT found moderate evidence for ASI (with fidelity criteria) for children with autism and SPD. Evidence is weaker for sensory-based activities without fidelity. The AOTA published a position paper in 2019 supporting ASI for children with specific conditions. Students must cite the specific evidence level when writing evidence-based assignments — "sensory integration has evidence" is not sufficient.
