#### H2: Hierarchy of Evidence in OT Assignments — From Systematic Reviews to Expert Opinion — *Major Query Need: Understanding and applying evidence levels*
> **Context vector**: Evidence classification framework — the structural tool students must apply in every critical appraisal assignment
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Level 1: Systematic reviews and meta-analyses (highest evidence)
> - Level 2: RCTs
> - Level 3: Cohort studies (prospective and retrospective)
> - Level 4: Case-control studies
> - Level 5: Case series and case reports
> - Level 6: Expert opinion, consensus, clinical guidelines without research base
> - How to correctly cite the evidence level in an OT assignment sentence
> - Why OT evidence often sits at Level 2–3 (ethical and practical constraints on RCT design in occupation-based research)
> **Predicate to use**: "classifies" — maintains classification context; avoid "supports" which shifts to clinical recommendation context
> **First word of first paragraph**: "The hierarchy of evidence classifies research studies..." — classification predicate locked in immediately
> **Writer note**: Present all six levels with their definitions and the specific study type for each. Address the common student error of citing a guideline as Level 1 evidence — NICE guidelines are not primary research and belong at Level 6 unless they are systematic review-based. The practical limitation of RCTs in OT (it is difficult to blind participants to an occupational therapy intervention) explains why Level 2–3 evidence is common and is a valid academic discussion point.
> **Word count target**: 300–400 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: Level 1 and Level 2 Evidence — Systematic Reviews and RCTs in OT Research
> **Attribute + Value**: Systematic reviews = comprehensive, reproducible search of all available evidence on a defined question; meta-analysis = quantitative pooling of RCT results; OT examples at Level 1: Cochrane reviews on CIMT, ADL training post-stroke, cognitive rehabilitation after TBI; RCTs in OT use standardised assessments (COPM, AMPS, Barthel) as outcome measures
> **Sequence modeling note**: Use "systematic reviews provide" not "evidence is provided by" — active predicate with research as the acting subject strengthens the classification context
> **Writer note**: Include at least one named OT systematic review as an example. This demonstrates the specific value of the evidence level, not just the abstract category.

##### H3: Levels 3–6 — Cohort Studies, Case Reports and Expert Opinion in OT Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**: Cohort studies follow a group with a shared characteristic over time — appropriate for studying occupational performance changes in specific populations; case-control studies compare those with/without an outcome retrospectively; case series = multiple case reports without controls; expert opinion includes clinical guidelines, professional position statements (RCOT, AOTA), and systematic review conclusions
> **Writer note**: Students need to know when Level 5–6 evidence is academically acceptable (e.g., when higher-level evidence does not exist for a specific OT population or intervention). This nuance is frequently required in MSc-level critical appraisal assignments.

#### H2: CASP Critical Appraisal Tools for OT Assignments — *Major Query Need: How to complete a CASP appraisal*
> **Context vector**: Critical appraisal methodology — the applied skill being assessed in OT assignments requiring evidence evaluation
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - CASP (Critical Appraisal Skills Programme) — purpose and relevance to OT assignments
> - CASP RCT checklist: 11 questions covering randomisation, blinding, allocation concealment, sample size, outcomes, generalisability
> - CASP qualitative checklist: 10 questions covering research design appropriateness, recruitment, data collection, reflexivity, ethical considerations, rigour, findings, transferability
> - CASP systematic review checklist: 10 questions covering review question clarity, search strategy, study quality assessment, result combination, precision of results
> - Common student errors in CASP completion (treating it as a tick-box exercise rather than justifying each answer with evidence from the paper)
> **Predicate to use**: "appraises" — maintains the evaluation/appraisal context throughout
> **First word of first paragraph**: "CASP appraisal tools provide..." — positions CASP as the active provider of the appraisal framework
> **Writer note**: The key academic skill here is justification — students must not just answer Yes/No/Can't Tell but explain WHY based on the paper's methodology. This is what differentiates a distinction-level appraisal from a pass. Cover this distinction explicitly.
> **Word count target**: 350–450 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: Using the CASP RCT Checklist for OT Evidence — All 11 Questions Explained
> **Attribute + Value**: CASP RCT 11 questions grouped into three sections: (A) validity of the trial results — questions 1-2 (randomisation, allocation concealment); (B) what are the results — questions 3-7 (blinding, group similarity at baseline, equal treatment apart from trial, primary outcome measure, precision of results); (C) will results help locally — questions 8-11 (outcome measure relevance to OT, generalisability, adverse effects considered, overall value of results to clinical practice)
> **Writer note**: Note the specific relevance of question 11 ("Is the evidence applicable to my OT practice context?") — this connects the clinical expertise pillar of EBP back to the appraisal process and is often missed by students.

##### H3: CASP Qualitative Checklist — Appraising Qualitative OT Research
> **Attribute + Value**: CASP qualitative 10 questions assess: (1) clear research question, (2) qualitative methodology appropriate, (3) research design appropriate to question, (4) recruitment strategy appropriate, (5) data collection addressed research question, (6) researcher-participant relationship considered, (7) ethical issues considered, (8) data analysis sufficiently rigorous, (9) clear statement of findings, (10) how valuable is the research; qualitative research is highly relevant to OT because occupation-based outcomes are often best captured through phenomenological or grounded theory approaches
> **Writer note**: Many OT students undervalue qualitative evidence because they assume it is lower on the hierarchy — challenge this: qualitative evidence at Level 3 can provide richer occupational meaning data than a Level 2 RCT for certain research questions.

#### H2: PEDro Scale — Rating RCT Quality in OT Assignments — *Major Query Need: PEDro scale use in OT critical appraisal*
> **Context vector**: PEDro as a quantitative supplement to CASP for RCT quality scoring — distinct from evidence level, focused on methodological quality
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - PEDro (Physiotherapy Evidence Database) scale — 11 items, scored 0–10 (item 1 not scored)
> - Score interpretation: ≥6 = high quality RCT; 4–5 = moderate quality; ≤3 = low quality
> - How PEDro differs from CASP: PEDro gives a numerical quality score; CASP gives a structured appraisal narrative
> - When to use PEDro in an OT assignment: when the assignment specifically asks for a quality score alongside the CASP narrative, or when comparing multiple RCTs
> - PEDro items relevant to OT RCT evaluation: random allocation (item 2), concealed allocation (item 3), therapist blinding (item 6 — often not achievable in OT), intention-to-treat analysis (item 9), between-group statistical comparisons (item 10)
> **Predicate to use**: "scores" — numerical evaluation predicate that distinguishes this section from qualitative appraisal
> **First word of first paragraph**: "The PEDro scale scores RCT methodological quality..." — immediate differentiation from CASP
> **Writer note**: Note that therapist blinding (item 6) is rarely achievable in OT research because the occupational therapist IS the intervention. Students should acknowledge this limitation explicitly in assignments — it does not disqualify an OT RCT; it is a known methodological constraint of the field.
> **Word count target**: 200–280 words

#### H2: FAQ — Evidence-Based Practice OT Assignment Questions
> **Critical rule**: These questions must NOT repeat Main Content. Each must extend toward new representative query variants and be self-contained enough for Passage Indexing.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **What is the difference between evidence-based practice and occupation-based practice in OT assignments?**
   → Answer direction: EBP and occupation-based practice are not mutually exclusive — EBP is the methodology for selecting interventions; occupation-based practice is a philosophical approach to OT that centres meaningful occupation. In OT assignments, the strongest arguments apply EBP methodology to evaluate occupation-based interventions. Reference the Level 1B evidence for occupation-based intervention noted in the main content, and acknowledge the ongoing debate about how to standardise "occupation-based" for research purposes.

2. **How do I choose between CASP and PEDro for critiquing an RCT in my OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: CASP provides a narrative appraisal with justification (better for assignments requiring critical discussion); PEDro provides a numerical quality score (better for assignments requiring comparison of multiple studies or explicit quality rating). Some OT assignments require both. If in doubt, use CASP as the primary tool and PEDro as a supplementary quality score.

3. **Which OT databases are most likely to return Level 1 evidence?**
   → Answer direction: OTseeker and the Cochrane Library are the most likely sources of Level 1 systematic reviews specific to OT. OTseeker limits results to RCTs and systematic reviews by design; Cochrane contains the most rigorously produced systematic reviews. For CINAHL and PubMed, apply study type filters (Systematic Review, RCT) to locate Level 1–2 evidence within their broader coverage.

4. **How do I justify using Level 3 or Level 4 evidence in an OT assignment when Level 1 evidence does not exist?**
   → Answer direction: State explicitly that the absence of higher-level evidence reflects the methodological challenges of OT research (difficulty blinding participants, ethical constraints on withholding treatment, heterogeneity of occupational performance outcomes) rather than a lack of practice effectiveness. Cite the principle that the best available evidence should be used, even if imperfect. Pair lower-level evidence with strong clinical reasoning and client-centred goal alignment to strengthen the argument.

5. **What does a distinction-level EBP paragraph look like in an OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: A distinction-level EBP paragraph (1) states the PICO question clearly, (2) identifies the evidence source with level AND quality rating (e.g., "Level 1A evidence from a Cochrane systematic review of 22 RCTs; individual RCTs rated ≥6 on PEDro"), (3) critically appraises the evidence (strength AND limitation), (4) applies the evidence to the specific client context using occupational performance language, and (5) incorporates client values as the deciding factor. Contrast this with a pass-level paragraph that cites evidence without appraisal or application.
