**Values per Attribute**:
- MDT → definition → multidisciplinary team working: professionals from different disciplines each contribute separate assessments and goals with information sharing; parallel working with a shared care plan but not necessarily shared goal-setting
- IDT → definition → interdisciplinary team: more integrated than MDT; shared goals are negotiated across disciplines; some role blurring; team members are more interdependent in goal-setting and intervention
- IPT → definition → interprofessional collaborative practice (IPCP): the most integrated form; professionals from different disciplines actively collaborate, share expertise, and may blur role boundaries in service of client-centred goals; endorsed by WHO as best practice
- IPE → CAIPE definition → "learning with, from, and about each other to improve collaboration and the quality of care" (CAIPE — Centre for the Advancement of Interprofessional Education); IPE occurs when two or more professions learn together
- Stroke MDT → composition → consultant/registrar (neurologist or stroke physician); specialist stroke nurse; physiotherapist; occupational therapist; speech and language therapist (SLT); dietitian; social worker; clinical psychologist; care coordinator/discharge planner; clinical pharmacist in some teams; radiographer for imaging interpretation
- Stroke MDT → OT-specific role → early supported discharge OT; upper limb rehabilitation (CIMT, bilateral training); ADL retraining; cognitive/perceptual assessment (neglect, executive function, memory); home modification and equipment; fatigue management; driving rehabilitation referral
- Mental health MDT → composition → consultant psychiatrist; psychiatric registrar/SHO; community psychiatric nurse (CPN); occupational therapist; psychologist (clinical or counselling); social worker; pharmacy; peer support worker; care coordinator
- Mental health MDT → OT-specific role → occupational formulation; MOHOST/OCAIRS assessment; activity group facilitation; meaningful occupation facilitation; vocational rehabilitation; independent living skills; sensory modulation; supported employment
- Paediatric MDT → composition → paediatrician/developmental paediatrician; occupational therapist; physiotherapist; speech and language therapist; special educational needs coordinator (SENCo); educational psychologist; school nurse; portage worker (early intervention); social worker where applicable
- Paediatric MDT → OT-specific role → sensory processing assessment; fine motor and gross motor assessment; school-based adaptation; EHCP contribution; play-based intervention; self-care skill development
- OT role within MDT → unique contributions → (1) Occupational performance assessment: AMPS, COPM, functional ADL assessment — no other AHP uses occupation as the primary assessment lens; (2) Activity analysis: uniquely OT; (3) Discharge planning that includes home environment modification; (4) Vocational rehabilitation; (5) Cognitive/perceptual assessments that link to ADL function
- Role boundaries → OT vs PT → PT focuses on movement, biomechanics, exercise, pain, and mobility; OT focuses on occupational function, ADL, IADL, work, leisure; overlap in functional mobility and transfers but OT contextualises function within occupation
- Role boundaries → OT vs nursing → nursing focuses on clinical care, medication management, wound care, and health monitoring; OT focuses on occupational performance and enabling independence; overlap in ADL support but OT's goal is independence, not care provision
- Role boundaries → OT vs SLT → SLT focuses on communication, swallowing, and language; OT may assess cognitive-communication function and its impact on occupational performance; OT does not provide communication or dysphagia therapy
- IPL requirements → UK OT placements → RCOT and HCPC require students to demonstrate interprofessional learning during placements; HCPC Standard 9 (work appropriately with others); many OT programmes require a placement IPL log or reflective entry
- CAIPE competency framework → domains → values and ethics; roles and responsibilities; interprofessional communication; teams and teamwork; (also includes person-centred care as a core element)
- Conflict resolution in MDT → for reflective essays → MDT conflict scenarios (e.g., disagreement about discharge destination, risk assessment disagreement) are common reflective essay topics; students should use structured reflection (Gibbs, Schon) combined with HCPC Standard 2 (ethical boundaries) and the CAIPE values and ethics domain

#### H2: FAQ — MDT and Interprofessional Practice in OT Assignments
> **Critical rule**: New representative query variants only.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. **What is the difference between MDT and interprofessional team working?** → Answer direction: MDT = multidisciplinary team — professionals from different disciplines work in parallel with information sharing but often separate goals. Interprofessional collaborative practice (IPT/IPCP) = professionals actively collaborate, share goals, and may blur role boundaries in the client's interest. WHO endorses IPCP as best practice. For assignments, students should use the correct term for the level of integration they are describing.
2. **Who is in a stroke MDT?** → Answer direction: The core stroke MDT includes consultant/registrar, specialist stroke nurse, physiotherapist, OT, SLT, dietitian, social worker, and clinical psychologist. Additional members may include a clinical pharmacist, care coordinator, and rehabilitation assistant. NICE NG128 specifies the MDT composition for stroke rehabilitation — cite this when writing stroke MDT descriptions in OT assignments.
3. **What does CAIPE stand for and why does it matter for OT students?** → Answer direction: CAIPE = Centre for the Advancement of Interprofessional Education. CAIPE provides the leading academic definition of IPE ("learning with, from, and about each other") and a competency framework for interprofessional practice. OT students are assessed against interprofessional competencies on placement (HCPC Standard 9) and many universities use CAIPE-derived frameworks for IPL assessment.
4. **How is OT different from physiotherapy in the MDT?** → Answer direction: OT focuses on occupational function — what the person can do in their daily life (ADL, IADL, work, leisure). PT focuses on movement, biomechanics, exercise, pain, and physical mobility. Both work on functional rehabilitation in settings like stroke, but OT's assessment lens is occupational performance; PT's is movement quality and physical capacity. Activity analysis is uniquely OT; gait analysis is PT's domain.
5. **How should I write about MDT conflict in a reflective essay?** → Answer direction: Use a structured reflective model (Gibbs, Johns, or Schon) to analyse the conflict. Identify: what happened, how you felt, what ethical principles apply (RCOT Code of Ethics — beneficence, autonomy, justice), what HCPC standards are relevant (Standard 2 — ethical boundaries, Standard 9 — work with others), what you learned, and what you would do differently. Do not describe conflict as exclusively the fault of other team members — reflective writing requires self-examination.
