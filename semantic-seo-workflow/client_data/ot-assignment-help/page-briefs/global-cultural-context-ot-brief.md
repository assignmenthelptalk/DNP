#### H2: Cross-Cultural Validity of Occupational Therapy Assessment Tools — *Major Query Need: Whether standard OT assessments work across cultures*
> **Context vector**: The psychometric and practical limitations of OT standardised assessments when used across cultural contexts
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - COPM: client-centred; validated in 35+ languages; cultural adaptation required for collectivist societies where the individual may not identify their own priorities separately from family/community; performance area labels (self-care, productivity, leisure) carry cultural assumptions
> - Barthel Index: validated cross-culturally; activity definitions carry cultural bias (e.g., toilet transfer assumes sitting toilet not squatting; bathing = shower/bath not communal bath or bucket wash); scoring thresholds for independence vary by cultural norm
> - MOHO: Iwama's (2006) critique — assumes individual agency as primary driver of occupational engagement; in collectivist cultures, environmental and relational determinism are stronger than individual volition; MOHO's volition construct does not translate directly
> **Predicate to use**: "assumes," "limits," "requires adaptation," "applies differently"
> **First word of first paragraph**: "Standardised" — "Standardised occupational therapy assessments were largely developed in Western, English-speaking contexts and carry cultural assumptions that affect their validity when applied across diverse populations."
> **Writer note**: This is factual EAV content — specific tools with specific cultural limitations. Give enough detail for a student to write a critical analysis section. Do not be vague. For the MOHO section, name Iwama directly with the year (2006) and summarise the critique in 2–3 specific sentences.
> **Word count target**: 400–500 words

##### H3: COPM Across Cultures — Adaptations and Limitations
> **Attribute + Value**: COPM validated in 35+ languages; Law et al. (2019) 5th edition is current manual; cultural adaptations include modified interviewing style for collectivist societies, family/proxy involvement in priority identification; occupation categories (self-care, productivity, leisure) carry Western assumptions — productivity = paid work in Western context may not translate
> **Writer note**: Provide enough detail for a student to include COPM cultural considerations in a methodology section or literature review. Note that using COPM "as designed" without cultural adaptation may produce invalid priorities in non-Western contexts.

##### H3: Iwama's Critique of MOHO in Non-Western Contexts
> **Attribute + Value**: Iwama (2006) "The Kawa Model: Culturally Relevant Occupational Therapy"; critique: MOHO's volition construct assumes individual choice as the primary driver of occupational engagement; this assumption is a Western individualist cultural product; in collectivist Asian, Indigenous, and some African cultural contexts, occupational engagement is determined primarily by family obligation, social role, and environmental context — not individual volition; Iwama proposed the Kawa Model as an alternative
> **Sequence modeling note**: "Iwama" in subject position — "Iwama (2006) argues that..." — positions the argument as citable academic critique, not student opinion
> **Writer note**: Students writing model comparison essays or critical analyses of OT models will need to cite this specific critique. Give enough detail to make it citable. Do not resolve the debate — present it as a live theoretical tension in OT literature.

#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions — Global and Cultural Context of Occupational Therapy

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **What is the difference between cultural competence and cultural humility in occupational therapy?**
   → Answer direction: Define cultural competence as the attainment of knowledge/skills/attitudes about cultural groups (Leavitt); contrast with cultural humility as an ongoing, self-reflective process that acknowledges power differentials and institutional accountability. Note that OT professional bodies have shifted toward cultural humility language. 3–4 sentences, self-contained.

2. **How many countries does WFOT represent, and what are its minimum education standards?**
   → Answer direction: WFOT represents 110+ member countries; was founded in 1952; minimum education standards require 1,000 practice hours for both BSc and MSc pre-registration programmes; WFOT recognition ensures international portability of OT qualifications. 2–3 sentences.

3. **Is the COPM valid across different cultural contexts?**
   → Answer direction: COPM has been translated and validated in 35+ languages; however, the occupation categories (self-care, productivity, leisure) and the individual priority-identification process carry Western assumptions that require adaptation in collectivist cultural contexts; cultural adaptation of the interviewing process is recommended (Law et al., 2019). 3–4 sentences.

4. **What is occupational justice and why does it matter for OT students?**
   → Answer direction: Occupational justice (Townsend & Wilcock, 2004) refers to the right of all people to participate in meaningful occupations; forms of occupational injustice include occupational apartheid, deprivation, marginalisation, and imbalance; relevant for OT students writing critical analysis essays at Level 6–7; WFOT's 2019 human rights position statement formalises OTs' advocacy obligation. 4 sentences.

5. **How does OT differ between the UK, USA, Australia, and Canada?**
   → Answer direction: UK — RCOT/HCPC regulated, HCPC registration mandatory; USA — AOTA/ACOTE accredited, OTPF-4, NBCOT licensure; Australia — OTA/OTBA, CMOP-E dominant; Canada — CAOT standards, CMOP-E dominant. Each country uses different theoretical frameworks and has different licensure pathways, though all meet WFOT minimum education standards. 4–5 sentences.
