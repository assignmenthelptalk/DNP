#### H2: Occupational Justice — Rights, Deprivation, and Marginalisation in OT Theory — *Major Query Need: occupational justice definition and subtypes*
> **Context vector**: Occupational justice as an OT theoretical construct and its sub-concepts
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Occupational justice → definition → Townsend & Wilcock: the right of every person to participate in meaningful occupation across the lifespan regardless of health status, ability, or social position
> - Occupational justice → subtypes (all 4 must be defined with values):
>   - Occupational apartheid → definition → systematic exclusion of groups from meaningful occupation through social, political, or economic structures
>   - Occupational deprivation → definition → external circumstances (e.g., incarceration, illness, displacement) that prevent a person from engaging in occupations they need or want to do
>   - Occupational imbalance → definition → mismatch between the amount and type of occupation engaged in and the person's needs — either too much obligatory occupation or too little meaningful occupation
>   - Occupational marginalisation → definition → occupation choices are undermined by others' expectations, norms, or cultural values — the person is denied the right to make occupational choices
> - Occupational justice → theoretical origin → Wilcock 1993/1998 (Ann Wilcock's occupation and health framework); Townsend & Wilcock 2004 (formal conceptualisation)
> **Predicate to use**: "defines," "distinguishes," "constitutes"
> **First word of first paragraph**: "Occupational justice describes..."
> **Writer note**: Students must be able to distinguish all four subtypes in essays — examiners mark on conceptual precision. Provide a concrete example sentence for each subtype. Do not reference the social model of disability here — that belongs in the Supplementary Content contextual bridge.
> **Word count target**: 350–420 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: Occupational Apartheid, Deprivation, Imbalance, and Marginalisation — Definitions and Differences
> **Attribute + Value**: All four occupational justice subtypes with specific definitional values. Occupational apartheid → example: people with severe disabilities systematically excluded from employment and community participation through inaccessible environments and discriminatory attitudes. Occupational deprivation → example: a refugee in temporary accommodation unable to maintain prior work occupation due to legal/structural barriers. Occupational imbalance → example: a carer whose entire daily schedule is consumed by caregiving, leaving no time for self-care or leisure occupations. Occupational marginalisation → example: a child with autism whose sensory preferences are dismissed, denying their right to choose occupational environments.
> **Sequence modeling note**: Define each subtype with "X refers to..." — not "X is when..." — to maintain academic register.
> **Writer note**: These definitions must be assignment-ready: precise enough to cite in an essay, with enough contextual example to demonstrate understanding. Writers should avoid using "deprivation" and "marginalisation" interchangeably — they are distinct constructs.

#### H2: Wilcock's "Doing, Being, Becoming, Belonging" — A Framework for Understanding Occupation — *Major Query Need: doing being becoming belonging Wilcock framework*
> **Context vector**: Wilcock's four-dimensional framework for understanding occupation as a human phenomenon
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Doing → definition → the physical and observable engagement in occupations; the most readily visible dimension
> - Being → definition → the reflective, existential dimension — who one is in the absence of activity; occupation as identity-expression
> - Becoming → definition → the transformational dimension — how engagement in occupation shapes who one is becoming over time; occupation as a vehicle for growth and development
> - Belonging → definition → the social and relational dimension — the sense of connectedness, community, and shared identity that comes from occupational participation with others
> - Framework → theoretical significance → challenges purely instrumental views of occupation (occupation as a means to an end) by positioning occupation as intrinsically valuable for health and well-being
> - Framework → assessment application → used in occupational profiling and goal-setting language in OT assignments; allows documentation of all four occupational dimensions for a client
> **Predicate to use**: "characterises," "describes," "encompasses"
> **First word of first paragraph**: "Wilcock's framework characterises occupation across four dimensions..."
> **Writer note**: This framework is tested at all levels (L4–L7). Students must be able to define all four terms and apply them in occupational profile writing. The belonging dimension is most frequently omitted in student essays. Give it equal definitional weight.
> **Word count target**: 280–350 words
> **Internal links**: None

#### H2: FAQ — Occupational Science Concepts Commonly Tested in OT Assignments
> **Critical rule**: These questions must NOT repeat Main Content. They expand toward new query variants — represented and representative query extensions, passage indexing targets, and People Also Ask candidates.
> Each answer must be self-contained enough to rank as a passage independently.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. **What is the difference between occupational science and occupational therapy?** → Answer direction: Occupational science is a pure academic discipline that studies occupation as a human phenomenon; occupational therapy is a health profession that uses occupation as its therapeutic medium. Occupational science generates the theoretical knowledge base; OT applies that knowledge. A student citing occupational science in an essay demonstrates theoretical grounding.
2. **What is occupational deprivation and how is it different from occupational marginalisation?** → Answer direction: Define each with precision — deprivation is externally imposed barriers to occupation; marginalisation is the denial of the right to occupational choice by others' expectations. Provide one-sentence example for each. Note both are Townsend and Wilcock constructs.
3. **How does the "doing, being, becoming, belonging" framework apply to OT practice?** → Answer direction: Wilcock's framework is used in occupational profiling and goal-setting. Clinicians use it to capture all four dimensions of occupation rather than focusing only on the doing/performance dimension. In assignments, it provides a structure for describing a client's occupational needs holistically.
4. **What is occupational identity and how does it relate to MOHO?** → Answer direction: Occupational identity is a MOHO construct — specifically from the volition and habituation subsystems — that describes a person's narrative sense of who they are as an occupational being. It is used in MOHO-based case studies to describe a client's sense of purpose, roles, and future occupational goals.
5. **Is occupational justice the same as social justice?** → Answer direction: Occupational justice is a specific application of social justice principles to the domain of occupation. It goes further than social justice by naming occupation-specific forms of injustice (apartheid, deprivation, imbalance, marginalisation) and arguing that participation in meaningful occupation is itself a human right.
