**Values per Attribute**:
- Deontology → Kant → categorical imperative: "Act only according to that maxim whereby you can at the same time will that it should become a universal law" — duty-based ethics; rightness of action judged by adherence to moral rules/duties regardless of outcome; OT application: duty of candour (must be honest even if disclosure is uncomfortable); respecting autonomy as a duty regardless of consequences
- Consequentialism/utilitarianism → Mill → "greatest happiness for the greatest number"; the rightness of an action judged by its outcomes/consequences; OT application: resource allocation decisions (maximising benefit across a caseload); weighing risk of OT intervention against likely outcome benefit
- Virtue ethics → MacIntyre/Aristotle → focuses on character and virtues of the moral agent rather than rules or consequences; virtues relevant to OT: honesty, compassion, prudence, justice; OT application: what kind of practitioner am I becoming? — connects to professional identity development
- Principlism → Beauchamp & Childress (1979, now 9th edition 2019) → 4 principles: autonomy (respect for persons' right to self-determination), beneficence (duty to act in best interests), non-maleficence (duty to avoid harm), justice (fair distribution of benefits and burdens)
- Autonomy → OT application → obtaining valid informed consent; respecting the client's right to refuse assessment or intervention; capacity assessment under MCA 2005
- Beneficence → OT application → selecting the intervention most likely to improve occupational participation; acting in the client's best interest when capacity is absent (MCA 2005 best interests decision)
- Non-maleficence → OT application → risk assessment in home visits; avoiding unnecessary or harmful interventions; recognising limits of competence (HCPC Standard 1)
- Justice → OT application → equitable access to OT services; occupational justice (structural barriers as ethics issue); resource allocation
- HCPC fitness to practise → definition → a registrant's fitness to practise is impaired if their standards of performance are so low that they risk the safety of service users; impaired fitness to practise grounds include misconduct, poor performance, adverse health, criminal conviction
- HCPC duty of candour → requirement → registrants must be open and honest with service users when something goes wrong that could harm them; connected to HCPC Standard 15 (fitness to practise)
- Professional identity → Hooper & Wood stages → (1) pre-professional identity (before OT degree); (2) student OT professional identity development (during degree — acquiring OT-specific knowledge, values, role); (3) newly qualified practitioner identity (post-graduation)
- Professional identity → threats in student placements → encountering practices that conflict with OT values; role confusion in MDT settings; inadequate supervision; placement failure anxiety; professional socialisation into non-OT cultures
- Moral distress → Jameton (1984) definition → "the experience of knowing the right action to take but being constrained from taking it by institutional or organisational barriers" — important for OT student reflective essays on ethical dilemmas in placement
- Moral distress → OT student context → common scenarios: student knows a client's home environment is unsafe but discharge is being rushed; student disagrees with an intervention plan but cannot influence the clinical decision; student witnesses practice that conflicts with OT values
- DECIDE model → 6 steps → D = Define the problem; E = Ethical review (identify relevant ethical principles); C = Consider the options; I = Investigate the outcomes; D = Decide on action; E = Evaluate the result; used in ethics assignment problem-solving
- MCA 2005 → 5-step capacity test → (1) Is there an impairment or disturbance in the functioning of the mind or brain? (2) Does this impairment cause inability to understand information relevant to the decision? (3) Retain the information? (4) Use or weigh the information? (5) Communicate a decision? All 5 elements must be present for incapacity to be established; capacity is decision-specific and time-specific
- MCA 2005 → best interests decision → when a person lacks capacity, decisions must be made in their best interests: consider their past wishes, present wishes as far as ascertainable, values and beliefs, views of family/carers; use the least restrictive option
- Confidentiality → GDPR 2018 (data protection legislation) + HCPC Standard 10 (maintain records) + HCPC Standard 2 (legal and ethical boundaries); OT must maintain confidentiality except in specific circumstances (risk of harm to self or others, safeguarding, court order, public interest)
- Resource allocation → ethical dilemma → prioritising clients on a waiting list; declining to provide a service a client would benefit from due to budget constraints; occupational injustice arising from resource decisions
- Dual-role boundaries → definition → situations where an OT has both a therapeutic and a non-therapeutic relationship with a client (e.g., providing OT to a friend or family member); HCPC and RCOT guidance prohibit or restrict dual-role therapeutic relationships
- Social media → HCPC guidance → registrants must not post identifiable client information; must maintain professional boundaries online; must not bring the profession into disrepute; applies to personal and professional social media accounts
- Cultural and religious considerations → ethical dimension → autonomy and beneficence may conflict when a client's cultural or religious values lead them to refuse treatment that would benefit them; OT must respect cultural values and seek culturally safe practice; Kleinman's explanatory models framework

#### H2: FAQ — OT Ethics Questions from Students
> **Critical rule**: New representative query variants only.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. **What is the difference between an ethical dilemma and moral distress in OT?** → Answer direction: An ethical dilemma involves two or more genuine ethical options where choosing one means compromising another value (e.g., respecting autonomy vs preventing harm). Moral distress (Jameton 1984) occurs when there is no genuine ethical choice available — the right action is known but institutional or structural constraints prevent it. Both are tested in OT reflective essays but they describe different ethical experiences.
2. **What are the 5 principles of the Mental Capacity Act 2005?** → Answer direction: (1) Assume capacity unless it is established otherwise; (2) All practicable steps must be taken to help a person make a decision before they are treated as lacking capacity; (3) A person is not to be treated as unable to decide because they make an unwise decision; (4) Any act done or decision made under the MCA must be done in the best interests of the person who lacks capacity; (5) Any act or decision must be the least restrictive of the person's rights and freedom of action.
3. **Which ethical framework should I use in my OT ethics assignment?** → Answer direction: It depends on the assignment question. If the question focuses on professional duty and legal obligations, deontological framing (Kant, HCPC) is most appropriate. If it focuses on outcomes and wellbeing, consequentialist/utilitarian framing applies. If it focuses on the character of the practitioner or professional values, virtue ethics is relevant. Most OT ethics assignments benefit from a principlism framework (Beauchamp & Childress) that allows multiple principles to be considered together — and from acknowledging when those principles conflict.
4. **What is professional identity in OT and why is it tested in assignments?** → Answer direction: Professional identity is a student's developing sense of themselves as an OT — the values, knowledge, skills, and ways of thinking that define occupational therapists as a professional group. It is tested in OT assignments because the HCPC and RCOT require registrants to maintain a strong professional identity as the foundation for ethical practice. Hooper and Wood's stages of professional identity development provide a theoretical framework for reflective essays on professional socialisation.
5. **What should I know about confidentiality for OT assignments?** → Answer direction: OTs have both a legal (GDPR 2018) and professional (HCPC Standard 10; RCOT Code of Ethics — fidelity) duty to maintain confidentiality. The duty is not absolute — exceptions include risk of harm, safeguarding, court orders, and serious public interest. In assignment writing, students should identify the type of disclosure, the ethical principle supporting or opposing it, and the relevant legal basis. Case scenarios involving confidentiality dilemmas are common in Level 5–6 OT professional practice essays.
