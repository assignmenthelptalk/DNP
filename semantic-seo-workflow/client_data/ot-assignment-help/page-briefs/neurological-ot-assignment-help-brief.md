##### H3: Compensatory vs Restorative Approach — Assignment Argument Structure
> **Attribute + Value**: Compensatory: change the task/environment/method to enable occupation despite the impairment; appropriate when neurological recovery is limited or plateau reached; examples: one-handed dressing technique, adapted cutlery, wheelchair for long-distance mobility. Restorative: aim to recover neurological function through activity and training; appropriate in early recovery phase; supported by neuroplasticity evidence (use-dependent plasticity); examples: CIMT, task-specific training, bilateral training.
> **Sequence modeling note**: Present compensatory AFTER restorative — restorative is the theoretically preferred approach in early rehabilitation; compensatory is selected when restorative potential is limited. The sequence of presentation reflects clinical priority.
> **Writer note**: Show how to structure an assignment argument: "A restorative approach was selected in the early post-stroke phase (4 weeks post-onset) given evidence of neurological recovery potential and the client's preference to regain functional hand use. Should upper limb recovery plateau at 6 months, compensatory strategies including one-handed dressing techniques will be introduced." 140–180 words.

#### H2: Frequently Asked Questions About Neurological OT Assignments — *Supplementary Content*

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **How do I correctly report FIM scores in an OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: When reporting FIM in an assignment, include: (1) the total FIM score with its interpretation band; (2) the motor subscale score (range 13–91) with interpretation; (3) the cognitive subscale score (range 5–35) with interpretation; (4) individual item scores for items most relevant to your case study focus. Example: "The client's FIM at initial assessment yielded a total score of 62/126 (Minimum Assistance band: 54–71), indicating approximately 25–49% assistance required for self-care and mobility tasks. The motor subscale score of 48/91 reflected significant ADL dependence, while the cognitive subscale score of 14/35 indicated moderate assistance required for communication and problem-solving tasks." Never report a FIM total score alone without the subscale breakdown — this provides insufficient clinical information for an OT assignment examiner.

2. **What is the CIMT protocol and how do I describe it accurately in an assignment?**
   → Answer direction: The Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy protocol involves: (1) Restraint: the less-affected upper limb is restrained using a padded mitt or sling for 90% of waking hours during the intervention period; (2) Massed practice: 3–6 hours per day of intensive, task-directed upper limb training using the affected limb; (3) Duration: typically 14 consecutive working days (2 weeks); (4) Technique: shaping — progressive approximations toward a target movement, with each attempt positively reinforced; (5) Eligibility: ≥10° active wrist extension, ≥10° active extension in thumb and at least 2 fingers, Modified Ashworth Scale spasticity ≤2, adequate cognition. In an assignment, do not simply write "CIMT was used" — specify the protocol parameters, justify eligibility, and cite the evidence base (EXCITE trial; Cochrane review).

3. **What is the difference between the Fugl-Meyer and the ARAT in neurological OT assignments?**
   → Answer direction: Both measure upper limb function post-stroke but from different perspectives. The Fugl-Meyer Upper Limb Assessment (0–66) is a performance-based measure of motor recovery using a Brunnstrom stage-based approach — it measures volitional movement across the recovery spectrum, including synergistic patterns and coordination. It is most sensitive in the early and middle stages of recovery. The ARAT (Action Research Arm Test, 0–57) is a performance-based measure of upper extremity function during specific upper limb movements — grasp, grip, pinch, and gross movement. It is more sensitive to functional performance and change in moderate-to-good recovery. In an assignment, select Fugl-Meyer when documenting neuromotor recovery trajectory; select ARAT when documenting functional upper limb performance and responsiveness to intervention (e.g., CIMT outcomes).

4. **How do I write about motor learning principles in a neurological OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: Motor learning principles provide the theoretical justification for task-specific training in neurological OT. In your assignment, reference the following with their specific implications: (1) Repetition — high repetition is required for use-dependent neuroplasticity; state approximate numbers (20–50+ repetitions per session); (2) Practice variability — random practice (varying tasks within a session) produces better transfer than blocked practice (repeating the same task); (3) Feedback — intermittent, delayed feedback (knowledge of results) promotes motor learning better than continuous feedback; avoid over-cueing; (4) Task specificity — practice the actual functional task, not isolated exercises; (5) Stages of learning — Fitts and Posner: cognitive (conscious attention required), associative (improving accuracy), autonomous (automatic performance). Reference Shumway-Cook and Woollacott's motor control text as the primary evidence source.

5. **What Hoehn and Yahr stage should I specify in a Parkinson's OT case study, and why does it matter?**
   → Answer direction: The Hoehn and Yahr (H&Y) scale (Stages 1–5) is the standard classification for disease progression in Parkinson's disease and directly determines OT goals and intervention focus. In a case study, always state the H&Y stage in the assessment section, justify it with clinical evidence, and explicitly connect it to your OT goals. Stage 2 (bilateral disease, no balance impairment): OT focuses on ADL assessment, handwriting, work modification, home safety education. Stage 3 (postural instability): OT focuses on falls risk, home environment assessment, ADL adaptations, driving assessment. Stage 4 (severe disability, still ambulant): major home modification, assistive technology, caregiver training. Failing to specify H&Y stage in a Parkinson's case study is one of the most common errors in neurological OT assignments and demonstrates a lack of condition-specific clinical reasoning.
