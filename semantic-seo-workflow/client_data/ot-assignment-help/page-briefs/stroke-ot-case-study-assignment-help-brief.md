#### H2: Stroke Types and Consequences — What OT Assignments Must Address — *Major Query Need: Stroke clinical background for OT case studies*
> **Context vector**: Stroke clinical classification and consequences as the foundational EAV layer for all OT assessment and intervention decisions in assignments
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Ischaemic stroke: 85% — arterial occlusion (thrombotic or embolic); more common left hemisphere = right hemiplegia + aphasia; more common right hemisphere = left hemiplegia + unilateral neglect
> - Haemorrhagic stroke: 15% — intracerebral (within brain tissue) or subarachnoid (around brain); typically more severe; different rehabilitation trajectory
> - TIA: transient ischaemic attack — symptoms resolve within 24 hours; OT role = risk factor education, activity modification
> - Motor consequences: hemiplegia (complete paralysis) or hemiparesis (partial weakness), contralateral to lesion side; affects ADL performance, transfers, mobility
> - Perceptual consequences: unilateral neglect (most common after right hemisphere stroke, left-sided neglect); hemianopia (visual field loss)
> - Communication: aphasia (language processing difficulty) and dysphasia (speech production difficulty); affect OT assessment validity — require alternative assessment methods
> - Dysphagia: swallowing difficulty; relevant to OT meal preparation and kitchen assessment
> - Cognitive: memory impairment, attention deficits (sustained/selective/divided), executive function changes (planning, initiation, organisation) — all affect occupational performance
> - Psychological: emotional lability (uncontrolled crying or laughing), post-stroke depression, post-stroke fatigue — affect engagement in OT
> **Predicate to use**: "produces" — stroke as the cause producing consequences that OT assesses
> **First word of first paragraph**: "Ischaemic stroke, comprising 85% of all strokes..." — specific value first
> **Writer note**: Connect each consequence directly to its OT assessment implication. Do not list consequences in isolation — each must be connected to "therefore, in an OT case study, the student must..." This keeps the assignment-writing context dominant.
> **Word count target**: 320–420 words
> **Internal links**: None

##### H3: Unilateral Neglect and Hemianopia — Assessment and Intervention Implications for OT Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**: Unilateral neglect = failure to attend to stimuli on one side of space despite intact sensory pathways — not a vision problem; common after right parietal cortex stroke; affects ADL, transfers, mobility; assessed in OT assignments using star cancellation test or line bisection (often referenced but not the primary standardised tool for OT case studies — Barthel and COPM are primary); hemianopia = visual field loss — both neglect and hemianopia must be distinguished in case study assignments as they require different interventions
> **Writer note**: Students frequently confuse unilateral neglect with hemianopia in assignments — address this directly as it is a common marker of clinical reasoning quality.

##### H3: Post-Stroke Cognitive Changes — Executive Function, Memory and Attention in OT Case Studies
> **Attribute + Value**: Executive function changes affect initiation of ADLs, task sequencing, and safety during home activities; MoCA cut-off score ≤25 indicates cognitive impairment requiring further OT assessment; attention subtypes (sustained/selective/divided) each produce different functional consequences documented in OT case studies; memory impairment affects learning new compensation techniques — impacts intervention approach selection
> **Writer note**: When a stroke case study patient has an MoCA ≤25, the student must explain what this means for assessment validity (can the patient self-report reliably for COPM?) and for intervention selection (can the patient learn new techniques?). This level of clinical reasoning is what separates distinction from pass at Level 6.

#### H2: OT Stroke Assessments — Scoring, Interpretation and Documentation — *Major Query Need: Which assessment tools to use and how to document scores*
> **Context vector**: Assessment tool selection and score interpretation for stroke — the specific documentation skill assessed in OT case study assignments
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Barthel Index: 10 items (feeding, bathing, grooming, dressing upper/lower, bowel control, bladder control, toilet use, transfers, mobility, stairs); 0/5/10/15 scoring; total 0–100; interpretation bands: 0–20 total dependence, 21–60 severe, 61–90 moderate, 91–99 slight, 100 independent; widely used in UK stroke units; admission-discharge comparison provides treatment outcome data
> - FIM (Functional Independence Measure): 18 items (13 motor + 5 cognitive); 7-point scale (7 = complete independence, 6 = modified independence, 5 = supervision/setup, 4 = minimal assist, 3 = moderate assist, 2 = maximal assist, 1 = total assist); motor subtotal 13–91; cognitive subtotal 5–35; total 18–126; used as functional outcome measure pre/post rehabilitation
> - AMPS (Assessment of Motor and Process Skills): 16 motor skills + 20 process skills; quality of skill performance (not task completion); requires certified rater; ADL motor ≥2.0 logits and ADL process ≥1.0 logits = independent functional performance; Rasch-based analysis
> - Rivermead ADL Assessment: 15 items covering self-care and household tasks; ordinal scale; used in subacute and community stroke rehabilitation
> - COPM: 10-point performance and satisfaction scales; client-centred interview; clinically significant change ≥2 points; used to identify occupational performance priorities; appropriate even with mild aphasia using adapted administration
> - MoCA (Montreal Cognitive Assessment): 0–30; ≤25 indicates cognitive impairment; used as cognitive screen before OT assessment; informs validity of self-report assessments
> - DASH (Disabilities of the Arm, Shoulder and Hand): 0–100; 0 = no disability, 100 = most severe; used in stroke when upper limb function is the primary OT focus
> **Predicate to use**: "measures" — quantitative assessment predicate; distinguishes from qualitative observation
> **First word of first paragraph**: "The Barthel Index measures ten ADL items..." — most commonly used OT stroke assessment, leads the section
> **Writer note**: For each assessment, document: what it measures, how many items, score range, how to interpret results in a stroke context, and what it tells the OT about occupational performance. Students most commonly lose marks by stating a score without interpreting what it means functionally for the client.
> **Word count target**: 400–500 words
> **Internal links**: None in this section

##### H3: Barthel Index Interpretation — Applying Score Bands to Stroke OT Case Studies
> **Attribute + Value**: Score 0–20 = total dependence (requires 24-hour care; OT priorities = carer education, equipment, positioning); 21–60 = severe dependence (requires assistance with most ADLs; OT priorities = transfers, basic ADL training, home assessment for discharge); 61–90 = moderate dependence (requires some assistance; OT priorities = specific ADL skill training, fatigue management, home modification); 91–99 = slight dependence (requires minimal assistance for one or two tasks; OT priorities = upper limb rehabilitation, return to complex IADLs); 100 = independence (OT priorities = return to work/driving, community participation)
> **Sequence modeling note**: "A Barthel score of 21–60 indicates..." (score as subject) is more semantically precise than "If the patient scores..." — keep the assessment tool in subject position
> **Writer note**: Students must link the Barthel score to specific OT goals and interventions in the case study — a Barthel of 45 should produce different OT goals than a Barthel of 85. This connection is mandatory in distinction-level assignments.

##### H3: AMPS and FIM — When to Use Which Tool in a Stroke OT Assignment
> **Attribute + Value**: FIM used primarily in inpatient rehabilitation settings for admission-discharge outcome comparison — 7-point scale captures small incremental functional changes; AMPS used when the quality of occupational performance matters (not just independence level) — requires certified rater so cannot be described as self-administered; in assignments, students should identify which setting determines which tool is appropriate, not select tools arbitrarily
> **Writer note**: Highlight that AMPS requires a certified rater — a student on placement is not qualified to administer AMPS independently; assignments must reflect this when incorporating AMPS data.

#### H2: OT Stroke Interventions — Evidence-Based Approaches and Protocols — *Major Query Need: Which interventions to include in a stroke OT case study*
> **Context vector**: Stroke rehabilitation interventions — the evidence-based action component of case study assignments
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - CIMT (Constraint-Induced Movement Therapy): constraint worn 90% of waking hours; 3–6 hours per day of task-specific practice; 10–15 day protocol; Level 1A evidence; appropriate for clients with at least 10° active wrist extension and 10° active finger extension in affected limb; not appropriate for total hemiplegia
> - Task-specific training: repetitive practice of meaningful functional tasks with progressive grading of difficulty; directly linked to top-down occupation-based approach; Level 1A evidence for ADL outcomes post-stroke
> - Bilateral training: simultaneous symmetrical movement of both upper limbs; used when CIMT criteria not met; moderate evidence
> - ADL retraining: top-down approach (practice the whole task in context, compensate rather than remediate underlying deficit) vs bottom-up approach (remediate underlying motor/cognitive deficit first); top-down approach has stronger evidence base for functional ADL outcomes
> - Mirror therapy: mirror placed at body midline; reflection of unaffected limb creates visual illusion of affected limb moving; 15–30 minutes per session; appropriate for hemiplegia when active movement is not yet possible; evidence for upper limb motor recovery
> - Driving rehabilitation: specialised OT domain post-stroke; requires on-road assessment; legal requirement for DVLA notification after stroke; relevant in chronic phase assignments
> - Home modification: environmental assessment and modification to support independence; handrails, grab rails, level access, bedroom/bathroom refit; relevant in subacute/discharge planning phase
> - Fatigue management: energy conservation techniques (activity prioritisation, rest breaks, task modification); pacing (activity monitoring against energy envelope); PERT (Post-stroke Fatigue Management Programme — structured 6-session group or individual format)
> **Predicate to use**: "employs" — OT employs interventions; avoids "uses" which is less contextually specific
> **First word of first paragraph**: "OT stroke interventions employ task-specific and occupation-based approaches..." — immediately positions OT as the actor and task-specific training as the primary evidence context
> **Writer note**: Each intervention description must include: what it is, the evidence level, the protocol parameters where known (CIMT specifics), when it is indicated in the stroke trajectory (which phase), and what occupational performance outcomes it targets. This is the level of specificity that distinguishes a distinction case study from a pass.
> **Word count target**: 400–500 words
> **Internal links**: None in this section

##### H3: CIMT Protocol — What OT Students Must Know for Stroke Assignments
> **Attribute + Value**: Constraint: worn 90% of waking hours (approximately 13–14 hours per day); affected limb practice: 3–6 hours per day of massed practice on task-specific activities; protocol duration: 10–15 days (2–3 weeks); eligibility: minimum 10° active wrist extension and some active finger extension; evidence: multiple Cochrane reviews confirm Level 1A; mechanism: forced use of affected limb combined with massed practice drives cortical reorganisation; assignment implication: students must state eligibility criteria when recommending CIMT — it is not appropriate for all stroke patients
> **Writer note**: The eligibility criteria (10° wrist extension) is the most commonly missed specific value in student CIMT descriptions. Including it demonstrates clinical reasoning depth.

##### H3: Top-Down vs Bottom-Up Approaches in Stroke ADL Retraining
> **Attribute + Value**: Top-down approach: starts with the occupation (dressing, cooking, transfers); practices the whole meaningful task in context; compensates rather than remediates underlying deficits; example: use one-handed dressing technique immediately rather than waiting for bilateral hand function to return; aligns with occupation-based OT philosophy; stronger evidence for functional outcomes; Bottom-up approach: remediates underlying impairment first (motor retraining, sensory re-education, cognitive rehabilitation) before functional task practice; example: hand strengthening and fine motor training before returning to cooking; appropriate when specific remediable deficits are identified through assessment
> **Writer note**: OT case study assignments at Level 6 and Level 7 must explicitly justify the choice of top-down vs bottom-up approach using assessment data (e.g., Barthel score, AMPS results, stroke phase) and evidence. This is a clinical reasoning requirement, not a style choice.

#### H2: Stroke Phases and OT Role — Hyperacute to Chronic — *Major Query Need: OT role across different stroke rehabilitation phases*
> **Context vector**: Temporal organisation of stroke rehabilitation — OT role differs substantially by phase, which must be reflected in case study temporal structure
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**:
> - Hyperacute phase (<24 hours): OT role = positioning to prevent contracture and pressure ulcers; early swallowing screen support (dysphagia); sensory stimulation; family communication; OT assessment is limited due to medical instability
> - Acute phase (24 hours–2 weeks): OT role = bedside ADL assessment using Barthel; early mobilisation; cognitive screen (MoCA); early goal setting with patient and family; carer education; preventing secondary complications (shoulder pain, contracture)
> - Subacute phase (2 weeks–6 months): OT role = intensive rehabilitation; full assessment (AMPS, COPM, DASH if relevant); CIMT if eligible; ADL retraining; cognitive rehabilitation if indicated; fatigue management; pre-discharge home visit assessment; home modification recommendations
> - Chronic phase (>6 months): OT role = community integration; maintenance programmes; driving rehabilitation assessment and DVLA notification; return to work assessment; complex IADL retraining; assistive technology; long-term adjustment support
> **Predicate to use**: "defines" — phase definitions establish the temporal structure; OT role "shifts" across phases (change predicate)
> **First word of first paragraph**: "The hyperacute phase, defined as the first 24 hours post-stroke..." — temporal entity in subject position
> **Writer note**: Students writing stroke case studies must locate their client within a stroke phase and justify OT goals and assessments appropriate to that phase. A subacute phase goal of "return to driving" is not phase-appropriate — this is a clinical reasoning error that loses marks at Level 6.

#### H2: FAQ — Stroke Occupational Therapy Assignment Questions
> **Critical rule**: Extend toward new representative query variants. Do not repeat main content.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:

1. **What Barthel Index score should I use in a stroke OT case study to demonstrate meaningful rehabilitation progress?**
   → Answer direction: Clinically meaningful change on the Barthel Index is generally considered ≥4–5 points in research literature, but this varies by admission score — a patient who starts at 20 (total dependence) improving to 45 (severe dependence) has made substantial progress, whereas 45 to 50 may not represent meaningful change at a functional level. In assignments, students should state the admission and discharge Barthel scores, calculate the change, and interpret it functionally in relation to the patient's occupational goals — "a change of 15 points representing transition from total to severe dependence, with independence now achieved in feeding and grooming."

2. **When is CIMT not appropriate for a stroke patient in an OT case study?**
   → Answer direction: CIMT is contraindicated when the patient has total hemiplegia (no active wrist extension) — the minimum eligibility criterion is 10° active wrist extension and some active finger movement. It is also not appropriate in the hyperacute or early acute phase (first 2 weeks) due to medical instability; in patients with significant cognitive impairment affecting the ability to engage in massed practice; or when the patient declines the treatment due to discomfort or preference (patient values pillar of EBP). Students must justify intervention eligibility in case study assignments.

3. **How do I write about unilateral neglect in a stroke OT assignment?**
   → Answer direction: Define unilateral neglect accurately — failure to attend to stimuli on the contralesional side, not a visual problem; more common after right parietal cortex damage; manifests as leftward ADL difficulties (bumping into objects on the left, incomplete grooming of left side, reading from the right half of a page only). Describe assessment approaches (star cancellation, behavioural inattention test) and OT interventions (scanning training, environmental cueing, mirror feedback). Distinguish neglect from hemianopia — an examiner expects this distinction at Level 6.

4. **What is the difference between aphasia and dysphasia in an OT stroke case study?**
   → Answer direction: Aphasia = impairment of language processing — affects understanding, reading, writing, and speaking; caused by left hemisphere stroke in most right-handed individuals. Dysphasia = impairment specifically of speech production — may understand language but cannot produce words. In OT assignments, the critical point is what this means for assessment validity: COPM requires verbal response; Barthel can be completed by observation; AMPS is performance-based. The student must address how communication impairment affects assessment choice and administration.

5. **Should my stroke OT case study use a top-down or bottom-up approach — which gets better marks?**
   → Answer direction: Neither is inherently better — the mark is gained by justifying the choice using assessment data and evidence. A top-down approach (whole-task practice in context) is better justified when the stroke is in the subacute/chronic phase, when assessment shows moderate functional independence (Barthel 61–90), and when the patient's occupational goals are specific and achievable. A bottom-up approach is better justified when specific remediable impairments are identified (targeted motor or cognitive deficits in the early subacute phase). The best distinction assignments justify the approach choice, acknowledge the alternative, and explain why the evidence and assessment data support the chosen direction.

## Micro Semantics Notes for Writer

- **Predicate choices**:
  - "produces" — stroke produces consequences; establishes causal clinical context
  - "measures" — assessment tools measure performance; quantitative evaluation context
  - "employs" — OT employs interventions; professional action context
  - "defines" — stroke phases are defined by temporal boundaries
  - "indicates" — assessment scores indicate functional level; clinical interpretation context
  - AVOID: "treats," "cures," "fixes" — inappropriate clinical claims; shift macro-context away from OT assignment writing toward medical practice

- **Subject/Object positioning**:
  - "The Barthel Index measures ten ADL items..." — assessment tool as subject
  - "CIMT constrains the unaffected limb..." — intervention as subject (not "OT uses CIMT to constrain...")
  - "A Barthel score of 21–60 indicates severe dependence..." — score interpretation with specific value in subject position

- **First paragraph first words**: Open with a specific clinical value or named entity — "Ischaemic stroke, comprising 85%..." or "The Barthel Index measures..." Not "Stroke is a condition that affects..."

- **Boolean questions**:
  - "Is CIMT appropriate for all stroke patients?" — No; requires eligibility criteria; consolidates the contraindication content
  - "Does Barthel Index score 100 mean the patient no longer needs OT?" — No; chronic phase OT covers driving, return to work, complex IADLs

- **Antonyms for contextual bridges**:
  - "ischaemic vs haemorrhagic" — the fundamental binary that structures all stroke OT content
  - "top-down vs bottom-up" — intervention philosophy opposition that requires clinical reasoning to navigate
  - "acute vs chronic" — temporal contrast that structures phase-based clinical reasoning

- **Word sequences to preserve**:
  - "constraint worn 90% of waking hours" — exact CIMT protocol descriptor; do not paraphrase
  - "clinically significant change ≥2 points" — exact COPM threshold; preserve numeric value
  - "ADL motor ≥2.0 logits and ADL process ≥1.0 logits" — exact AMPS threshold values; must appear verbatim

- **Terms to avoid**:
  - "the affected side" without specifying laterality — assignments at Level 6 require specification of which hemisphere and which side is affected
  - "brain injury" as a generic substitute for stroke — different macro-context
  - "stroke survivors" as the primary term — use "post-stroke patients" or "people with stroke" for academic register
