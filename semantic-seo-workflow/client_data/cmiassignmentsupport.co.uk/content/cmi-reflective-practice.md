# CMI Reflective Practice — Gibbs, Kolb, Rolfe and What CMI Expects

Reflective practice is one of the most commonly misunderstood requirements in CMI assignments. Students at every level — from Level 3 to Level 7 — submit reflective accounts and reflective sections that are detailed, thoughtfully written, and entirely descriptive. They explain what happened, in order, with professional precision. They do not reflect.

This guide explains what CMI assessors mean by reflective practice, which models are most useful for CMI assignments, how the depth of reflection changes across levels, and what separates a submitted description from a genuine reflective account that achieves Merit or Distinction.

---

## What Is CMI Reflective Practice?

Reflective practice is the deliberate, structured process of examining your own professional experience, decisions, and behaviours in order to generate learning and improve future practice. The operative word is *deliberate* — reflective practice is not the same as thinking about your day. It is a structured analytical process applied to professional experience.

In CMI assignments, reflective practice appears in several forms:

- **Reflective accounts** — standalone documents of 1,500–3,000 words focused entirely on a specific professional experience, decision, or development period
- **Reflective sections within unit assignments** — typically the final section of a report or paper, connecting the academic analysis to personal leadership development
- **Personal Development Plans (PDPs)** — structured planning documents that combine self-assessment, identification of development needs, and action planning
- **CPD reflective logs** — ongoing records of professional learning connected to specific experiences

Across all of these formats, CMI assessors are looking for the same thing: evidence that you have moved beyond describing your experience to genuinely examining it — interrogating why things happened the way they did, what that means for your understanding of management and leadership, and how your practice will change as a result.

## The Four Main Reflective Models for CMI

### Gibbs Reflective Cycle (1988)

Graham Gibbs developed his reflective cycle as a response to Kolb's experiential learning cycle — a more structured, stepwise model designed to guide practitioners through systematic reflection. It remains the most widely used reflective framework in CMI assignments.

The six stages are:

1. **Description** — What happened? Describe the experience, situation, or decision without interpretation. Keep this section brief — it is the foundation, not the focus.
2. **Feelings** — What were you thinking and feeling at the time? What were others' reactions? This stage is often skipped by management students who find emotional content uncomfortable to include in a professional document. CMI expects it — feelings affect decisions and performance.
3. **Evaluation** — What was good about the experience? What was difficult? This is an honest assessment of what worked and what did not — not a performance review, but a genuine evaluation of the event's value and limitations.
4. **Analysis** — What can you make of the situation? This is where management theory enters. How does what you experienced connect to leadership frameworks, motivation theory, team dynamics, or performance management? What does the theory say about why things happened the way they did?
5. **Conclusion** — What else could you have done? If you were in the same situation again, what would you do differently? This requires genuine consideration of alternatives — not a token "I could have communicated better" statement.
6. **Action Plan** — What are you going to do differently as a result of this experience? Specific, concrete, time-bound commitments to changed practice.

The strength of Gibbs is its structure — it prevents students from staying in the Description stage. The Analysis stage specifically requires connection to management theory, making it a natural fit for CMI assessment criteria that require Evaluate or Critically Analyse depth.

### Kolb Experiential Learning Cycle (1984)

David Kolb's model frames learning as a cycle of four stages:

<!-- Alt text: "Kolb Experiential Learning Cycle — four stages: Concrete Experience, Reflective Observation, Abstract Conceptualisation, Active Experimentation" -->

1. **Concrete Experience** — the experience itself: doing, encountering, feeling
2. **Reflective Observation** — stepping back from the experience to review and reflect on what happened and what you observed
3. **Abstract Conceptualisation** — drawing conclusions and connecting the experience to theory or existing knowledge — making meaning
4. **Active Experimentation** — planning what to do differently based on what you have learned, and testing it in practice

Kolb's model is less prescriptive than Gibbs — it does not give you six specific questions but rather a framework for understanding how learning happens through experience. It is most useful when the focus of your reflective account is on how a specific experience changed your practice, rather than on analysing a single complex event.

Kolb also identifies four learning styles (Diverger, Assimilator, Converger, Accommodator) based on preferences for different stages of the cycle. At Level 5 and Level 7, Critically Evaluating Kolb includes engaging with the critique that learning styles theory lacks robust empirical support (Coffield et al., 2004) — the cycle itself is more empirically grounded than the associated styles framework.

### Rolfe, Freshwater and Jasper — "What? So What? Now What?" (2001)

Rolfe and colleagues' framework simplifies reflection to three questions:

- **What?** — Describe the situation: what happened, what did you do, what was the outcome?
- **So What?** — What does it mean? What have you learned? How does it connect to theory and professional knowledge?
- **Now What?** — What will you do differently? What actions will you take?

This framework is appropriate for shorter reflective sections within larger assignments, or for reflective logs where each entry needs a concise structure. It avoids the more elaborate emotional and evaluative stages of Gibbs, making it faster to apply but less analytically rich. At Level 3 and Level 4, it is a perfectly appropriate framework. At Level 7, the three-stage structure may need supplementing — the "So What?" stage requires significantly more depth than a single section can contain.

### Johns Model of Structured Reflection (1994)

Christopher Johns developed his model specifically for healthcare practice, but its structured questioning approach is applicable across management contexts. It uses a set of guided questions organised around five aspects:

1. **Description** — Describe the experience
2. **Reflection** — What was I trying to achieve? Why did I act as I did?
3. **Influencing factors** — What factors influenced my decision-making?
4. **Could I have dealt with it better?** — What other options were available?
5. **Learning** — How do I now feel about this? How has this changed my way of knowing?

Johns is particularly useful when the reflective account involves an ethically or interpersonally complex situation — conflict, difficult performance conversations, patient or stakeholder distress. The explicit attention to influencing factors (cognitive, emotional, contextual) makes it well-suited to NHS managers and other professionals working in high-stakes relational contexts.

## The Core Mistake: Description vs Reflection

The most common reason CMI reflective accounts fail to achieve Merit or Distinction is not that they are poorly written — it is that they are not reflective. They are descriptive.

**Description** answers: what happened?

*"In the meeting, I presented the quarterly performance data. Three team members questioned the methodology. I explained the approach, but the discussion became tense. The meeting ended without resolution."*

**Reflection** answers: what does it mean, why did it happen, what did I learn, and what will I do differently?

*"On reflection, the tension in the meeting arose partly from the team's lack of involvement in the data collection process — consistent with Edmondson's (1999) finding that psychological safety depends on inclusion in decision-making. My decision to present final data rather than involve the team in its interpretation created the conditions for challenge rather than engagement. Applying the transformational leadership principle of involving staff in goal-setting (Bass and Avolio, 1994), I would change my approach for the following quarter: involving two team representatives in the data collection design phase before any presentation."*

The reflective version is not longer because it contains more events. It is more substantive because it asks different questions — it moves from what to why, and from past to future.

## How Reflective Depth Changes by CMI Level

<!-- Alt text: "CMI reflective practice depth by level — Level 3 describes and identifies, Level 5 evaluates and connects to theory, Level 7 critically analyses systemic factors and questions assumptions" -->

Reflective practice is required across all CMI levels, but the expected depth increases substantially as the level rises.

**Level 3 — Describe and Identify**

At Level 3, reflective accounts should accurately describe the experience and identify what you learned. The primary command verbs at this level are *Describe* and *Identify*. You do not need to Critically Evaluate frameworks or challenge theoretical assumptions. A clear, honest account of a professional situation — what happened, what you did, what you noticed, what you would do differently — is appropriate. The Rolfe "What? So What? Now What?" framework is sufficient.

**Level 5 — Evaluate and Connect to Theory**

At Level 5, the primary command verb is *Evaluate* — and this applies to reflective writing as much as to analytical sections. You are expected to evaluate your own practice against management frameworks, not simply describe events. This means: identifying which theories or models explain what you experienced, weighing the evidence for what worked and what did not, and reaching a justified conclusion about how your practice should change. Gibbs' Reflective Cycle maps onto this expectation well — the Analysis stage requires theoretical connection, the Conclusion and Action Plan stages require justified evaluation.

A Level 5 reflective account should explicitly reference at least two to three management frameworks relevant to the experience being reflected upon. References should be to published sources, not to personal opinion alone.

**Level 7 — Critically Analyse and Question Assumptions**

At Level 7, reflective practice moves from examining individual decisions to examining the systemic, structural, and cultural factors that shape leadership behaviour. The expectation is that you do not just reflect on what you did — you interrogate why you did it, what organisational, cultural, and power-related factors constrained your options, and what that reveals about your own leadership assumptions.

This is transformational reflection — connecting to authentic leadership theory (George, 2003), interrogating the ways in which organisational culture shapes leadership decisions, and questioning the assumptions that underlie your professional identity. A Level 7 reflective account should engage with whether your leadership behaviour reflects genuinely held values or organisational conformity pressures.

## Structuring a CMI Reflective Account

A reflective account is not a chronological narrative. It is a structured analysis of a professional experience using a reflective framework. A practical structure for a 2,000-word reflective account using Gibbs:

| Section | Content | Approximate word count |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | What experience you are reflecting on and why — brief context | 150–200 words |
| Description (Gibbs Stage 1) | What happened — factual, concise | 200–250 words |
| Feelings (Gibbs Stage 2) | Your responses and the responses of others | 150–200 words |
| Evaluation (Gibbs Stage 3) | What worked, what was difficult — honest assessment | 200–250 words |
| Analysis (Gibbs Stage 4) | Theoretical frameworks — what management theory explains this experience | 400–500 words |
| Conclusion (Gibbs Stage 5) | What you would do differently | 200–250 words |
| Action Plan (Gibbs Stage 6) | Specific, concrete, time-bound development commitments | 200–250 words |

The Analysis stage should contain the majority of your academic referencing. This is where Maslow, Tuckman, Kotter, Lewin, Edmondson, or whichever frameworks are relevant to your specific experience enter the account. The framework does not need to fit perfectly — critically engaging with where it fits and where it does not is itself reflective.

## Reflective Practice and Professional Development

A well-written reflective account does more than satisfy an assessment criterion. The practice of structured reflection — applied consistently across a CMI programme — develops the self-awareness, learning orientation, and adaptive capacity that Senge (1990) identifies as core to the learning organisation. Strategic leaders who cannot reflect on their own practice cannot learn from failure, cannot recognise the assumptions driving their decisions, and cannot model the psychological safety that Edmondson (2018) identifies as essential to organisational learning.

CMI's emphasis on reflective practice is not an add-on to the technical content of management theory. It is the mechanism by which management theory becomes leadership behaviour.

## FAQ

**What is reflective practice in CMI?**

Reflective practice is the structured, deliberate examination of your own professional experience to generate learning and improve future practice. In CMI assignments it appears in reflective accounts (standalone documents), reflective sections within unit assignments, Personal Development Plans, and CPD logs. At all levels, CMI assessors expect genuine reflection — not description of events.

**Which reflective model should I use for CMI?**

Gibbs Reflective Cycle (1988) is the most commonly used for CMI assignments and maps cleanly onto CMI assessment formats — particularly because its Analysis stage requires connecting experience to management theory. Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle (1984) is appropriate when the focus is how learning leads to changed practice. Rolfe et al's "What? So What? Now What?" (2001) is suitable for shorter reflective sections. Johns' Model (1994) is useful for complex interpersonal or ethical situations.

**What is the difference between description and reflection?**

Description explains what happened. Reflection examines why it happened, what it means, what you learned, and how your practice will change. Description answers the question "what?" Reflection answers "what does this mean?" and "what will I do differently?" CMI assessors are looking for reflection. Submitting a description of events — however detailed — does not meet the assessment criteria.

**How long is a CMI reflective account?**

Typically 1,500–3,000 words depending on unit and level. Level 3 reflective accounts are at the shorter end. Reflective sections within larger Level 5 or Level 7 assignments may be 500–800 words but require greater analytical depth — multiple framework references and explicit connection to assessment criteria.

**How does reflective practice change at Level 7?**

At Level 7, reflective practice moves from examining individual decisions and team experiences (Level 3–5) to examining systemic and structural factors — how organisational culture, power dynamics, and governance shape leadership behaviour. Level 7 reflection connects to authentic and transformational leadership theory, interrogates the assumptions underlying your own leadership identity, and examines whether your behaviour reflects genuinely held values or organisational conformity pressures.
