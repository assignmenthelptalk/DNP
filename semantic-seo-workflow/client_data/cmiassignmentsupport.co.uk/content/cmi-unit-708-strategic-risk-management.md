## What Is CMI Unit 708?

CMI Unit 708 — Strategic Risk Management — is a Level 7 Diploma unit assessing how strategic leaders identify, evaluate, govern, and culturally embed risk management at organisational scale. The typical assessment criteria are:

- **AC1**: Critically analyse frameworks and approaches to identifying and assessing strategic risk
- **AC2**: Critically analyse approaches to managing and mitigating strategic risk at board level
- **AC3**: Evaluate the strategic leader's role in developing a risk-aware organisational culture

All three criteria operate at Level 7 command verb depth. AC1 and AC2 require **Critically Analyse** — which means identifying the assumptions, limitations, and contextual conditions of each framework, and evaluating it against empirical evidence and alternative approaches. AC3 requires **Evaluate** — which means weighing the evidence on what effective risk-aware leadership looks like and reaching a justified conclusion.

The output is a strategic paper of 5,000–6,000 words, supported by 15–20 peer-reviewed sources. The primary frameworks are ISO 31000, the COSO Enterprise Risk Management Framework, risk appetite and tolerance, scenario planning, and Black Swan theory. Understanding the tension between predictive risk management and organisational resilience is essential for achieving Merit or Distinction.

## Format and Academic Standard

CMI 708 is submitted as a strategic paper. There is no executive summary requirement (unlike Level 5 management reports), but the document requires a logical, argumentative structure: a clear position developed across sections, not a framework-by-framework description.

At Level 7, your assessor expects:
- Peer-reviewed sources — journals such as the *Journal of Risk Research*, *Risk Management*, *Strategic Management Journal*, and relevant governance publications
- Frameworks Critically Analysed, not described — ISO 31000 does not get two paragraphs of explanation; it gets interrogated
- A consistent analytical thread — your argument about how strategic risk should be managed at board level should be coherent across AC1, AC2, and AC3
- Harvard referencing to a minimum of 15 peer-reviewed sources

The distinction between a Pass and a Distinction in CMI 708 lies in whether you have engaged with the limits and tensions of risk management — particularly whether you have addressed what happens when risk cannot be predicted.

## Key Frameworks for CMI 708

### ISO 31000 (2018) — International Risk Management Standard

ISO 31000 is the international standard for risk management, providing principles, a framework, and a process. It defines risk as "the effect of uncertainty on objectives" — a deliberately broad definition that covers upside (opportunity) and downside (threat) risk. The standard is structured around:

- **Principles**: value creation, integration into governance, systematic and structured approach, use of best available information, human and cultural factors, continual improvement
- **Framework**: the organisational arrangements for managing risk — leadership commitment, integration, design, implementation, evaluation, improvement
- **Process**: the operational cycle of communication, scope and context, risk assessment (identification, analysis, evaluation), risk treatment, monitoring and review

**Critical Analysis at Level 7** does not stop at describing these components. It interrogates the standard's limitations:

ISO 31000 is **principles-based, not prescriptive**. It does not specify quantitative methods, does not define risk appetite thresholds, and does not dictate governance structures. This makes it highly adaptable but also highly variable in implementation — two organisations can both claim ISO 31000 alignment with radically different risk management practices. The standard provides little guidance on how to manage risks that are fundamentally uncertain rather than merely probabilistic.

The 2018 revision strengthened the emphasis on integration with strategy and leadership commitment, but critics note that the standard still treats risk management as a distinct process rather than an embedded feature of strategic decision-making (Power, 2007). The compliance-orientation risk — where ISO 31000 becomes a box-ticking certification exercise rather than a genuine risk capability — is well-documented.

### COSO Enterprise Risk Management Framework (2017)

The COSO ERM Framework, updated in 2017, integrates enterprise risk management with strategy and performance. It represents a significant evolution from the original 2004 COSO ERM framework, which was criticised for being overly process-focused and disconnected from strategic decision-making.

The 2017 framework is structured around five components:

1. **Governance and Culture** — board risk oversight, operating model, commitment to core values, risk culture
2. **Strategy and Objective-Setting** — business context, risk appetite, alternative strategies, business objectives
3. **Performance** — risk identification, risk assessment, risk prioritisation, risk response
4. **Review and Revision** — substantive change assessment, ERM review and revision, pursuing improvement
5. **Information, Communication and Reporting** — risk information, risk reporting, technology

**Critically Analysing COSO ERM** at Level 7 requires engaging with its central contribution and its limitations. COSO 2017's integration of risk appetite with strategy-setting is its most significant advance: risk appetite is not a stand-alone policy statement but a boundary condition on strategic options. The organisation should only pursue strategies within its declared risk appetite. This is theoretically sound, but empirically, risk appetite frameworks are frequently decoupled from strategic planning processes (Mikes and Kaplan, 2013).

The COSO framework also assumes that enterprise risks can be identified, categorised, and managed through a structured process. This assumption is challenged by complexity theory and by Black Swan events (Taleb, 2007) — risks that are genuinely outside the range of historical experience cannot be captured by any enterprise risk register, however comprehensive.

### Risk Appetite and Risk Tolerance

Risk appetite and risk tolerance are related but distinct concepts that the COSO and ISO frameworks handle differently:

- **Risk appetite**: the board-level direction on the amount and type of risk the organisation is willing to accept in pursuing its objectives. Expressed at strategic level — for example, "we maintain a low appetite for compliance and reputational risk and a higher appetite for strategic investment risk"
- **Risk tolerance**: the acceptable deviation from risk appetite in specific business contexts — the operational boundary within which management can act without board escalation

**Critically Analysing risk appetite at Level 7** requires examining how it is set, by whom, and whether it genuinely constrains strategic decisions or exists as a governance document with little operational effect. Mikes and Kaplan (2013) identify two dominant approaches: "quantitative stalwarts" who use numerical models and statistical limits, and "qualitative pragmatists" who use narrative descriptions and management judgement. Neither approach is inherently superior — the appropriate method depends on the organisation's risk profile and the nature of the risks it faces.

The operationalisation of risk appetite is where most organisations struggle. A board-level appetite statement must translate into business-unit-level risk tolerances, performance metrics, and escalation thresholds. Where this translation is absent or inconsistent, risk appetite becomes aspirational rather than constraining.

### Quantitative Risk Assessment — Monte Carlo and Probability-Impact Models

Quantitative risk assessment methods — Monte Carlo simulation, probability-impact matrices, Value-at-Risk (VaR) models — attempt to express risk in numerical terms, enabling comparison and prioritisation. The probability-impact matrix is the most widely used tool: risks are scored on likelihood and impact, plotted on a heat map, and prioritised accordingly.

**Critical Analysis of quantitative approaches** must engage with their fundamental limitations. Power (2007) argues that the quantification of risk creates an illusion of control — the precision of a probability estimate does not reflect the underlying uncertainty, particularly for low-frequency, high-impact events. Monte Carlo simulation requires probability distributions derived from historical data; it produces output distributions that are only as reliable as the input assumptions.

The 2008 global financial crisis demonstrated the failure of quantitative risk models at scale. Value-at-Risk models used by major financial institutions systematically underestimated tail risk because they were calibrated on historical periods of relative stability. The models were internally consistent but epistemically flawed — they could not account for events that had no precedent in their training data.

### Black Swan Theory — Taleb (2007, 2012)

Nassim Nicholas Taleb's concept of the Black Swan — a high-impact event that is outside the realm of regular expectations because nothing in the past can convincingly point to its possibility — represents a fundamental challenge to predictive risk management.

Taleb's critique is epistemological: humans systematically underestimate the probability of rare events, overfit explanations to past data, and construct models that are precise but fragile. Risk management systems designed around historical data and probability estimation are not just imperfect — they can actively increase fragility by encouraging over-optimisation for known risks at the expense of resilience to unknown ones.

**At Level 7, Critically Analysing Black Swan theory** requires examining both its contribution and its strategic implications:

- **Contribution**: exposes the epistemic limits of probabilistic risk management; argues for strategies that benefit from volatility (antifragility) rather than merely surviving it
- **Strategic implication**: organisations cannot manage Black Swan risk by better prediction; they can only manage it by building adaptive capacity, maintaining slack, diversifying, and avoiding over-optimisation
- **Limitation**: the Black Swan framework is more a critique than a prescriptive management tool; it tells you what will fail but not what to do instead

The strategic implication for board-level risk management is a reorientation from predicting and preventing failure to building the capacity to absorb and adapt to unexpected events — which connects directly to organisational resilience.

### Scenario Planning

Scenario planning — associated with Shell's strategic planning methodology and formalised by Schoemaker (1995) — addresses uncertainty that cannot be reduced to probability estimates. Instead of asking "how likely is X?", scenario planning asks "what are the plausible alternative futures the organisation might face, and are our strategies robust across those futures?"

The process involves identifying key uncertainties (not risks), constructing four to six internally consistent scenarios, and testing current strategies against each. Scenarios are not forecasts — they are tools for developing strategic flexibility and identifying the pre-conditions that would make each scenario more likely.

**Critically Analysing scenario planning** at Level 7 requires engaging with its use case: scenario planning is most valuable when the future is genuinely uncertain (not merely risky), when the planning horizon is long, and when the organisation has strategic flexibility to respond to different futures. It is less useful in stable, predictable environments or where the organisation has no ability to change its strategy in response.

### Risk Governance — Three Lines of Defence and Board Accountability

The Three Lines of Defence model (IIA, 2020) is the dominant framework for structuring risk governance accountability:

- **First Line**: operational management — owns risks, implements controls, manages day-to-day risk within risk appetite
- **Second Line**: risk management and compliance functions — monitors and oversees first-line risk management, provides risk frameworks and tools, reports to the board
- **Third Line**: internal audit — provides independent assurance to the board and audit committee that the first and second lines are operating effectively

The FRC UK Corporate Governance Code (2024) requires boards of UK listed companies to maintain robust risk management and internal control frameworks, and to report on principal risks and how they are managed. The Audit Committee has specific responsibility for overseeing the internal audit function and the integrity of risk reporting.

**Critically Analysing the Three Lines model** requires engaging with its limitations. Power (2007) argues that risk management functions (second line) can become compliance-oriented — focused on risk documentation and reporting rather than genuine risk management capability. The separation of risk ownership from risk oversight can create accountability gaps where neither line takes full responsibility. The 2020 IIA update acknowledged these limitations, emphasising the primacy of the board's role in directing and overseeing risk management rather than delegating it to a compliance function.

### Organisational Resilience — Beyond Prediction

Hollnagel's Safety-II perspective (2014) — developed initially in safety-critical industries but increasingly applied to organisational management — argues that traditional risk management focuses on preventing failures (Safety-I: "if it goes wrong, fix it"). Safety-II focuses on understanding why things go right, and building the adaptive capacity that enables organisations to succeed under varying and unexpected conditions.

**Organisational resilience** is the capacity to absorb disruption, adapt to change, and transform in response to circumstances that could not have been predicted by conventional risk models. Weick and Sutcliffe's (2007) High Reliability Organisation research identifies five characteristics: preoccupation with failure, reluctance to simplify interpretations, sensitivity to operations, commitment to resilience, and deference to expertise.

The distinction between risk management and resilience is strategically significant. Risk management asks: what could go wrong, and how do we prevent it? Resilience asks: given that unexpected things will go wrong, how do we build the capacity to absorb, adapt, and recover? At Level 7, Critically Analysing whether your organisation's risk approach balances prediction and resilience — rather than treating them as equivalent — is a marker of Distinction-level thinking.

## What Critically Analyse Requires Across AC1–AC3

**AC1 — Critically analyse frameworks for identifying and assessing strategic risk**

Do not describe what ISO 31000 says. Analyse what it assumes, what it cannot handle, and what evidence exists for its effectiveness. Compare its principles-based approach with the prescriptive detail of COSO ERM. Engage with Power's (2007) critique of the risk management industry. Explain why quantitative methods are epistemically limited for low-frequency, high-impact events. Reference Taleb's challenge to the entire probabilistic paradigm.

**AC2 — Critically analyse approaches to managing and mitigating strategic risk at board level**

Do not describe what a Risk Committee does. Analyse how risk appetite is operationalised from board-level statement to business-unit tolerance. Examine the Three Lines of Defence model — where does accountability genuinely reside, and where does it fall between functions? Critically engage with whether board-level risk governance produces genuine risk management capability or compliance documentation.

**AC3 — Evaluate the strategic leader's role in developing a risk-aware culture**

Do not describe training programmes. Evaluate the evidence on what risk culture is, how it is shaped, and what leaders actually do to create it. Draw on Edmondson's (2018) work on psychological safety — risk culture requires that people feel safe surfacing bad news and challenging risk assessments without personal consequence. Evaluate whether governance structures (incentive design, escalation procedures, board information quality) support or undermine this. Reach a justified conclusion about the strategic leader's most significant contribution to risk culture.

## Common Mistakes in CMI 708

**Describing frameworks as management solutions.** ISO 31000 and COSO ERM are frameworks — structured ways of thinking about risk, not guaranteed approaches to managing it. Critically Analysing them means examining their underlying assumptions and the conditions under which they succeed or fail.

**Treating risk appetite as a statement rather than a constraint.** Risk appetite only functions if it constrains strategic decisions. Describing how risk appetite is documented is not the same as Critically Analysing whether and how it operates as a genuine boundary on strategy.

**Ignoring Black Swan and complexity.** Many CMI 708 assignments discuss ISO 31000 and COSO ERM without engaging with the fundamental epistemological challenge posed by Taleb. At Level 7, the assessor expects you to know that the dominant risk frameworks have systematic blind spots for genuinely unprecedented events.

**Reducing risk culture to training.** AC3 is about what strategic leaders do to shape culture — not the training calendar. Leadership behaviour, psychological safety, governance design, and incentive structures are the mechanisms through which risk culture is built or eroded.

**Conflating risk management and resilience.** Resilience and risk management are related but distinct. An organisation can have a comprehensive risk management framework and still be fragile — if it has over-optimised for predicted risks and built no adaptive capacity for unexpected ones.

## Merit vs Distinction in CMI 708

A **Merit** submission in CMI 708 Critically Analyses the major frameworks (ISO 31000, COSO ERM, Three Lines of Defence) with genuine engagement with their limitations, applies the appropriate command verb across AC1–AC3, references 15+ peer-reviewed sources, and addresses risk culture beyond a description of training programmes.

A **Distinction** submission does all of the above and engages with the fundamental tension in the field: risk management assumes that risk can be identified, assessed, and managed; resilience theory assumes that significant threats are often genuinely unprecedented and that adaptive capacity matters more than prediction. The Distinction-level argument positions board risk governance within this tension — acknowledging what risk management frameworks can and cannot achieve, and identifying the conditions under which each approach is more or less appropriate.
