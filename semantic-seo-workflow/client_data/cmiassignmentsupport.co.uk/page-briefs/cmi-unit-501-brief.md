**Key theories covered in CMI 501:**

| Theory/Model | What it covers | How it's used in 501 |
|---|---|---|
| Transformational leadership (Bass and Avolio) | Leaders who inspire and motivate beyond transactional exchange | Evaluated for applicability in motivating teams — criteria: adaptability, impact on performance |
| Transactional leadership (Burns, Bass) | Management through reward and punishment, exchange relationships | Evaluated as effective in compliance-heavy or stable environments; contrasted with transformational |
| Situational leadership (Hersey and Blanchard) | Adjusting leadership style to the development level of the follower | Evaluated for practical applicability; widely cited in CMI 501 for its direct management application |
| Contingency theory (Fiedler) | Effectiveness of a leader depends on the situational match | Evaluated for limitations — assumes style is fixed, which modern management challenges |
| Management vs Leadership distinction (Kotter, Mintzberg) | Management = controlling and planning; Leadership = inspiring and visioning | Analysed in AC1 — this is a required Learning Outcome distinction |
| Emotional Intelligence in leadership (Goleman) | Self-awareness, empathy, social skills as leadership competencies | Evaluated as complementary to style theories — EI explains HOW a style is applied |
| Blake Mouton Managerial Grid | Two-axis model: concern for people vs concern for production | Evaluated as a behavioural framework; useful for AC2's range requirement |
| Trait theory | Leaders are born with innate qualities | Evaluated critically — limitations are well-evidenced (situational dependency undermines trait universality) |

**Most common failure points — differentiate this page:**
- Describing transformational leadership instead of Evaluating it (most common referral cause)
- Omitting criteria before Evaluating — "it is effective because..." without naming what "effective" means in the management context

- Missing the management vs leadership distinction (often a discrete Assessment Criterion)

##### H3: Transformational and Transactional Leadership — Bass and Avolio
> Transformational: leaders inspire beyond self-interest, motivate through vision and values, associated with Bass and Avolio's Full Range Leadership Model. Evaluated against criteria: applicability in team performance improvement, suitability for change contexts.
> Transactional: management through exchange — reward for performance, correction for failure. More effective in stable, compliance-heavy environments.
> How to Evaluate both: establish criteria (e.g. effectiveness in NHS management context for Band 7 manager) → apply each against criteria → conclude which better serves the context → distinguish from personal preference

##### H3: Situational Leadership — Hersey and Blanchard
> Adjusts leadership style (directive vs supportive) to the development level of the follower (D1–D4). Four styles: Directing, Coaching, Supporting, Delegating.
> Why assessors value this in CMI 501: directly practical for managers who supervise individuals at different skill levels. Easy to apply with Evaluate depth — criteria = follower readiness, task complexity.
> Limitation (needed for Distinction): model has been criticised for oversimplifying follower development into four stages; does not account for task-specific readiness variation.

##### H3: Contingency Theory — Fiedler
> Leader effectiveness is contingent on the match between leadership style (task- or relationship-oriented) and situational favourability. Measured via Least Preferred Co-worker (LPC) scale.
> How to Evaluate: criteria = adaptability, situational applicability. Limitation: assumes style is fixed — contradicted by Hersey and Blanchard and by Goleman's EI framework. Valuable for AC2's range requirement — shows breadth of theoretical engagement.

##### H3: Management vs Leadership — Kotter and Mintzberg
> Kotter: management = coping with complexity (planning, organising, controlling); leadership = coping with change (setting direction, aligning people, motivating). Two different but complementary functions.
> Mintzberg: management roles (interpersonal, informational, decisional) — challenges the romanticised view of leadership as separate from management.
> How this appears in CMI 501: AC1 requires an analysis of this relationship — not a definition of each. The analysis must show how they interact in an organisational context.

##### H3: Emotional Intelligence — Goleman
> Five domains: self-awareness, self-regulation, motivation, empathy, social skill. Goleman's EI framework explains the interpersonal dimension of leadership — HOW a leader applies a style, not WHAT style they choose.
> Evaluative use: EI is not a standalone leadership theory for CMI 501, but a complementary lens applied to explain why transformational or situational approaches succeed or fail in a given manager's hands. Useful for connecting theory to organisational context in AC3.

##### H3: What Does Not Count as Evaluate in CMI 501
> Common student error: writing "Transformational leadership has strengths such as X and weaknesses such as Y." This is Describe with labels — not Evaluate. Evaluate requires:
> 1. Establish criteria (what are you measuring against?)
> 2. Apply each theory against those criteria with evidence
> 3. Draw a reasoned conclusion — which approach is more effective in this context, and why
> Assessors at Level 5 are looking for this structure explicitly. A response that describes four theories without applying criteria will not pass.
