**Key theories:**
- Maslow's Hierarchy of Needs (1943) — five-level motivational framework; physiological → safety → belonging → esteem → self-actualisation
- Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory (1959) — hygiene factors (prevent dissatisfaction) vs motivators (drive satisfaction and performance); the distinction between what demotivates and what motivates
- Vroom's Expectancy Theory (1964) — motivation as a product of expectancy (effort → performance), instrumentality (performance → reward), and valence (value of the reward)
- Locke and Latham's Goal-Setting Theory — SMART goals, goal specificity and difficulty as drivers of performance
- Tannenbaum and Schmidt Leadership Continuum — delegation across a spectrum from manager-centred to subordinate-centred decision-making
- McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y — assumptions about human motivation that shape management style

**Common failure points:**
1. Treating Maslow and Herzberg as interchangeable — they answer different questions; conflating them produces an incoherent analysis
2. Describing motivation theories historically rather than evaluating their applicability to a management context
3. Treating delegation as a single action rather than evaluating frameworks and conditions that make delegation effective
4. No criteria before evaluating — conclusions presented as assertions rather than evaluated judgements
5. Performance standards addressed vaguely — AC1 requires evaluation of how standards are set, communicated, and maintained, not a general statement about their importance
