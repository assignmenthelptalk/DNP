##### H3: Title Page
> **Attribute + Value**: Unit code and title, student name and registration number, training provider name, submission date, word count — typically on a separate page; does not count toward word count
> **Common error**: Students omit the executive summary as a distinct section and begin the report with an introduction. At Level 5+, the executive summary is a marked section.

##### H3: Executive Summary — 250–300 Words, Written Last
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Purpose: provide a standalone summary of the entire report — findings, conclusions, and recommendations — for a reader who may not read the full report
> - Length: 250–300 words (some training providers allow up to 350 words)
> - Written last: the executive summary is written after the full report is complete, even though it appears first
> - Content: key findings from the analysis, the overall conclusion, and the top 2–3 recommendations in brief
> - Does NOT: introduce background context, repeat the introduction, or include detailed argument — it summarises outcomes only
> **Common error**: Students write the executive summary first (as an extended introduction) and then repeat the same material in the introduction section. Both sections are then thin. Write the body first, conclusion and recommendations second, executive summary last.
> **Writer note**: Many training providers state explicitly that the executive summary is assessed as a separate criterion. A missing or weak executive summary costs marks on a specific AC line, not just on overall quality.

##### H3: Table of Contents
> **Attribute + Value**: Numbered heading list with page numbers, generated after the document is finalised — particularly important for reports over 3,000 words; some training providers require it for all submitted reports
> **Writer note**: Brief section. Note that the table of contents is updated last to reflect final page numbers after formatting.

##### H3: Introduction — 200–250 Words
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Purpose: establish the context of the report — what organisation it relates to, what the management challenge or topic is, what the report will cover, and how it is structured
> - Does NOT: summarise findings (that is the executive summary's role); does NOT include analysis or recommendations
> - Typical structure: one sentence establishing the organisational context, one sentence stating the report's purpose and scope, one sentence outlining the structure
> **Common error**: Students write a very long introduction that begins to analyse the topic — this dilutes both the introduction and the first analysis section by creating a contextual overlap.

##### H3: Main Body — Analysis Sections — 2,000–2,500 Words (for a 3,500–4,000-word report)
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Divided into headed sub-sections (H2 and H3 headings within the report document) — each sub-section addresses one aspect of the Learning Outcome
> - Each section applies the command verb to a specific component: if Evaluating leadership styles, one section per style evaluated against the named criteria
> - Theory must be applied, not described: the named theory or model is the analytical framework, not the topic itself
> - Evidence: at least 2–3 academic or professional sources cited per major analysis section (Harvard in-text citations throughout)
> - Word count proportion: 55–65% of the total word count
> **Common error**: Students write descriptive sections (what a theory says) rather than analytical sections (what this theory reveals about the management situation in question). Applying theory to an organisational context is the Level 5+ standard.

##### H3: Conclusion — 200–300 Words
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Purpose: draw together the key findings from the analysis — no new information, no new citations
> - Structure: synthesis of what the analysis revealed about the management topic, leading to the overall judgement that the recommendations are based on
> - Does NOT: introduce new arguments, repeat section summaries verbatim, list the recommendations (those go in the recommendations section)
> **Common error**: Students either make the conclusion too short (2–3 sentences) or too long (turning it into a second analysis section). The conclusion should be 200–300 words and function as a bridge from analysis to recommendations.

##### H3: Recommendations — 400–500 Words, Minimum 3 SMART Recommendations
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Purpose: propose specific, actionable steps the organisation should take based on the conclusions reached
> - Format: numbered list of recommendations, each structured as a SMART statement (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) with a brief justification (1–2 sentences connecting the recommendation to the analytical findings)
> - Minimum: 3 recommendations (most marking criteria assess whether recommendations are made, how specific they are, and whether they are justified)
> - Must NOT: repeat analysis or introduce new evidence — recommendations are forward-looking outputs of the analysis already presented
> **Common error**: Students write vague recommendations ("the organisation should improve communication") rather than specific ones ("implement a structured weekly team briefing with a standard agenda template, piloted in Q2 2025 across the operations division"). Vague recommendations score at pass level; specific, justified recommendations score at merit and distinction.
> **Writer note**: SMART format applied: "This report recommends that [specific action] is implemented by [date / timeframe], with [responsible party] accountable for delivery, measured by [KPI or success criterion]."

##### H3: Bibliography — Harvard Format
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - All sources cited in-text must appear in the bibliography; no uncited sources should appear
> - Format: alphabetical by author surname, hanging indent format (second line indented)
> - Minimum sources: Level 5 — 10–12 sources; Level 6 — 12–15 sources; Level 7 — 15–20 sources
> - Source quality: academic journals, management textbooks, CMI publications, ManagementDirect, reputable professional bodies (CIPD, ACAS, GOV.UK) — NOT Wikipedia, business news articles as sole sources
> **Writer note**: The bibliography is not counted in the word count. Students sometimes underprioritise it — but missing or incorrect Harvard referencing costs marks on a specific assessment criterion. State clearly that references must be checked against the Harvard format standard.

##### H3: Appendices
> **Attribute + Value**: Supplementary materials that support but do not contain the main argument — organisational charts, survey data, financial tables, frameworks applied; each appendix is labelled (Appendix A, Appendix B) and referenced in the main body ("as shown in Appendix A..."); appendices do not count toward the word count and are not assessed as the primary submission — they are supporting evidence only
> **Common error**: Students place key analytical content in appendices to "save" word count. This is a structural error — the assessor marks the main body; appendix content may not be formally credited.

#### H2: CMI Essay Structure — Section by Section — Major Query Need: Essay format
> **Context vector**: CMI essay as the primary format at Level 3–4 — structure for discursive and analytical assignments
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**: Introduction, PEEL body paragraphs, conclusion, bibliography — each explained with purpose and word count guidance
> **Predicate to use**: "follows" / "uses" / "requires" / "argues"
> **First word of first paragraph**: "A CMI essay uses an academic argument structure..."
> **Word count target**: 450–550 words
> **Internal links**: "CMI essay writing help" → `/cmi-essay-writing-help/`; "CMI Level 3 assignment help" → `/cmi-level-3-assignment-help/`

##### H3: Introduction — 200–250 Words
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Define key terms used in the essay question (e.g., if the question asks about "transformational leadership," define it precisely with a citation)
> - State the essay's argument direction or approach — what position will be argued, or what the essay will cover
> - Outline the essay's structure in one sentence ("This essay first considers... then examines... before concluding...")
> - Does NOT: include evidence or analysis — the introduction sets up the argument, it does not make it
> **Common error**: Students write a very long introduction that begins their analysis. The rule: if a sentence includes evidence, it belongs in the body, not the introduction.

##### H3: Body Paragraphs — PEEL Structure, One Paragraph Per Point
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - PEEL: Point (state the argument or claim), Evidence (cite supporting theory, research, or data), Explanation (explain WHY the evidence supports the point), Link (connect back to the essay question or signal the next point)
> - One clear argument per paragraph — students who mix multiple arguments in a single paragraph produce unfollowable analysis
> - Typical body paragraph length: 250–350 words
> - Total body word count: 70–75% of the total word count (e.g., for a 2,500-word essay: 1,750–1,875 words in the body)
> - Command verb compliance: paragraphs must apply the command verb — Discuss paragraphs present balanced perspectives; Analyse paragraphs decompose and explain relationships; Critically Analyse paragraphs include limitation statements
> **Common error**: Students write descriptive paragraphs — long explanations of what a theory says without applying it to the essay question. Every body paragraph must connect the evidence to the specific question or topic.

##### H3: Conclusion — 200–250 Words
> **Attribute + Value**:
> - Synthesise the key arguments made in the body — no new evidence, no new citations
> - Reach a final position based on the evidence presented (for Discuss essays: which perspective is better supported and why)
> - Does NOT: introduce new information, list what was covered (summative conclusions are weaker than synthetic ones)
> **Common error**: Students end the essay by saying "in conclusion, this essay has discussed..." followed by a bullet-point list of topics. This is a summary, not a synthesis. The conclusion should take a final position, not repeat chapter headings.

##### H3: Bibliography — Harvard Format
> Same requirements as management report bibliography. Minimum 6–8 sources at Level 3–4; 8–10 sources at Level 5.

#### H2: CMI Reflective Account Structure — Gibbs' Reflective Cycle Section by Section — Major Query Need: Reflective account format
> **Context vector**: CMI reflective account as the required format for professional development and reflective practice units — Gibbs' Reflective Cycle as the structuring framework
> **Main entity + attributes to cover**: All six stages of Gibbs' Reflective Cycle applied to a CMI reflective account, with each stage as a distinct section; also brief mention of Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle as an alternative
> **Predicate to use**: "follows" / "describes" / "reflects" / "connects"
> **First word of first paragraph**: "A CMI reflective account is structured around a recognised reflective model..."
> **Word count target**: 500–600 words
> **Internal links**: "CMI assignment tutoring" → `/cmi-assignment-tutoring/`

##### H3: Stage 1 — Description (What Happened?) — 300–400 Words
> **Attribute + Value**: Factual account of the specific experience, event, or situation being reflected upon — what happened, who was involved, what the context was; first-person voice; past tense; no analysis at this stage — description only
> **Common error**: Students combine description and analysis in Stage 1. Gibbs separates these deliberately — the description is the raw data; analysis comes later.

##### H3: Stage 2 — Feelings (What Were You Thinking and Feeling?) — 200–300 Words
> **Attribute + Value**: Personal and emotional response to the experience — what the student thought and felt at the time and afterwards; first-person voice; honest account is required (assessors recognise sanitised responses); does NOT evaluate — just describes the emotional and cognitive reaction
> **Writer note**: Many students (particularly working managers) are uncomfortable with this stage. Reassure the writer that professional reflection includes emotional acknowledgement — CMI Level 5+ explicitly assesses self-awareness as a leadership quality.

##### H3: Stage 3 — Evaluation (What Was Good and Bad About the Experience?) — 400–500 Words
> **Attribute + Value**: Value judgement about the experience — what went well, what did not, and WHY; criteria for the evaluation should be stated (organisational standards, professional expectations, personal development goals); this is where the command verb Evaluate first applies in a reflective account
> **Common error**: Students mix evaluation and analysis. Evaluation (Stage 3) judges good/bad; Analysis (Stage 4) explains why using theory.

##### H3: Stage 4 — Analysis (What Sense Can You Make of the Situation?) — 500–600 Words
> **Attribute + Value**: Theoretical explanation of the experience — apply management theory to explain WHY the experience unfolded as it did; this is where academic sources are cited; typical theories used in reflective accounts: leadership models (Transformational, Situational), team dynamics (Tuckman, Belbin), change management (Kotter, Lewin), communication frameworks; the theory must be applied to the specific experience — not just described
> **Writer note**: This is the highest-weighted section in reflective accounts at Level 5+. The quality of theory application here determines whether the account achieves merit or distinction.

##### H3: Stage 5 — Conclusion (What Else Could You Have Done?) — 200–300 Words
> **Attribute + Value**: Reflects on what the student would do differently in the same situation, and why; draws on the analysis in Stage 4; does NOT repeat what happened (Stage 1) — focuses on what alternative actions were available
> **Common error**: Students write a final summary of the essay rather than a reflective conclusion. The question being answered is: "Given what I now know, what would I do differently?"

##### H3: Stage 6 — Action Plan (If It Arose Again, What Would You Do?) — 200–300 Words + Plan Table
> **Attribute + Value**: Specific, actionable professional development plan — what the student will do to improve the skills, knowledge, or behaviours identified in the reflection; SMART format; often presented as a table: Development Need | Action | Timeline | Resource | Success Measure
> **Writer note**: This stage converts the reflection into professional development action — CMI assessors use this to evaluate whether the student has genuinely engaged with the reflective process or produced an academic exercise. A vague action plan signals the latter.

##### H3: Bibliography — Harvard Format in Reflective Accounts
> Harvard referencing applies in reflective accounts even though the document is in first-person voice. Theories cited in Stage 4 (Analysis) must be referenced with in-text citations and appear in the bibliography.

#### H2: FAQ — CMI Assignment Structure
> **Critical rule**: Must not repeat Main Content — these must address edge cases, clarifications, or level-specific questions not covered above.

**Questions and Answer Directions**:
1. Does a CMI assignment need an executive summary? → It depends on the format. Management reports at Level 5 and above typically require an executive summary as an assessed section — check your unit's assessment criteria or ask your training provider. Essays and reflective accounts do not use an executive summary. If in doubt, include one — it demonstrates professional document structure and will not count against you.
2. How many words should each section of a CMI management report be? → For a 4,000-word management report: Executive Summary (250–300 words), Introduction (200–250 words), Analysis sections (2,200–2,600 words), Conclusion (200–300 words), Recommendations (400–500 words). These are proportional guidelines — always follow your unit brief's specific word count allocation if provided.
3. Can I use bullet points in a CMI assignment? → In management reports, bullet points are appropriate in the recommendations section and in appendices. In the analysis sections, continuous prose is expected — bullet-pointed analysis does not demonstrate the depth of reasoning that Evaluate and Justify command verbs require. In essays, avoid bullet points throughout. In reflective accounts, the action plan table uses structured format rather than prose.
4. Do CMI assignments need a title page and table of contents? → Most training providers require a title page for all submissions. A table of contents is required for management reports and recommended for any report over 3,000 words. Check your training provider's submission guidelines — some have templates.
5. What is the difference between the conclusion and the recommendations in a CMI management report? → The conclusion summarises what the analysis revealed — it reaches a judgement based on the evidence. The recommendations propose specific actions the organisation should take based on those conclusions. They are separate sections with different functions. A conclusion that lists recommendations, or recommendations that include analysis, indicates structural confusion.
6. How many sources do I need for a CMI assignment? → Source requirements scale with level: Level 3 essays typically require a minimum of 6 sources; Level 5 management reports 10–12 sources; Level 7 strategic reports 15–20 sources. Quality matters as much as quantity — academic journals and management publications are preferred over general web sources.

## Micro Semantics Notes for Writer
- **Predicate choices**: "requires" (format requirements), "includes" (section composition), "follows" (structural sequence), "applies" (theory in reflective accounts), "separates" (distinguishing sections from each other)
- **Subject/Object**: "A CMI management report includes..." (format as subject). "The executive summary summarises..." (section as subject). "CMI assessors expect..." (assessor as subject setting expectations)
- **First paragraph**: Establish that format is determined by assessment criteria — not preference. This removes ambiguity and frames the guide as a compliance tool
- **Boolean questions for FAQ**: "Does a CMI assignment need an executive summary?" / "Can I use bullet points in a CMI essay?"
- **Terms to avoid**: "essay" when referring to a management report (many students use these interchangeably — the guide should reinforce the distinction); "just" as a minimiser ("just include a conclusion") — undercuts the importance of structural compliance
- **Active voice throughout**: Sections as subjects performing functions — "the conclusion synthesises the key findings" rather than "the key findings are synthesised in the conclusion"
