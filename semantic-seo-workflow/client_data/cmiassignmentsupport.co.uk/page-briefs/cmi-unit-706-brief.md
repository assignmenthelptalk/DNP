**Key frameworks — all requiring Critically Analytical engagement:**
- Ethical theories: consequentialism/utilitarianism (Mill, Bentham) — judge actions by outcomes, greatest good for the greatest number; deontological ethics (Kant) — categorical imperative, duty-based ethics independent of outcomes; virtue ethics (Aristotle, MacIntyre) — character and practical wisdom (phronesis) rather than rules or outcomes; care ethics (Gilligan, Noddings) — relational, contextual ethics emphasising responsibility to particular others
- Stakeholder ethics — Freeman's stakeholder theory (1984) vs shareholder primacy (Friedman, 1970); the ethical case for stakeholder orientation; ESG (Environmental, Social, Governance) as a governance expression of stakeholder ethics
- Ethical leadership theory — Brown, Treviño and Harrison (2005) — ethical leaders as role models of normatively appropriate conduct; social learning theory mechanism; the distinction between ethical leadership and authentic leadership (George, 2003)
- Organisational culture and ethics — Schein's culture model applied to ethical values; how leaders embed ethics through artefacts, espoused values, and underlying assumptions; the gap between stated ethics and actual behaviour
- Whistleblowing and speak-up culture — the conditions under which employees raise ethical concerns; Edmondson's psychological safety as a precondition for ethical voice; the structural barriers to whistleblowing (career risk, retaliation, organisational silence); UK legal framework (Public Interest Disclosure Act 1998)
- Corporate governance — UK Corporate Governance Code (2024); seven Nolan Principles of Public Life; principal-agent theory and governance mechanisms; board composition and independence; audit and risk committee functions
- Moral disengagement — Bandura (1999) — the psychological mechanisms through which individuals or organisations justify unethical behaviour: moral justification, displacement of responsibility, dehumanisation of victims; relevant for Critically Analysing why ethical frameworks fail in practice

**Common failure points:**
1. Ethical theories described as alternative views rather than Critically Analysed — at L7, consequentialism and deontology need to be examined for their assumptions, contradictions, and practical limitations in complex organisational decisions
2. AC2 reduced to "leaders should model ethical behaviour" — does not engage with the mechanisms (culture embedding, governance design, psychological safety) through which leaders actually shape ethical culture
3. No moral disengagement — a significant gap; explaining why organisations with stated ethical values behave unethically requires engaging with Bandura's mechanisms
4. Corporate governance treated as a compliance checklist rather than Critically Analysed — the UK Corporate Governance Code and Nolan Principles need to be examined for what they achieve and where they fail
5. Whistleblowing absent or superficial — the conditions for ethical voice are central to AC2; omitting them produces an incomplete analysis
