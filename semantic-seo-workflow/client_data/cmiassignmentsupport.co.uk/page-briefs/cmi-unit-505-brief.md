## Critical Distinction from Level 3 EDI Content

CMI Level 3 covers awareness of equality and diversity — knowing what protected characteristics are and what the Equality Act requires. CMI Level 5 requires **Evaluating** organisational EDI practice — examining what the organisation is doing, why it may or may not be effective, and reaching a justified conclusion about what needs to change. The shift is from compliance awareness to critical management practice.

**Key frameworks:**
- Equality Act 2010 — nine protected characteristics (age, disability, gender reassignment, marriage and civil partnership, pregnancy and maternity, race, religion or belief, sex, sexual orientation); direct discrimination, indirect discrimination, harassment, victimisation; employer's duty to make reasonable adjustments; public sector equality duty
- Diversity and Inclusion vs Equality — the distinction: equality = same treatment; equity = fair treatment adjusted for need; diversity = representation; inclusion = belonging and participation; belonging = the felt experience (Shore et al., 2011)
- Unconscious bias — Kahneman System 1 (fast, automatic) vs System 2 (slow, deliberate) thinking; affinity bias, confirmation bias, halo/horns effect, attribution bias; training effectiveness limited (Dobbin and Kalev, 2016)
- Inclusive leadership — Carmeli, Reiter-Palmon and Ziv (2010); Nembhard and Edmondson (2006); creating conditions for voice, participation, and belonging across difference; connection to psychological safety
- Organisational EDI approaches — diversity training (limited evidence), sponsorship and mentoring programmes, structural interventions (blind recruitment, structured interviews), data-driven accountability (pay gap reporting, representation metrics), culture change
- Intersectionality — Crenshaw (1989); overlapping protected characteristics create compound disadvantage; an organisation that is gender-inclusive but not race-inclusive does not have an inclusive culture

**Common failure points:**
1. Listing the nine protected characteristics as the main content of AC1 — legal awareness is not Evaluation; Evaluating legislation means assessing what the Equality Act requires of managers, where it leaves gaps, and how organisational policy extends or falls short of legal requirements
2. AC2 described as HR statistics rather than Evaluated — the impact of discrimination on individual wellbeing (psychological, physical, career) and on organisational performance (disengagement, attrition, litigation, reputational risk) requires evidence-based evaluation
3. AC3 reduced to "we should do diversity training" — the evidence on training effectiveness is weak (Dobbin and Kalev, 2016); structural interventions have stronger evidence but require more from managers
4. No intersectionality — treating each protected characteristic as a separate dimension misses the compound disadvantage experienced at the intersection of multiple characteristics
5. Conflating diversity (representation) with inclusion (belonging) — an organisation can have diverse headcount and still have an exclusionary culture
