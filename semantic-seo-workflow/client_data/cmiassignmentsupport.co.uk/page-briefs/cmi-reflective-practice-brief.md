## EAV Depth Map

**Primary Entity**: CMI reflective practice

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| Definition | The deliberate, structured process of examining one's own professional experience, decisions, and behaviours in order to generate learning and improve future practice — distinct from general self-evaluation |
| CMI context | Appears in reflective accounts (a specific CMI assessment format), in personal development plan (PDP) sections of unit assignments, and in reflective statements that link theory to practice |
| Primary reflective models | Gibbs Reflective Cycle (1988) — 6 stages: Description, Feelings, Evaluation, Analysis, Conclusion, Action Plan; Kolb Experiential Learning Cycle (1984) — 4 stages: Concrete Experience, Reflective Observation, Abstract Conceptualisation, Active Experimentation; Rolfe et al "What? So What? Now What?" (2001) — simplified 3-stage model; Johns Model of Structured Reflection (1994) — guided questions framework |
| Key distinction | Reflection ≠ description. Describing what happened is not reflective practice. Reflective practice requires examining why, what it means, what you learned, and how your practice will change |
| CMI level variation | L3: basic reflection using Gibbs or Rolfe; L5: critical reflection — examining assumptions, challenging own practice, using theory to interpret experience; L7: transformational reflection — examining systemic and structural factors, questioning underlying beliefs, connecting to strategic leadership development |
| Common assessment formats | Reflective account (standalone document, 1,500–3,000 words); reflective section within a unit assignment (typically the final section); CPD reflective log; Personal Development Plan (PDP) |
| What CMI assessors look for | Genuine learning, not just description; connection to management theory; honest examination of what went wrong or could be improved; concrete action planning; use of a reflective model explicitly or implicitly |

## FAQ Items (for FAQPage schema)

1. What is reflective practice in CMI? → The structured, deliberate examination of your own professional experience to generate learning and improve practice. Appears in reflective accounts, PDP sections, and reflective statements within unit assignments across all CMI levels.
2. Which reflective model should I use for CMI? → Gibbs Reflective Cycle (1988) is the most commonly used for CMI assignments — its six-stage structure maps cleanly onto CMI assessment formats. Kolb's Experiential Learning Cycle is useful when the focus is on how learning leads to changed practice. Rolfe et al's "What? So What? Now What?" is simpler and appropriate for shorter reflective sections.
3. What is the difference between description and reflection? → Description explains what happened. Reflection examines why it happened, what it means, what you learned, and how your behaviour or practice will change as a result. CMI assessors are looking for reflection — submitting a description of events does not meet the assessment criteria.
4. How long is a CMI reflective account? → Typically 1,500–3,000 words depending on the unit and level. Level 3 reflective accounts are at the shorter end. Level 5 and Level 7 reflective sections within larger assignments may be shorter but require greater analytical depth.
5. How does reflective practice change at Level 7? → At Level 7, reflective practice moves from examining individual decisions and experiences (Level 3–5) to examining systemic and structural factors — how organisational culture, power structures, and governance shape leadership behaviour. Level 7 reflection connects to transformational and authentic leadership theory and interrogates the assumptions underlying your own leadership practice.
6. Can you help me write a CMI reflective account? → Yes — we support CMI students with reflective accounts at all levels. The most common gap is writing description rather than genuine reflection. We structure your experience through the appropriate reflective model, connect it to relevant management theory, and produce the concrete action planning that CMI assessors require.
