**Key theories and frameworks:**
- Mendelow's Power/Interest Matrix (1991) — 2×2 grid: Keep Satisfied (high power, low interest), Manage Closely (high power, high interest), Monitor (low power, low interest), Keep Informed (low power, high interest)
- Freeman's Stakeholder Theory (1984) — stakeholders as any group or individual who can affect or is affected by the organisation's objectives; internal vs external stakeholder distinction
- Stakeholder salience model — Mitchell, Agle and Wood (1997) — three attributes: power, legitimacy, urgency — used to prioritise stakeholder engagement
- Communication strategy frameworks — tailoring message, channel, and frequency to stakeholder type and position on the Power/Interest matrix
- Conflict management in stakeholder relationships — Thomas-Kilmann conflict model applied to stakeholder conflict (five modes: Competing, Collaborating, Compromising, Avoiding, Accommodating)
- Stakeholder engagement ladder — Arnstein's Ladder of Citizen Participation (adapted for management contexts) — levels from non-participation to full empowerment

**Common failure points:**
1. Mendelow's matrix drawn but not evaluated — mapping stakeholders without evaluating the effectiveness or limitations of the framework
2. Communication strategies listed by stakeholder quadrant without Evaluate depth — what approach, why, with what evidence of effectiveness
3. AC3 (conflicting stakeholder expectations) treated as an extension of AC2 rather than a discrete criterion requiring its own evaluation of conflict management approaches
4. Stakeholders identified but not defined — Freeman's definition and the internal/external distinction are expected as foundational context
5. No organisational context — stakeholder mapping must be applied to a specific project, change, or management scenario
